# REVISION BRIEF — "How Abundance Always Creates Scarcity"

**Purpose:** Guide an AI agent through a revision that fortifies the paper's novelty claims by engaging with prior work that anticipates, overlaps with, or challenges its contributions.

---

## 1. CRITICAL PROBLEM: The core claim has a 55-year lineage the paper ignores

The paper's title claim — "abundance always creates scarcity" — is presented as if the paper discovers this. It does not. The paper must **acknowledge prior articulations** and **reposition its contribution as the formalization of a mechanism**, not the discovery of the phenomenon.

### What to do:

- **Add to Introduction (para 4, gap statement):** Acknowledge that the abundance-creates-scarcity link has been observed before but never formalized into a mechanism with a typology and policy framework. Cite:
  - Simon (1971) — "a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention"
  - Anderson (2009) — "every abundance creates a new scarcity" (p. 243), operationalized as business strategy (monetize the scarce complement)
  - Desai & Lemley (2023) — posed "Will the abundance of some things just create new forms of scarcity in others?" as an open research question
  - Madison, Frischmann, Sanfilippo & Strandburg (2022) — argued "the sources and impacts of information abundance are interwoven with the sources and impacts of resource scarcity"

- **Reframe the contribution statement** (end of Introduction) from "we identify scarcity channeling" to "we formalize scarcity channeling as the first systematic mechanism explaining *how* and *why* abundance converts into specific scarcity types — a phenomenon previously observed (Simon, 1971; Anderson, 2009) but never theorized."

## 2. CONTEXT: This paper is ATARCA work — position it accordingly

This paper emerges from the ATARCA (Accounting Technologies for Anti-Rival Coordination and Allocation) EU Horizon 2020 project (2021–2023). The paper should **explicitly state this provenance** and position scarcity channeling as ATARCA's theoretical contribution — extending the project's earlier work on anti-rival business model patterns (the 2022 ATARCA toolkit of 20 patterns) into a full mechanism-level framework with policy implications. This strengthens rather than weakens novelty: it shows the concept evolved through a funded multi-year research program. Consider adding an acknowledgment or footnote noting ATARCA funding and how this paper advances beyond earlier ATARCA deliverables.

## 3. GOVERNANCE TRAP — engage with close precursors

The "governance trap" (surface metrics healthy while governance erodes) is a strong concept but has near-equivalents:

- **Halfaker et al. (2013)** — already cited, but the paper should note their term "calcification" describes the same dynamic
- **Atlantic Council (2023)** — "Avoiding the Success Trap" report explicitly frames the problem of open source appearing healthy while harboring structural vulnerabilities. Very close to "governance trap."
- **Eghbal (2020)** — *Working in Public* describes how GitHub stars and downloads mask maintainer exhaustion

### What to do:

- **In the governance trap section:** Acknowledge "success trap" (Atlantic Council 2023) and "calcification" (Halfaker et al. 2013) as prior articulations of the same pattern, and position "governance trap" as a more precise formalization grounded in the coupled resource model.

---

## 4. GOVERNANCE INFRASTRUCTURE AS PUBLIC GOOD — strongest novel claim, but cite Eghbal

The paper's most genuinely novel policy contribution is identifying **governance infrastructure** (moderation tools, dispute resolution, contribution tracking) as a distinct public good category. Even Eghbal's *Roads and Bridges* (2016) — the foundational "digital infrastructure = public good" argument — focused on code maintenance, not governance tools. Existing government programs (Sovereign Tech Fund, OpenSSF, NGI Zero) fund code and security, not governance infrastructure.

### What to do:

- **Add to Policy section:** Cite Eghbal (2016) as establishing that open source code is a public good, then show that this paper extends the argument to a previously unrecognized category: governance infrastructure (moderation tools, dispute resolution platforms, contribution tracking). Note that all existing programs (Sovereign Tech Fund ~€23.5M invested, OpenSSF, NGI Zero Commons Fund) fund code/security, not governance tools. This gap validates the paper's policy contribution.
- **Also cite Frischmann (2012)** — *Infrastructure: The Social Value of Shared Resources* — as the theoretical foundation for infrastructure-as-public-good arguments.

---

## 5. COUPLED RESOURCE MODEL — acknowledge it synthesizes existing ideas

The "coupled resource model" has clear precursors:

- Anderson (2009): give away the abundant resource, monetize the scarce complement
- Rochet & Tirole (2003): two-sided market pricing formalization
- Nikander et al. (2020): "data and many other information goods are anti-rival while our economy is centred around rival structures"

### What to do:

- **Add a sentence in Theoretical Foundations § Coupled Resource Model:** Acknowledge that the coupling of abundant and scarce complements has been observed in business strategy (Anderson, 2009) and platform economics (Rochet & Tirole, 2003), but that this paper formalizes it as a *resource-level model* that generates specific predictions about carrying capacity and scarcity channeling.

## 6. MINOR: Clarify the "175 patterns" claim

Gassmann et al. (2014) contains 55 patterns, not 175. The 175 figure appears to come from the paper's own synthesis of 24 articles. The paper already explains this in the Methods section, but the abstract and introduction should not imply "175 business model patterns (Gassmann et al.)" — make clear it is 175 from 24 studies anchored in the Gassmann taxonomy.

## 7. EU POLICY LANDSCAPE — strengthen the policy framework

The paper's three policy instruments map onto an evolving EU landscape that validates some claims and challenges others:

- **Governance infrastructure investment**: Partially anticipated by Germany's Sovereign Tech Fund (2022, now Sovereign Tech Agency) and EU Sovereign Tech Fund proposal (€350M, 2028–2035), but these fund code, not governance tools — the gap the paper identifies is real
- **Business model transparency**: The **least anticipated** instrument — neither the DSA, DMA, nor CRA requires disclosure of how firms benefit from digital commons. The EU CRA (2024) introduced "open-source software steward" as a legal category, beginning to formalize commercial-commons relationships
- **Ecosystem plurality**: Implicitly present in GAIA-X, "Public Money Public Code," and the new EDIC Digital Commons (Dec 2025), but never articulated as a standalone policy goal for constraining scarcity channeling

### What to do:

- **Add to Policy Discussion:** Briefly note these real-world developments as partial validations, and highlight where the paper's framework goes further (especially on governance tools and business model transparency).
