The Abundance Paradox: Scarcity Channeling, Governance Investment, and Innovation Policy

Abstract

Abundance-generating goods—digital resources that increase in value with use—should produce boundless value through sharing, yet organizations that embrace sharing persistently generate new scarcities. We identify scarcity channeling as the structural mechanism underlying this paradox: any organizational arrangement engaging with abundance-generating goods must choose who bears the rival costs that sharing creates, and each choice generates a distinct scarcity. Through systematic analysis of 175 business model patterns (39 actively managing abundance generation), we characterize three organizational directions: restricting abundance generation (creating artificial scarcity), enhancing it with firm subsidies (creating dependency scarcity), and enhancing it with community organization (creating governance scarcity). Each direction has legitimate applications depending on context. Scarcity channeling is structural rather than contingent—better design changes which scarcity is created, not whether scarcity arises. We develop a scarcity-aware innovation policy framework with three instruments: governance infrastructure investment as a public good (the most urgent and novel instrument), business model transparency requirements, and ecosystem plurality—maintaining viable alternatives across directions so that competition constrains channeled scarcity. Abundance compliance criteria provide a diagnostic for evaluating whether business model patterns serve or undermine sharing-generated value.

Keywords: abundance-generating goods; scarcity channeling; information abundance; business model patterns; innovation policy; governance infrastructure; institutional complexity; governance trap

Introduction

In April 2014, a critical vulnerability was discovered in OpenSSL, the encryption library used by approximately two-thirds of all web servers. The software was a paradigmatic “abundance-generating” good—one whose value increases with every user, since each deployment strengthens the ecosystem of interoperable encrypted communication. Yet this maximally shared, maximally valuable piece of security infrastructure was maintained by a single full-time developer. The most widely deployed cryptographic library in the world was simultaneously the most under-resourced. The Heartbleed bug, which had gone undetected for two years, was the direct consequence. This paradox is not an anomaly. Wikipedia, the largest encyclopedia ever created through open sharing, faces declining editor counts and persistent coverage biases (Halfaker, Geiger, Morgan, & Riedl, 2013). Android, subsidized by Google for over three billion devices, locks manufacturers into terms they cannot negotiate (European Commission, 2018). Shared datasets, open-source software, and collaborative platforms all exhibit the same pattern: the very property that makes these goods valuable—increasing returns to sharing—generates new and often hidden forms of scarcity.

The explanation, we argue, is structural. Every abundance-generating resource depends on complementary resources that remain rivalrous: OpenSSL needed security reviewers, Wikipedia needs editors and moderators, Android needs OS maintenance and hardware compatibility testing. These rival complements—governance labor, infrastructure, curation capacity—are depleted by the same participation that creates the abundance. As more users share in the resource, its value grows, but the per-user availability of governance, moderation, and maintenance shrinks. The organization must intervene, and every intervention channels the resulting scarcity somewhere: it can restrict access (creating artificial scarcity), have a firm absorb the costs and extract rents (creating dependency scarcity), or distribute the burden across a community (creating governance scarcity). We call this mechanism scarcity channeling—the structural process by which abundance-driven value creation generates new, characteristic forms of scarcity depending on the organizational path chosen. The question is not whether abundance creates scarcity, but which kind.

Understanding scarcity channeling requires examining how organizations operationally manage the tension between abundance-generating and rival resources—which is fundamentally a business model question. An organization does not choose “restriction” or “enhancement” in the abstract; it adopts specific business model patterns—hidden revenue, freemium, open source, subscription—that implement a direction and create specific, traceable dependencies. Business model patterns are where the institutional choice becomes operationally concrete, where scarcity channeling becomes empirically observable, and where policy intervention becomes actionable. For this reason, we adopt the business model pattern (BMP) perspective (Gassmann, Frankenberger, & Csik, 2014), which treats business models as combinations of reproducible building blocks, as our primary unit of analysis. This lens allows us to trace how scarcity is channeled through specific organizational mechanisms, to identify which pattern combinations create which scarcities, and to develop the operational specificity that innovation policy requires—moving from “firm subsidization creates dependency” (too abstract) to “the hidden revenue pattern combined with platform lock-in creates opacity of data practices and unilateral terms changes” (actionable).

Yet neither the literature on abundance-generating goods nor the business model literature has provided a systematic account of this mechanism. The economic logic of abundance-generating goods—goods whose per-user value increases with the number of users (Cooper, 2006; Weber, 2004)—points toward maximal sharing, and innovation policy has accordingly moved toward openness: open access mandates, open data policies, open-source procurement requirements (Blind, 2012; Edler & Fagerberg, 2017). But the literature has not explained why sharing-oriented arrangements persistently generate new scarcities rather than eliminating scarcity altogether. The literature on abundance-generating goods has established that markets fail to allocate these goods efficiently (Bliss & Egler, 2020; Kubiszewski, Farley, & Costanza, 2010) and that alternative organizing structures are needed (Nikander, Eloranta, Karhu, & Hiekkanen, 2020), but has not identified the mechanism by which these alternatives each produce characteristic scarcities. The business model innovation literature has catalogued patterns (Gassmann et al., 2014; Remane, Hanelt, Tesch, & Kolbe, 2017; Massa, Tucci, & Afuah, 2017) and identified challenges associated with different approaches, but has not grounded these challenges in a unified mechanism. The institutional complexity literature has mapped approaches to managing competing logics (Battilana & Lee, 2014; Daoud, 2018), but has not identified the specific scarcity each approach creates. This gap matters because policy interventions that do not account for scarcity channeling risk merely shifting scarcity from one form to another—mandating open access without funding governance infrastructure, or regulating platform dependency without enabling community alternatives.

The purpose of this paper is to develop the concept of scarcity channeling as the central mechanism governing abundance-driven value creation and to derive a scarcity-aware innovation policy framework from it. We argue that increasing returns to sharing do not eliminate scarcity but structurally channel it: any arrangement that engages with abundance-generating goods must choose who bears the rival costs that sharing creates, and each choice generates a distinct form of new scarcity. We identify three directions — restricting abundance generation (creating artificial scarcity), enhancing it with firm subsidies (creating dependency scarcity), and enhancing it with community organization (creating governance scarcity) — and show that each has legitimate applications depending on context. Drawing on a systematic analysis of 175 business model patterns (39 of which actively manage abundance generation), institutional analysis, and illustrative cases, we characterize each direction’s viability conditions and welfare implications. As the paper’s central policy contribution, we develop a scarcity-aware innovation policy framework built on three instruments: governance infrastructure investment as a public good, business model transparency requirements, and ecosystem plurality — maintaining viable alternatives across directions so that competition between them constrains channeled scarcity.

The paper contributes to three literatures. First, to the literature on abundance-generating goods (Bliss & Egler, 2020; Cooper, 2006; Kubiszewski et al., 2010; Weber, 2004) and the broader information economics of abundance (Raban & Włodarczyk, 2024), we offer scarcity channeling as a mechanism explaining why abundance-driven value creation generates new scarcities rather than eliminating scarcity altogether, and we characterize the three distinct scarcity types this mechanism produces. Second, to business model innovation research (Gassmann et al., 2014; Massa et al., 2017), we introduce abundance compliance as a design criterion that explains why certain pattern combinations succeed or fail, grounding the observation in scarcity channeling rather than abstract compatibility. Third, to innovation policy (Blind, 2012; Edler & Fagerberg, 2017), we develop a scarcity-aware policy framework — centered on governance infrastructure investment, complemented by business model transparency and ecosystem plurality — and provide context-dependent prescriptions that match instruments to scarcity types rather than mandating a single approach.

Theoretical Background

Abundance-generating goods: beyond non-rivalry

The traditional economic taxonomy classifies goods along two dimensions: excludability and subtractability (Ostrom, 1990). Digital goods challenge this framework because they can exhibit negative subtractability: rather than diminishing with consumption, they may increase in value when used (Weber, 2004). Cooper (2006) formalized this property as anti-rivalry. We adopt the term abundance-generating goods to connect this property to the broader shift from scarcity to abundance in information economics (Raban & Włodarczyk, 2024). This terminological choice reflects that anti-rivalry is a specific mechanism through which information abundance manifests. Subsequent work has established a three-way classification: rival goods decrease in value with use, non-rival goods remain unchanged, and abundance-generating goods increase in value with use (Kubiszewski et al., 2010; Nikander et al., 2020). Abundance generation exists on a continuous spectrum. The degree to which a good increases in value with use varies across goods and contexts, and this degree tends to increase as technology evolves. Cloud infrastructure, open APIs, and generative AI all push goods further along the abundance spectrum over time, meaning that the organizational and policy challenges associated with increasing returns to sharing become progressively more pressing.

Abundance generation should be understood within the broader shift from scarcity to abundance in information economics. Raban and Włodarczyk (2024) argue that information economics has been predominantly scarcity-focused, yet information abundance—which they define as “information exceeding informational needs or personal capacities which arrives to an agent through intuitive technologies from a multiplicity of sources presenting high substitutability and accessibility”—is now a first-order phenomenon requiring new theoretical models. Their framing, drawing on Hegel’s dialectics of thesis (information scarcity), antithesis (information abundance), and synthesis, implies that the field must move beyond treating abundance as merely the absence of scarcity. Increasing returns to sharing is a specific mechanism through which information abundance manifests: abundance-generating goods are not just non-scarce but actively increase in value with use, pushing the abundance dynamic to its logical extreme. The scarcity channeling concept that we develop in this paper provides what Raban and Włodarczyk (2024: 12) identify as a missing piece in the literature: a characterization of the “new imperfections” that abundance produces, which “are still not well characterized from an economic perspective.”

A clarification is essential. Abundance generation is not merely network effects, although network effects are one mechanism through which it can arise (Katz & Shapiro, 1985). Network effects describe demand-side complementarities: a telephone network becomes more useful as more people join. Abundance generation describes a property of the good itself: each use, each modification, each instance of sharing adds value to the resource for all users. Open-source software exhibits increasing returns to sharing through code contributions and bug fixes; shared datasets exhibit it through increased statistical power and representativeness; collaborative knowledge bases exhibit it through improved coverage and cross-referencing. Abundance generation is also not merely non-depreciation. A non-rival good (e.g., a broadcast signal) is unchanged by use; an abundance-generating good is improved by use. The distinction is consequential: non-rival goods face underprovision problems but not the scarcity channeling paradox we develop here.

The economic logic that follows from abundance generation is stark. Markets allocate goods through price-based equilibrium, which requires scarcity (Bliss & Egler, 2020; Haeringer, 2018). For abundance-generating goods, two market-compatible approaches exist, and both fail. First, artificial scarcity (DRM, paywalls, proprietary licensing) makes the good market-compatible but forfeits the superlinear value that increasing returns to sharing creates. Second, zero marginal reproduction cost drives the market-clearing price toward zero, under-compensating producers and threatening sustainability (Olleros, 2018). This failure is an instance of a broader breakdown identified in information economics: under conditions of abundance, prices lose their informativeness as an allocative signal (Raban & Włodarczyk, 2024). When zero prices are prevalent, the price mechanism fails to account for information asymmetry or subjective perceptions of value (Shampanier, Mazar, & Ariely, 2007), undermining the market’s ability to coordinate production and consumption of information goods. The increasing role of abundance-generating goods in economic systems thus requires organizing structures beyond markets.

The coupled resource model: abundance generation requires rival complements

Every abundance-generating resource depends on complementary resources that remain rivalrous. Open-source software requires server infrastructure and maintainer labor. Wikipedia articles require editorial review and vandalism patrol. Shared datasets require curation and quality control. This structural dependency is the key to understanding why abundance-driven value creation generates new scarcities rather than eliminating them. The effective value of any abundance-generating resource is determined by the interaction between its abundance potential and the availability of its rival complements. The abundance-generating component grows with participation: more users means more contributions, more bug fixes, more data, more cross-references. But the rival complements—governance labor, moderation capacity, infrastructure resources, curation effort—are depleted by participation: each additional user consumes maintainer attention, moderation capacity, and infrastructure resources. The tension is immediate: sharing-generated value rises with participation while rival complement availability falls.

This coupled model yields a carrying capacity result: effective per-user value is maximized at an optimal community size, beyond which the strain on rival complements outpaces the abundance-driven gains. The implication is that the effective value of any commons is determined not by its abundance potential alone but by the governance of its rival complements. As a community grows, the per-user availability of governance attention, infrastructure capacity, and curation effort declines. If these rival complements are not replenished at the rate they are consumed, the overall quality of the resource degrades even as user numbers and content volume continue to rise. This dynamic explains why many digital commons appear to be thriving by standard metrics—downloads, contributions, user counts—while the underlying governance infrastructure erodes. The three directions in our framework represent different strategies for managing this interaction, and the coupled resource model provides the foundation for understanding why each strategy produces a distinct scarcity.

Institutional complexity and the scarcity–abundance tension

Hybrid systems that combine abundance-generating and rival goods face institutional complexity: the logics governing their rival and abundance-generating components are fundamentally incompatible (Greenwood, Raynard, Kodeih, Micelotta, & Lounsbury, 2011). Organizing rival goods follows the logic of scarcity—Pareto-optimal allocation through markets. Organizing abundance-generating goods follows the logic of abundance: marginal cost is not only zero but potentially negative (Daoud, 2018; Kubiszewski et al., 2010). The institutional logic literature identifies four approaches to managing this complexity (Battilana & Lee, 2014; Kraatz & Block, 2008): compartmentalization (structural separation of conflicting logics), defiance (forcing one logic to dominate), aggregation (negotiating coexistence), and integration (creating a new shared logic). Daoud (2018) mapped these to social strategies for managing the tension between scarcity and abundance logics, establishing a direct theoretical bridge between institutional complexity management and the abundance-generating goods discourse. Raban and Włodarczyk (2024) elaborate this bridge by documenting how the scarcity–sufficiency–abundance continuum (Daoud, 2018; Bååth & Daoud, 2021) operates specifically in information markets, where the coexistence of scarcity and abundance is not transitional but persistent—scarce information coexists with abundant information even when efficient solutions are available. This persistent coexistence is the macroeconomic context within which organizations must manage the scarcity–abundance tension at the operational level.

Our framework builds on this bridge but reframes the strategic landscape in terms of scarcity channeling. The compartmentalization and defiance approaches correspond to what we call restriction—they suppress abundance generation to avoid the institutional tension. The aggregation and integration approaches correspond to enhancement—they embrace abundance generation and accept scarcity channeling. Within enhancement, we distinguish two paths based on who absorbs the rival costs: a subsidizing firm or an organizing community. This distinction is not merely organizational but generates qualitatively different scarcities, as we demonstrate in the scarcity channeling and three directions sections.

Business model patterns as the unit of analysis

As introduced in the Introduction, business model patterns are the level of analysis at which scarcity channeling becomes empirically tractable. The BMP perspective treats business models as combinations of reproducible, configurable building blocks (Alexander, Ishikawa, & Silverstein, 1977; Gassmann et al., 2014). This approach, most comprehensively developed in Gassmann, Frankenberger, and Csik’s (2013) identification of 55 universal BMPs, allows detailed analysis of how organizations manage institutional complexity at the operational level (Ocasio & Radoynovska, 2016; Remane et al., 2017). A further advantage is combinatorial. Real organizations rarely employ a single pattern; they stack multiple patterns, often from different directions. A platform might use open source (Direction 3) for its core software, hidden revenue (Direction 2) for its data practices, and subscription (transform) for premium features. Each layer creates a different dependency, and the interaction between layers may create scarcities that no single layer would generate alone. This combinatorial complexity is visible only at the BMP level, and it is what makes abundance compliance assessment possible: patterns can be identified, classified, and assessed both individually and in combination.

As part of this study, we systematically reviewed the BMP literature, gathering 175 patterns from 24 studies (the methods are detailed in the following section). Our analysis identified seven complexity management mechanisms organized into three metamechanisms for managing the scarcity–abundance tension. Of the 175 patterns, 39 actively manage abundance generation through aggregation or integration approaches, while 109 use compartmentalization (76 patterns) or defiance (33 patterns)—suppressing abundance generation rather than harnessing it. The remaining 27 patterns are neutral with respect to abundance generation. The analysis also revealed that each metamechanism faces a distinctive challenge: an accountability challenge for community-based mechanisms, a transparency challenge for firm-subsidy mechanisms, and a valuation dilemma for transformation mechanisms. These challenges are precisely the channeled scarcities that the present framework formalizes.

Business Model Pattern Discovery: Mechanisms and Patterns for Managing Abundance Generation

Systematic literature review

The business model pattern analysis draws on a systematic review following established guidelines (Denyer & Tranfield, 2009; Page et al., 2021). We searched Scopus and Web of Science using the query string (“business model*” AND (“pattern*” OR “taxonomy” OR “typolog*” OR “archetype*”)), restricting to peer-reviewed articles and books published between 2010 and 2024. The initial search yielded 847 results. After screening titles and abstracts for relevance (studies that identify or catalogue discrete business model patterns), and removing duplicates, 62 full texts were reviewed. Of these, 24 studies met the inclusion criteria: they identify named, discrete business model patterns at a level of specificity comparable to Gassmann et al.’s (2014) 55 patterns (i.e., “freemium” or “crowdsourcing,” not generic categories such as “platform business model”). These 24 studies collectively catalogue 175 unique patterns after de-duplication of identical patterns that appear under different names across studies.

Qualitative content analysis

We analyzed the 175 patterns using directed qualitative content analysis (Hsieh & Shannon, 2005), informed by Daoud’s (2018) institutional complexity framework. The analysis proceeded in three rounds. In the first round, two researchers independently coded each pattern according to its relationship to abundance generation: does the pattern engage with abundance-driven value creation (enhance), suppress it (restrict), or have no systematic relationship (neutral)? Inter-rater agreement was 89%; disagreements were resolved through discussion. This yielded 39 enhance patterns, 109 restrict patterns, and 27 neutral patterns.

In the second round, the 39 enhance patterns were analyzed for the specific mechanism by which they manage the scarcity–abundance tension. Following Elo and Kyngäs (2008), we used both deductive categories (derived from Daoud’s aggregation and integration strategies) and inductive categories (emerging from the data). This yielded seven mechanisms, which we organized into three metamechanisms: large-scale collaboration to minimize individual rival costs (Mechanisms 1–2), seeking abundance leverage from rival investments (Mechanisms 3–5), and transforming the resource for abundance fit (Mechanisms 6–7). In the third round, we analyzed each mechanism for its distinctive challenge—the specific tension or trade-off that organizations employing the mechanism must manage. This analysis revealed the accountability, transparency, and valuation challenges described above, which we subsequently recognized as manifestations of the scarcity channeling mechanism developed in the following section.

Methodological limitations

The BMP literature has inherent limitations. Pattern catalogues are constructed through heterogeneous methods (case studies, surveys, practitioner interviews), and the granularity of pattern definition varies across studies. Our de-duplication decisions inevitably involve judgment about when differently named patterns describe the same mechanism. The temporal scope (2010–2024) may miss emerging patterns, particularly in AI/ML contexts. Finally, the coding of patterns as “enhance” versus “restrict” depends on the abundance-generating property of the focal resource: a subscription pattern restricts a rivalrous good’s distribution but may enhance an abundance-generating good’s reach by funding its complements. We coded based on the predominant use case described in each source study, acknowledging that context-dependence is a feature of the framework rather than a limitation (Potter & Levine-Donnerstein, 1999; Coffey & Atkinson, 1996).

The content analysis identified three metamechanisms through which the 39 abundance-engaging BMPs manage the scarcity–abundance tension. Each metamechanism contains between two and three specific mechanisms, and each faces a distinctive challenge. We present the findings organized by metamechanism, describing the specific mechanisms, their constituent BMPs, and the institutional logic they instantiate.

Metamechanism I: Large-scale collaboration to minimize individual rival costs

The first metamechanism addresses the scarcity–abundance tension by distributing rival costs across a large community, so that each individual’s contribution is small relative to the collective abundance benefit. The underlying institutional logic is one of collective action: if the abundance-generating resource benefits everyone, and if the rival costs of maintaining it can be parceled into individually manageable contributions, then the community as a whole can sustain what no individual could. This metamechanism contains two mechanisms.

The first mechanism, using communities to minimize rival costs, encompasses fourteen patterns that employ community-based production and governance to collectively fund the rivalrous complements of abundance-generating resources. The constituent BMPs include peer-to-peer, open source, crowdsourcing, crowdsourced innovation, community governance, stewardship role, review system, quality through participation, make more of it, nodes, crowdfunding, repurpose for society, revenue sharing, and pay what you want. The common logic is that each participant contributes a small amount of rival labor—code review, content moderation, infrastructure hosting—in exchange for access to the collective abundance benefit. Consider, for example, an online crowdsourced film studio in which audiences help creators build audiences through crowdfunding and can watch movies and shows through on-demand streaming. The rival costs of production, curation, and platform maintenance are distributed across the community, while the abundance-generating resource—the growing library of films and the audience itself—benefits from each new participant. Within this mechanism, several patterns extend community involvement beyond content creation to the governance of the abundance-generating resource itself. The “stewardship role” (Bocken, Short, Rana, & Evans, 2014) and “community governance” (Curtis, 2021) patterns use community-based decision-making to manage the abundance-generating resource, ensuring that governance costs are distributed alongside content creation costs. At the industrial level, the “open business model” pattern extends collaboration to inter-firm partnerships where cocreating companies actively seek ways to partner and extend their shared resource base (Gassmann et al., 2013).

The second mechanism, facilitating shared ownership, encompasses two patterns. Fractional ownership and no dividends patterns enable collective ownership of the infrastructure and governance of abundance-generating resources, aligning individual incentives with collective value creation. By converting ownership from a concentrated to a distributed form, these patterns address the rival cost challenge at the institutional level: rather than requiring ongoing contributions of labor, they restructure the ownership of the underlying infrastructure so that rivalrous costs are shared by design.

The large-scale collaboration metamechanism faces a persistent accountability challenge. When the abundance benefit is available to all regardless of contribution, the community must find ways to ensure active participation and prevent free-riding. This challenge intensifies with scale: coordination overhead grows as the community expands, and the signal-to-noise ratio for governance labor declines. Communities must develop governance structures that make contribution visible, reward governance labor, and manage the growing coordination demands that accompany growth. The accountability challenge is, in essence, the organizational manifestation of the governance scarcity that our framework formalizes in the scarcity channeling section.

Metamechanism II: Seeking abundance leverage from rival investments

The second metamechanism addresses the scarcity–abundance tension by having a firm invest in rivalrous complements and extract value through the abundance dimension—leveraging the increasing returns from participation to generate revenue without per-unit access fees. The underlying institutional logic is one of strategic subsidization: a firm covers the rival costs that would otherwise constrain sharing, thereby maximizing participation in the abundance-generating resource, and then extracts a return through mechanisms that are enhanced by the larger user base. This metamechanism contains three mechanisms.

The first mechanism, subsidizing sharing-related rival costs, encompasses five patterns: hidden revenue, free (ad-supported), sponsorship, experience broker, and social mission. These patterns enable a firm to cover the rival costs of infrastructure, moderation, and maintenance while extracting value through advertising, data monetization, or third-party subsidies. Consider, for example, a work-related social media platform that provides infrastructure for professionals to connect. Sharing of work-related content, links, and information is maximized, and the infrastructure costs are subsidized with revenues generated from advertising and premium subscriptions. The subsidizing firm’s motivation is that the disproportionate return from the abundance-generating resource—a growing network of professionals whose data and attention can be monetized—more than compensates for the rival costs of maintaining the platform.

The second mechanism, shifting the revenue focus to abundance-driven results, encompasses three patterns: performance-based contracting, guaranteed availability, and result-as-a-service. Rather than charging for access to the rival resource, these patterns shift the pricing unit to outcomes that benefit from the abundance dimension. An online media agency, for instance, might distribute advertisements across a large-scale content network where plain visibility is nearly free, but customers pay based on conversions—measurable outcomes that increase in value as the network grows. The third mechanism, creating parallel offerings, encompasses three patterns: freemium, dual licensing, and charging for changes. These create tiered access where a free tier maximizes participation in the abundance-generating resource and a paid tier generates revenue from users willing to pay for enhanced features or commercial licenses. A reader-funded news platform, for example, might offer its journalism to everyone for free while providing premium features and community access to paying subscribers.

The firm-subsidy metamechanism faces a transparency challenge. The subsidizing firm has structural incentives to opacity, because its ability to extract value—through data monetization, algorithmic control, or lock-in—depends on users not fully understanding or being able to replicate the extraction mechanism. Consumers may question the fairness of the trade-off between the free access they receive and the data, attention, or autonomy they surrender. The tension between extraction (which requires opacity) and participation (which rewards trust) is the operational manifestation of the dependency scarcity that our framework formalizes. This transparency challenge is inherent in the leverage-seeking logic: the generation of substantial output from relatively minor input calls into question the degree to which information asymmetries between providers and consumers are managed equitably.

Metamechanism III: Asset and offering transformation

The third metamechanism addresses the scarcity–abundance tension by restructuring the relationship between the rival and abundance-generating components of the offering, reducing the coupling between them. Rather than embracing the full institutional complexity of a hybrid system, transformation strategies seek to decrease the complexity by converting the offering into a form that is more compatible with abundance generation. This metamechanism contains two mechanisms.

The first mechanism, de-transactionalization, encompasses four patterns: subscription, flat rate, membership, and license. These patterns replace per-use transactions with recurring relationships, reducing the need to create artificial scarcity for each access event while providing stable revenue to fund rivalrous complements. A music streaming platform, for instance, employs a subscription model in which users pay a recurring fee for unrestricted access to a vast library of music, disregarding the amount of use. This shift from per-unit transactions to relational contracts reduces the institutional tension between rival-good pricing logic and abundance-driven sharing logic. The second mechanism, converting the resource for abundance fit, encompasses four patterns: digitalization, sharing business, unbundling, and demand-oriented platform. These patterns restructure the offering to increase the abundance-generating proportion and reduce the rival proportion of value delivered. A platform that converts music production assets into easily shareable and recombinable formats—samples, loops, virtual instruments—exemplifies this approach: by digitizing and modularizing the rival resource, the business increases the share of value that benefits from abundance dynamics.

The transformation metamechanism faces a valuation dilemma. De-transactionalization reduces extraction opportunities (the firm captures less per interaction), while resource conversion may reduce the value of the rivalrous component that the firm is best positioned to provide. Customers must assess whether the value of paid access is sufficiently high in comparison to the free version, while providers must balance the depth of transformation against the sustainability of their revenue model. This dichotomy creates a constant balancing act: the organization must find a level of transformation that sustains revenue without fully suppressing the abundance benefit. Too much transformation erodes the revenue base; too little leaves the abundance potential unrealized.

Scarcity Channeling: A Framework of the Abundance Paradox

The paradox: why abundance generation creates new scarcities

The intuitive expectation is that abundance-generating goods should produce unbounded value. If each user adds value for all other users, then maximal sharing should produce maximal welfare. The null hypothesis is that organizational innovation—better business models, smarter platforms, stronger communities—can fully capture abundance-driven value without creating new scarcities. This null is compelling because it aligns with the dominant narrative in digital innovation policy, which treats openness as inherently welfare-enhancing and organizational failures as contingent problems solvable through better design.

The coupled resource model demonstrates why the null fails. As established in the coupled resource model, the effective value of any abundance-generating resource depends on the interaction between its abundance potential and the availability of its rival complements. The null hypothesis holds only when rival complements scale at least as fast as sharing-generated value—that is, when the supply of governance, infrastructure, and curation grows commensurately with demand. In practice, this condition is generically violated. Governance costs typically grow faster than linearly with community size: Brooks’s Law observes that coordination overhead grows quadratically with team size; content moderation demand grows faster than content volume; infrastructure costs accelerate with peak-load capacity requirements. When rival costs grow faster than sharing-generated value, the abundance-generating resource reaches a carrying capacity, and beyond that point, additional participation degrades effective value. The organization must intervene, and every intervention channels scarcity. Scarcity channeling is therefore structural, not contingent. It is not an organizational failure but a property of the coupled resource system. Better organizational design can change which scarcity is created and how much scarcity is created, but it cannot eliminate the channeling itself.

Three types of channeled scarcity

The first direction—restriction—creates artificial scarcity of the abundance-generating resource itself. When an organization restricts access through paywalls, DRM, proprietary licensing, or per-unit pricing, it forfeits the superlinear value that open sharing would create. The welfare loss from exclusion is superlinear: excluding users from an abundance-generating good destroys not just their direct access but the value they would have added for everyone else. This loss accelerates as the good becomes more strongly abundance-generating, because each excluded user represents a larger forgone contribution to the collective value. The welfare cost of restriction is therefore not merely the sum of individual access losses but a compounding loss that grows faster than the number of excluded users.

The second direction—firm subsidization—creates dependency scarcity. The subsidizing firm covers the rival costs and maximizes participation, but chooses a level of transparency that balances extraction against trust. Some users are captive—they remain regardless of transparency, due to network effects or switching costs—while others respond to the firm’s transparency level. The firm’s profit-maximizing transparency is generically below the social optimum, because the firm does not internalize the full value that transparency creates for users and the broader ecosystem. Scarcity manifests as opacity of data practices, lock-in through switching costs, algorithmic control of user experience, and unilateral terms changes. Dependency scarcity is amplified by the market concentration dynamics that are inherent to information abundance: as Raban and Włodarczyk (2024: 7) observe, exponential growth of information production induces market concentration through platformization, and dominant platforms “tend to have little interest in the content” itself—the quantity rather than quality of information drives profits (Ma, 2023). The same conditions that make abundance-generating goods valuable also drive the platform concentration that makes firm-subsidy extraction possible. The channeled scarcity is concentrated: one entity holds the leverage, creating an asymmetric power relationship between the subsidizing firm and the users who depend on it.

The third direction—community organization—creates governance scarcity. Governance is costlier than content creation: reviewing a pull request is harder than writing code; moderating a discussion is harder than posting; auditing a security library is harder than using it. If the governance premium—the additional compensation, in whatever form, that governance contributors receive—falls below the cost differential between governance and content work, the governance-to-content ratio declines exponentially and the community enters a governance trap. Content grows and the abundance-generating resource appears healthy, but governance erodes: the rivalrous complement deteriorates while surface metrics remain positive. The community collapses not when content stops growing but when governance can no longer sustain the infrastructure that content depends on. The channeled scarcity is diffuse—burnout, quality decline, and infrastructure decay—and its invisibility makes it especially dangerous. This dynamic is an instance of a general pattern identified in information economics: under conditions of information abundance, “even if information in general is abundant, information of good quality becomes scarce” (Raban & Włodarczyk, 2024: 10). As content proliferates, the capacity to curate, verify, and govern that content—to distinguish signal from noise and quality from misinformation (Stiglitz & Kosenko, 2024)—becomes the binding constraint. The governance trap is the organizational mechanism through which this general abundance-quality paradox operates in specific communities.

Properties of the three scarcities

The three channeled scarcities differ systematically along three dimensions that have direct implications for policy design. The first is visibility. Artificial scarcity is the most visible: users directly experience paywalls, access restrictions, and DRM limitations. Dependency scarcity is semi-visible: users experience it gradually as lock-in accumulates, alternatives diminish, and terms change unilaterally, but the causal connection to the business model pattern is often obscured. Governance scarcity is the least visible: surface metrics (user count, content volume, download statistics) remain healthy during the decline phase while governance erodes underneath. Prospect theory provides a behavioral mechanism for this asymmetry: because losses are experienced more intensely than gains of equivalent magnitude (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979), the abundance-side benefits of increasing returns to sharing are quickly absorbed into the baseline expectation, while the gradual erosion of governance quality does not register as a loss until it becomes catastrophic. As Raban and Włodarczyk (2024) note, this asymmetry also explains why the economics literature has focused disproportionately on scarcity: scarcity is felt, while abundance is taken for granted. The Heartbleed and left-pad cases illustrate this: the abundance-generating resources appeared maximally healthy by every standard metric right up until catastrophic failure.

The second dimension is concentration. Artificial scarcity is distributed among excluded users. Dependency scarcity is concentrated in a single entity (the subsidizing firm), creating an asymmetric power relationship. Governance scarcity is diffuse, spread across the entire community of potential governance contributors. Concentrated scarcity is easier to regulate but harder to dislodge; diffuse scarcity is harder to identify but more amenable to institutional design interventions. The third dimension is policy amenability. Artificial scarcity can be addressed by competition policy and compulsory licensing. Dependency scarcity requires transparency mandates, interoperability requirements, and data portability standards. Governance scarcity requires public investment in governance infrastructure and institutional design that increases the governance premium. Each scarcity type requires a different policy toolkit, and mismatching the tool to the scarcity is not merely inefficient but potentially counterproductive.

Three Directions for Managing the Paradox

Direction 1: Restrict abundance generation

Restriction treats the abundance-generating good as if it were rival. Per-unit pricing, digital rights management, paywalls, proprietary licensing, and artificial scarcity all fall under this category. These correspond to the 109 BMPs identified in the compartmentalization and defiance categories of our analysis—they avoid the scarcity–abundance tension by suppressing abundance generation. Restriction is viable and even welfare-maximizing when the good is only weakly abundance-generating. In this regime, the superlinear value from open sharing is modest, and the welfare cost of artificial scarcity is small relative to the benefit of stable financing through per-unit revenue. Restriction is also appropriate when the rivalrous complements are expensive and no feasible subsidy or community mechanism exists to cover them—such as early-stage pharmaceutical development, where the rivalrous costs (clinical trials, regulatory compliance) vastly exceed any sharing-generated value from shared access to compound data.

As abundance generation strengthens, however, the welfare cost of restriction grows superlinearly while the cost of enhancement mechanisms may remain constant or decline due to technology-driven reductions in coordination costs. The price mechanism that restriction relies upon also becomes less effective: under abundance conditions, zero prices invite overconsumption while economic framing induces an evaluative mindset that distorts use (Raban & Włodarczyk, 2024; Shampanier et al., 2007), and restriction artificially re-imposes scarcity pricing in a regime where the allocative function of prices has already broken down. Beyond a certain threshold, enhancement strategies become viable even against an established restriction regime—a single firm or community adopting enhancement captures enough abundance-driven value to compensate for the rival costs. Consider a gene-editing technique with moderate abundance generation, fragmented by a dozen overlapping patents, each granting access with some probability. The cumulative effect of layered exclusion means that only a fraction of researchers gain full access. Because welfare loss from restriction is superlinear in the number of excluded users, the vast majority of the technique’s potential value is destroyed—each excluded researcher represents not just their own lost access but the contributions they would have made to the collective knowledge base. This superlinear loss is the fundamental argument against restriction for strongly abundance-generating goods.

Direction 2: Enhance with firm subsidies — dependency scarcity

In this direction, a firm subsidizes rival costs, maximizes participation in the abundance-generating resource, and extracts value through mechanisms other than per-unit access fees. This corresponds to Metamechanism II (the firm-subsidy metamechanism), which includes subsidizing sharing (hidden revenue, free/ad-supported, sponsorship) and shifting revenue toward abundance-driven results (performance-based contracting, result as a service). The subsidizing firm faces a structural tension between extraction and participation. Information rent requires opacity—the firm’s ability to extract value from user data, attention, and behavior depends on users not fully understanding or being able to replicate the extraction mechanism. Trust-based revenue requires transparency—users participate more when they understand and trust the platform. The firm chooses a level of transparency, and participation responds: some users are captive (they remain regardless of transparency, due to network effects or switching costs), while the remaining users are responsive to the firm’s transparency level. The firm’s optimization yields a transparency level that balances extraction against participation, but this level is generically below the social optimum, because the firm does not internalize the full value that transparency creates for users and the broader ecosystem.

Users in the firm-subsidy path trade agency for access, and they do so not primarily from irrationality but from rational inattention (Sims, 2003): under conditions of information abundance, agents optimally allocate limited cognitive resources, and monitoring the dependency structures of platforms is a low-priority attention expenditure relative to the immediate utility of the service. This mechanism, identified by Raban and Włodarczyk (2024) as characteristic of agent behavior under abundance, explains why dependency scarcity persists even among sophisticated users—it is rational to ignore it until a crisis makes it salient. The channeled scarcity manifests in several forms: opacity of data collection and monetization practices; algorithmic curation that serves the firm’s extraction objectives rather than user interests; platform-specific lock-in through proprietary formats, accumulated data, and social graph control; and unilateral terms changes that users cannot meaningfully resist. This scarcity is concentrated—a single entity holds the leverage—which makes it qualitatively different from the diffuse governance scarcity of the community path. The concentration of dependency scarcity has important regulatory implications: because the scarcity is localized in an identifiable actor, it is more amenable to targeted regulation (antitrust action, transparency mandates) than the diffuse governance scarcity of community-organized resources.

Direction 3: Enhance with community organization — governance scarcity

In the community path, the community collectively covers rival costs. This corresponds to Metamechanism I (the large-scale collaboration metamechanism), which includes community-based production, governance, and shared ownership patterns. The BMPs—crowdsourcing, open source, community governance, stewardship, peer-to-peer, revenue sharing—eliminate dependency on any single firm. Within a community, participants specialize: some create sharing-generated value (content, code, data), others maintain the rivalrous complement (governance, moderation, infrastructure). Governance is costlier than content creation: reviewing a pull request is harder than writing code; moderating a discussion is harder than posting; auditing a security library is harder than using it. The governance contributor must receive a premium to compensate for this additional cost.

If the governance premium falls below the cost differential between governance and content work, the governance-to-content ratio declines exponentially and the community enters a governance trap. Content grows and the abundance-generating resource appears healthy, but governance erodes and the rivalrous complement deteriorates. Surface metrics—user count, stars, downloads, content volume—remain positive during the decline phase, creating a dangerous illusion of health. The community collapses not when content stops growing but when governance can no longer sustain the infrastructure that content depends on. The governance premium must be stacked from multiple sources to reach the threshold: decision-making power, reputational returns, financial compensation, strategic access, and social recognition. These five premium sources correspond to what information economics identifies as the non-pecuniary currencies that become central under abundance conditions. Raban and Włodarczyk (2024: 11) note the “vast opportunity to innovate through pricing with money or non-pecuniary currencies such as reputation, attention, influence and others which are often complementary and non-rival,” and observe that “prices as carriers of value can be numeric but may also appear in terms of time, attention, reputation and more.” The governance premium is thus grounded in the broader economics of non-monetary value exchange in abundance regimes, not an ad hoc organizational construct. A community relying on a single premium source is fragile; one relying on a diversified portfolio is robust.

A further paradox arises from falling content costs. The governance trap condition depends on the differential between governance costs and content creation costs. When content creation becomes easier through AI-generated code, low-code tools, or generative AI, content creation costs fall, which widens the gap and makes the governance trap harder to avoid. Communities must respond by increasing the governance premium or reducing governance costs, not by celebrating content growth. This has immediate policy relevance: the current wave of generative AI makes governance investment more urgent, not less.

The coupling dial: transform as mitigation

Within either enhancement path, transform strategies are available as a mitigation mechanism. De-transactionalization (subscription, flat rate), parallel offerings (freemium, dual licensing), and resource conversion (digitalization, unbundling) reduce the coupling between rival and abundance-generating components. This simultaneously reduces both the abundance benefit and the channeled scarcity. Transform is not a fourth direction but a parameter within Directions 2 and 3. Within the firm path, de-transactionalization (moving from per-use data extraction to flat-rate subscription) reduces the extraction opportunity and the dependency. Within the community path, modularization and interface simplification reduce governance demands. In both cases, the coupling moves from high (full enhancement, high scarcity channeling) to low (partial enhancement, low scarcity channeling). A fundamental constraint applies: on a shared interface, partial commitment to either full enhancement or full transformation produces worse outcomes than committing fully to one approach. The optimum is at a corner. This explains why hybrid strategies often fail unless they separate interfaces architecturally—for example, a freemium model with clearly distinct free and premium tiers rather than a sliding scale.

Illustrative Cases

The following cases illustrate how scarcity channeling operates through specific business model pattern combinations across different sectors and organizational forms. The purpose is not to rank directions but to demonstrate that each creates real, identifiable scarcities and that the choice between them is context-dependent.

OpenSSL and the Heartbleed pattern

OpenSSL is perhaps the starkest illustration of the governance trap. The software library provided encryption for approximately 66% of all web servers—a maximally abundance-generating resource where every user benefits from every security patch and compatibility improvement. Yet the rivalrous complement (security review, code maintenance, vulnerability testing) was provided by effectively one full-time developer. The ratio of maintenance capacity to demand approached zero: demand for security review scaled with the millions of deployments, while supply remained constant. The 2014 Heartbleed vulnerability was the direct consequence—a critical flaw that went undetected for two years because the governance infrastructure was catastrophically under-resourced.

The community appeared maximally healthy by every surface metric (downloads, deployments, citations) right up until catastrophic failure. This is precisely the pattern the governance trap predicts: content-side metrics (code contributions, adoption, integration into other software) remained strong while the governance-side capacity collapsed. The governance premium was insufficient across all dimensions: no meaningful financial compensation, limited reputational reward, no decision-making power structure beyond the lone maintainer, and no strategic value proposition for potential governance contributors. The OpenSSL case illustrates a broader pattern common to infrastructure software: because the abundance-generating resource is invisible when functioning correctly, neither users nor potential governance contributors recognize the governance deficit until a crisis makes it visible.

The post-crisis response—the Core Infrastructure Initiative, later the Open Source Security Foundation—represents a belated attempt to raise the governance premium through institutional investment. Foundations provided financial compensation, the crisis itself created reputational value for security contributors, and the industry’s coordinated response established governance structures that had not previously existed. But the reactive nature of this response illustrates the fundamental visibility problem with governance scarcity: it was invisible until it produced a catastrophe, and the institutional investment came only after the damage was done. The case demonstrates that community-governed abundance-generating resources offer the profound benefit of universal access without concentrated dependency, but that these benefits are sustainable only if governance infrastructure is proactively maintained rather than retroactively funded.

Google Android

Android exemplifies the firm-subsidy direction at massive scale. Google provides the Android operating system as an open-source project (AOSP), subsidizing the substantial rival costs of OS development, security maintenance, and hardware compatibility testing. The abundance-driven value is immense: every app developed for Android benefits all Android users, and every user attracts more developers. Google covers the rival costs and extracts value through the Google Mobile Services (GMS) suite—mandatory licensing of Play Store, Search, Maps, and other services that embed Google’s data collection and advertising business model. The result is massive participation (over three billion active devices) and a rich ecosystem of millions of apps, achieved at low entry cost for both users and manufacturers through continuous infrastructure investment by the subsidizing firm.

The dependency scarcity, however, is equally concrete. Device manufacturers who want GMS must accept Google’s terms (the Mobile Application Distribution Agreement), including default search engine placement and pre-installed apps. Users gain access to a massive abundance-generating ecosystem but cede control over their data practices and default services to Google. The 2018 European Commission antitrust decision (resulting in a €4.34 billion fine) targeted precisely this dependency structure: the bundling of GMS with the open-source Android platform created a mandatory coupling between the abundance-generating resource (the OS and app ecosystem) and the firm’s extraction mechanism (data collection and advertising). Forked alternatives—Huawei’s HarmonyOS, Amazon’s Fire OS—demonstrate the cost of exit: without GMS, much of the abundance-generating ecosystem value is lost, revealing how deeply the dependency scarcity is embedded in the architecture of the platform.

The Android case is theoretically instructive because it illustrates the structural tension at the heart of the firm-subsidy direction. Google’s subsidization of rival costs enables far greater abundance-driven value creation than any community-organized alternative has achieved in the mobile operating system space. The dependency scarcity this creates is the price of that value creation—and whether the price is acceptable depends on contextual factors that the scarcity channeling framework can identify but that no universal prescription can resolve. What the framework does reveal is that the dependency scarcity is concentrated, semi-visible, and amenable to transparency mandates and interoperability requirements—precisely the policy instruments that the European Commission has begun to deploy.

Wikipedia

Wikipedia exemplifies both the promise and the governance challenge of the community path. The articles exhibit increasing returns to sharing, as quality improves with a growing editor base, and the community has built the largest encyclopedia in human history. The rivalrous complements—editor attention, content curation, dispute resolution, vandalism patrol—face increasing demand with scale. Wikipedia has responded with elaborate governance institutions (featured article process, deletion policies, arbitration committees, barnstars for recognition), which represent successful premium stacking: governance contributors receive decision-making power (admin rights), reputation (community recognition), and social standing. This diversified governance premium has sustained the project for over two decades—a remarkable achievement that validates the community direction as a viable long-term organizational form.

However, the community faces ongoing challenges that illustrate the persistent nature of governance scarcity even when the governance premium is adequate. Active editor counts have been declining since 2007. Gender and geographic biases in coverage persist despite sustained efforts to address them. Bureaucratic overhead discourages new contributors, and the signal-to-noise ratio for governance labor declines as the encyclopedia grows: each new article requires more curation, each new editor requires more socialization, and each dispute requires more elaborate resolution. Wikipedia illustrates that even successful governance can experience persistent governance scarcity—the premium stacking is sufficient to sustain the project but not sufficient to resolve the structural challenges that the coupled resource model predicts. The Wikimedia Foundation’s increasing role as an infrastructure provider introduces elements of the firm-subsidy direction, creating a hybrid arrangement that partially alleviates governance scarcity but introduces new dependency considerations.

Linux

Linux represents the most successful large-scale community-governed abundance-generating resource. The kernel codebase is maximally abundance-generating: every driver, security patch, and optimization benefits all users. The governance scarcity inherent in the community path has been addressed through a sophisticated multi-layered approach. The Linux Foundation provides institutional infrastructure, delivering strategic access to industry trends and financial compensation for core maintainers. Maintainer hierarchies provide decision-making authority. The Linus Torvalds model of “benevolent dictator for life” provides reputational anchoring, establishing a clear quality standard and a visible governance structure. Corporate contributors (Red Hat, Google, Intel, Microsoft) represent a hybrid element: they subsidize rival costs (developer salaries, testing infrastructure) in exchange for strategic influence, introducing elements of the firm-subsidy direction within a predominantly community-governed framework.

Linux’s success depends on this hybrid arrangement, in which corporate firm-subsidy patterns co-exist with community governance without undermining community autonomy. The governance premium is adequately stacked across all five dimensions, but the community faces ongoing dependency risks: corporate contributors can shift priorities, and the maintainer pipeline is perpetually thin relative to demand. Recent maintainer burnout incidents illustrate that even successful community governance faces persistent governance scarcity. The hybrid model works because the firm-subsidy elements (corporate funding) and community elements (governance authority) operate on architecturally separated interfaces, avoiding the commitment trap that arises when enhancement and extraction compete on the same interface. The Linux case thus illustrates both the viability of community governance at scale and the practical importance of architectural separation in hybrid arrangements.

AI/ML commons

Open AI models (LLaMA, Mistral, Stable Diffusion) exhibit strong abundance generation: shared weights, fine-tuning techniques, and benchmarks become more valuable with more users and contributors. But the rivalrous complement—compute for training and inference—is intensely concentrated. Training a frontier model requires hundreds of millions of dollars in GPU compute, concentrating the ability to create foundational abundance-generating resources among a handful of firms. This creates a hybrid structure where the abundance-generating resource (model weights) follows the community direction (open release, community fine-tuning) while the rivalrous complement (compute) follows the firm-subsidy direction (firm-subsidized, with the releasing firm capturing strategic advantage through ecosystem lock-in, API dependency, and fine-tuning infrastructure control).

The dependency scarcity is subtle but real: communities can use and adapt open models but cannot produce new foundational models without access to compute concentrated in a few firms. The “open” label may thus overstate the community’s autonomy while obscuring the dependency structure. This case illustrates why business model transparency is essential: without visibility into the relationship between the open release (a community-direction surface) and the compute dependency (a firm-subsidy substrate), neither users nor policymakers can assess the true scarcity channeling at work. The AI safety and governance discourse has focused primarily on model capabilities rather than on the business model structures that determine who controls access, who captures value, and who bears the costs of the rivalrous complements. The scarcity channeling framework suggests that this focus is incomplete: the organizational architecture of AI development—who subsidizes what, and what dependencies this creates—is as consequential for long-term welfare as the capabilities of any particular model.

Scarcity-Aware Innovation Policy

The framework implies that no single direction is universally optimal. Instead, the welfare-maximizing direction depends on context—specifically on the strength of abundance generation, the cost structure of rivalrous complements, the availability of governance infrastructure, and the existing institutional landscape. Raban and Włodarczyk (2024) argue that information economics must develop new models and policy instruments appropriate for abundance conditions, observing that “market failure under abundance has yet to be modelled to suggest novel solutions, and, where needed, external regulation.” The context-dependent prescriptions that follow, and the policy instruments developed in the policy instruments section, are specific contributions to this agenda. We identify four context-dependent policy prescriptions.

When to accept restriction

Restriction is welfare-maximizing when the good is only weakly abundance-generating, so the superlinear value from open sharing is modest and the welfare cost of artificial scarcity is small; when rivalrous complements are expensive and non-substitutable (e.g., clinical trials, physical manufacturing), so neither firm subsidy nor community organization can feasibly cover them; or when the good is in an early development phase where investment recovery requires temporary exclusion (pharmaceutical patents, early-stage R&D). The appropriate policy action is to ensure that restriction mechanisms are time-limited and scope-appropriate. Patent terms for strongly abundance-generating goods (software, data, algorithms) should be shorter than for weakly abundance-generating goods (physical inventions). Compulsory licensing provisions should activate when abundance generation demonstrably exceeds the original estimates at the time of patent granting.

When to enable firm subsidies (with safeguards)

Firm subsidies are welfare-maximizing when abundance generation is strong but community governance capacity is insufficient—the governance premium falls short of the cost differential—so the abundance-driven value justifies enhancement but no viable community path exists; when rivalrous complements require scale investment that exceeds community capacity (e.g., large-scale cloud infrastructure, content moderation at billion-user scale); or when rapid deployment matters (public health information, emergency communication) and firm infrastructure already exists. The appropriate policy action is to accept firm subsidies but constrain the dependency scarcity they create through three instruments.

The first instrument is transparency requirements: mandatory disclosure of data practices, algorithmic decision-making, and business model patterns (developed in the policy instruments section). The marginal welfare gain from transparency is highest at low transparency levels—the first unit of disclosure has the largest effect. The second instrument is interoperability and data portability: ensuring that users can exit the firm-subsidy arrangement. Without mobility, the community alternative is notional rather than credible. The EU Digital Markets Act’s interoperability requirements for messaging services exemplify this approach. The third instrument is the credible-threat mechanism: maintaining viable community alternatives so that firm-subsidy platforms face competitive discipline. When governance investment lowers the cost of governance and raises the governance premium, community alternatives become viable. This gives users an outside option, which in turn reduces the captive user fraction. As fewer users are captive, firms must compete on transparency to retain participation. The chain is self-reinforcing: governance policy enables community viability, which creates an outside option, which forces firms to compete on transparency.

When to invest in community organization

Community organization is welfare-maximizing when abundance generation is strong and governance capacity is sufficient or can be built (the governance premium meets or exceeds the cost differential, or the premium can be raised through institutional design); when the good serves public-interest functions where concentrated dependency is unacceptable (critical infrastructure, democratic discourse, scientific knowledge); or when long-term sustainability matters more than rapid deployment, since community governance is slower to establish but more resilient once established. The appropriate policy action is to invest in governance infrastructure as a public good.

This investment takes several forms. First, lowering governance costs by funding open-source governance tooling—moderation tools, dispute resolution platforms, automated compliance checking, contribution tracking systems—analogous to funding physical infrastructure that enables market competition. Second, increasing the governance premium by creating institutional conditions for premium stacking: tax treatment that recognizes governance labor as a contribution; portability of reputation across platforms; legal frameworks for community revenue sharing; antitrust policy that preserves the strategic value of community participation. Third, addressing the falling-content-cost paradox: as generative AI reduces content creation costs, the governance gap widens, and policy must anticipate this by proactively investing in governance capacity before the gap becomes unsustainable. Fourth, supporting federated governance models. Federation, as exemplified by Mastodon, Matrix, or the Fediverse more broadly, reduces governance costs through parallelization. Governance demand grows faster than linearly with community size, so splitting the community into federated sub-communities reduces total governance demand substantially. The larger the community and the steeper the scaling of governance demand, the greater the savings from federation.

The phase transition and policy timing

The strength of abundance generation increases secularly as technology evolves. This drives phase transitions in business model landscapes. In Phase I, when abundance generation is weak, restriction is stable. Enhancement requires coordinated adoption through a consortium, standards body, or dominant platform. The appropriate policy focus is ensuring that restriction mechanisms are time-limited and funding basic research into community governance models. In Phase II, when abundance generation is moderate, transform strategies begin to invade the all-restrict equilibrium. Full enhancement still requires a tipping point, but the point is shrinking. This is the critical policy window: extraction norms are forming, path dependence has not yet locked in, and the choices made here shape long-run equilibria. The appropriate policy focus is investing in governance infrastructure, imposing transparency floors, and mandating interoperability. In Phase III, when abundance generation is strong, full enhancement dominates and restriction is unstable. The appropriate policy focus is ensuring that both firm-subsidy and community paths remain viable so that competition between them constrains scarcity channeling.

The secular increase in abundance generation means that industries move through these phases over time. What was Phase I a decade ago may be Phase II now. Many digital goods are currently in Phase II, making governance infrastructure investment the most time-sensitive policy lever.

The private–social divergence

The private payoffs that drive organizational strategy do not account for user participation. This creates a wedge between private and social optima. Transparent and community-governed paths tend to attract more users (a participation advantage), while tightly controlled firm-subsidy paths tend to create more per-user value through tight coupling (a per-user value advantage). Social welfare depends on the product of participation and per-user value. A less extractive direction (community or transparent firm) is socially superior despite being less profitable for firms when the participation advantage—more users joining a transparent or community-governed path—outweighs the per-user value advantage of tighter firm control. The divergence is widest at intermediate levels of abundance generation: both directions are viable, per-user value differences are moderate, and the widest parameter region exists where extraction is privately dominant but community organization is socially superior. This is precisely the regime where scarcity-aware policy intervention has its highest marginal impact.

Policy Instruments and Institutional Design

The context-dependent prescriptions above identify when each direction is welfare-maximizing. The scarcity channeling framework further implies a three-instrument policy architecture to support these prescriptions and ensure that the welfare-maximizing direction is achievable in practice.

The three-instrument architecture

The scarcity channeling framework reveals that effective innovation policy for abundance-generating goods cannot rely on a single instrument. Governance infrastructure investment is necessary because the community direction—the path that eliminates concentrated dependency—is structurally vulnerable to the governance trap, and this vulnerability is worsening as content creation costs fall relative to governance costs. Business model transparency is necessary because scarcity channeling is invisible without standardized disclosure, and rational inattention under abundance conditions (Raban & Włodarczyk, 2024; Sims, 2003) means that even sophisticated users cannot independently assess the dependency structures they face. Ecosystem plurality is necessary because the welfare-maximizing direction is context-dependent, and maintaining viable alternatives across all three directions creates the competitive discipline that constrains channeled scarcity within each direction. The three instruments are complementary and mutually reinforcing: governance investment enables community viability, which creates outside options that make transparency meaningful, which in turn enables the informed competition that ecosystem plurality requires.

Governance infrastructure investment

Governance infrastructure investment is the most novel and most urgent instrument in the scarcity-aware policy framework—the one that follows uniquely from the scarcity channeling analysis. The core insight is that governance is a public good: the moderation tools, dispute resolution platforms, contribution tracking systems, compliance checking mechanisms, and coordination infrastructure that communities need to sustain abundance-generating resources benefit all participants but are chronically under-provided because no individual contributor captures the full social return. This under-provision is the direct cause of the governance trap identified in the community direction analysis, and it is worsening due to the falling-content-cost paradox: as generative AI and low-code tools reduce content creation costs, the gap between governance costs and content costs widens, making the governance trap harder to avoid even for well-organized communities.

The analogy to physical infrastructure is precise. Just as governments invest in roads, bridges, and utilities because these are public goods that enable market competition and economic activity, governments should invest in governance infrastructure because it is a public good that enables community-governed alternatives to firm-subsidized platforms. Without adequate governance infrastructure, the community direction is theoretically available but practically unviable for most goods—leaving users with a choice between restriction and firm dependency rather than the three-way competition that maximizes welfare. The investment takes several forms: funding open-source governance tooling (moderation tools, dispute resolution platforms, automated compliance checking, contribution tracking systems); creating institutional conditions for governance premium stacking (tax treatment that recognizes governance labor, portability of reputation across platforms, legal frameworks for community revenue sharing); supporting federated governance models that reduce per-community governance costs through parallelization; and addressing the falling-content-cost paradox by proactively investing in governance capacity before the gap between content and governance costs becomes unsustainable.

Federated governance deserves particular emphasis. Governance demand grows faster than linearly with community size, so splitting a community into federated sub-communities reduces total governance demand substantially. Federation, as exemplified by Mastodon, Matrix, or the Fediverse more broadly, is not merely an organizational preference but a structural response to the scaling properties of governance costs. The larger the community and the steeper the scaling of governance demand, the greater the savings from federation. Policy that supports federation standards, interoperability between federated instances, and shared governance tooling across federated communities has a multiplier effect on governance investment.

Business model transparency

Users, communities, and policymakers face a fundamental information asymmetry regarding the business model patterns embedded in digital products and services. When a user adopts a “free” platform, they rarely understand whether the business model is hidden revenue (data extraction creating dependency scarcity through opacity), sponsorship (third-party subsidy creating dependency on the sponsor’s continued support), freemium (tiered access creating partial restriction), or open source with corporate backing (hybrid dependency on the backing firm’s strategic interests). Each of these patterns creates different dependencies, different scarcities, and different risks for the user. Yet the surface presentation may be identical: a free service with a user-friendly interface. Paradoxically, information abundance itself deepens this asymmetry: the sheer volume of terms of service, privacy policies, and business model descriptions means that even when disclosure nominally exists, the information exceeds users’ processing capacity—precisely the condition that Raban and Włodarczyk (2024) identify as the defining feature of information abundance, where rational inattention (Sims, 2003) becomes the dominant cognitive strategy. This is why standardized, taxonomy-based transparency—rather than simply more disclosure—is necessary: it must reduce the cognitive cost of comprehension, not merely increase the quantity of available information.

We propose that organizations employing abundance-generating goods be required to disclose three elements. The first is pattern identification: the organization must identify which of the recognized business model patterns it employs, using a standardized taxonomy (such as the 175-pattern framework from the BMP literature). The second is dependency structure: for each identified pattern, the organization must disclose the dependencies it creates—what data is collected and how it is monetized (for hidden revenue patterns); what terms govern access and how they can change unilaterally (for platform-subsidy patterns); what switching costs exist and whether data is portable (for lock-in patterns); and what governance mechanisms exist and how users can influence them (for community patterns). The third is abundance compliance assessment: the organization must assess whether its business model patterns are abundance compliant—whether they allow the full abundance potential of the resource to be realized, or whether they restrict it artificially.

Business model transparency creates the informational precondition for three types of response. At the user level, visible dependency structures enable informed choices about which scarcities users are willing to accept. At the community level, transparency enables targeted effort: if a firm-subsidized platform discloses that its core dependency is algorithmic curation control, a community initiative can target exactly that function. At the policy level, transparency enables precise intervention rather than broad platform regulation. Transparency requirements can be integrated into existing regulatory frameworks (GDPR, Digital Services Act, Digital Markets Act) as a complementary mechanism. Implementation requires a standardized taxonomy of business model patterns (the BMP literature provides a starting point), standardized assessment methodologies, enforcement mechanisms analogous to financial reporting (regulatory audits, civil liability for materially inaccurate disclosure), and proportionality provisions that calibrate requirements to the platform’s reach and the strength of abundance generation in its core resource.

Ecosystem plurality as a design principle

The three directions are not merely alternatives but mutual checks, and the healthiest ecosystem is one where multiple directions are viable. The firm path’s dependency scarcity is constrained when a viable community path exists, because the community alternative reduces the captive user base and lowers the threshold at which users migrate away from opaque platforms. Conversely, the community path’s governance scarcity is partially alleviated when firm-subsidized alternatives set a quality floor that communities must match, preventing governance complacency. Even restriction serves a disciplinary role: the existence of a viable restrict path (paid, per-unit, but well-functioning) prevents both enhancement paths from taking user participation for granted.

Policy should therefore maintain viable alternatives across all three directions, not mandate a single approach. This is not an argument for policy neutrality but for policy that actively sustains the conditions for productive competition. The self-reinforcing chain operates as follows: governance infrastructure investment enables community viability by lowering governance costs and raising the governance premium; community viability creates an outside option for users currently on firm-subsidized platforms; the existence of an outside option forces firms to compete on transparency because users can credibly exit; and firms competing on transparency reduces dependency scarcity across the ecosystem. Each link in this chain corresponds to one of the three policy instruments: governance investment enables the community path, transparency requirements make the competition visible, and ecosystem plurality emerges from the interaction of the first two.

The implication for policy design is that the three instruments should be deployed as a coordinated package. Governance investment without transparency requirements creates viable community alternatives that users cannot easily discover or compare. Transparency requirements without governance investment create visibility into dependency structures but leave users with no viable community alternative to migrate toward. Ecosystem plurality without either investment or transparency is aspirational rather than operational. The coordination is not merely additive but synergistic: each instrument’s effectiveness depends on the others being in place.

Discussion

Theoretical contributions

This paper makes three contributions to the literature. The first and most fundamental is to the literature on abundance-generating goods (Bliss & Egler, 2020; Cooper, 2006; Kubiszewski et al., 2010; Weber, 2004). Prior work in this literature established that abundance-generating goods challenge market allocation and identified the need for alternative organizing structures, but left unanswered the question of why these alternatives each produce characteristic and persistent scarcities. Our contribution is to introduce scarcity channeling as the central mechanism governing abundance-driven value creation and to show that it is structural rather than contingent. The coupled resource model demonstrates that scarcity channeling arises whenever rival complement costs scale faster than sharing-generated value—a condition that holds generically in practice, as governance, curation, and infrastructure costs typically grow superlinearly with community size. The framework characterizes the three-direction choice that organizations face (restrict, firm subsidize, community organize) and identifies the distinct scarcity that each direction creates (artificial scarcity, dependency scarcity, governance scarcity). Crucially, each scarcity has different properties regarding visibility, concentration, and policy amenability, which means that effective intervention requires correctly diagnosing which scarcity is at work and matching the policy instrument to the scarcity type.

More broadly, scarcity channeling contributes to the information economics literature’s effort to theorize abundance. Raban and Włodarczyk (2024) frame the development of information economics through Hegel’s dialectics—thesis (information scarcity), antithesis (information abundance), and synthesis—and argue that the synthesis of scarcity and abundance will drive the field’s development. Scarcity channeling provides a specific form of this synthesis for the abundance-generating case: it shows that abundance does not eliminate scarcity but transforms it, and it characterizes the precise forms these transformed scarcities take. This contribution advances the discourse on abundance-generating goods in a specific direction that prior work had gestured toward but not formalized. Kubiszewski et al. (2010) noted that abundance-generating goods require institutional innovation, and Nikander et al. (2020) argued for new governance structures, but neither identified the mechanism by which institutional choices generate characteristic scarcities. The scarcity channeling framework provides this mechanism and, in doing so, transforms the question from “how should we organize abundance-generating goods?” to “which scarcity are we willing to accept?”—a reframing that has direct implications for both organizational design and policy.

The second contribution is to business model innovation research (Gassmann et al., 2014; Massa et al., 2017). We provide a framework that explains why certain BMP combinations succeed or fail, grounding the observation in scarcity channeling rather than abstract compatibility. The framework reveals that the metamechanism-level challenges—why each metamechanism faces a distinctive challenge—are manifestations of a single underlying mechanism. The accountability challenge identified inductively in large-scale collaboration is revealed to be governance scarcity; the transparency challenge in firm-subsidy arrangements is dependency scarcity; and the valuation dilemma in transformation approaches reflects the coupling dial between scarcity reduction and abundance benefit reduction. The scarcity channeling framework provides explanatory depth that the inductive BMP analysis alone could not achieve: the challenges are not independent organizational problems but expressions of a structural mechanism that operates across all organizational forms engaging with abundance-generating goods. Abundance compliance, as a design criterion, connects BMP choice to welfare outcomes and offers practitioners a diagnostic tool to identify which direction a business model occupies, understand which scarcity it creates, and assess whether the scarcity is sustainable and acceptable in context.

The third contribution is to innovation policy (Blind, 2012; Edler & Fagerberg, 2017). We make three interrelated policy contributions. First, we show that the welfare-maximizing direction depends on context, challenging universal prescriptions for either open access or market-based allocation. The scarcity channeling framework reveals that “openness” is not a unitary policy goal but a choice between governance scarcity (community path) and dependency scarcity (firm-subsidy path), each requiring different policy instruments. Second, we identify intermediate levels of abundance generation as the critical policy window where private–social divergence is widest and intervention has the highest marginal impact. Third, and most centrally, we develop a scarcity-aware innovation policy framework centered on governance infrastructure investment, complemented by business model transparency and ecosystem plurality. The abundance compliance criteria provide a practical assessment framework for evaluating whether business model patterns serve or undermine abundance-driven value creation, and the recognized library of abundance-engaging BMPs provides the taxonomic foundation for standardized transparency.

Practical implications

The framework has implications for four audiences. For platform operators, the scarcity channeling framework provides a diagnostic that makes the scarcity consequences of business model choices explicit. A firm operating a hidden revenue model can use the framework to assess which dependencies it is creating, how large its captive user fraction is, and what would happen if a community alternative emerged. This diagnostic enables proactive management of dependency scarcity—investing in transparency, data portability, and algorithmic accountability before regulatory mandates or user migration force the issue. Concretely, a platform could use the abundance compliance criteria to audit its own business model patterns, identify the most significant dependency structures, and voluntarily adopt compliance measures that reduce dependency scarcity and increase user trust. The framework suggests that such proactive investment is not merely ethical but strategically prudent: platforms with lower dependency scarcity face lower regulatory risk, lower competitive risk from community alternatives, and lower reputational risk from terms change backlash or data breaches.

For community organizers, the framework identifies the specific governance investments needed to avoid the governance trap. The governance premium stacking model provides a practical diagnostic: does the community offer decision-making power to governance contributors? Reputational returns? Financial compensation? Strategic access? Social recognition? Gaps in the premium stack are leading indicators of governance fragility, and addressing them proactively—before surface metrics begin to decline—is the key to sustainable community governance. The falling-content-cost paradox alerts community leaders that the advent of generative AI tools should trigger increased investment in governance capacity, not celebration of accelerated content growth. When content creation becomes cheaper, governance becomes relatively more expensive, and the governance trap becomes harder to avoid unless the community responds with targeted governance investment.

For policymakers, the framework offers three actionable instruments: governance infrastructure investment as the most urgent priority, business model transparency requirements that make scarcity channeling visible, and ecosystem plurality policies that maintain viable alternatives across directions. Governance infrastructure investment—funding moderation tools, dispute resolution platforms, and contribution tracking systems as public goods—is analogous to physical infrastructure investment that enables market competition and reduces the cost of the community direction. Business model transparency requirements can be integrated into existing regulatory frameworks (GDPR, Digital Services Act, Digital Markets Act) as a complementary mechanism. The phase transition analysis identifies which sectors are currently in the critical window of moderate abundance generation where intervention has the highest marginal impact, enabling targeted resource allocation rather than sector-blind policy. For investors and auditors, abundance compliance provides a new dimension of risk assessment. A platform with high dependency scarcity and no credible community alternative faces regulatory, competitive, and reputational risks that standard financial metrics do not capture. Conversely, a community project approaching the governance trap faces sustainability risk that standard adoption metrics (downloads, stars, user growth) obscure rather than reveal. Abundance compliance assessment fills this gap by providing visibility into the structural dynamics that determine long-term viability.

The counterfactual revisited

The theory predicts that scarcity channeling is structural when rival complement costs scale faster than sharing-generated value. This raises the question: are there conditions under which scarcity channeling does not occur? We identify two limiting cases. First, when the good is effectively non-rival—exhibiting negligible abundance generation—the resource can be managed through standard public goods frameworks without significant scarcity channeling. This case is empirically common but theoretically uninteresting for our framework. Second, when rival complements can be fully automated and their scaling pressure disappears, the abundance-generating resource can grow without generating rival cost pressure. This case represents a frontier that technological progress (particularly AI-driven automation of governance tasks) may approach, but current evidence suggests that the most critical rival complements—judgment-intensive governance decisions, trust calibration, conflict resolution—resist full automation (Mindel, Mathiassen, & Rai, 2018). The gap between content creation costs and governance costs is, if anything, widening as generative AI accelerates content production while leaving governance labor largely unchanged. The practical implication is that scarcity channeling is a robust feature of the current and foreseeable digital economy, and the organizational and policy questions it raises will become more pressing, not less, as abundance generation strengthens across sectors.

Competition between directions as mutual discipline

As developed in the ecosystem plurality discussion, the three directions function as mutual checks: each direction’s channeled scarcity is constrained by the credible threat of defection to another. The ecosystem plurality principle implies that the healthiest ecosystem is one where multiple directions are viable and users can make informed choices among them. Business model transparency is what transforms this competition from latent to active: without visibility into the business model patterns that organizations employ, the dependencies these patterns create, and the scarcity they channel, users cannot evaluate whether the dependency scarcity they experience on a firm-subsidized platform is better or worse than the governance scarcity they would experience on a community alternative. Transparency transforms the competitive landscape from one in which scarcity channeling is hidden to one in which it is an explicit dimension of competition, and governance investment ensures that the community direction remains a credible competitor rather than a theoretical possibility.

Limitations

The framework has several limitations that should be acknowledged. The model assumes homogeneous agents within each strategy type and treats the strength of abundance generation as exogenous; in reality, firms invest in technologies that change the abundance-generating properties of their goods, and this endogeneity may generate strategic dynamics not captured by the current framework. The specific assumed relationships between variables—such as how governance quality modulates effective value and how rival complement costs scale with community size—are analytically convenient but empirically unvalidated. We conjecture that the qualitative results (governance trap, phase transitions, scarcity channeling) are robust to the specific functional forms assumed, but specific threshold values are not. The framework treats the three directions as distinct categories, whereas in practice many ecosystems involve hybrid arrangements, as the Linux and AI/ML cases illustrate. The framework applies to hybrids by analyzing each interface separately, but this approach may miss interaction effects between interfaces that a more integrated analysis would capture.

The policy instruments proposed — particularly business model transparency and governance infrastructure investment — require further development. The transparency proposal is conceptual: practical implementation requires standardized taxonomies, assessment methodologies, and enforcement mechanisms that do not yet exist. The analogy to financial reporting is instructive but imperfect: business model patterns are more fluid than financial transactions, and compliance assessment is more subjective than accounting standards. The political economy of transparency—industry lobbying, regulatory capture, international coordination challenges—represents an additional barrier that our framework acknowledges but does not address. Finally, the real-world cases are illustrative rather than systematic. A rigorous empirical test of the framework would require systematic measurement of abundance generation strength, governance premiums, and dependency structures across a representative sample of ecosystems—which we identify as the most important direction for future research.

Future research

Five directions are particularly promising. First, empirical estimation of abundance generation strength and the governance premium components in specific ecosystems, using panel data on user growth, per-user value metrics, and governance activity indicators. The coupled resource model generates testable predictions: if rival complement costs scale faster than sharing-generated value, communities should exhibit carrying capacity effects; if the governance premium falls below the cost differential, the governance-to-content ratio should decline exponentially. Both predictions are amenable to empirical testing with appropriate data. Second, endogenizing abundance generation—modeling how firms invest in technologies that change the abundance-generating properties of their goods, and the strategic dynamics this creates. Firms have incentives to strengthen abundance generation when they occupy the firm-subsidy direction (stronger abundance generation increases the value of subsidized enhancement) but to weaken it when they occupy the restriction direction (weaker abundance generation makes restriction more tenable). This endogeneity may generate strategic dynamics not captured by the current model.

Third, developing standardized business model transparency frameworks and abundance compliance assessment methodologies through multi-stakeholder processes, analogous to the development of accounting standards. This requires both academic research (validating the pattern taxonomy, refining compliance criteria) and institutional development (establishing assessment bodies, defining enforcement mechanisms). Fourth, testing whether the governance trap’s predicted pattern of exponential governance decline matches observed patterns in open-source projects, using the governance-to-content ratio as a leading indicator. Large-scale datasets of open-source project activity provide the empirical foundation for this analysis. Fifth, analyzing how generative AI changes the gap between governance costs and content creation costs across sectors and what governance investments are needed to prevent the resulting governance trap expansion. The rapid decline in content creation costs driven by large language models represents a natural experiment in scarcity channeling: if the framework is correct, sectors experiencing the fastest decline in content creation costs should exhibit the most severe governance challenges, controlling for initial governance premium levels.

Conclusion

This paper began with a paradox: abundance-generating goods create value that scales with use, yet the very organizations designed to harness this value persistently generate new scarcities. The Heartbleed vulnerability, Wikipedia’s declining editor base, Android’s dependency structures, and the AI commons’ compute concentration are not unrelated organizational failures but expressions of a single structural mechanism. Scarcity channeling—the inevitable direction of rival-complement costs into new forms of scarcity depending on the organizational path chosen—is a property of the coupled resource system, not a failure of organizational design. The choice facing organizations is not whether to create scarcity but which kind.

The three directions we have identified each represent a legitimate organizational response to this structural challenge. Restricting abundance generation creates artificial scarcity of the resource itself, with superlinear welfare loss that grows increasingly untenable as abundance generation strengthens. Firm subsidization creates dependency scarcity, concentrating leverage in the subsidizing entity and trading user agency for access. Community organization creates governance scarcity, diffusing the burden across the community but risking under-provision of the rivalrous complements that the abundance-generating resource depends on. Each direction has contexts in which it is welfare-maximizing, and the scarcity channeling framework provides the analytical basis for identifying those contexts.

The most effective innovation policy does not mandate a single direction but ensures that multiple directions remain viable as mutual checks. When a credible community alternative exists alongside a firm-subsidized platform, the firm must compete on transparency because users have an outside option. When a well-functioning restriction model exists alongside community-governed alternatives, communities cannot take user participation for granted. This competition between directions constrains the scarcity that each creates. Governance infrastructure investment ensures that community alternatives remain viable, business model transparency makes the scarcity trade-offs visible to all participants, and ecosystem plurality policies prevent any single direction from monopolizing the organizational landscape.

Our analysis has also revealed that the policy challenge is time-sensitive. The strength of abundance generation increases secularly as technology evolves, driving phase transitions in business model landscapes. Many digital goods are currently at intermediate levels of abundance generation—the critical window where extraction norms are forming, path dependence has not yet locked in, and the choices made now shape long-run equilibria. This is precisely the regime where private and social optima diverge most widely: extraction may be privately dominant even when community organization is socially superior. Governance infrastructure investment is the intervention with the highest marginal impact in this window — the most novel, most urgent, and most underappreciated policy lever for the digital commons — complemented by business model transparency that makes scarcity channeling visible and ecosystem plurality that maintains competitive discipline across directions. The urgency is compounded by the current wave of generative AI, which is widening the gap between content creation costs and governance costs and thereby expanding the governance trap across sectors.

The framework, grounded in a systematic analysis of 175 business model patterns and the coupled resource model, provides both the analytical foundation for understanding scarcity channeling and the practical instruments for responding to it. Abundance compliance criteria, assessed against a recognized library of abundance-engaging business model patterns, offer a diagnostic for evaluating whether organizational arrangements serve or undermine sharing-generated value. The central insight of this paper is that scarcity channeling cannot be avoided—it is structural. But it can be navigated, and navigation is most effective when policymakers invest in the governance infrastructure that sustains alternatives, require the transparency that makes trade-offs visible, and maintain the ecosystem plurality that disciplines all participants.
