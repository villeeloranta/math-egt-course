Model III — The Subsidization–Transparency Game: Case Studies

Companion to Section 5 of the EGT Formalization

Model Recap

A platform subsidises free anti-rival sharing and extracts information rent. Platform payoff:

\[ \pi_P(\tau) = (1-\tau)\,I(n) + \tau\,T(n) - c_{\text{sub}}\cdot n \]

User participation: \(n(\tau)=n_0[\alpha+(1-\alpha)\,\tau^\psi]\). In the evolutionary game, High-transparency (\(H\)) and Opaque (\(O\)) platforms compete. Opaque platforms suffer erosion as transparency expectations rise: \(n_O(p)=n_0\,\alpha\,(1-\lambda p)\).

Case A: LinkedIn — The Archetypal Subsidization Model

Case Context

LinkedIn (acquired by Microsoft for $26.2B in 2016) provides free professional networking infrastructure. Users share professional profiles, connections, endorsements, and content (all anti-rival: a recommendation letter becomes more valuable when more people can see it). Revenue comes from three streams: Talent Solutions (recruiter tools, ~65% of revenue), Marketing Solutions (advertising, ~20%), and Premium Subscriptions (~15%). As of 2025, LinkedIn has approximately 1 billion members with ~310 million monthly active users.

Parameter Estimation

ParameterValueRationale
\(n_0\)1,000M (members)Total registered user base.
\(\alpha\)0.75High baseline: LinkedIn is near-mandatory for professionals. Most users would join regardless of data practices because the professional cost of not having a profile is high.
\(\tau\)0.35Partial transparency. LinkedIn discloses that it uses data for ad targeting and recruiter tools. But the extent of data sharing with Microsoft, the specifics of algorithmic feed manipulation, and the use of user data for AI training (LinkedIn’s AI features) are not transparent.
\(\psi\)0.4Low transparency sensitivity in professional context: users tolerate data extraction because the professional networking benefit is high and alternatives are weak.
\(I(n)\)\(I_0\cdot n^{1.2}\) with \(I_0=0.008\)Information rent scales superlinearly because recruiter intelligence becomes more valuable as the talent pool grows. Normalised to revenue per member-unit.
\(T(n)\)\(T_0\cdot n\) with \(T_0=0.003\)Trust-based revenue (premium subscriptions) scales linearly with user base.
\(c_{\text{sub}}\)0.002Per-user infrastructure cost of providing free networking.
\(\beta\)0.3Anti-rival: professional connections, endorsements, content all increase in value with more users.

Participation at Current Transparency (\(\tau=0.35\))

\[ n(0.35) = 1000\times[0.75 + 0.25\times 0.35^{0.4}] = 1000\times[0.75 + 0.25\times 0.636] \] \[ = 1000\times[0.75 + 0.159] = 909\text{M} \]

This is reasonably consistent with the ~310M MAU / ~1B registered members split (active participation ≈ 30–35% of registered base).

Platform Payoff at \(\tau=0.35\)

\[ \pi_P(0.35) = (1-0.35)\times 0.008\times 909^{1.2} + 0.35\times 0.003\times 909 - 0.002\times 909 \]

Computing \(909^{1.2}\): \(\ln(909)=6.81\), \(6.81\times 1.2=8.17\), \(e^{8.17}\approx 3546\).

\[ = 0.65\times 0.008\times 3546 + 0.35\times 2.73 - 1.82 \] \[ = 18.44 + 0.955 - 1.82 = 17.58 \]

Payoff at Full Transparency (\(\tau=1\))

\[ n(1) = 1000\times[0.75+0.25\times 1] = 1000 \] \[ \pi_P(1) = 0\times I(1000) + 1\times 0.003\times 1000 - 0.002\times 1000 = 0 + 3.0 - 2.0 = 1.0 \]

Payoff at Full Opacity (\(\tau=0\))

\[ n(0) = 1000\times 0.75 = 750 \] \[ \pi_P(0) = 1\times 0.008\times 750^{1.2} + 0 - 0.002\times 750 \]

\(750^{1.2}\approx 2641\): \(\pi_P(0) = 0.008\times 2641 - 1.5 = 21.13 - 1.5 = 19.63\)

Finding

The payoff structure is: \(\pi_P(0)=19.63 > \pi_P(0.35)=17.58 > \pi_P(1)=1.0\). The platform is incentivised toward opacity: information rent dominates trust-based revenue. LinkedIn’s partial transparency (\(\tau=0.35\)) is not the payoff-maximising strategy; full opacity would be more profitable. The fact that LinkedIn maintains partial transparency reflects regulatory constraints (GDPR), reputational risk management, and anticipation of competitive pressure—factors external to the basic model.

The anti-rival value of the professional network (\(\beta=0.3\)) creates lock-in: users tolerate data extraction because the network effect makes LinkedIn irreplaceable. This is captured by the high \(\alpha=0.75\)—most users are “captive” regardless of transparency.

Case B: WhatsApp Privacy Policy Change (2021)

Case Context

In January 2021, WhatsApp (owned by Meta) announced a privacy policy update requiring users to share data with Facebook for ad targeting. The backlash was immediate: Signal downloads surged from ~10M to ~50M in a week; Telegram gained 25M users in 72 hours. WhatsApp’s daily downloads dropped 43%. This is a natural experiment in transparency dynamics.

Pre-Announcement State

PlatformStrategy\(\tau\)Users (M)
WhatsAppOpaque (\(O\))0.252,000
SignalHigh transparency (\(H\))0.9520
TelegramModerate (\(M\))0.5500

Fraction of high-transparency platforms (by user-weighted market share): \(p\approx 20/(2000+20+500)=0.008\).

Post-Announcement Shift

Modelling the User Migration

The privacy policy change effectively lowered WhatsApp’s transparency from \(\tau\approx 0.25\) to \(\tau\approx 0.10\) (explicitly admitting data sharing with Facebook). Using the erosion model \(n_O(p) = n_0\,\alpha\,(1-\lambda p)\):

But the critical dynamic was not the change in \(p\) (which was tiny); it was a shock to \(\alpha\)—the baseline participation rate. The privacy policy announcement made users aware that their “captive” assumption was wrong: alternatives existed. This reduced effective \(\alpha\) from ~0.95 to ~0.90 temporarily.

\[ \Delta n_{\text{WhatsApp}} = 2000\times(0.95-0.90) = -100\text{M} \]

This is roughly consistent with the observed migration (~75M to Signal + Telegram in the first two weeks).

Finding

The WhatsApp case illustrates two model dynamics:

  1. Anti-rivalry creates resilience for opaque platforms. Despite the backlash, WhatsApp retained ~95% of its user base because the anti-rival value of the network (everyone you know is on WhatsApp) creates enormous lock-in. The \(\alpha\) shock was temporary; within months, most users who left returned. Signal’s user base stabilised at ~40M, far below its peak surge. The messaging network’s anti-rivalry (\(\beta\approx 0.4\)) overwhelmed the transparency penalty.
  2. The tipping point was not reached. For transparency to become self-reinforcing, Signal’s share would need to exceed a critical threshold where WhatsApp’s erosion becomes permanent. With Signal at ~2% market share, the negative frequency dependence is too weak. The model predicts that transparency can only dominate messaging if a coordinated event pushes Signal/equivalent past approximately 15–20% market share.

Case C: Firefox vs Chrome — The Well-Funded Opacity Advantage

Case Context

Mozilla Firefox (non-profit, transparent about data practices, minimal telemetry by default) competes against Google Chrome (data collection feeds Google’s advertising ecosystem). Despite Firefox’s transparency advantage, Chrome dominates with ~65% market share vs Firefox’s ~3%. This appears to contradict the model’s prediction that transparency should be favoured — but the case reveals a critical structural asymmetry.

Parameter Comparison

ParameterFirefoxChrome
\(\tau\)0.90.2
\(c_{\text{sub}}\) per user$4.50/year (from Google search deal + donations)$0.10/year (subsidised by Google’s ad revenue)
\(I(n)\) (information rent/user)~$0 (no data extraction)~$12/year (search data, browsing history for ad targeting)
\(T(n)\) (trust-based revenue/user)~$4.50/year (search deal priced on trust/user base)~$0.50/year (minimal trust premium)

Payoff Comparison

Firefox payoff per user: \(\pi_F = 0 + 4.50 - 4.50 = 0\) (break-even).

Chrome payoff per user: \(\pi_C = 0.8\times 12 + 0.2\times 0.50 - 0.10 = 9.6 + 0.1 - 0.1 = 9.6\).

Chrome’s payoff is vastly higher. Google can afford to invest heavily in browser quality (speed, features, developer tools) because each Chrome user generates $9.60/year in information rent. Firefox cannot match this investment on $0/user profit.

Finding

The Firefox/Chrome case reveals a fundamental limitation of the basic model: it assumes symmetric subsidization capacity. When one platform has access to massive cross-subsidy (Google’s $280B ad revenue), it can simultaneously offer a superior product (higher effective \(V_a\)) and maintain low transparency. The transparency advantage of Firefox is overwhelmed by the quality advantage that opacity-funded investment provides.

Formally, the model needs a modification: \(\pi_H\) and \(\pi_O\) should include a quality investment term \(q(I)\) that is increasing in information rent. The platform that extracts more information rent can reinvest in product quality, creating a feedback loop that the basic model misses:

\[ \pi_P(\tau) = (1-\tau)\,I(n) + \tau\,T(n) - c_{\text{sub}}\cdot n + q\bigl((1-\tau)I(n)\bigr) \]

where \(q(I) > 0\) represents quality improvements funded by information rent. This quality reinvestment loop explains Google Chrome’s dominance despite its opacity.

Critical Assessment

The Firefox/Chrome case provides the strongest empirical challenge to Model III. The model predicts that transparency should be evolutionarily advantaged when trust-based revenue exceeds information rent and when negative frequency dependence erodes opaque platforms’ user bases. Neither condition holds in the browser market: (1) information rent vastly exceeds trust-based revenue, and (2) Chrome’s quality advantage (funded by information rent) generates positive frequency dependence for the opaque strategy. The model requires the quality-reinvestment extension to account for cases where information rent funds competitive advantage.

Key Insights

  1. Anti-rivalry protects opaque platforms. In all three cases, the anti-rival nature of the platform (network connections, messaging network, web ecosystem) creates user lock-in that buffers against transparency-motivated switching. The higher the anti-rivalry (\(\beta\)), the higher the captive rate (\(\alpha\)), and the weaker the transparency tipping dynamic.
  2. The transparency tipping point has not been reached in any major consumer market. This is the model’s most sobering prediction: opacity is the evolutionarily dominant strategy in consumer platforms as of 2026, because information rent consistently exceeds trust-based revenue and anti-rival lock-in suppresses user switching.
  3. The model needs a quality-reinvestment extension. The Chrome case demonstrates that information rent can fund product quality improvements that further entrench opacity. The basic model treats platform quality as exogenous; endogenising it would capture the most empirically important dynamic in platform competition.
  4. Regulatory intervention may be the only mechanism to reach the tipping point. GDPR, the Digital Markets Act, and similar regulations effectively impose a minimum \(\tau\) floor, reducing \(I(n)\) and narrowing the gap with transparent alternatives. Without regulation, the market equilibrium appears to be stable opacity.
