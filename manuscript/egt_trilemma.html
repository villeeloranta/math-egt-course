Scarcity Displacement in Anti-Rival Goods: A Formal Analysis

Analytical Extension of “Managing the Complexities of Digital Goods’ Anti-Rivalry in Contemporary Business Model Innovation”

Working Paper — March 2026

Abstract

Anti-rival goods create value that scales with use, but rival costs—infrastructure, governance, curation—must be covered. The first-order choice is binary: restrict anti-rivalry (traditional approach) or enhance it. Enhancing inevitably displaces scarcity: firm-subsidized enhancement creates scarcity of transparency, agency, and control; community-based enhancement creates scarcity of governance labor, attention, and curation. In either case, a coupling dial—transform—can reduce the intensity of the displaced scarcity, but only by limiting the anti-rival benefit. Using evolutionary game theory, we formalize both enhancement paths and the coupling dial, characterize their viability conditions and failure modes, and show that private and social optima diverge at intermediate anti-rivalry levels. The framework yields four main results: (i) phase transitions in business model landscapes driven by the anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\); (ii) a governance trap theorem giving sufficient conditions for community collapse; (iii) an incompatibility theorem showing that full enhancement and full decoupling cannot coexist on a shared interface; and (iv) a private–social divergence result driven by the tension between value capture and participation reach.

Keywords: anti-rival goods; scarcity displacement; evolutionary game theory; business model innovation; replicator dynamics; phase transitions; governance trap

1. Introduction: Restrict or Enhance

Anti-rival goods—goods whose per-user value increases with the number of users—create value that is superlinear in participation. Shared data improves with more contributors; open-source software gains from every patch; collaborative knowledge bases become more useful as they grow. The logic of anti-rivalry points toward maximal sharing. But sharing is not free: every anti-rivalrous resource depends on rivalrous complements—server infrastructure, editorial curation, conflict resolution, maintainer attention—that must be paid for. This creates a first-order choice: restrict anti-rivalry to avoid the rival-cost problem, or enhance it and face the consequences.

Restriction is the traditional approach—treat the good as rival, limit access, charge per unit. This avoids the rival-cost problem entirely but forfeits the superlinear value that anti-rivalry creates. Enhancement embraces anti-rivalry and covers rival costs through some other mechanism. But enhancement inevitably displaces scarcity. The rival costs do not disappear; they are absorbed by some entity, and whoever absorbs them gains leverage. There are exactly two entities that can absorb rival costs at scale. A firm can subsidize them, maximizing participation and extracting information rent—creating scarcity of transparency, agency, and control (the firm decides terms). A community can collectively cover them, eliminating dependency on any single firm—creating scarcity of governance labor, attention, curation, and infrastructure (someone must do the work).

In either enhancement path, a coupling dial is available: transform strategies reduce the coupling between rival and anti-rival components (de-transactionalization, flat-rate pricing, interface simplification), lowering both the anti-rival benefit and the intensity of the displaced scarcity. Transform is not a third path but a parameter within either path—a trade-off between benefit and scarcity intensity.

This paper formalizes both enhancement paths and the coupling dial using evolutionary game theory. Five sets of results structure the analysis. First, each path has a precise viability boundary: enhancement requires the anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\) to exceed a threshold that depends on costs and standalone efficiency (Proposition 2.1), and the community path collapses whenever governance premiums fall below the cost differential \(c_G - c_C\) (Theorem 4.1). Second, as \(\beta\) increases secularly with technology, the business model landscape undergoes phase transitions—from restriction-dominant, through a critical intermediate regime where partial enhancement is viable without coordination, to full-enhancement dominance (Theorem 6.1). Third, on any shared interface, full enhancement and full decoupling are mathematically incompatible: the coupling dial is a single parameter, and the mixed payoff is convex, forcing a corner solution (Theorem 5.1). Fourth, privately optimal and socially optimal strategies diverge in a precisely characterized parameter regime—firms optimize for value capture while welfare depends on participation reach, and the gap is widest at intermediate \(\beta\) (Theorem 7.1). Fifth, each boundary condition implies a specific policy lever and a measurable sensitivity, yielding prescriptive implications for ecosystem designers, platform operators, and regulators (Section 7.1).

2. Foundations

We parameterize the rivalry–anti-rivalry spectrum through an exponent \(\beta\) in the per-user value function \(V(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{\beta}\), where \(\beta < 0\) is rival, \(\beta = 0\) is non-rival, and \(\beta > 0\) is anti-rival. Every anti-rivalrous resource depends on rivalrous complements:

Definition 2.1 (Coupled Value Function)

For an anti-rivalrous resource with \(n\) users depending on a rivalrous complement with total supply \(M\):

\[ V(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{\beta} \cdot h\!\left(\frac{M}{n^{\gamma}}\right), \qquad h(s) = \frac{s}{s + \kappa} \]

where \(\gamma > 0\) is the demand scaling exponent for the rivalrous complement and \(\kappa > 0\) is the half-saturation constant. The condition \(\gamma > \beta\) ensures an optimal community size exists.

We model a population of firms choosing among three complexity management strategies: \(L\) (Leverage—full enhancement, maximizes rival–anti-rival coupling), \(T\) (Transform—partial enhancement, reduces coupling), and \(R\) (Restrict—avoids anti-rivalry). In the restrict-vs-enhance framing, \(R\) is the restrict strategy; \(L\) and \(T\) are both enhance strategies that differ in their coupling intensity. The population state \((x_L, x_T, x_R)\) with \(x_L + x_T + x_R = 1\) lives on the 2-simplex \(\Delta^2\).

Anti-rival value is endogenous to the population state:

Definition 2.2 (Ecosystem Anti-Rival Value)

\[ V_a(x_L, x_T) = V_0 \left(1 + a_L \cdot x_L + a_T \cdot x_T\right)^{\eta} \]

where \(a_L = \beta \lambda_L\), \(a_T = \beta \lambda_T\), with \(\lambda_L \geq 1 > \lambda_T > 0\) and \(\eta > 0\). The coupling intensities \(\lambda_L > \lambda_T\) reflect the key distinction: Leverage enhances anti-rivalry more strongly but also displaces more scarcity. Governance quality is \(h(x_L, x_T) = \frac{x_L + \omega x_T}{x_L + \omega x_T + \kappa_G}\) with \(0 < \omega < 1\).

Definition 2.3 (Strategy Payoffs)

\[ \pi_L = \beta\psi_L + \varphi_L \cdot V_a \cdot h - c_L, \qquad \pi_T = \beta\psi_T + \varphi_T \cdot V_a \cdot h - c_T, \qquad \pi_R = R_0 - \delta \cdot V_a \cdot h \]

where \(\psi_L > \psi_T > 0\) (standalone efficiency), \(\varphi_L > \varphi_T > 1\) (value capture), \(c_L > c_T > 0\) (costs), and \(\delta > 0\) (displacement). The replicator dynamics are \(\dot{x}_i = x_i(\pi_i - \bar{\pi})\).

The fitness differences reveal two channels. The standalone channel \(\beta\psi\) is state-independent; the ecosystem channel \((\varphi + \delta) V_a h\) creates positive feedback. These determine when each enhancement strategy can invade the all-Restrict state:

Proposition 2.1 (Invasion Conditions)

At the all-Restrict vertex, the ecosystem channel vanishes (\(h(0,0) = 0\)). Enhancement requires the standalone channel alone to exceed costs:

\[ \text{Transform invades:}\quad \beta > \beta_{\text{inv}}^T = \frac{c_T + R_0}{\psi_T}, \qquad \text{Leverage invades:}\quad \beta > \beta_{\text{inv}}^L = \frac{c_L + R_0}{\psi_L} \]

The ordering of these thresholds depends on parameter values. Below both thresholds, all-Restrict is locally stable—anti-rivalry is too weak to justify enhancement.

3. Enhancement via Firm Subsidy

In the first enhancement path, a firm subsidizes rival costs, maximizes sharing, and extracts information rent. Leverage creates the strongest per-user ecosystem value through tight coupling (\(\lambda_L \geq 1\)), but it requires opacity to sustain extraction. The “transparency challenge” identified in the original manuscript is not merely ethical but strategic: it is a direct consequence of the scarcity that firm-subsidized enhancement creates.

Definition 3.1 (Subsidization Game)

A platform subsidizes anti-rival sharing and chooses transparency \(\tau \in [0,1]\). User participation responds to perceived fairness:

\[ n(\tau) = n_0\left[\alpha + (1-\alpha)\tau^{\psi}\right] \]

where \(\alpha \in (0,1)\) is baseline participation. Platform payoff is \(\pi_P(\tau) = (1-\tau) I(n) + \tau T(n) - c_{\text{sub}} n\), where \(I(n)\) is information rent (decreasing in \(\tau\)) and \(T(n)\) is trust-based revenue (increasing in \(\tau\)).

In a population of platforms choosing between high-transparency (\(\tau_H\)) and opaque (\(\tau_O\)) strategies, with \(p\) the transparent fraction, opaque platforms face negative frequency dependence: as transparency becomes the norm, opaque platforms lose users via \(n_O(p) = n_0 \alpha (1 - \lambda p)\). The replicator equation \(\dot{p} = p(1-p)[\pi_H(p) - \pi_O(p)]\) admits a tipping point \(p^*\) where both strategies earn equal fitness. Below \(p^*\), opacity dominates; above it, transparency dominates.

Scarcity created. The firm controls the terms of the subsidy. Opacity is required to sustain information rent extraction, which suppresses participation (Section 7). The displaced scarcity is transparency, agency, and control—the firm decides who participates and under what conditions. Users gain anti-rival value but lose autonomy over the terms of their participation.

Transform within the firm path. A firm can reduce coupling by de-transactionalizing—moving from per-use pricing to flat-rate or subscription models. This reduces the extraction opportunity (lower information rent) but also reduces the displaced scarcity (less dependency). The coupling dial \(\lambda\) moves from \(\lambda_L\) toward \(\lambda_T\): lower anti-rival benefit, lower scarcity intensity. Section 5 formalizes this trade-off.

Prescriptive implication. Firms should time transparency moves to market composition. Once the transparent fraction crosses \(p^*\), opacity becomes self-undermining through the negative frequency dependence on \(n_O(p)\). Early transparency adoption may be disadvantageous, but delaying past the tipping point accelerates user loss.

4. Enhancement via Community

In the second enhancement path, the community collectively covers rival costs, eliminating dependency on any single firm. This removes the transparency–agency scarcity of the firm path but creates a different one. Within a community-based ecosystem, participants choose among three strategies:

Definition 4.1 (Community Strategies)

  • Content Contributor (C): Creates anti-rival value. Cost \(c_C\).
  • Governance Contributor (G): Maintains the rivalrous complement (moderation, infrastructure, conflict resolution). Cost \(c_G > c_C\). Receives governance premium \(\sigma\).
  • Free-Rider (F): Consumes without contributing.

With differential access \(\varphi > 1\) for contributors: \(\pi_C = \varphi V(x,y) - c_C\), \(\pi_G = \varphi V(x,y) - c_G + \sigma\), \(\pi_F = V(x,y)\), where \(V(x,y) = V_0(1+\beta x)^{\eta} \cdot y/(y+\kappa)\).

Theorem 4.1 (Governance Trap)

A sufficient condition for community collapse is \(\sigma < c_G - c_C\). When this holds:

  1. The governance-to-content ratio declines exponentially: \(\frac{d}{dt}\ln(y/x) = \sigma - (c_G - c_C) < 0\).
  2. Eventually \(y\) becomes small enough that \(h(y) \approx 0\), causing \(V(x,y) \to 0\).
  3. When \(V \to 0\), content contribution is also dominated: \(\pi_C - \pi_F = (\varphi-1) \cdot 0 - c_C < 0\).
  4. The dynamics are driven toward collapse near the all-free-rider state.

Proof

The fitness difference \(\pi_G - \pi_C = \sigma - (c_G - c_C)\) is negative and constant. In any population with both \(G\) and \(C\), the ratio \(y/x\) decreases exponentially at rate \(|c_G - c_C - \sigma|\). The absolute share \(y(t)\) may temporarily increase (when \(\pi_G > \bar{\pi}\)) but eventually the declining ratio forces \(y\) down, collapsing governance quality, community value, and finally content contribution.

■

Proposition 4.1 (Minimum Viable Governance Premium)

For governance not to be systematically dominated, the aggregate premium must satisfy:

\[ \sigma_{\text{total}} = \sigma_{\text{power}} + \sigma_{\text{rep}} + \sigma_{\text{fin}} + \sigma_{\text{strat}} + \sigma_{\text{social}} \geq c_G - c_C \]

This requires stacking multiple governance-oriented business model patterns (community governance, revenue sharing, stewardship roles). A community relying on a single premium source will not sustain governance if that source alone falls short of \(c_G - c_C\).

Scarcity created. Governance labor becomes the displaced scarce resource. The governance trap is insidious: surface metrics (user count, content volume) remain healthy during the burnout phase while the governance-to-content ratio \(y/x\) declines exponentially underneath. Theorem 4.1 gives the precise condition under which this collapse is inevitable. Unlike the firm path, where scarcity is concentrated (one entity controls access), community scarcity is diffuse—it manifests as burnout, declining moderation quality, and infrastructure decay.

Transform within the community path. A community can reduce coupling by simplifying interfaces, limiting the scope of shared governance, or modularizing so that each subgroup governs its own rivalrous complement. This reduces governance demands (lower \(c_G\)) but also limits the anti-rival value created across the full community. The coupling dial moves from \(\lambda_L\) toward \(\lambda_T\): less governance scarcity, less anti-rival benefit.

Prescriptive implication. Monitor \(y/x\), not surface metrics. Stack governance premiums from multiple sources. The minimum viable premium \(\sigma_{\min} = c_G - c_C\) is a design target, not a naturally occurring property.

5. Transform: The Coupling Dial

Transform is not a third path to anti-rival value but a coupling parameter available within either enhancement path. It reduces the intensity of rival–anti-rival coupling, which simultaneously limits the anti-rival benefit and the displaced scarcity. The mechanism: by reducing per-transaction friction (flat-rate pricing, subscription, digitalization), Transform lowers the marginal rival cost of each additional user, weakening the coupling that makes scarcity displacement necessary in the first place.

Definition 5.1 (Demand-Friction Model of De-Transactionalization)

Per-use pricing creates demand friction that reduces the realized network size:

\[ n(p) = \frac{n_0}{1 + \eta p}, \qquad \eta > 0 \]

The value ratio between subscription (zero per-use friction) and transactional models is:

\[ \frac{V_{\text{sub}}}{V_{\text{trans}}} = (1 + \eta p)^{\beta_0} \]

The advantage scales with both price friction \(\eta p\) and intrinsic anti-rivalry \(\beta_0\).

Proposition 5.1 (De-Transactionalization Viability)

A subscription model with fee \(f\) and cost \(C_0 + c_m n^{\gamma}\) (\(\gamma > 1\)) is viable within a band \(n_{\min} < n < n_{\max}\), where \(n_{\min}\) is the critical subscription mass. The competitive advantage over transactional models is transformative for high \(\beta_0\) and high \(\eta p\), and marginal otherwise.

Why transform is partial. Transform reduces coupling, which limits the channels through which anti-rival sharing generates superlinear returns. The ecosystem contribution weight \(a_T = \beta\lambda_T\) with \(\lambda_T < \lambda_L\) means Transform generates less ecosystem value per firm. The governance weight \(\omega < 1\) means Transform contributes less to ecosystem governance. Transform gains its advantage from lower costs (\(c_T < c_L\)) and lower coordination requirements, not from stronger anti-rival value creation. The coupling dial trades benefit for relief from displaced scarcity.

Available to both paths. Within the firm path, de-transactionalization reduces the extraction opportunity but also the dependency: a flat-rate subscriber is less surveilled than a per-use consumer. Within the community path, modular interfaces reduce governance scope but also limit cross-module anti-rival spillovers. In both cases, transform moves the coupling parameter \(\lambda\) toward \(\lambda_T\), with the same formal trade-off.

5.1 The Incompatibility of Full Enhancement and Full Decoupling

Can a firm simultaneously maximize anti-rival coupling (full enhancement) and minimize it (full decoupling) on a single interface? The answer is no—the coupling dial is a single parameter.

Theorem 5.1 (Incompatibility of Leveraging and Transforming Patterns)

Under a shared-interface model where a coupling parameter \(\theta \in [0,1]\) determines effective anti-rivalry—the L-component gets \(\beta_0\theta\), the T-component gets \(\beta_0(1-\theta)\)—the mixed payoff \(\pi_{\text{mix}}(w, \theta)\) for a firm with fraction \(w\) Leverage and \((1-w)\) Transform is convex in \(\theta\):

\[ \frac{\partial^2 \pi_{\text{mix}}}{\partial \theta^2} = V_0(\beta_0 \ln n)^2 h(y)\left[w\varphi_L n^{\beta_0\theta} + (1-w)\varphi_T n^{\beta_0(1-\theta)}\right] > 0 \]

for \(n > 1\), \(\beta_0 > 0\), \(w \in (0,1)\). Therefore:

  1. Any interior \(\theta^*\) is a minimum, not a maximum. The optimum is at \(\theta = 0\) or \(\theta = 1\).
  2. At either boundary, one component receives \(n^{\beta_0}\) (full benefit) while the other receives \(n^0 = 1\) (dead weight).
  3. Joint optimization over \((\theta, w)\): since the payoff after optimizing \(\theta\) is convex in \(w\), the global optimum is at a pure strategy \((w,\theta) \in \{(0,0), (1,1)\}\).

Proof

The second derivative is strictly positive because \(n^{\beta_0\theta}\) and \(n^{\beta_0(1-\theta)}\) are convex in \(\theta\) (exponentials of a linear function). The interior critical point from the FOC is therefore a minimum. On \(\theta \in [0,1]\), the maximum is at the boundary. The pointwise maximum \(\pi^*(w) = \max\{\pi_{\text{mix}}(w,0), \pi_{\text{mix}}(w,1)\}\) is convex in \(w\) (pointwise max of affine functions), so is maximized at \(w \in \{0,1\}\).

■

Scope condition. This result applies to products sharing a single rival–anti-rival interface. The coupling dial is a single parameter on that interface—you cannot simultaneously turn it up (full enhancement) and down (full decoupling). Multi-interface firms can operate different coupling levels on separate interfaces without incurring the incompatibility cost. The result holds for all \(n > 1\), with magnitude proportional to \((\beta_0 \ln n)^2\).

6. Dynamics: When Enhancement Becomes Viable

The anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\) is determined by the nature of the good and technological infrastructure, not by firm choice. As technology evolves (cloud, open APIs, AI), \(\beta\) increases secularly. We study the induced transitions from restriction to enhancement.

Theorem 6.1 (Phase Transitions in Business Model Landscapes)

Assumption: \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T < \beta_{\text{inv}}^L\) (Transform invades all-Restrict at lower \(\beta\) than Leverage). The landscape undergoes three phases:

Phase I (\(\beta < \beta_{\text{inv}}^T\)): Restrict is locally stable—both enhancement strategies have negative invasion eigenvalues. Neither can invade through the standalone channel. However, the \(L\)–\(R\) edge is bistable: all-Leverage is also stable on this edge, separated from all-Restrict by a tipping point \(x^*\). Full enhancement requires ecosystem coordination exceeding \(x^*\). The binding constraint is coordination, not anti-rivalry.

Phase II (\(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T \leq \beta < \beta_{\text{inv}}^L\)): Transform (partial enhancement) can invade all-Restrict (eigenvalue positive in the \(T\)-direction), making all-Restrict a saddle. The \(L\)–\(R\) tipping point \(x^*\) shrinks as \(\beta\) grows. Multiple stable states (all-Transform, all-Leverage) may coexist on their respective edges. Partial enhancement is available without coordination; full enhancement still requires a tipping point.

Phase III (\(\beta \geq \beta_{\text{inv}}^L\)): Restrict is unstable in both enhancement directions. The \(L\)–\(R\) tipping point vanishes. Full enhancement dominates through the combination of standalone efficiency and stronger ecosystem returns. Standalone efficiency suffices; coordination is no longer the constraint.

6.1 The Transform-to-Leverage Transition

On the \(L\)–\(T\) edge (no Restrict firms), Transform bootstraps ecosystem governance: \(h(0,1) = \omega/(\omega + \kappa_G) > 0\). This is the critical role of partial enhancement—it creates the governance infrastructure that full enhancement requires. Leverage can invade this Transform population when:

\[ \beta(\psi_L - \psi_T) + (\varphi_L - \varphi_T) V_0(1+a_T)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{\omega}{\omega + \kappa_G} > c_L - c_T \]

For strongly anti-rival goods (\(\beta\) large), the standalone advantage \(\beta(\psi_L - \psi_T)\) grows and full enhancement dominates. For weakly anti-rival goods, partial enhancement’s cost advantage prevails. This creates a second bifurcation within the enhancement strategy space. The practical implication: use partial enhancement (Transform) as a stepping stone to build ecosystem governance capacity, then transition to full enhancement (Leverage) as \(\beta\) increases.

7. Welfare: When Private and Social Optima Diverge

The payoffs \(\pi_L, \pi_T, \pi_R\) measure private fitness in the firm population game. They do not account for user participation. Full enhancement maximizes per-user ecosystem value through tight coupling, but its information rent extraction requires opacity, which reduces participation. This creates a wedge between private and social optima—a direct consequence of the transparency scarcity that firm-subsidized enhancement creates.

Definition 7.1 (Strategy-Dependent Transparency and Participation)

Each strategy has a characteristic transparency level \(\tau_s\) compatible with its value capture mechanism, with \(\tau_T > \tau_L\). User participation under strategy \(s\) is:

\[ n_s = n_0\left[\alpha + (1-\alpha)\tau_s^{\psi}\right] \]

Since participation is increasing in \(\tau\) and \(\tau_T > \tau_L\), we have \(n_T > n_L\): partial enhancement ecosystems attract more users.

Definition 7.2 (Social Welfare)

Social welfare at vertex equilibrium \(s\) is \(W_s = n_s \cdot E_s - c_s\), where the per-user ecosystem values are:

\[ E_L = V_0(1+\beta\lambda_L)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{1}{1+\kappa_G}, \qquad E_T = V_0(1+\beta\lambda_T)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{\omega}{\omega + \kappa_G} \]

\(E_L > E_T\) for all \(\beta > 0\) (full enhancement creates more per-user value). But \(n_T > n_L\) (partial enhancement attracts more users). Social welfare depends on the product \(n_s \cdot E_s\).

Theorem 7.1 (Private–Social Optimality Divergence)

Define the participation ratio \(P = n_T/n_L > 1\) and the per-user value ratio \(Q = E_L/E_T > 1\). Transform is socially superior (\(W_T > W_L\)) if and only if:

\[ P > Q - \frac{c_L - c_T}{n_L \cdot E_T} \]

Leverage is privately dominant when \(\beta(\psi_L - \psi_T) + \varphi_L E_L - \varphi_T E_T > c_L - c_T\). The divergence occurs when both conditions hold. The structural source: private fitness weights value by capture multipliers \(\varphi\); social welfare weights it by participation \(n\). Firms optimize for capture; welfare depends on reach.

Corollary 7.1 (Opacity Increases Suppression Cost)

The anti-rivalry suppression cost \(\Gamma = W_T - W_L\) is strictly decreasing in Leverage transparency:

\[ \frac{\partial \Gamma}{\partial \tau_L} = -E_L \cdot n_0(1-\alpha)\psi\tau_L^{\psi-1} < 0 \]

As full enhancement becomes more opaque, the welfare loss grows. The magnitude scales with \(E_L\): the more per-user value full enhancement creates, the larger the loss from each unit of participation sacrificed to opacity.

Proposition 7.1 (Phase II as the Critical Regime)

The per-user value ratio \(Q\) is increasing in \(\beta\) (approaching a finite limit); the participation ratio \(P\) is independent of \(\beta\). The welfare condition is most easily satisfied at lower \(\beta\), where \(Q\) is closer to 1. Since private dominance requires sufficient \(\beta\), the divergence region is concentrated at intermediate \(\beta\) (Phase II): both equilibria exist and \(Q\) is moderate, creating the widest parameter region where full enhancement is privately dominant but partial enhancement is socially superior.

A. Knowing your phase: where is \(\beta\)?

The phase thresholds \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T\) and \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^L\) from Proposition 2.1 determine which strategies are available. The practical question is: can you estimate \(\beta\) for your ecosystem? Since \(V(n) = V_0 n^{\beta}\), \(\beta\) is the elasticity of per-user value with respect to user count. If doubling users increases per-user value by 40%, then \(\beta \approx \log_2(1.4) \approx 0.49\). Ecosystems with high data complementarity (recommendation engines, collaborative filtering) will have higher \(\beta\); those with primarily consumptive use (streaming catalogs) will have lower \(\beta\).

Sensitivity. The thresholds \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^s = (c_s + R_0)/\psi_s\) are linearly increasing in costs \(c_s\) and the restrict payoff \(R_0\), and inversely proportional to standalone efficiency \(\psi_s\). This means:

  • Reducing enhancement costs (through better tooling, cloud infrastructure, automation) lowers the invasion threshold—making enhancement viable at weaker anti-rivalry.
  • When the restrict baseline \(R_0\) is high (strong incumbent rents from per-unit pricing), the threshold rises—ecosystems with valuable legacy business models are harder to transition.
  • Higher standalone efficiency \(\psi_s\) (the value a firm captures from anti-rivalry even without ecosystem coordination) lowers the threshold—this is the parameter that platform design most directly affects.

Recommendation. Estimate \(\beta\) empirically before choosing strategy. In Phase I (\(\beta < \beta_{\text{inv}}^T\)), enhancement requires coordinated adoption exceeding the tipping point \(x^*\)—a consortium, standard-setting body, or dominant platform must catalyze collective action. In Phase II, partial enhancement (Transform) is available unilaterally—use it to build ecosystem governance capacity. In Phase III, full enhancement is self-sustaining and the strategic question shifts to which enhancement path (firm or community), not whether to enhance.

B. Firm path: timing the transparency tipping point

The subsidization game (Definition 3.1) produces a tipping point \(p^*\) in the transparent fraction of platforms. Below \(p^*\), opacity is the dominant strategy; above it, transparency dominates through negative frequency dependence on opaque platforms (\(n_O(p) = n_0\alpha(1-\lambda p)\)).

Sensitivity. Three parameters govern the tipping point’s location:

  • Baseline participation \(\alpha\): High \(\alpha\) (users participate regardless of transparency) makes opacity less costly, pushing \(p^*\) higher—the market tolerates more opacity before switching. When users have strong outside options, \(\alpha\) falls and \(p^*\) drops—transparency becomes dominant earlier.
  • Transparency elasticity \(\psi\): Higher \(\psi\) means participation is more responsive to transparency. This steepens the transition around \(p^*\)—the switch from opacity-dominant to transparency-dominant is more abrupt, reducing the window for strategic timing.
  • Competitive penalty \(\lambda\): Higher \(\lambda\) means opaque platforms lose users faster as transparent competitors gain share. This lowers \(p^*\)—fewer transparent competitors are needed before opacity becomes unsustainable.

Recommendation. For a platform on the firm path: monitor the transparent fraction \(p\) among competitors. The optimal timing depends on the distance to \(p^*\). If \(p \ll p^*\), early transparency adoption sacrifices information rent without competitive benefit. If \(p\) approaches \(p^*\), delaying transparency is high-risk: crossing the tipping point triggers accelerating user loss from opaque platforms through the \((1-\lambda p)\) term. The asymmetry is crucial—the cost of moving too early is bounded (reduced rent), but the cost of moving too late is unbounded (cascading user loss). For regulators: disclosure requirements that push \(p\) above \(p^*\) trigger a self-reinforcing transition to transparency that does not require ongoing enforcement.

C. Community path: avoiding the governance trap

Theorem 4.1 gives a knife-edge condition: if \(\sigma < c_G - c_C\), governance-to-content ratio \(y/x\) declines exponentially at rate \(|c_G - c_C - \sigma|\). The condition is binary (collapse or not), but the speed of collapse is continuous and depends on the gap.

Sensitivity. Three levers affect the governance trap boundary:

  • Governance cost \(c_G\): Anything that reduces the marginal cost of governance labor—better moderation tools, AI-assisted curation, automated conflict resolution—lowers \(c_G\) and widens the survival region. This is the most direct lever for community designers.
  • Content cost \(c_C\): Lower \(c_C\) (easier content creation) paradoxically tightens the governance trap by widening the gap \(c_G - c_C\). As content creation becomes frictionless (AI-generated content, low-code tools), governance demands grow relative to content supply—the trap becomes harder to avoid.
  • Premium stacking (Proposition 4.1): The aggregate premium \(\sigma_{\text{total}} = \sigma_{\text{power}} + \sigma_{\text{rep}} + \sigma_{\text{fin}} + \sigma_{\text{strat}} + \sigma_{\text{social}}\) must exceed \(c_G - c_C\). Each individual premium source may be insufficient. The practical implication is that community governance design must stack multiple premium types simultaneously: decision-making power, reputational returns, financial compensation, strategic access, and social recognition.

Recommendation. (i) Monitor \(y/x\), the governance-to-content ratio, not surface metrics (user count, content volume). Surface metrics remain healthy during the burnout phase—the governance trap is invisible from the outside until collapse is imminent. (ii) Design governance premiums as a portfolio: no single premium source is likely to meet the threshold alone. Revenue sharing (financial) combined with decision-making power (power) and public recognition (social/reputational) is more robust than any one in isolation. (iii) Invest in governance cost reduction: AI-assisted moderation, dispute resolution tooling, and infrastructure automation lower \(c_G\) and widen the survival margin. (iv) Be alert to falling \(c_C\): when content creation becomes easier (e.g., generative AI lowers creation costs), the gap \(c_G - c_C\) widens and the governance premium requirement increases—communities must respond by increasing \(\sigma\) or reducing \(c_G\), not by celebrating the content growth.

D. Transform: viability band and the stepping-stone role

Proposition 5.1 establishes that de-transactionalization is viable within a band \(n_{\min} < n < n_{\max}\), and the competitive advantage over transactional models scales with \(\beta_0\) and price friction \(\eta p\). The advantage ratio is \((1+\eta p)^{\beta_0}\)—exponential in \(\beta_0\).

Sensitivity. Two regimes:

  • High \(\beta_0\), high \(\eta p\) (strongly anti-rival goods with significant per-use friction): The advantage is transformative. Moving from per-use to subscription dramatically increases realized network size and per-user value. Example: Spotify vs. iTunes—subscription removed per-track friction on a good with strong playlist and recommendation anti-rivalry.
  • Low \(\beta_0\) or low \(\eta p\) (weakly anti-rival goods or low per-use friction): The advantage is marginal. De-transactionalization reduces friction but the anti-rival upside is small. In this regime, transform is not worth the revenue model risk.

Recommendation. Transform is most valuable as a stepping stone in Phase II (Theorem 6.1). It invades the all-Restrict state at lower \(\beta\) than full enhancement, building ecosystem governance capacity (\(h(0,1) = \omega/(\omega+\kappa_G) > 0\)) that full enhancement later requires. The transition from transform to full enhancement occurs when \(\beta(\psi_L - \psi_T) + (\varphi_L - \varphi_T)V_a h > c_L - c_T\). Ecosystem designers in Phase II should adopt transform strategies with the explicit intention of transitioning to full enhancement as \(\beta\) grows, rather than treating transform as the final state.

E. Shared interfaces: when mixing is costly

Theorem 5.1 shows that on a shared interface, the incompatibility cost of mixing Leverage and Transform is strictly positive with magnitude proportional to \((\beta_0 \ln n)^2\). The practical question is when this cost is large enough to matter.

Sensitivity. The incompatibility cost grows in three dimensions:

  • Anti-rivalry \(\beta_0\): Quadratic in \(\beta_0\). For weakly anti-rival goods (\(\beta_0 \approx 0.1\)), the cost is negligible—mixing is approximately free. For strongly anti-rival goods (\(\beta_0 > 1\)), the cost dominates and pure strategies are strongly favored.
  • Network size \(n\): Grows as \((\ln n)^2\). For small communities (\(n \approx 100\)), the cost is modest. For large platforms (\(n > 10^6\)), it is substantial—the dead-weight component \(n^0 = 1\) versus the active component \(n^{\beta_0}\) creates an enormous performance gap.
  • Scope: shared vs. separate interfaces: The theorem applies only to products sharing a single rival–anti-rival interface. Multi-interface firms (e.g., a platform offering both a curated community feed and a flat-rate API) can operate different coupling levels on separate interfaces without incurring the cost. The interface architecture decision is thus a precondition for the strategy mix.

Recommendation. If your product has a single interface and \(\beta_0 \ln n > 1\), commit to either full enhancement or full transform on that interface—do not hedge. If you need both strategies (e.g., a community-driven content layer and a subscription access layer), architect them as separate interfaces with independent coupling parameters. The cost of architectural separation is paid once; the cost of mixing on a shared interface grows with \((\beta_0 \ln n)^2\) and is paid continuously.

F. Regulation: targeting the divergence

Theorem 7.1 identifies the structural source of private–social divergence: private fitness weights value by capture multipliers \(\varphi\), while social welfare weights it by participation \(n\). The divergence is widest in Phase II, where both full and partial enhancement coexist and the per-user value ratio \(Q = E_L/E_T\) is moderate.

Sensitivity. Corollary 7.1 gives the marginal welfare effect of transparency regulation: \(\partial\Gamma/\partial\tau_L = -E_L n_0(1-\alpha)\psi\tau_L^{\psi-1} < 0\). The welfare gain from increasing Leverage transparency is:

  • Increasing in \(E_L\): The more per-user value full enhancement creates, the larger the welfare gain from increasing its transparency. Regulation has the highest payoff in ecosystems where the firm path creates substantial value but suppresses participation through opacity.
  • Increasing in \((1-\alpha)\): When baseline participation is low (users are sensitive to terms), transparency regulation has a large effect on participation. When \(\alpha \approx 1\) (users participate regardless), regulation has little marginal effect.
  • Increasing in \(\psi\): Higher transparency elasticity means participation responds more strongly to disclosure. This amplifies the welfare effect of regulation.
  • Decreasing in \(\tau_L\): The term \(\tau_L^{\psi-1}\) with \(\psi < 1\) means the marginal return to transparency is highest at low transparency levels. The first unit of disclosure has the largest effect. This favors minimum disclosure requirements over graduated transparency mandates.

Recommendation. (i) Target transparency directly (\(\tau_L\)), not the enhancement strategy itself. Mandating community over firm paths forfeits the per-user value advantage of firm subsidy; increasing transparency on the firm path preserves its value creation while reducing participation suppression. (ii) Regulation is most productive in Phase II, where extraction norms are forming and path dependence has not yet locked in opacity. In Phase III, established platforms with entrenched opacity are harder to regulate and the capture multipliers \(\varphi\) are already calibrated to low transparency. (iii) Minimum disclosure requirements (raising the floor on \(\tau_L\)) are more efficient than graduated mandates, because the welfare gain per unit of transparency is highest at low \(\tau_L\). (iv) In ecosystems where \(P > Q\) already holds (participation advantage of partial enhancement exceeds the per-user value advantage of full enhancement), regulation may be unnecessary—the social optimum aligns with a viable private strategy. Focus regulatory resources on ecosystems where \(Q\) is large (high per-user value from full enhancement) and \(P\) is close to the boundary.

G. The credible-threat mechanism: community viability as market discipline

Subsection F treats direct transparency regulation as the primary policy lever. But the model contains a more powerful indirect mechanism: ensuring the community path is viable disciplines the firm path through market competition, without requiring ongoing regulatory enforcement.

The formal channel. The baseline participation \(\alpha\) in Definition 3.1 measures the fraction of users who participate regardless of the platform’s transparency level. This parameter is not truly exogenous—it is conditioned on whether users have an alternative. When no community-governed alternative exists, users are captive: even opaque platforms retain high participation (\(\alpha\) large). When a viable community alternative exists (i.e., the governance trap condition \(\sigma \geq c_G - c_C\) from Theorem 4.1 is satisfied), users who would otherwise tolerate opacity can defect. This reduces the effective \(\alpha\) that opaque firms face. Three consequences follow from the formal structure:

  1. The transparency tipping point drops. From subsection B, \(p^*\) is increasing in \(\alpha\)—higher captive participation sustains opacity longer. Reducing \(\alpha\) through community viability lowers \(p^*\), meaning fewer transparent competitors are needed before opacity becomes self-undermining. The transition to transparency occurs earlier and with less coordination.
  2. The welfare effect of any transparency improvement is amplified. From Corollary 7.1, \(\partial\Gamma/\partial\tau_L = -E_L n_0(1-\alpha)\psi\tau_L^{\psi-1}\). The magnitude is proportional to \((1-\alpha)\). Reducing \(\alpha\) through community viability increases \((1-\alpha)\), which means each unit of transparency improvement—whether voluntary or regulated—has a larger welfare effect. Community viability and transparency regulation are complements: each amplifies the other.
  3. The competitive penalty for opacity steepens. The negative frequency dependence \(n_O(p) = n_0\alpha(1-\lambda p)\) means opaque platforms lose users as transparent competitors gain share. When the community path provides a viable transparent alternative, it increases the effective competitive penalty \(\lambda\) and reduces the pool of captive users \(\alpha\). Both effects accelerate the tipping-point dynamics.

The policy chain. Rather than directly mandating transparency (\(\tau_L\)), the credible-threat approach works through an indirect chain:

\[ \underbrace{\text{Lower } c_G, \text{ increase } \sigma}_{\text{governance policy}} \;\;\longrightarrow\;\; \underbrace{\sigma \geq c_G - c_C}_{\text{community viable}} \;\;\longrightarrow\;\; \underbrace{\alpha \downarrow, \; \lambda \uparrow}_{\text{outside option exists}} \;\;\longrightarrow\;\; \underbrace{p^* \downarrow}_{\text{tipping point drops}} \;\;\longrightarrow\;\; \underbrace{\tau_L \uparrow}_{\text{firms compete on transparency}} \]

Each link in the chain corresponds to a formal result. The advantage over direct transparency regulation is that the equilibrium is self-enforcing: once the community alternative is viable, market competition sustains the pressure on firm transparency without ongoing regulatory enforcement. Direct regulation of \(\tau_L\) requires continuous monitoring and enforcement; community viability requires a one-time investment in governance infrastructure.

Concrete policy levers. The governance trap condition \(\sigma \geq c_G - c_C\) identifies two target parameters:

  • Reduce \(c_G\): Fund governance infrastructure—open-source moderation tools, shared dispute resolution systems, community management tooling, automated compliance. Each unit of \(c_G\) reduction widens the survival margin and makes governance sustainable for communities with smaller premium budgets. This is analogous to funding physical infrastructure (roads, courts) that enables market competition—governance infrastructure is the rivalrous complement that community-based anti-rival ecosystems require.
  • Increase \(\sigma\): Create institutional conditions for premium stacking. Tax treatment that recognizes governance labor as a contribution (not just volunteering), portability of reputation across platforms (\(\sigma_{\text{rep}}\)), legal frameworks for community revenue sharing (\(\sigma_{\text{fin}}\)), and antitrust policy that preserves strategic value of community participation (\(\sigma_{\text{strat}}\)). Each premium source that is institutionally supported increases \(\sigma_{\text{total}}\) and widens the set of communities that clear the governance trap threshold.

Sensitivity and failure modes. The credible-threat mechanism has two vulnerabilities. First, it requires the community alternative to be genuinely viable, not merely notionally present. If \(\sigma\) is close to \(c_G - c_C\) (the community barely survives), the threat is weak—\(\alpha\) decreases only slightly, and the tipping-point shift is marginal. The margin \(\sigma - (c_G - c_C)\) measures the credibility of the threat: larger margins produce more robust competitive discipline. Second, the mechanism depends on user mobility—users must be able to migrate from the firm path to the community path. Switching costs, data lock-in, and network effects that are specific to the firm platform reduce effective mobility, sustaining \(\alpha\) even when a community alternative exists. Interoperability requirements and data portability mandates complement the credible-threat mechanism by ensuring that users can actually exercise the outside option.

Recommendation. The most effective policy portfolio combines three elements: (i) governance infrastructure investment to ensure \(\sigma \geq c_G - c_C\) with margin (making the community path viable); (ii) interoperability and data portability requirements to reduce switching costs (ensuring users can exercise the outside option); and (iii) minimum transparency floors as a backstop for ecosystems where the community alternative is not yet viable. Elements (i) and (ii) are the primary instruments—they create a self-enforcing equilibrium. Element (iii) is a transitional measure for Phase II ecosystems where the community path has not yet cleared the governance trap threshold. This portfolio keeps the playing field level: it does not mandate which enhancement path ecosystems must follow, but ensures that the existence of a viable community alternative disciplines firm behavior through competition rather than regulation.

8. Discussion

Scarcity displacement as the core thesis. Anti-rival goods do not eliminate scarcity; they displace it. Enhancement via firm subsidy displaces scarcity to the subsidy relationship—who provides rival-cost coverage and on what terms (transparency, agency, control). Enhancement via community displaces it to governance labor—who maintains the rivalrous complement (attention, curation, infrastructure). The choice is not whether to create scarcity but which kind. Restriction avoids displacement entirely but forfeits the superlinear value that anti-rivalry creates.

Transform as mitigation, not resolution. The coupling dial (transform) reduces scarcity displacement by reducing the coupling that causes it—but this simultaneously reduces the anti-rival benefit. Theorem 5.1 shows that on a shared interface, you cannot simultaneously maximize coupling (full enhancement) and minimize it (full decoupling). The dial is a single parameter: turning it reduces both the benefit and the scarcity. This is why transform is a mitigation strategy, not a resolution of the underlying tension.

The binary choice structures everything. The restrict-vs-enhance framing is more fundamental than the firm-vs-community distinction. Once the decision to enhance is made, scarcity displacement is inevitable—the only remaining choice is which scarcity to create. The phase structure (Theorem 6.1) shows that restriction becomes untenable as \(\beta\) increases, making the scarcity choice increasingly urgent. Technological progress does not resolve the tension; it forces the choice.

Competition between enhancement paths disciplines both. The formal results reveal that the two enhancement paths are not merely alternatives but mutual checks. The firm path’s scarcity (opacity, dependency) is constrained when a viable community path exists, because the community alternative reduces the captive user base \(\alpha\) and lowers the transparency tipping point \(p^*\) (Section 7.1G). Conversely, the community path’s scarcity (governance labor) is partially alleviated when firm-subsidized alternatives set a quality floor that communities must match, preventing governance complacency. The healthiest ecosystem configuration is not one where either path dominates, but one where both are viable—each path’s displaced scarcity is constrained by the credible threat of defection to the other. Policy that ensures the viability of both paths (governance infrastructure for communities, transparency requirements for firms) creates a competitive equilibrium that is more robust than either path alone.

Limitations. The model assumes homogeneous agents within each strategy type, exogenous \(\beta\) (in reality firms invest in changing anti-rivalry), and commitment to fixed strategies (no switching or experimentation). The power-law and Michaelis–Menten functional forms are mathematically convenient but empirically unvalidated. We conjecture that the bistability and incompatibility results are robust to functional form, but specific phase thresholds are not.

9. Conclusion

Anti-rival goods create value that scales with use, but rival costs must be covered. The first-order choice is binary: restrict anti-rivalry or enhance it. Enhancement inevitably displaces scarcity—to transparency and control (firm path) or to governance and attention (community path). The coupling dial (transform) mitigates displacement at the cost of reduced benefit, but cannot eliminate it: on a shared interface, full enhancement and full decoupling are incompatible (Theorem 5.1). This paper formalizes both enhancement paths and the coupling dial, derives precise conditions for viability and failure (Propositions 2.1, 4.1, 5.1; Theorems 4.1, 5.1, 6.1), characterizes phase transitions as anti-rivalry increases, and identifies the parameter regime where private and social optima diverge (Theorem 7.1). The most effective policy does not mandate which path ecosystems follow but ensures that both paths remain viable: a credible community alternative disciplines firm opacity through market competition (by reducing captive participation \(\alpha\) and lowering the transparency tipping point \(p^*\)), while transparency requirements provide a backstop where community alternatives are not yet established. Scarcity displacement cannot be avoided—only navigated, and competition between the two enhancement paths constrains the scarcity that each creates.
