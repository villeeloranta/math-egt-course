Model V — The Incompatibility Theorem: Case Studies

Companion to Section 7 of the EGT Formalization

Model Recap

Combining leverage-seeking (symbiosis: amplifying rival–anti-rival coupling) and synergy-seeking (decreasing institutional complexity: reducing rival–anti-rival coupling) patterns within a single product line incurs an incompatibility cost. Leverage requires tight coupling between rival and anti-rival components (connecting them for mutual optimization), while Transform requires loose coupling (minimizing bidirectional interference). These opposing interface requirements cannot both be satisfied simultaneously.

Consider a mixed firm that allocates fraction \(w\) to Leverage and fraction \(1-w\) to Transform. If both patterns share a single interface, the firm must choose one interface parameter \(\theta\in[0,1]\), where \(\theta=1\) means full coupling (optimal for Leverage) and \(\theta=0\) means full decoupling (optimal for Transform). The mixed payoff is:

\[ \pi_{\text{mix}}(w,\theta) = w\,\varphi_L\,V_0\,n^{\beta_0\theta}\,h(y) + (1-w)\,\varphi_T\,V_0\,n^{\beta_0(1-\theta)}\,h(y) - w\,c_L - (1-w)\,c_T \]

The key result follows from the second derivative with respect to \(\theta\):

\[ \frac{\partial^2 \pi_{\text{mix}}}{\partial \theta^2} = V_0\,h(y)\,(\beta_0 \ln n)^2\left[w\,\varphi_L\,n^{\beta_0\theta} + (1-w)\,\varphi_T\,n^{\beta_0(1-\theta)}\right] > 0 \]

Since every term is positive, \(\pi_{\text{mix}}\) is strictly convex in \(\theta\). Convexity means any interior critical point \(\theta^*\) is a minimum, not a maximum. The firm maximises payoff at a boundary: either \(\theta=1\) (full coupling) or \(\theta=0\) (full decoupling).

  • At \(\theta=1\): the Leverage component gets \(n^{\beta_0}\) (full anti-rival scaling), but the Transform component gets \(n^{\beta_0(1-1)}=n^0=1\) — dead weight.
  • At \(\theta=0\): the Transform component gets \(n^{\beta_0}\), but the Leverage component gets \(n^0=1\) — dead weight.

The incompatibility is therefore derived from the convexity of the payoff function, not assumed. At any interior \(\theta\), both components underperform their pure-strategy benchmarks. At scale (\(n\) large), the convexity strengthens (\(\propto (\beta_0\ln n)^2\)), making pure strategies strictly dominant.

Case A: MongoDB — The Open Core Collapse

Case Context

MongoDB operated with an open-core model from 2007 to 2018: the core database used a Leverage approach (tightly coupling community database development with infrastructure optimization through open-source AGPL) while MongoDB Atlas and Enterprise Advanced used a Transform approach (minimizing infrastructure limitations on database-as-a-service sharing while minimizing the effect of cloud usage on infrastructure costs). In October 2018, MongoDB switched the server license to SSPL, moving from a mixed \(L+T\) strategy to a predominantly \(T\) strategy—restructuring the rival–anti-rival interface toward reduced coupling.

θ-Based Incompatibility Analysis

MongoDB’s open-core had an interface coupling that tried to serve both L (AGPL core) and T (Atlas/Enterprise) through a shared interface. The convexity result means the optimal interface is at a boundary. Setting \(V_0\,h(y)=1\) for normalisation, we compute payoffs at each boundary.

Preliminary: \(\ln(35{,}000)=10.46\), so \(n^{\beta_0}=35000^{0.30}=e^{3.14}=23.1\) and \(n^0=1\).

At \(\theta=1\) (full coupling — optimise for Leverage):

\[ \pi_{\text{mix}}(w,1) = 0.65\times 1.7\times 23.1 + 0.35\times 1.4\times 1 - 0.65\times 6 - 0.35\times 4 \] \[ = 25.53 + 0.49 - 3.90 - 1.40 = 20.72 \]

The Transform component contributes only 0.49 (dead weight: \(n^0=1\)).

At \(\theta=0\) (full decoupling — optimise for Transform):

\[ \pi_{\text{mix}}(w,0) = 0.65\times 1.7\times 1 + 0.35\times 1.4\times 23.1 - 0.65\times 6 - 0.35\times 4 \] \[ = 1.105 + 11.32 - 3.90 - 1.40 = 7.12 \]

The Leverage component contributes only 1.105 (dead weight: \(n^0=1\)).

Convexity strength: \((\beta_0\ln n)^2 = (0.30\times 10.46)^2 = (3.14)^2 = 9.86\). This strong convexity means any interior \(\theta\) is far below the boundary payoffs.

Comparing Pure vs Mixed Payoffs

Pure Leverage (\(w=1\), \(\theta=1\)): \(\pi_L = 1.7\times 23.1 - 6 = 39.3 - 6 = 33.3\)

Pure Transform (\(w=0\), \(\theta=0\)): \(\pi_T = 1.4\times 23.1 - 4 = 32.3 - 4 = 28.3\)

Best mixed boundary (\(w=0.65\), \(\theta=1\)): \(\pi_{\text{mix}}^{\theta=1} = 20.72\) (computed above).

Alternate mixed boundary (\(w=0.65\), \(\theta=0\)): \(\pi_{\text{mix}}^{\theta=0} = 7.12\) (computed above).

Ranking: \(\pi_L=33.3 > \pi_T=28.3 > \pi_{\text{mix}}^{\theta=1}=20.72 > \pi_{\text{mix}}^{\theta=0}=7.12\).

Both pure strategies dominate the best mixed strategy. At the better boundary (\(\theta=1\)), the mixed firm loses \(33.3-20.72=12.58\) relative to pure Leverage — this is the dead-weight cost of the Transform component receiving \(n^0=1\) instead of its own optimal scaling. The incompatibility is not an assumed penalty but a direct consequence of the convexity of \(\pi_{\text{mix}}\) in \(\theta\).

Finding

The model predicts that at MongoDB’s scale (\(n=35{,}000\) companies), pure leverage would be strictly superior to the open-core mix. Yet MongoDB moved toward more decoupling (SSPL), not less. Why?

The answer lies in who captures the value. Under pure leverage, the anti-rival value (\(V_0\times n^{0.30}=23.1\)) would be captured by the entire ecosystem, including cloud providers (AWS DocumentDB, Azure Cosmos DB) who contribute nothing to governance. MongoDB Inc. would capture only \(\varphi_L\) share, but \(\varphi_L\) was insufficient to cover its costs as a public company with shareholder obligations. The incompatibility theorem assumes the leverage firm captures the full \(\varphi_L\times V_a\); in practice, industrial-scale free-riders siphoned the anti-rival value, reducing MongoDB Inc.’s effective \(\varphi_L\).

This reveals a boundary condition of the theorem: it holds when the leverage firm is the primary beneficiary of the ecosystem it creates. When large free-riders (cloud hyperscalers) extract disproportionate value, the effective \(\varphi_L\) for the firm that bears \(c_L\) drops below the threshold, making Transform preferable despite the incompatibility cost.

Case B: GitLab — Navigating the Mixed Strategy at Growth Stage

Case Context

GitLab operates an open-core model (GitLab CE is MIT-licensed; GitLab EE is proprietary) that has persisted since 2011 without the license crises that hit MongoDB, Redis, or Elastic. As of 2025, GitLab has ~30M registered users, $580M ARR, and a market capitalisation of ~$8B. GitLab is the most successful sustained open-core company.

θ-Based Incompatibility Analysis

Preliminary: \(\ln(30\text{M})=17.22\), so \(n^{\beta_0}=30\text{M}^{0.20}=e^{3.444}=31.3\) and \(n^0=1\). Setting \(V_0\,h(y)=1\).

At \(\theta=1\) (full coupling): \(\pi_{\text{mix}}(0.75,1) = 0.75\times 1.5\times 31.3 + 0.25\times 1.3\times 1 - c = 35.21 + 0.33 - c\).

At \(\theta=0\) (full decoupling): \(\pi_{\text{mix}}(0.75,0) = 0.75\times 1.5\times 1 + 0.25\times 1.3\times 31.3 - c = 1.13 + 10.17 - c\).

The gap between boundaries: \(35.54 - 11.30 = 24.24\), favouring \(\theta=1\).

Convexity strength: \((\beta_0\ln n)^2 = (0.20\times 17.22)^2 = (3.44)^2 = 11.86\).

The incompatibility (dead-weight cost at the better boundary) is the Transform component’s loss: \(0.25\times 1.3\times(31.3-1)=9.85\). This is qualitatively smaller relative to total payoff than MongoDB’s case for two reasons: (1) the Leverage fraction \(w=0.75\) is higher (less weight on the dead-weight component), and (2) the lower \(\beta_0=0.20\) means the convexity strength \((\beta_0\ln n)^2\) is comparable to MongoDB’s \((3.14)^2=9.86\) only because GitLab’s much larger \(n\) compensates. For a same-scale comparison, lower \(\beta_0\) produces weaker convexity and a more tolerable incompatibility.

Finding

GitLab survives the mixed strategy for three reasons the theorem’s asymptotic analysis does not capture:

  1. The incompatibility cost is smaller because DevOps platforms have lower intrinsic anti-rivalry than databases. The convexity strength \((\beta_0\ln n)^2\) that drives boundary optimality is weaker for low-\(\beta_0\) goods at comparable scale.
  2. GitLab is still at growth stage. The theorem holds “for sufficiently large \(n\)”. At 30M users, GitLab may not yet be in the asymptotic regime. The theorem’s own caveat acknowledges that mixed strategies can be locally optimal at intermediate scale.
  3. No hyperscaler free-rider problem. Unlike MongoDB (where AWS built DocumentDB), no cloud provider has built a competing hosted GitLab service. GitLab’s effective \(\varphi_L\) is not eroded by industrial-scale free-riding.

The model predicts that if a cloud provider launches a managed GitLab-compatible service, GitLab will face the same pressure that drove MongoDB’s license change. This is a testable prediction.

Case C: WordPress — Successful Coexistence of Leverage and Transform

Case Context

WordPress (open source, GPL) powers 43% of all websites. Automattic (the company behind WordPress.com) operates a Transform strategy (minimizing server limitations on website sharing through managed hosting; minimizing the effect of site creation on infrastructure costs through tiered services) alongside the open-source Leverage strategy (connecting community plugin/theme development to each site’s quality optimization). This coexistence has been stable for nearly 20 years. Does this contradict the Incompatibility Theorem?

Parameter Analysis

ParameterValueKey Difference
\(\beta_0\)0.30High anti-rivalry: themes, plugins, tutorials, community knowledge all benefit from more users.
InterfaceSeparate \(\theta\)WordPress’s Leverage patterns (community plugin/theme ecosystem) and Transform patterns (premium hosting, managed services) target different interfaces. The L component has its own \(\theta_L\) (community software coupling) and the T component has its own \(\theta_T\) (hosting service decoupling). Each can be independently optimised.

Finding: The Complement Exception as a Precise Boundary Condition

WordPress does not contradict the theorem because its Leverage and Transform patterns target different interfaces. The L component (community plugin/theme ecosystem) controls its own interface parameter \(\theta_L\) and sets \(\theta_L=1\) (full coupling). The T component (premium hosting, managed services) controls its own interface parameter \(\theta_T\) and sets \(\theta_T=0\) (full decoupling). Each component independently achieves its optimal boundary without constraining the other.

Formally, the convexity argument requires a shared \(\theta\) constraint: both L and T components must use the same interface parameter. When this constraint is absent — when each component has its own \(\theta\) — the payoff decomposes into two independent optimisation problems, each solved at its own boundary. No convexity argument applies because there is no single \(\theta\) over which the joint payoff is convex.

\[ \pi = w\,\varphi_L\,V_0\,n^{\beta_0\theta_L}\,h(y) + (1-w)\,\varphi_T\,V_0\,n^{\beta_0(1-\theta_T)}\,h(y) - w\,c_L - (1-w)\,c_T \]

With independent \(\theta_L\) and \(\theta_T\), optimising yields \(\theta_L^*=1\) and \(\theta_T^*=0\) simultaneously. The mixed firm captures the full benefit of both components: no dead weight, no incompatibility.

This identifies a precise boundary condition: the theorem applies if and only if Leverage and Transform patterns share a single interface parameter \(\theta\). When they target different interfaces (community software development vs hosting services), they can coexist indefinitely. Anyone can download, install, and use WordPress without any interaction with Automattic’s paid offerings.

Key Insights

  1. The theorem’s boundary conditions matter as much as its conclusion. It applies when Leverage and Transform patterns share a single interface parameter \(\theta\) (MongoDB, GitLab) but not when each pattern controls its own \(\theta\) (WordPress). The shared-vs-separate \(\theta\) distinction is a precise, testable boundary condition.
  2. The severity of incompatibility scales with convexity strength \((\beta_0\ln n)^2\). High anti-rivalry goods (databases: \(\beta_0=0.30\)) at moderate scale produce strong convexity and severe dead-weight costs at the optimal boundary. Lower anti-rivalry goods (DevOps: \(\beta_0=0.20\)) produce weaker convexity at comparable scale, making the incompatibility more tolerable.
  3. Hyperscaler free-riding is the precipitating event. The theorem predicts that pure strategies dominate mixed strategies for all \(n > 1\). But firms only abandon the mixed strategy when large free-riders erode their effective \(\varphi_L\). Without the free-rider trigger, the mixed strategy can persist even when it is theoretically suboptimal (GitLab).
  4. Mixed strategies may be optimal transitional configurations. The theorem’s own caveat — that mixed strategies may be locally optimal at intermediate scale — is empirically supported. GitLab’s open-core model has enabled growth that a purely open or purely proprietary approach might not have achieved.
