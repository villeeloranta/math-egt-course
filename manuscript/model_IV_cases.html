Model IV — De-Transactionalization and Anti-Rivalry Amplification: Case Studies

Companion to Section 6 of the EGT Formalization

Model Recap

Transaction-based models impose a per-use price \(p\) that dampens effective anti-rivalry:

\[ \beta_{\text{trans}} = \beta_0 - \frac{p}{V_0}, \qquad \beta_{\text{sub}} = \beta_0 \]

Subscription viability requires \(n_{\min} < n < n_{\max}\) where \(n\cdot f > C_0 + c_m\,n^\gamma\).

Case A: Spotify vs iTunes — Music’s De-Transactional Revolution

Case Context

The music industry’s shift from iTunes (per-track purchase at $0.99–$1.29) to Spotify (monthly subscription at $9.99 for unlimited streaming) is the canonical example of de-transactionalization enabling anti-rivalry. Under iTunes, each play was a transaction; under Spotify, consumption is decoupled from payment, enabling unrestricted sharing and discovery.

Parameter Estimation

ParameteriTunes (Transactional)Spotify (Subscription)
\(V_0\)5 (base value of a music library)
\(\beta_0\)0.25 (intrinsic anti-rivalry: shared playlists, collaborative recommendations, social listening improve with more users)
\(p\) (per-use price)$1.00 per track$0 per track
\(\beta_{\text{eff}}\)\(0.25 - 1.0/5 = 0.05\)\(0.25\)
\(f\) (subscription fee)—$9.99/month
\(n\) (users, millions)~800M accounts (2014 peak)~640M users (2025)

Anti-Rivalry Amplification

The effective anti-rivalry shift from transactional to subscription:

\[ \Delta\beta = \beta_{\text{sub}} - \beta_{\text{trans}} = 0.25 - 0.05 = 0.20 \]

Per-user value comparison at \(n=500\)M users:

\[ V_{\text{iTunes}}(500) = 5\times 500^{0.05} = 5\times e^{0.05\ln 500} = 5\times e^{0.317} = 5\times 1.373 = 6.87 \] \[ V_{\text{Spotify}}(500) = 5\times 500^{0.25} = 5\times e^{0.25\times 6.215} = 5\times e^{1.554} = 5\times 4.73 = 23.65 \]

The de-transactionalized model delivers 3.4× more per-user value at the same scale. This value manifests as algorithmic recommendations trained on collective listening data, collaborative playlists, artist discovery through social features, and Spotify Wrapped — all of which are anti-rival features impossible under a per-track model.

Subscription Viability Band

Spotify’s cost structure (estimated): \(C_0\approx\)$200M/month (fixed), \(c_m\approx\)$0.004 per user-month at scale (\(\gamma\approx 1.05\), slight superlinear scaling from licensing costs that increase with usage volume).

\[ n\cdot 9.99 > 200 + 0.004\cdot n^{1.05} \]

At \(n=200\text{M}\): Revenue = $2.0B, Cost = $200M + $0.004\times 200^{1.05}\text{M} = $200M + $0.004\times 224\text{M} = $200M + $896M = $1.1B. Viable (revenue > cost).

At \(n=20\text{M}\): Revenue = $200M, Cost = $200M + $0.004\times 21.4\text{M} = $200M + $86M = $286M. Not viable. \(n_{\min}\approx 30\text{M}\).

Spotify was founded in 2006 and reached profitability only in 2023 with ~600M users, suggesting \(n_{\min}\) for full profitability (including content investment) was approximately 500–600M. The 17-year journey from launch to profitability illustrates the perilous nature of subscription models’ viability band.

Finding

The Spotify/iTunes transition validates the model’s core mechanism: de-transactionalization amplifies anti-rivalry by a factor of \(\Delta\beta/\beta_{\text{trans}} = 0.20/0.05 = 4\times\), unlocking features (recommendations, social listening, discovery) that are impossible under transactional pricing. However, the model also correctly predicts the peril: the critical subscription mass is large, and the firm must survive an extended unprofitable period before reaching \(n_{\min}\). Spotify survived this gap through venture capital (\(>$4B\) raised before profitability) — a form of subsidy that the basic model does not endogenise.

Case B: Netflix vs Blockbuster — Subscription Destroying Transactional Rivals

Case Context

Blockbuster Video (per-rental transaction: ~$4/rental + late fees) vs Netflix (monthly subscription: $7.99–$22.99). Blockbuster filed for bankruptcy in 2010 with $900M in debt. Netflix reached 283M subscribers by 2025. The transition destroyed $6B in Blockbuster’s peak market capitalization while Netflix’s reached $300B+.

Analysis

ParameterBlockbusterNetflix
\(\beta_0\)0.20 (intrinsic anti-rivalry: shared viewing data enables better recommendations; communal viewing culture creates conversation value)
\(p\) (per use)$4.00/rental$0/view
\(V_0\)8 (entertainment value)
\(\beta_{\text{eff}}\)\(0.20 - 4/8 = -0.30\) (rival!)\(0.20\)

Striking Result: Transactional Pricing Makes Entertainment Rival

Under Blockbuster’s pricing, \(\beta_{\text{trans}} = 0.20 - 0.50 = -0.30\). The per-rental fee was so high relative to base value that it converted an intrinsically weakly anti-rival good into an effectively rival good. Each rental was a scarce, zero-sum transaction. Late fees further increased the rivalry: your retention of a DVD literally prevented others from watching it.

Netflix’s subscription eliminates this artificial rivalry entirely, restoring \(\beta_{\text{sub}}=0.20\). The shift from \(\beta=-0.30\) to \(\beta=+0.20\) crosses the rivalry–anti-rivalry boundary — a qualitative transformation, not merely a quantitative improvement.

Finding

The Netflix/Blockbuster case provides the strongest validation of the de-transactionalization model because the per-use price was so high that it actually reversed the sign of the anti-rivalry parameter. The model predicts that de-transactionalization is most disruptive when \(p/V_0\) is large enough to push \(\beta_{\text{trans}}\) below zero — converting an intrinsically anti-rival good into an effectively rival one. This is precisely what happened: physical video rental’s transaction costs made entertainment rival, and subscription streaming made it anti-rival. The entire value proposition changed qualitatively, not just quantitatively.

Case C: Adobe Creative Cloud — De-Transactionalization with Resistance

Case Context

Adobe transitioned from perpetual licenses (~$2,600 for Creative Suite) to monthly subscriptions ($54.99/month for Creative Cloud) in 2013. Unlike music or video, design software’s anti-rivalry is mediated through shared assets: templates, plugins, fonts, community tutorials, and shared project files. The transition was controversial, with significant user resistance.

Analysis

ParameterPerpetual LicenseCreative Cloud
\(\beta_0\)0.15 (moderate anti-rivalry: shared templates, plugins, tutorials benefit all users, but core tool functionality is non-rival rather than anti-rival)
\(p/V_0\)Amortised: ~$100/month over 26 months = \(p/V_0 \approx 0.08\)$0 per use
\(\beta_{\text{eff}}\)\(0.15 - 0.08 = 0.07\)\(0.15\)
\(\Delta\beta\)\(0.08\)

Anti-Rivalry Amplification

\[ \frac{V_{\text{CC}}}{V_{\text{perp}}} = \frac{n^{0.15}}{n^{0.07}}=n^{0.08} \]

At \(n=30\text{M}\) (Creative Cloud subscribers): \(30^{0.08}=e^{0.08\times 3.40}=e^{0.272}=1.31\).

Only a 31% improvement in per-user value — significant but not transformative. Compare with Spotify’s 3.4× or Netflix’s sign-reversal.

Finding

The Adobe case illustrates why de-transactionalization is less disruptive for weakly anti-rival goods. Design software’s intrinsic anti-rivalry (\(\beta_0=0.15\)) is weaker than music or video because the core value proposition (image editing, layout) is non-rival, not anti-rival. The community layer (templates, plugins, tutorials) adds anti-rivalry, but it is secondary to the core tool functionality. Consequently, \(\Delta\beta=0.08\) is small, and the value amplification modest.

This explains the user resistance to Creative Cloud: users paid roughly the same over time ($55/month × 12 = $660/year vs $2,600/26 months = $100/month) but perceived a loss of ownership (perpetual license) in exchange for a modest anti-rivalry benefit (cloud sync, collaboration features, community assets). The de-transactionalization was financially motivated by Adobe (recurring revenue is more predictable) rather than driven by unlocking anti-rival value.

Critical Assessment

The Adobe case challenges the model’s assumption that de-transactionalization always amplifies anti-rivalry. When the good’s core value is non-rival (tool functionality) rather than anti-rival (community effects), de-transactionalization primarily shifts the revenue model without meaningfully changing \(\beta_{\text{eff}}\). The model should distinguish between goods where anti-rivalry is primary (music, social networks) and goods where it is secondary (productivity tools). For the latter, de-transactionalization may be better understood as a financial engineering decision than an anti-rivalry amplification strategy.

Key Insights

  1. De-transactionalization is most disruptive when \(p/V_0\) is large. When per-use pricing is high relative to base value, removing it produces a large \(\Delta\beta\) and potentially reverses the sign of effective anti-rivalry. Netflix/Blockbuster is the extreme case.
  2. Intrinsic anti-rivalry determines the payoff. De-transactionalization only amplifies anti-rivalry that already exists. For goods with high \(\beta_0\) (music, social), the amplification is transformative. For goods with low \(\beta_0\) (productivity tools), it is marginal.
  3. The viability band is the critical constraint. Subscription models must cross \(n_{\min}\) before running out of capital. Spotify took 17 years; Netflix took 10; Adobe achieved it quickly because it converted existing perpetual-license customers to subscribers (a captive conversion, not a cold start).
