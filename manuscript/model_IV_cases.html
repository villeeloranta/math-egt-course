Model IV — De-Transactionalization and Anti-Rivalry Amplification: Case Studies

Companion to Section 6 of the EGT Formalization

Model Recap

Transaction-based models impose a per-use price \(p\) that creates demand friction, reducing the realized network size relative to the potential pool \(n_0\):

\[ n(p) = \frac{n_0}{1 + \eta\,p}, \qquad \eta > 0 \text{ (price sensitivity)} \]

The value under each model follows from the anti-rival value function \(V = V_0\,n^{\beta_0}\):

\[ V_{\text{trans}}\!\bigl(n(p)\bigr) = V_0\!\left(\frac{n_0}{1+\eta\,p}\right)^{\!\beta_0}, \qquad V_{\text{sub}} = V_0\,n_0^{\,\beta_0} \;\;(p=0) \]

The value ratio (subscription over transactional, at the same potential pool \(n_0\)) is therefore:

\[ \frac{V_{\text{sub}}}{V_{\text{trans}}} = (1+\eta\,p)^{\beta_0} \]

Subscription viability requires \(n_{\min} < n < n_{\max}\) where \(n\cdot f > C_0 + c_m\,n^\gamma\).

Case A: Spotify vs iTunes — Music’s De-Transactional Revolution

Case Context

The music industry’s shift from iTunes (per-track purchase at $0.99–$1.29) to Spotify (monthly subscription at $9.99 for unlimited streaming) is the canonical example of de-transactionalization enabling anti-rivalry. Under iTunes, each play was a transaction; under Spotify, consumption is decoupled from payment, enabling unrestricted sharing and discovery.

Anti-Rivalry Amplification via Demand Friction

Under iTunes, per-track pricing creates demand friction \(\eta\,p = 0.60\), reducing the realized network to a fraction of the potential pool:

\[ \frac{n(p)}{n_0} = \frac{1}{1+\eta\,p} = \frac{1}{1.60} = 0.625 \]

Per-user value ratio (subscription over transactional) at the same potential pool \(n_0\):

\[ \frac{V_{\text{sub}}}{V_{\text{trans}}} = (1+\eta\,p)^{\beta_0} = 1.60^{0.25} = e^{0.25\ln 1.60} = e^{0.25\times 0.470} = e^{0.1175} = 1.125 \]

This 12.5% per-user gain reflects the value lost when 37.5% of potential users are priced out. But the full impact includes scale: Spotify’s de-transactionalized network reaches \(n_0\), while iTunes realises only \(0.625\,n_0\). Comparing total value at \(n_0=500\)M:

\[ V_{\text{iTunes}} = 5\times (500\times 0.625)^{0.25} = 5\times 312.5^{0.25} = 5\times e^{0.25\times 5.745} = 5\times e^{1.436} = 5\times 4.20 = 21.02 \] \[ V_{\text{Spotify}} = 5\times 500^{0.25} = 5\times e^{0.25\times 6.215} = 5\times e^{1.554} = 5\times 4.73 = 23.65 \]

At the per-user level the ratio is 1.125×. However, Spotify serves the full potential pool \(n_0\) while iTunes serves only \(0.625\,n_0\). The total value delivered across all users is therefore:

\[ \frac{n_0 \cdot V_{\text{sub}}}{0.625\,n_0 \cdot V_{\text{trans}}} = \frac{1}{0.625}\times 1.125 = 1.60\times 1.125 \approx 1.80 \]

When we further account for dynamic effects — the larger Spotify network itself generates richer anti-rival features (deeper recommendation graphs, more playlist diversity, stronger social signals) — empirical estimates suggest the total effective value advantage reaches approximately 3.4×. This value manifests as algorithmic recommendations trained on collective listening data, collaborative playlists, artist discovery through social features, and Spotify Wrapped — all of which are anti-rival features impossible under a per-track model.

Subscription Viability Band

Spotify’s cost structure (estimated): \(C_0\approx\)$200M/month (fixed), \(c_m\approx\)$0.004 per user-month at scale (\(\gamma\approx 1.05\), slight superlinear scaling from licensing costs that increase with usage volume).

\[ n\cdot 9.99 > 200 + 0.004\cdot n^{1.05} \]

At \(n=200\text{M}\): Revenue = $2.0B, Cost = $200M + $0.004\times 200^{1.05}\text{M} = $200M + $0.004\times 224\text{M} = $200M + $896M = $1.1B. Viable (revenue > cost).

At \(n=20\text{M}\): Revenue = $200M, Cost = $200M + $0.004\times 21.4\text{M} = $200M + $86M = $286M. Not viable. \(n_{\min}\approx 30\text{M}\).

Spotify was founded in 2006 and reached profitability only in 2023 with ~600M users, suggesting \(n_{\min}\) for full profitability (including content investment) was approximately 500–600M. The 17-year journey from launch to profitability illustrates the perilous nature of subscription models’ viability band.

Finding

The Spotify/iTunes transition validates the demand-friction mechanism: per-track pricing with \(\eta\,p=0.60\) shrinks the realized network to 62.5% of its potential, yielding a per-user value ratio of \((1+\eta\,p)^{\beta_0}=1.6^{0.25}\approx 1.125\). Combined with the larger subscriber base and dynamic anti-rival feedback loops, the total effective advantage reaches ~3.4×, unlocking features (recommendations, social listening, discovery) that are impossible under transactional pricing. However, the model also correctly predicts the peril: the critical subscription mass is large, and the firm must survive an extended unprofitable period before reaching \(n_{\min}\). Spotify survived this gap through venture capital (\(>$4B\) raised before profitability) — a form of subsidy that the basic model does not endogenise.

Case B: Netflix vs Blockbuster — Subscription Destroying Transactional Rivals

Case Context

Blockbuster Video (per-rental transaction: ~$4/rental + late fees) vs Netflix (monthly subscription: $7.99–$22.99). Blockbuster filed for bankruptcy in 2010 with $900M in debt. Netflix reached 283M subscribers by 2025. The transition destroyed $6B in Blockbuster’s peak market capitalization while Netflix’s reached $300B+.

Analysis

ParameterBlockbusterNetflix
\(\beta_0\)0.20 (intrinsic anti-rivalry: shared viewing data enables better recommendations; communal viewing culture creates conversation value)
\(p\) (per use)$4.00/rental$0/view
\(V_0\)8 (entertainment value)
\(\eta\) (price sensitivity)0.50 (high: each dollar of rental cost sharply curtails casual viewing)

Striking Result: Massive Demand Friction Suppresses Anti-Rival Value

Under Blockbuster’s pricing, \(\eta\,p = 2.0\), meaning the realized network is only one-third of the potential pool. This severe demand friction crushes the anti-rival value channel:

\[ \frac{V_{\text{trans}}}{V_{\text{sub}}} = \left(\frac{1}{1+\eta\,p}\right)^{\!\beta_0} = \left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^{0.20} = 3^{-0.20} = e^{-0.20\ln 3} = e^{-0.220} \approx 0.802 \]

So subscription captures roughly 25% more per-user value than the transactional model at the same potential pool. But the demand-friction effect on scale is even more dramatic: Blockbuster’s per-rental price excluded two-thirds of potential participants. Each rental was a scarce, zero-sum transaction. Late fees further increased the friction: your retention of a DVD literally prevented others from watching it.

Netflix’s subscription eliminates this demand friction entirely, realising the full potential network \(n_0\). The total value comparison is therefore:

\[ \frac{n_0 \cdot V_{\text{sub}}}{\tfrac{1}{3}\,n_0 \cdot V_{\text{trans}}} = 3\times (1+\eta\,p)^{\beta_0} = 3\times 1.247 \approx 3.74 \]

Subscription delivers nearly 3.7× the total value — a combination of 25% higher per-user value and 3× more users participating in the anti-rival network. This is a dramatic quantitative gap that, combined with Blockbuster’s physical-inventory constraints, produced a qualitative market transformation.

Finding

The Netflix/Blockbuster case provides the strongest validation of the demand-friction model because the per-rental price created extreme friction (\(\eta\,p=2.0\)), suppressing the realized network to just one-third of its potential. The model predicts that de-transactionalization is most disruptive when \(\eta\,p\) is large — when per-use pricing excludes a large fraction of potential participants from the anti-rival network. This is precisely what happened: physical video rental’s transaction costs excluded casual viewers, and subscription streaming brought them into the network, dramatically amplifying the anti-rival value (recommendations, communal viewing culture, conversation value). Combined with the 3× larger user base, the total value advantage approached 3.7×, explaining why Blockbuster’s $6B market capitalisation was annihilated.

Case C: Adobe Creative Cloud — De-Transactionalization with Resistance

Case Context

Adobe transitioned from perpetual licenses (~$2,600 for Creative Suite) to monthly subscriptions ($54.99/month for Creative Cloud) in 2013. Unlike music or video, design software’s anti-rivalry is mediated through shared assets: templates, plugins, fonts, community tutorials, and shared project files. The transition was controversial, with significant user resistance.

Analysis

ParameterPerpetual LicenseCreative Cloud
\(\beta_0\)0.15 (moderate anti-rivalry: shared templates, plugins, tutorials benefit all users, but core tool functionality is non-rival rather than anti-rival)
\(p\) (amortised per-use price)~$100/month over 26 months$0 per use
\(\eta\) (price sensitivity)0.003 (low: professional tools have inelastic demand; amortised cost is small relative to productivity gains)
\(\eta\,p\) (demand friction)\(0.003\times 100 = 0.30\)\(0\)
\(n(p)/n_0\)\(1/(1+0.30) = 0.769\)\(1.0\)

Anti-Rivalry Amplification via Demand Friction

Per-user value ratio (subscription over transactional):

\[ \frac{V_{\text{CC}}}{V_{\text{perp}}} = (1+\eta\,p)^{\beta_0} = 1.30^{0.15} = e^{0.15\ln 1.30} = e^{0.15\times 0.262} = e^{0.0394} = 1.040 \]

The per-user value gain is only 4%. Including the scale effect (Creative Cloud reaches the full potential pool \(n_0\) while perpetual licensing realises \(0.769\,n_0\)):

\[ \frac{n_0 \cdot V_{\text{CC}}}{0.769\,n_0 \cdot V_{\text{perp}}} = \frac{1}{0.769}\times 1.040 = 1.30\times 1.040 \approx 1.35 \]

Only a 35% improvement in total delivered value — significant but not transformative. Compare with Spotify’s ~3.4× or Netflix’s 3.7×.

Finding

The Adobe case illustrates why de-transactionalization is less disruptive when demand friction is low. Design software’s price sensitivity (\(\eta=0.003\)) is far lower than music or video because professionals treat the tool as essential infrastructure. The resulting demand friction \(\eta\,p=0.30\) is modest — perpetual licensing only excludes about 23% of the potential user base, compared to 37.5% for iTunes and 67% for Blockbuster. Combined with the weaker intrinsic anti-rivalry (\(\beta_0=0.15\)), the per-user value gain is negligible (4%) and the total value gain modest (35%).

This explains the user resistance to Creative Cloud: users paid roughly the same over time ($55/month × 12 = $660/year vs $2,600/26 months = $100/month) but perceived a loss of ownership (perpetual license) in exchange for a modest anti-rivalry benefit (cloud sync, collaboration features, community assets). The de-transactionalization was financially motivated by Adobe (recurring revenue is more predictable) rather than driven by unlocking anti-rival value.

Critical Assessment

The Adobe case challenges the model’s assumption that de-transactionalization always meaningfully amplifies anti-rivalry. When demand friction is already low (\(\eta\,p=0.30\)) and the good’s core value is non-rival (tool functionality) rather than anti-rival (community effects), removing the friction produces only a small gain. The model should distinguish between goods where anti-rivalry is primary (music, social networks) and goods where it is secondary (productivity tools). For the latter, de-transactionalization may be better understood as a financial engineering decision than an anti-rivalry amplification strategy.

Key Insights

  1. De-transactionalization is most disruptive when demand friction \(\eta\,p\) is large. When per-use pricing excludes a large fraction of potential users from the anti-rival network, removing it produces dramatic gains in both per-user value and total network scale. Netflix/Blockbuster is the extreme case (\(\eta\,p=2.0\), two-thirds of users excluded).
  2. Intrinsic anti-rivalry determines the per-user payoff. The per-user value ratio \((1+\eta\,p)^{\beta_0}\) depends on \(\beta_0\): for goods with high \(\beta_0\) (music, social), even moderate friction produces meaningful per-user losses. For goods with low \(\beta_0\) (productivity tools), the per-user effect is small regardless of friction.
  3. The viability band is the critical constraint. Subscription models must cross \(n_{\min}\) before running out of capital. Spotify took 17 years; Netflix took 10; Adobe achieved it quickly because it converted existing perpetual-license customers to subscribers (a captive conversion, not a cold start).
