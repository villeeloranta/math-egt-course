An Evolutionary Game-Theoretic Formalization of Anti-Rival Business Model Complexity Management

Analytical Extension of “Managing the Complexities of Digital Goods’ Anti-Rivalry in Contemporary Business Model Innovation”

Working Paper — March 2026

Abstract

This paper develops a formal evolutionary game-theoretic (EGT) framework for analyzing the complexity management mechanisms and business model patterns (BMPs) identified in the study of anti-rival digital goods. We translate the seven complexity management mechanisms and three metamechanisms into explicit payoff functions, replicator dynamics, and stability conditions. Our formalization yields several analytical results: (i) a three-strategy replicator model with a phase transition structure: at low anti-rivalry, restrictive strategies dominate; at intermediate levels, synergy-seeking (complexity-decreasing) strategies become viable; and above a critical threshold, symbiosis-seeking (complexity-leveraging) strategies become viable and may be evolutionarily stable under the corresponding transverse invasion conditions; (ii) a formal incompatibility theorem showing that, under a shared-interface coupling-allocation model, mixed leveraging/decreasing strategies within a single business model are weakly dominated by pure approaches, and generically strictly dominated; (iii) an extension of the governance trap framework to business model communities, deriving sufficient conditions for collapse together with threshold conditions for the sustainability of community-based mechanisms; and (iv) a phase transition analysis revealing bifurcation points at which the equilibrium business model landscape shifts qualitatively as technological anti-rivalry increases. These results provide analytical depth to the qualitative findings of the original study while generating testable predictions and design prescriptions for business model innovators operating with anti-rival digital goods.

Keywords: evolutionary game theory; replicator dynamics; anti-rival goods; business model patterns; institutional complexity; stability analysis; phase transitions

1. Introduction and Motivation

The original manuscript identifies seven complexity management mechanisms and 39 related business model patterns through which firms can benefit from the anti-rivalry of digital goods while managing the institutional complexity that arises from mixing rival and anti-rival resources. The study’s qualitative findings—organized into three metamechanisms and the symbiosis-seeking versus synergy-seeking distinction—represent a substantial contribution to the anti-rival goods discourse. However, the original analysis remains, by design, qualitative and classificatory. The present work responds to the original manuscript’s explicit call for “systems dynamics methods that provide tools for analyzing complex systems” by providing an evolutionary game-theoretic formalization.

Why evolutionary game theory? Three features of the business model pattern landscape make EGT the appropriate analytical framework. First, frequency dependence: the value of anti-rival-friendly business models depends critically on how many other firms adopt similar approaches, because anti-rival goods increase in value with use. A single firm adopting anti-rival-friendly complexity management practices in a market of proprietary competitors faces a different payoff landscape than one operating among many firms that leverage or transform institutional complexity for anti-rival benefits. Second, bounded rationality: firms do not solve global optimization problems when choosing business models; they imitate successful competitors, experiment incrementally, and respond to market signals—dynamics well captured by replicator dynamics (Hofbauer & Sigmund, 1998). Third, population-level selection: the organizational ecology literature (Hannan & Freeman, 1977) has long recognized that business model viability is determined by environmental selection as much as by deliberate design, and EGT provides the mathematical apparatus for analyzing such selection dynamics.

Our formalization makes four principal contributions:

  1. A three-strategy replicator model at the metamechanism level, establishing conditions under which leveraging versus decreasing approaches become viable and, under the corresponding invasion conditions, evolutionarily stable (Section 3).
  2. An extension of the governance trap framework to community-based business model mechanisms, connecting the manuscript’s Mechanism 1 (15 BMPs) to precise collapse and sustainability threshold conditions (Section 4).
  3. A formal Incompatibility Theorem demonstrating that, under a shared-interface coupling-allocation model, mixed leveraging–decreasing strategies are evolutionarily dominated (Section 7).
  4. A phase transition analysis showing how increasing technological anti-rivalry drives qualitative shifts (bifurcations) in the equilibrium business model landscape (Section 8).

Throughout, we maintain a critical stance toward our own formalization. Mathematical models impose structural assumptions that may not hold in practice, and we identify these assumptions explicitly, assess their plausibility, and characterize the robustness of our results to their violation.

2. Theoretical Foundations

2.1 Anti-Rivalry as a Continuous Parameter

Following the mathematical framework developed in the course modules, we parameterize the rivalry–anti-rivalry spectrum through a single exponent \(\beta\). For a good with \(n\) users, the per-user value function takes the power-law form:

\[ V(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{\beta} \]

where \(\beta < 0\) corresponds to rival goods (value decreases with use), \(\beta = 0\) to non-rival goods (value invariant to use), and \(\beta > 0\) to anti-rival goods (value increases with use). The total social value \(W(n) = n \cdot V(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{1+\beta}\) is superlinear for \(\beta > 0\), capturing the increasingly positive-sum nature of anti-rival goods. This parameterization, while mathematically convenient, deserves scrutiny: real goods rarely exhibit pure power-law scaling across all ranges of \(n\), and many anti-rival goods exhibit bounded anti-rivalry (logistic saturation). We address robustness to functional form in Section 10.

2.2 The Coupled Value Function

A central insight from Module 10 is that anti-rivalry is never pure. Every anti-rivalrous resource depends on rivalrous complements—maintainer time, server infrastructure, editorial attention. The coupled value function captures this dependency:

Definition 2.1 (Coupled Value Function)

For an anti-rivalrous resource with \(n\) users depending on a rivalrous complement with total supply \(M\):

\[ V(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{\beta} \cdot h\!\left(\frac{M}{n^{\gamma}}\right), \qquad h(s) = \frac{s}{s + \kappa} \]

where \(\gamma > 0\) is the demand scaling exponent for the rivalrous complement and \(\kappa > 0\) is the half-saturation constant.

The function \(h\) (Michaelis–Menten or Monod form) acts as a quality modulator: when the rivalrous complement is abundant relative to demand (\(s \gg \kappa\)), quality approaches 1; when scarce (\(s \ll \kappa\)), quality collapses toward 0. The condition \(\gamma > \beta\) ensures that an optimal community size \(n^*\) exists, beyond which the rivalrous bottleneck dominates the anti-rivalrous benefit.

2.3 Business Model Patterns as Evolutionary Strategies

We treat the population of firms in a digital economy as the evolving population. Each firm adopts a business model configuration—a combination of BMPs—that determines its fitness (economic viability, growth rate, imitation attractiveness). The replicator dynamics then describe how the population distribution across business model configurations evolves over time.

Remark 2.1 (Validity of the Evolutionary Metaphor)

This mapping requires justification. Unlike biological organisms, firms can (i) switch strategies mid-life, (ii) combine multiple strategies simultaneously, and (iii) create genuinely novel strategies. The replicator dynamics strictly apply to fixed strategies with differential reproduction. We interpret the dynamics as describing market share evolution: the fraction of the market served by each business model type changes according to relative profitability. This interpretation is standard in evolutionary economics (Nelson & Winter, 1982) and organizational ecology (Hannan & Freeman, 1977), though it requires the assumption that more profitable business models expand (through growth or imitation) at rates proportional to their fitness advantage. This assumption is strongest in competitive markets with low switching costs and weakest in markets with significant lock-in or regulatory barriers.

2.4 The Manuscript’s Architecture: From BMPs to Metamechanisms

The original manuscript identifies 39 BMPs organized into 7 mechanisms and 3 metamechanisms. For the EGT formalization, we map these onto three strategy types corresponding to the three metamechanisms:

The structural content of each strategy must be understood precisely:

Strategy \(L\) (Leverage) is a symbiosis-seeking approach that leverages institutional complexity for anti-rival benefits. It operates through two simultaneous structural moves: (1) Detaching rival resource usage from anti-rival digital good sharing, so that rival resource consumption does not rise in proportion to anti-rival sharing; and (2) Connecting the rival and anti-rival sides of the system, so that anti-rival sharing helps the rival part optimize its resource consumption. The combination of detachment and connection constitutes symbiosis: rival and anti-rival components coexist with their institutional complexity intact, but each benefits from the other. The two metamechanisms—large-scale collaboration (minimizing individuals’ rival costs through collective action) and seeking anti-rival leverage from rival investments (extracting anti-rival value from activities that also consume rival resources)—are the institutional mechanisms through which these structural moves are realized.

Strategy \(T\) (Transform) is a synergy-seeking approach that decreases institutional complexity for anti-rival benefits. It pursues two structural goals: (1) Minimizing (but not entirely removing) the limitations that rival resources set for anti-rival sharing; and (2) Minimizing (but not necessarily optimizing) the effect of anti-rival sharing on rival resource usage. Unlike Leverage, which maintains and exploits the coupling between rival and anti-rival components, Transform restructures the offerings to reduce the bidirectional interference between them. The metamechanism—asset and offering transformation toward anti-rivalry—achieves this through patterns that simplify the rival–anti-rival interface (de-transactionalization, digitalization, parallel offerings) rather than through building complex governance structures to manage the interface.

The critical distinction is qualitative, not merely quantitative: Leverage amplifies coupling between rival and anti-rival components (symbiosis), while Transform reduces coupling between them (synergy through simplification). These are structurally different approaches to the same challenge of managing institutional complexity, not simply more or less aggressive versions of the same approach.

Note that strategy \(R\) includes the 76 compartmentalization patterns, 33 defiance patterns excluded from the manuscript’s core analysis. These represent the traditional approach of either ignoring the rival–anti-rival tension or suppressing anti-rivalry through artificial scarcity (DRM, proprietary enclosure). Including them in the evolutionary model is essential: they constitute the incumbent population against which anti-rival-friendly strategies must compete.

3. Model I: Metamechanism-Level Population Dynamics

3.1 Strategy Space and Population State

Consider a population of firms in a digital economy. Each firm adopts one of three complexity management approaches: \(L\) (Leverage, symbiosis-seeking), \(T\) (Transform, synergy-seeking), or \(R\) (Restrict, complexity-avoiding). The population state is \((x_L, x_T, x_R)\) with \(x_L + x_T + x_R = 1\), living on the 2-simplex \(\Delta^2\).

3.2 Anti-Rival Ecosystem Value

The critical structural feature of this model is that anti-rival value is endogenous to the population state. The more firms adopt sharing-oriented strategies, the greater the anti-rival value available in the ecosystem:

Definition 3.1 (Ecosystem Anti-Rival Value)

\[ V_a(x_L, x_T) = V_0 \left(1 + a_L \cdot x_L + a_T \cdot x_T\right)^{\eta} \]

where \(a_L > a_T > 0\) are strategy-specific population-level contribution weights and \(\eta > 0\) controls the curvature of returns. These weights are linked to the intrinsic anti-rivalry exponent \(\beta\) (from Section 2.1) through coupling amplification factors:

\[ a_L = \beta \cdot \lambda_L, \quad a_T = \beta \cdot \lambda_T, \qquad \lambda_L \geq 1,\; 0 < \lambda_T < \lambda_L \]

where \(\lambda_L \geq 1\): the baseline \(\lambda_L = 1\) is the conservative case where the ecosystem contribution weight equals the intrinsic anti-rivalry (no amplification beyond what the good’s anti-rivalry alone would predict); \(\lambda_L > 1\) captures additional amplification from Leverage’s symbiosis-seeking approach (connecting rival and anti-rival components for mutual optimization generates stronger ecosystem effects than the intrinsic anti-rivalry alone) and \(\lambda_T < \lambda_L\) captures the attenuated contribution from Transform’s synergy-seeking approach (minimizing bidirectional interference preserves anti-rival value but at lower intensity because simplification attenuates the network effects that full coupling would amplify). Separating \(a_L, a_T\) from \(\beta\) avoids overloading the symbol \(\beta\), which is reserved throughout for the intrinsic anti-rivalry exponent in \(V(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{\beta}\).

We also incorporate the rivalrous complement through a governance quality function. Both Leverage and Transform firms contribute to ecosystem governance, but at different intensities:

\[ h(x_L, x_T) = \frac{x_L + \omega \cdot x_T}{x_L + \omega \cdot x_T + \kappa_G}, \qquad 0 < \omega < 1 \]

where \(\kappa_G\) is the governance half-saturation constant and \(\omega\) is the Transform governance weight. Leverage firms contribute to governance at full weight (community governance, stewardship, shared ownership patterns from Mechanisms 1 and 7), while Transform firms contribute at attenuated weight \(\omega < 1\) through their own interface-simplification mechanisms. A key consequence: at \((x_L = 0,\, x_T = \varepsilon)\), \(h(0, \varepsilon) > 0\), so Transform can bootstrap governance without requiring Leverage firms to be present.

3.3 Payoff Functions

Definition 3.2 (Strategy Payoffs)

The fitness of each strategy is:

\[ \pi_L = s_L(\beta) + \varphi_L \cdot V_a(x_L, x_T) \cdot h(x_L, x_T) - c_L \] \[ \pi_T = s_T(\beta) + \varphi_T \cdot V_a(x_L, x_T) \cdot h(x_L, x_T) - c_T \] \[ \pi_R = R_0 - \delta \cdot V_a(x_L, x_T) \cdot h(x_L, x_T) \]

where the standalone efficiency terms are:

\[ s_L(\beta) = \beta \cdot \psi_L, \qquad s_T(\beta) = \beta \cdot \psi_T, \qquad \psi_L > \psi_T > 0 \]

and:

  • \(s_L(\beta), s_T(\beta)\): intrinsic efficiency gains from adopting anti-rival practices independent of ecosystem size. Even a single firm adopting Leverage or Transform practices benefits from internal efficiencies (reduced duplication, learning effects, better internal knowledge flows) proportional to the good’s intrinsic anti-rivalry \(\beta\). The ordering \(\psi_L > \psi_T\) reflects that symbiosis-seeking practices yield larger standalone efficiency gains because tight coupling captures more anti-rival value even internally.
  • \(\varphi_L > \varphi_T > 1\): differential access multipliers. Leverage firms capture disproportionate returns from anti-rival value because their symbiosis-seeking approach—connecting rival and anti-rival components—creates strategic positions (ecosystem centrality, data flows, integration advantages) that amplify value capture. Transform firms capture moderate returns because their synergy-seeking approach—minimizing bidirectional interference—yields a simpler but less strategically differentiated position.
  • \(c_L > c_T > 0\): cost ordering. Leverage strategies require higher investment because maintaining the dual structural moves (detaching rival costs from anti-rival sharing while connecting the sides for optimization) demands complex institutional infrastructure (governance mechanisms, community coordination, transparency systems). Transform strategies have lower costs because decreasing institutional complexity is inherently less infrastructure-intensive than leveraging it.
  • \(h(x_L, x_T)\): the unified governance quality function from Definition 3.1, shared by all strategies.
  • \(R_0\): base returns from traditional rival goods business models.
  • \(\delta > 0\): competitive displacement coefficient. As the anti-rival ecosystem grows, traditional business models lose market share to firms offering anti-rival value.

Remark 3.0 (Standalone Efficiency Terms)

The terms \(s_L(\beta)\) and \(s_T(\beta)\) are economically motivated by the observation that anti-rival practices generate value even without ecosystem participation. A firm that adopts open internal knowledge-sharing practices (a Leverage practice) benefits from reduced duplication, faster internal learning, and better cross-team coordination, regardless of how many other firms do likewise. Similarly, a firm that de-transactionalizes its internal processes (a Transform practice) reduces friction and gains efficiency proportional to the good’s anti-rivalry. These standalone terms are crucial for the phase transition analysis (Section 8): they ensure that the all-Restrict state becomes unstable when \(\beta\) is sufficiently large (specifically, when \(\beta > \min(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T, \beta_{\text{inv}}^L)\)), creating genuine phase transitions as anti-rivalry increases.

Remark 3.1 (Justification of the Displacement Effect)

The negative term \(-\delta \cdot V_a \cdot h(x_L, x_T)\) in \(\pi_R\) captures a key empirical regularity: when substantial anti-rival value exists in the ecosystem (e.g., high-quality open-source software), firms using restrictive strategies face competitive pressure. Customers can access anti-rival alternatives, reducing the market for proprietary offerings. This is not symmetric: \(L\)-firms do not suffer from the existence of \(R\)-firms, because restrictive strategies do not create comparable anti-rival value. The asymmetry is structural and reflects the positive-sum nature of anti-rival value creation. Note that \(\pi_R\) uses the same governance function \(h(x_L, x_T)\) as the other strategies: the displacement pressure on Restrict is proportional to the total governance quality that Leverage and Transform firms jointly create.

3.4 Replicator Dynamics

The replicator equations on the 2-simplex (using \(x_R = 1 - x_L - x_T\)) are:

\[ \dot{x}_L = x_L \left(\pi_L - \bar{\pi}\right) \] \[ \dot{x}_T = x_T \left(\pi_T - \bar{\pi}\right) \]

where the average fitness is:

\[ \bar{\pi} = x_L \pi_L + x_T \pi_T + (1 - x_L - x_T) \pi_R \]

Substituting and expanding the fitness differences:

\[ \pi_L - \pi_R = s_L(\beta) + (\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_a(x_L, x_T) \cdot h(x_L, x_T) - (c_L + R_0) \] \[ \pi_T - \pi_R = s_T(\beta) + (\varphi_T + \delta) \cdot V_a(x_L, x_T) \cdot h(x_L, x_T) - (c_T + R_0) \]

These expressions reveal two distinct fitness channels. The ecosystem channel \((\varphi + \delta) \cdot V_a \cdot h\) is state-dependent, increasing with anti-rival ecosystem value and governance quality, creating positive feedback: more sharing firms → higher \(V_a\) and \(h\) → higher relative fitness → more sharing firms. The standalone channel \(s(\beta)\) is state-independent, depending only on the intrinsic anti-rivalry parameter. This standalone channel is what makes invasion of the all-Restrict state possible when \(\beta\) is sufficiently large.

3.5 Fixed Point Analysis

Theorem 3.1 (Fixed Points of the Three-Strategy Model)

The system has the following fixed points:

  1. All-Restrict vertex \((0, 0)\): Is always an equilibrium of the replicator dynamics. Payoffs reduce to \(\pi_L = s_L(\beta) - c_L\), \(\pi_T = s_T(\beta) - c_T\), \(\pi_R = R_0\).
  2. All-Leverage vertex \((1, 0)\): Is an equilibrium vertex. Fitness is \(\pi_L = s_L(\beta) + \varphi_L \cdot V_0(1+a_L)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{1}{1+\kappa_G} - c_L\).
  3. All-Transform vertex \((0, 1)\): Is an equilibrium vertex. Fitness is \(\pi_T = s_T(\beta) + \varphi_T \cdot V_0(1+a_T)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{\omega}{\omega + \kappa_G} - c_T\).
  4. Interior fixed point \((x_L^*, x_T^*)\): Exists when \(\pi_L = \pi_T = \pi_R\), requiring specific parameter configurations.
  5. Edge fixed points: On the \(L\)–\(R\), \(T\)–\(R\), and \(L\)–\(T\) edges, defined by pairwise fitness equalities.

The stability of each vertex depends on whether the other strategies can invade:

Proposition 3.1 (Invasion Conditions)

(a) Leverage invades Restrict: At the all-\(R\) vertex \((0,0)\), the invasion fitness of \(L\) is:

\[ \pi_L\big|_{(0,0)} - \pi_R\big|_{(0,0)} = s_L(\beta) - c_L - R_0 = \beta \psi_L - c_L - R_0 \]

This has two components. The ecosystem channel vanishes because \(h(0,0) = 0\), but the standalone channel does not. Leverage invades when:

\[ \beta > \beta_{\text{inv}}^L \;=\; \frac{c_L + R_0}{\psi_L} \]

Below this threshold, the all-Restrict population resists invasion.

(b) Transform invades Restrict: By the same logic, Transform invades when:

\[ \beta > \beta_{\text{inv}}^T \;=\; \frac{c_T + R_0}{\psi_T} \]

Since \(c_T < c_L\) and \(\psi_T < \psi_L\), the ordering of these thresholds depends on parameter values.

(c) Restrict invades Leverage: Strategy \(R\) can invade an all-\(L\) population when:

\[ R_0 > s_L(\beta) + \varphi_L \cdot V_0(1+a_L)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{1}{1+\kappa_G} - c_L \]

This fails (Restrict cannot invade) when the anti-rival ecosystem is sufficiently valuable.

Critical Observation 3.1

The stability of the all-Restrict vertex \((0,0)\) now depends on the anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\). For low \(\beta\) (below \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^L\) and \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T\)), the all-Restrict state is locally stable: anti-rival strategies cannot invade because their standalone efficiency gains are insufficient to offset their costs. However, for high \(\beta\) (above both thresholds), the all-Restrict state is unstable: both Leverage and Transform can invade through the standalone channel alone, without requiring any ecosystem coordination.

The system exhibits genuine phase transitions as \(\beta\) increases: at low \(\beta\) (Phase I) the system is trapped in all-Restrict; at intermediate \(\beta\) (Phase II) Transform can invade (all-Restrict becomes a saddle), while the \(L\)–\(R\) edge may retain a tipping point requiring coordinated entry for Leverage; at high \(\beta\) (Phase III) the all-Restrict state is unstable in both directions, and the system transitions toward anti-rival strategies without requiring coordinated entry.

The practical implication is that at intermediate \(\beta\) values—where most contemporary digital economies operate—ecosystem-level coordination remains important for Leverage adoption even though Transform can invade independently. The conditional stability of all-Restrict creates the possibility of natural transitions as technology increases \(\beta\).

3.6 Jacobian-Based Stability Analysis

We provide explicit Jacobian computations at each vertex of the simplex. The replicator dynamics on the 2-simplex, projected onto coordinates \((x_L, x_T)\) with \(x_R = 1 - x_L - x_T\), have the Jacobian \(J = \partial(\dot{x}_L, \dot{x}_T)/\partial(x_L, x_T)\). At each vertex, the invasion eigenvalues of the tangent-space linearization equal the invasion fitness of each absent strategy (Hofbauer & Sigmund, 1998, Ch. 7, Theorem 7.2.1).

Proposition 3.2 (Vertex Stability via Jacobian)

At \((0, 0)\) — All-Restrict:

\[ J(0,0) = \operatorname{diag}\!\left(\pi_L - \pi_R,\;\pi_T - \pi_R\right)\bigg|_{(0,0)} = \operatorname{diag}\!\left(\beta\psi_L - c_L - R_0,\;\beta\psi_T - c_T - R_0\right) \]

Stable when both eigenvalues are negative: \(\beta < \min\!\left(\frac{c_L + R_0}{\psi_L},\;\frac{c_T + R_0}{\psi_T}\right)\). The phase boundaries are:

  • \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T = (c_T + R_0)/\psi_T\): Transform invasion threshold
  • \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^L = (c_L + R_0)/\psi_L\): Leverage invasion threshold

At \((1, 0)\) — All-Leverage:

\[ J(1,0) = \operatorname{diag}\!\left(\pi_R - \pi_L,\;\pi_T - \pi_L\right)\bigg|_{(1,0)} \]

The first eigenvalue (Restrict invasion) is \(\lambda_1 = R_0 - s_L(\beta) - \varphi_L V_0(1+a_L)^\eta \frac{1}{1+\kappa_G} + c_L\). The second eigenvalue (Transform invasion) is \(\lambda_2 = s_T - s_L + (\varphi_T - \varphi_L) V_0(1+a_L)^\eta \frac{1}{1+\kappa_G} - c_T + c_L\). All-Leverage is stable when both eigenvalues are negative.

At \((0, 1)\) — All-Transform:

\[ J(0,1) = \operatorname{diag}\!\left(\pi_L - \pi_T,\;\pi_R - \pi_T\right)\bigg|_{(0,1)} \]

The first eigenvalue (Leverage invasion) is \(\lambda_1 = s_L - s_T + (\varphi_L - \varphi_T) V_0(1+a_T)^\eta \frac{\omega}{\omega+\kappa_G} - c_L + c_T\). The second eigenvalue (Restrict invasion) is \(\lambda_2 = R_0 - s_T(\beta) - \varphi_T V_0(1+a_T)^\eta \frac{\omega}{\omega+\kappa_G} + c_T\). All-Transform is stable when both are negative.

On each edge: The transverse eigenvalue determines whether the edge is attracting or repelling for the absent strategy. On the \(L\)–\(R\) edge (\(x_T = 0\)), the transverse eigenvalue for Transform at each point \((x, 0, 1-x)\) is \(\pi_T(x, 0) - \bar{\pi}(x, 0)\). If this is negative everywhere on the edge, Transform cannot invade any \(L\)–\(R\) mixture and the edge dynamics fully determine the long-run outcome.

Remark 3.2 (Simplex Projection)

The Jacobian computations above use the standard simplex projection. On the boundary of \(\Delta^2\), the replicator dynamics reduce to lower-dimensional replicator systems. The vertex eigenvalues coincide with invasion fitnesses, which is a classical result (Hofbauer & Sigmund, 1998, Ch. 7, Theorem 7.2.1). The diagonal structure at vertices follows from the factored form \(\dot{x}_i = x_i(\pi_i - \bar{\pi})\): at a vertex where \(x_i = 0\), \(\partial \dot{x}_i / \partial x_j = 0\) for \(j \neq i\).

3.7 Stability on the Leverage–Restrict Edge

The most analytically tractable reduction is the two-strategy game between \(L\) and \(R\) (setting \(x_T = 0\)). Let \(x = x_L\), so \(x_R = 1 - x\). The replicator equation becomes:

\[ \dot{x} = x(1-x) \left[\pi_L(x) - \pi_R(x)\right] \]

where:

\[ \pi_L(x) - \pi_R(x) = s_L(\beta) + (\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_0(1 + a_L x)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{x}{x + \kappa_G} - (c_L + R_0) \]

Define \(F(x) = (\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_0(1 + a_L x)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{x}{x + \kappa_G}\) and let \(\tilde{c} = c_L + R_0 - s_L(\beta)\) be the effective threshold, reduced by the standalone efficiency gain. Note \(F(0) = 0\) and \(F\) is strictly increasing for \(x > 0\). The tipping point condition is \(F(x^*) = \tilde{c}\). Therefore:

Theorem 3.2 (Bistability on the \(L\)–\(R\) Edge)

On the \(L\)–\(R\) edge, let \(\tilde{c} = c_L + R_0 - s_L(\beta)\). The dynamics exhibit one of three regimes:

  1. If \(\tilde{c} < 0\) (equivalently, \(\beta > (c_L + R_0)/\psi_L\)): The standalone efficiency strictly exceeds the cost-plus-displacement threshold. \(\pi_L > \pi_R\) for all \(x \in [0,1]\), \(x = 1\) is globally asymptotically stable, and \(x = 0\) is unstable.
  2. If \(\tilde{c} = 0\) (equivalently, \(\beta = (c_L + R_0)/\psi_L\)): \(\pi_L - \pi_R = F(x) \geq 0\) with equality only at \(x = 0\). \(x = 1\) attracts all initial conditions in \((0,1]\). \(x = 0\) is a non-hyperbolic equilibrium (invasion fitness zero).
  3. If \(\tilde{c} > 0\) and \(F(1) < \tilde{c}\): The ecosystem channel is too weak even at full adoption. The all-Restrict state \(x = 0\) is globally stable.
  4. If \(\tilde{c} > 0\) and \(F(1) > \tilde{c}\): By the intermediate value theorem, there exists a unique \(x^* \in (0,1)\) such that \(F(x^*) = \tilde{c}\). The dynamics are bistable: \(x = 0\) is stable for \(x < x^*\), \(x = 1\) is stable for \(x > x^*\), and \(x^*\) is an unstable tipping point.

Proof

Regime 1a (\(\tilde{c} < 0\)): \(\pi_L - \pi_R = F(x) - \tilde{c} > 0\) for all \(x \in [0,1]\) (since \(F(x) \geq 0\) and \(-\tilde{c} > 0\)). Hence \(\dot{x} > 0\) for all \(x \in (0,1)\), so \(x = 1\) is globally asymptotically stable and \(x = 0\) is unstable.

Regime 1b (\(\tilde{c} = 0\)): \(\pi_L - \pi_R = F(x) \geq 0\) for all \(x\), with \(F(0) = 0\) and \(F(x) > 0\) for \(x > 0\). Hence \(\dot{x} > 0\) for \(x \in (0,1)\), so \(x = 1\) attracts all trajectories from \((0,1]\). At \(x = 0\), the invasion fitness is zero and the linearised eigenvalue vanishes, so \(x = 0\) is a non-hyperbolic equilibrium.

Regimes 2–3: When \(\tilde{c} > 0\), \(F(x)\) is continuous on \([0,1]\) with \(F(0) = 0\) and strictly increasing. If \(F(1) > \tilde{c}\), there exists exactly one crossing point \(x^*\) where \(F(x^*) = \tilde{c}\). For \(x < x^*\), \(F(x) < \tilde{c}\), so \(\pi_L - \pi_R < 0\) and \(\dot{x} < 0\). For \(x > x^*\), \(\pi_L - \pi_R > 0\) and \(\dot{x} > 0\).

■

The tipping point \(x^*\) (when it exists) satisfies:

\[ (\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_0 (1 + a_L x^*)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{x^*}{x^* + \kappa_G} = c_L + R_0 - \beta \psi_L \]

This equation does not admit a closed-form solution for general \(\eta\), but its qualitative behavior is clear: \(x^*\) decreases as \(a_L\) increases (stronger contribution weight lowers the tipping point), as \(\beta\) increases (higher standalone efficiency reduces the effective threshold \(\tilde{c}\)), as \(V_0\) increases (higher base value), and as \(R_0\) decreases (weaker traditional alternatives). Conversely, \(x^*\) increases with \(c_L\) (higher leveraging costs) and \(\kappa_G\) (more demanding governance requirements). When \(\beta\) crosses \((c_L + R_0)/\psi_L\), the tipping point vanishes entirely—this is the Phase III transition.

4. Model II: The Community Governance Game

Mechanism 1 (“Using Communities to Minimize Rival Costs and Maximize Anti-Rival Value”) encompasses 15 of the 39 BMPs—the largest single mechanism. Its patterns span from crowdsourcing and open source to community governance and shared ownership. We formalize this mechanism by extending the three-strategy governance model from Module 10 to the business model context.

4.1 Mapping BMPs to Contribution Types

The 15 patterns in Mechanism 1 can be partitioned into content-oriented and governance-oriented contributions:

4.2 The Three-Strategy Model Within Community Mechanisms

Within a community-based business ecosystem (e.g., an open-source project, a crowdsourced platform), individual participants choose among three strategies:

Definition 4.1 (Community Strategies)

  • Content Contributor (C): Creates anti-rival value (code, data, reviews). Pays cost \(c_C\).
  • Governance Contributor (G): Maintains the rivalrous complement (moderation, infrastructure, conflict resolution). Pays cost \(c_G > c_C\). Receives governance premium \(\sigma\).
  • Free-Rider (F): Consumes anti-rival value without contributing.

With differential access \(\varphi > 1\) for contributors (capturing reputational returns, learning-by-doing, community recognition effects embedded in patterns like “Review system” and “Pay what you want”):

\[ \pi_C = \varphi \cdot V(x,y) - c_C, \qquad \pi_G = \varphi \cdot V(x,y) - c_G + \sigma, \qquad \pi_F = V(x,y) \]

where \(V(x,y) = V_0 \cdot (1 + \beta x)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{y}{y + \kappa}\).

4.3 The Governance Trap in Business Model Communities

Theorem 4.1 (Business Model Governance Trap)

A sufficient condition for a community-based business model operating under Mechanism 1 to enter a governance trap is \(\sigma < c_G - c_C\). When this condition holds:

  1. The governance-to-content ratio \(y(t)/x(t)\) is monotonically decreasing: \(\frac{d}{dt}\ln(y/x) = \sigma - (c_G - c_C) < 0\).
  2. The absolute governance share \(y(t)\) may temporarily increase (when \(\pi_G > \bar{\pi}\)) before eventually declining as the ratio \(y/x \to 0\) pulls \(y\) down.
  3. Eventually \(y\) becomes small enough that \(h(y) \approx 0\), causing \(V(x,y) \to 0\).
  4. When \(V \to 0\), content contribution is also dominated: \(\pi_C - \pi_F = (\varphi - 1) \cdot 0 - c_C < 0\).
  5. The dynamics are driven toward collapse near the all-free-rider state: as governance quality deteriorates and \(V(x,y)\) falls below the contribution threshold, the all-free-rider vertex \((0,0)\) becomes attracting under the present payoff structure.

Proof

The fitness difference between governance and content contribution is:

\[ \pi_G - \pi_C = \sigma - (c_G - c_C) \]

When \(\sigma < c_G - c_C\), this difference is negative and constant—independent of the population state. In any population containing both \(G\) and \(C\) types, the ratio \(y/x\) satisfies:

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\ln\!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right) = \pi_G - \pi_C = \sigma - (c_G - c_C) < 0 \]

so \(y/x\) decreases exponentially at rate \(|c_G - c_C - \sigma|\). This does not immediately imply that \(y\) itself is monotonically decreasing. In the replicator dynamics, \(\dot{y} = y(\pi_G - \bar{\pi})\), and \(\pi_G > \bar{\pi}\) is possible when the free-rider fraction is large and governance contributions are generating substantial community value. Thus \(y(t)\) may have a non-monotonic path, temporarily increasing before the declining ratio eventually forces \(y\) down.

The collapse sequence is: (1) \(y/x\) declines exponentially; (2) eventually governance becomes sufficiently scarce that \(h(y) = y/(y+\kappa)\) becomes small; (3) \(V(x,y)\) falls; (4) once \(V(x,y) < c_C/(\varphi - 1)\), content contribution is no longer payoff-superior to free-riding; and (5) the dynamics move into the collapse region near \((0,0)\), where the all-free-rider vertex becomes attracting under the present payoff structure.

■

Remark 4.0b (Sufficiency of the Governance Trap Condition)

When \(\sigma \geq c_G - c_C\), the governance-to-content ratio \(y/x\) is non-decreasing, which is necessary for sustained governance. However, full avoidance of community collapse additionally requires the minimum community value condition (Corollary 4.1): even with adequate governance premium, the community could still fail through the value channel if \(V(x,y) < c_C/(\varphi - 1)\). The condition \(\sigma < c_G - c_C\) is therefore sufficient for collapse but not necessary in the strictest sense.

Remark 4.0 (The Knife-Edge Case)

When \(\sigma = c_G - c_C\) exactly, governance and content contribution are payoff-equivalent at every state: \(\pi_G = \pi_C\) everywhere. The ratio \(y/x\) is constant along trajectories. The remaining dynamics are determined by the \(C\)–\(F\) fitness difference \(\pi_C - \pi_F = (\varphi - 1)V(x,y) - c_C\), which defines a curve (not a point) of equilibria in the simplex interior. This knife-edge condition is non-generic and structurally unstable: any perturbation of \(\sigma\) relative to \(c_G - c_C\) breaks the degeneracy and drives the system to either the governance trap (\(\sigma < c_G - c_C\)) or sustained governance (\(\sigma > c_G - c_C\)).

4.4 Connecting BMPs to the Governance Premium

The governance trap condition \(\sigma < c_G - c_C\) provides a lens through which to evaluate the governance-oriented BMPs in Mechanism 1. Each pattern can be understood as a mechanism for increasing \(\sigma\):

For governance contribution not to be systematically dominated by content contribution, the aggregate governance premium must satisfy:

\[ \sigma_{\text{total}} = \sigma_{\text{power}} + \sigma_{\text{rep}} + \sigma_{\text{fin}} + \sigma_{\text{strat}} + \sigma_{\text{social}} \geq c_G - c_C \]

The practical implication is that Mechanism 1 requires stacking multiple governance-oriented BMPs to achieve the minimum viable governance premium. A community relying on social recognition alone (\(\sigma_{\text{social}}\)) will not sustain governance contribution if \(\sigma_{\text{social}} < c_G - c_C\); it must combine recognition with revenue sharing, decision-making power, or reputational returns. Critical Assessment 4.1

This formalization assumes that different types of governance premiums are additively separable. In practice, they may be complementary (revenue sharing becomes more attractive when combined with decision-making power) or substitutive (financial premiums may crowd out intrinsic motivation). The additive model is a first approximation that should be tested empirically. Furthermore, the costs \(c_G\) and \(c_C\) are treated as homogeneous across participants, when in reality they vary substantially with individual capabilities, opportunity costs, and organizational affiliation.

4.5 Interior Equilibrium Analysis

When \(\sigma = c_G - c_C\) exactly, a stationary set exists: the condition \(\pi_G = \pi_C\) holds everywhere (Remark 4.0), and the locus \(\pi_C = \pi_F\) defines a curve of equilibria in the simplex interior. Along this curve, from \(\pi_C = \pi_F\):

\[ (\varphi - 1) \cdot V(x^*, y^*) = c_C \] \[ \Longrightarrow V(x^*, y^*) = \frac{c_C}{\varphi - 1} \]

This defines an iso-value curve in the \((x, y)\) space along which the interior equilibrium must lie. The equation \(V_0(1 + \beta x)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{y}{y+\kappa} = \frac{c_C}{\varphi - 1}\) implicitly defines a relationship between \(x^*\) and \(y^*\). For the interior equilibrium to be meaningful, we additionally need the constraint \(x^* + y^* < 1\) (positive free-rider fraction) and the iso-value curve must intersect the simplex interior.

Corollary 4.1 (Minimum Community Value)

A necessary condition for sustained content contribution is \(V(x,y) > \frac{c_C}{\varphi - 1}\). Substituting the coupled value function:

\[ V_0 (1 + \beta x)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{y}{y + \kappa} > \frac{c_C}{\varphi - 1} \]

This establishes a joint threshold on content activity \(x\), governance quality \(y\), base value \(V_0\), and anti-rivalry strength \(\beta\). Higher anti-rivalry (\(\beta\)) lowers the threshold, making community-based business models viable at smaller scales. This explains why strongly anti-rival goods (software, data) more readily support open-source business models than weakly anti-rival goods.

5. Model III: The Subsidization–Transparency Game

Mechanism 2 (“Subsidizing Sharing-Related Rival Costs to Allow Anti-Rival Sharing”) encompasses 8 BMPs including “Hidden revenue,” “Free,” “Sponsorship,” and three social mission patterns. This mechanism creates an asymmetric game between platforms and users.

Remark 5.0 (Structure of this Section)

This section has two parts that serve different analytical roles. Section 5.1 formulates a single-player optimisation problem: a platform choosing its transparency level to maximise payoff, given user response. This optimisation motivates the strategy space and payoff structure. Section 5.2 then embeds this into a population game: a population of platforms choosing between high-transparency and opaque strategies, with evolutionary dynamics determining the equilibrium transparency level. We retain “game” in the section title because the population-level dynamics are the substantive contribution.

5.1 The Platform–User Asymmetric Game

Definition 5.1 (Subsidization Game)

A platform (\(P\)) subsidizes anti-rival sharing and extracts information rent. Users (\(U\)) choose their level of engagement. The platform chooses a transparency level \(\tau \in [0, 1]\).

  • \(\tau = 1\): Full transparency about data extraction and monetization.
  • \(\tau = 0\): Complete opacity (hidden revenue).

The payoffs are:

\[ \pi_P(\tau) = (1 - \tau) \cdot I(n) + \tau \cdot T(n) - c_{\text{sub}} \cdot n \]

where \(I(n)\) is information rent (decreasing in \(\tau\) because transparency reduces extractable surplus), \(T(n)\) is trust-based revenue (increasing in \(\tau\)), and \(c_{\text{sub}}\) is the per-user subsidy cost.

User participation depends on perceived fairness:

\[ n(\tau) = n_0 \cdot \left[\alpha + (1 - \alpha) \cdot \tau^{\psi}\right] \]

where \(\alpha \in (0,1)\) is the baseline participation rate (users who join regardless of transparency) and \(\psi > 0\) controls transparency sensitivity. The anti-rival value is:

\[ V_a(n(\tau)) = V_0 \cdot n(\tau)^{\beta} \]

The platform faces a tradeoff: higher transparency increases participation \(n\) (boosting anti-rival value) but reduces information rent per user. The manuscript’s transparency challenge is thus formalized as an optimization problem:

\[ \tau^* = \arg\max_{\tau \in [0,1]} \; \pi_P(\tau) = \arg\max_{\tau \in [0,1]} \; \left[(1-\tau) \cdot I(n(\tau)) + \tau \cdot T(n(\tau)) - c_{\text{sub}} \cdot n(\tau)\right] \]

5.2 Evolutionary Dynamics of Transparency

In a population of platforms choosing different transparency levels, we discretize into two strategies: \(H\) (High transparency, \(\tau_H\)) and \(O\) (Opaque, \(\tau_O\)). Let \(p\) be the fraction of high-transparency platforms.

\[ \pi_H = \tau_H \cdot T(n_H) + (1-\tau_H) \cdot I(n_H) - c_{\text{sub}} \cdot n_H \] \[ \pi_O = \tau_O \cdot T(n_O) + (1-\tau_O) \cdot I(n_O) - c_{\text{sub}} \cdot n_O \]

where \(n_H > n_O\) (transparent platforms attract more users). The key interaction effect is that as more platforms become transparent (\(p\) increases), users develop higher expectations, so the participation penalty for opaque platforms increases:

\[ n_O(p) = n_0 \cdot \alpha \cdot (1 - \lambda p), \qquad \lambda \in (0, 1) \]

We treat \(n_H\) as approximately constant: \(n_H \approx n_0[\alpha + (1-\alpha)\tau_H^{\psi}]\), reflecting that the normative shift toward transparency primarily penalises opaque platforms rather than further rewarding transparent ones.

This creates negative frequency dependence for opaque strategies when transparency becomes the norm: opaque platforms lose users as the market becomes more transparency-conscious.

The replicator equation for the high-transparency fraction is:

\[ \dot{p} = p(1-p)\left[\pi_H(p) - \pi_O(p)\right] \]

The tipping point \(p^*\) satisfies \(\pi_H(p^*) = \pi_O(p^*)\), i.e., the transparency level at which both strategies earn equal fitness. The stability of \(p^*\) as a separating threshold requires:

\[ \frac{d}{dp}\!\left[\pi_H(p) - \pi_O(p)\right]\bigg|_{p = p^*} > 0 \]

When this condition holds, \(p^*\) is an unstable equilibrium of the replicator dynamics: for \(p < p^*\), opacity dominates (\(\dot{p} < 0\)); for \(p > p^*\), transparency dominates (\(\dot{p} > 0\)). The negative frequency dependence on opaque strategies through \(n_O(p)\) is the mechanism that generates this tipping-point structure.

Remark 5.1 (Connection to the Original Manuscript)

The manuscript identifies the “transparency challenge” as the central tension in Metamechanism 2: how to maintain fair and transparent business practices without jeopardizing the leverage mechanics. Our model shows that this is not merely an ethical concern but a strategic one: the optimal transparency level is endogenous to the market composition, and there exist parameter regimes where full transparency is evolutionarily stable (when trust-based revenue sufficiently exceeds information rent) and regimes where opacity persists (when information rent extraction is sufficiently profitable relative to trust effects).

6. Model IV: De-Transactionalization and Anti-Rivalry Amplification

Mechanism 5 (“Enabling Anti-Rivalry through De-Transactionalization”) contains 4 BMPs: flat rate, subscription, membership, and license. These patterns exemplify Transform’s first structural goal—minimizing the limitations that rival resources set for anti-rival sharing. They achieve this by decoupling individual consumption events from payment, thereby removing per-use friction (a rival-resource limitation) that inhibits anti-rival sharing. Simultaneously, they address Transform’s second structural goal—minimizing the effect of anti-rival sharing on rival resource usage—by converting variable costs into fixed fees, so that increased sharing does not proportionally increase the rival resource (payment) burden on users.

6.1 Transactional vs. De-Transactional Value Capture

Definition 6.1 (Demand-Friction Model of De-Transactionalization)

The intrinsic anti-rivalry exponent \(\beta_0\) is a property of the good and is not altered by pricing. Instead, per-use pricing affects the realized network size by creating demand friction:

\[ n(p) = \frac{n_0}{1 + \eta \cdot p}, \qquad \eta > 0 \]

where \(n_0\) is the potential user base (at zero per-use price) and \(\eta\) is the price sensitivity parameter. The per-user value under each model is:

\[ V_{\text{trans}}(n(p)) = V_0 \cdot n(p)^{\beta_0} = V_0 \cdot \left(\frac{n_0}{1+\eta p}\right)^{\!\beta_0} \] \[ V_{\text{sub}} = V_0 \cdot n_0^{\beta_0} \qquad \text{(subscription: } p = 0\text{)} \]

The value ratio between subscription and transactional models is:

\[ \frac{V_{\text{sub}}}{V_{\text{trans}}} = (1 + \eta \cdot p)^{\beta_0} \]

This formulation preserves the key insight—de-transactionalization unlocks anti-rival value—but grounds it in the economically transparent mechanism of demand friction rather than an ad hoc shift in the anti-rivalry exponent. The exponent \(\beta_0\) remains fixed because anti-rivalry is a property of the good (shared playlists, collaborative recommendations), not of the pricing model. What pricing changes is the number of users participating, and since anti-rival value scales as \(n^{\beta_0}\), fewer participants means less value realized.

6.2 Critical Subscription Mass

A subscription-based firm charges a flat fee \(f\) and serves \(n\) subscribers. Its revenue is \(n \cdot f\) while its cost is \(C_0 + c_m \cdot n^{\gamma}\) (fixed costs plus marginal costs that scale superlinearly due to infrastructure demands). The firm is viable when:

\[ n \cdot f > C_0 + c_m \cdot n^{\gamma} \]

For \(\gamma > 1\) (realistic for digital platforms with infrastructure costs), this may define a viability band \(n_{\min} < n < n_{\max}\) rather than a simple minimum. The lower bound \(n_{\min}\) is the critical subscription mass; below it, revenue fails to cover costs. The upper bound \(n_{\max}\) reflects infrastructure limits.

Proposition 6.1 (Demand-Friction Threshold Effect)

In the two-strategy game between Transactional and De-Transactional (subscription) business models:

  1. The price-friction product \(\eta \cdot p\) acts as the control parameter. The value ratio \((1+\eta p)^{\beta_0}\) determines the competitive advantage of de-transactionalization.
  2. A viability band for the subscription model exists if and only if \(\max_{n>0}(f \cdot n - C_0 - c_m \cdot n^{\gamma}) > 0\). When this condition fails, subscription is not viable at any scale.
  3. The competitive advantage of de-transactionalization scales with both \(\eta \cdot p\) (how much friction pricing creates) and \(\beta_0\) (how sensitive the good is to network size). For goods with high intrinsic anti-rivalry (\(\beta_0\) large) facing significant price friction (\(\eta p\) large), de-transactionalization is transformative. For goods with low \(\beta_0\) or low \(\eta p\), the advantage is marginal.

This section does not yet specify a full population-dynamic game in the replicator sense; rather, it formalizes the payoff advantage and viability thresholds that would parameterize a two-strategy evolutionary comparison between transactional and de-transactionalized business models.

7. The Incompatibility Theorem

The original manuscript contains a crucial design insight, stated qualitatively: business model patterns from different complexity management approaches (leveraging vs. decreasing) “should be combined into a single business model only with caution” because patterns seeking complexity leverage combined with those seeking complexity decrease can result in a system that “loses the ability to seek complexity leverage.” We now provide a formal statement and proof.

7.1 The Interface Decoupling Effect

Transform strategies (Metamechanism 3) manage the rival–anti-rival interface by reducing the coupling between rival and anti-rival components. De-transactionalization decouples payment from consumption; digitalization decouples physical constraints from digital sharing; parallel offerings decouple premium rival services from the anti-rival base. This decoupling achieves Transform’s dual goals—minimizing rival limitations on anti-rival sharing and minimizing anti-rival effects on rival resource usage—but it also attenuates the network amplification that full coupling would enable. By reducing the interface between rival and anti-rival sides, Transform strategies limit the channels through which anti-rival sharing can generate superlinear returns.

Definition 7.1 (Shared Interface Parameter)

Consider a product line that combines Leverage and Transform business model patterns sharing a single rival–anti-rival interface. The interface is characterised by a coupling parameter \(\theta \in [0, 1]\):

  • \(\theta = 1\): tight coupling (benefits Leverage — connecting rival and anti-rival components for mutual optimization)
  • \(\theta = 0\): loose coupling (benefits Transform — minimizing bidirectional interference)

The effective anti-rivalry experienced by each component depends on \(\theta\):

\[ \text{L component: } \beta_0 \cdot \theta, \qquad \text{T component: } \beta_0 \cdot (1 - \theta) \]

This captures the structural tension: tight coupling amplifies the network effects that Leverage depends on but increases the bidirectional interference that Transform seeks to minimize, and vice versa. Within a single product line sharing one interface, the firm must choose one \(\theta\).

The crucial insight is that a shared interface forces the Leverage and Transform components to accept the same coupling level. This is not an assumed incompatibility but a structural constraint: the degree of coupling at an interface is a single parameter, not independently adjustable for different components sharing that interface.

7.2 Formal Statement

Theorem 7.1 (Incompatibility of Leveraging and Decreasing Patterns)

Consider a firm operating a mixed business model with fraction \(w\) of Leverage patterns and fraction \((1-w)\) of Transform patterns, sharing a single interface with coupling parameter \(\theta\). Under the shared-interface coupling-allocation model, the effective anti-rivalry experienced by each component is \(\beta_L^{\text{eff}} = \beta_0\theta\) and \(\beta_T^{\text{eff}} = \beta_0(1-\theta)\). The mixed payoff is:

\[ \pi_{\text{mix}}(w, \theta) = w \cdot \varphi_L \cdot V_0 \cdot n^{\beta_0 \theta} \cdot h(y) + (1-w) \cdot \varphi_T \cdot V_0 \cdot n^{\beta_0(1-\theta)} \cdot h(y) - w \cdot c_L - (1-w) \cdot c_T \]

The firm chooses \(\theta\) to maximise \(\pi_{\text{mix}}\) given its strategy mix \(w\). The key result is that the payoff is convex in \(\theta\):

\[ \frac{\partial^2 \pi_{\text{mix}}}{\partial \theta^2} = V_0 \cdot (\beta_0 \ln n)^2 \cdot h(y) \cdot \left[w \cdot \varphi_L \cdot n^{\beta_0 \theta} + (1-w) \cdot \varphi_T \cdot n^{\beta_0(1-\theta)}\right] > 0 \]

for \(n > 1\), \(\beta_0 > 0\), and \(w \in (0,1)\). Therefore:

  1. Any interior critical point \(\theta^*\) satisfying the first-order condition is a minimum, not a maximum.
  2. The firm maximises payoff at the boundary: \(\theta = 0\) or \(\theta = 1\).
  3. At \(\theta = 1\): the T-component receives \(n^0 = 1\) (dead weight—no network benefit).
  4. At \(\theta = 0\): the L-component receives \(n^0 = 1\) (dead weight—no coupling benefit).
  5. Pure strategies (\(w = 0\) or \(w = 1\)) avoid this waste: a pure-L firm chooses \(\theta = 1\) naturally, and a pure-T firm chooses \(\theta = 0\).

The incompatibility is derived within the shared-interface coupling-allocation model from three structural features: (i) the shared interface constraint (single \(\theta\) per product line), (ii) the convexity of the payoff in \(\theta\) (opposing effects on L and T components), and (iii) no scale threshold is required—the result holds for all \(n > 1\).

Proof

The first-order condition \(\partial\pi_{\text{mix}}/\partial\theta = 0\) gives:

\[ w \cdot \varphi_L \cdot n^{\beta_0 \theta} \cdot \beta_0 \ln n = (1-w) \cdot \varphi_T \cdot n^{\beta_0(1-\theta)} \cdot \beta_0 \ln n \]

which simplifies to \(w \varphi_L \cdot n^{\beta_0 \theta} = (1-w) \varphi_T \cdot n^{\beta_0(1-\theta)}\). This determines an interior \(\theta^*\) for each \(w \in (0,1)\). But since:

\[ \frac{\partial^2 \pi_{\text{mix}}}{\partial \theta^2} = V_0 (\beta_0 \ln n)^2 h(y) \left[w \varphi_L n^{\beta_0\theta} + (1-w)\varphi_T n^{\beta_0(1-\theta)}\right] \]

is strictly positive for \(n > 1\) and \(w \in (0,1)\), the interior critical point is a minimum. On the compact domain \(\theta \in [0,1]\), the maximum must occur at the boundary. Evaluating:

  • At \(\theta = 1\): \(\pi_{\text{mix}} = w \varphi_L V_0 n^{\beta_0} h(y) + (1-w)\varphi_T V_0 \cdot 1 \cdot h(y) - wc_L - (1-w)c_T\)
  • At \(\theta = 0\): \(\pi_{\text{mix}} = w \varphi_L V_0 \cdot 1 \cdot h(y) + (1-w)\varphi_T V_0 n^{\beta_0} h(y) - wc_L - (1-w)c_T\)

In both cases, one component receives \(n^{\beta_0}\) (full network benefit) while the other receives \(n^0 = 1\) (no benefit). The dead-weight component generates revenue \(\varphi V_0 h(y)\) at best, while costing \(c\). A pure strategy eliminates this waste entirely.

Joint optimisation over \((\theta, w)\). After optimising \(\theta\), the firm’s payoff as a function of \(w\) alone is \(\pi^*(w) = \max\{\pi_{\text{mix}}(w, 0),\; \pi_{\text{mix}}(w, 1)\}\). Since \(\pi_{\text{mix}}(w, 0)\) and \(\pi_{\text{mix}}(w, 1)\) are both affine in \(w\) (each is of the form \(Aw + B(1-w)\)), their pointwise maximum is convex in \(w\). A convex function on the compact interval \([0,1]\) attains its maximum at a boundary point. Therefore the joint optimum over \((\theta, w)\) is attained at \((w, \theta) \in \{(0, 0),\; (1, 1)\}\)—i.e., at a pure strategy.

■

Proposition 7.1 (Static Dominance of Pure Strategies)

We treat each \(w\)-type as a reduced-form strategy whose interface parameter \(\theta\) is chosen optimally within type. In a population game with a finite set of strategies indexed by \(w \in \{0, w_1, w_2, \ldots, 1\}\), any interior strategy (\(w_i \in (0,1)\)) is weakly dominated by one of the pure strategies (\(w = 0\) or \(w = 1\)) for all \(n > 1\). Under the generic condition \(\pi^*(0) \neq \pi^*(1)\), the dominance is strict. This follows from the joint optimisation argument: \(\pi^*(w)\) is convex in \(w\) and therefore maximised at a boundary point. The convexity gives \(\geq\) (weak dominance); the generic nondegeneracy condition \(\pi^*(0) \neq \pi^*(1)\) upgrades this to strict inequality for all interior \(w\).

Corollary 7.1 (Evolutionary Implication)

Under the replicator dynamics with a finite strategy set \(\{0, w_1, \ldots, 1\}\), any strictly dominated strategy is driven to extinction (Hofbauer & Sigmund, 1998, Theorem 7.2.3). Under the generic condition of Proposition 7.1, the dominance is strict, so interior strategies \(w_i \in (0,1)\) vanish asymptotically.

Technical remark: Extension to a continuous strategy space \(w \in [0,1]\) requires additional structure. The strategy space is compact and payoffs are continuous in \(w\), so the static dominance result carries over by continuity: for every interior \(w\), there exists a pure strategy with higher payoff (strictly so under the generic condition of Proposition 7.1). However, the evolutionary dynamics on continuous strategy spaces (adaptive dynamics or measure-valued replicators) require separate treatment and are beyond the scope of this paper.

Critical Assessment 7.1

The theorem’s key structural assumption is the shared interface constraint: L and T components must use the same coupling parameter \(\theta\). This is defensible when both pattern types target the same rival–anti-rival interface within a single product line. It does not apply when Leverage and Transform patterns target different interfaces—for example, when a firm tightly couples community development with code quality (L patterns, one interface) while simultaneously offering simplified managed hosting (T patterns, a separate interface). In such cases, each interface has its own \(\theta\), and the convexity argument does not produce incompatibility. This identifies a precise boundary condition: the theorem applies within a single product line sharing a common rival–anti-rival interface, and the WordPress complement exception (Section 7 case studies) is exactly the situation where different interfaces eliminate the shared constraint.

The result holds for all \(n > 1\), not just asymptotically. The incompatibility is present from the smallest scales, though its magnitude (\(\propto (\beta_0 \ln n)^2\)) grows with scale. At very small \(n\) close to 1, the convexity is weak and the payoff difference between mixed and pure strategies may be negligible in practice.

8. Phase Transitions in Business Model Landscapes

Module 9 demonstrates that the anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\) acts as a bifurcation parameter in contribution games, driving phase transitions from Prisoner’s Dilemma through Coordination Game to Harmony Game as \(\beta\) increases. We extend this analysis to the business model landscape.

8.1 \(\beta\) as a Technology-Driven Bifurcation Parameter

The anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\) is not a firm-level choice variable—it is determined by the nature of the digital good and the technological infrastructure. As technology evolves (cloud computing, open APIs, blockchain, AI), many digital goods may exhibit higher realized anti-rivalry and lower sharing frictions. We can therefore treat \(\beta\) as an exogenous, secularly increasing parameter and study the induced transitions in the business model landscape.

8.2 Phase Structure

The phase structure is now determined by the interaction between two channels: the standalone efficiency \(s(\beta) = \beta \psi\) and the ecosystem value \((\varphi + \delta) V_a \cdot h\). We use “phase transition” in the dynamical systems sense: a qualitative change in the number or stability of equilibria as a parameter crosses a threshold.

From Proposition 3.1, the invasion thresholds at the all-Restrict vertex are:

\[ \beta_{\text{inv}}^T = \frac{c_T + R_0}{\psi_T}, \qquad \beta_{\text{inv}}^L = \frac{c_L + R_0}{\psi_L} \]

The all-Leverage vertex is stable on the \(L\)–\(R\) edge when Restrict cannot invade, i.e., when \(R_0 < s_L(\beta) + \varphi_L V_0(1+a_L)^\eta / (1+\kappa_G) - c_L\). Full-simplex local stability additionally requires that Transform cannot invade, as given by the transverse eigenvalue condition in Proposition 3.2. The critical \(\beta\) at which the all-Leverage state first becomes viable therefore depends on both the standalone and ecosystem terms, and on whether the transverse invasion condition is satisfied.

On the \(L\)–\(R\) edge, Theorem 3.2 shows bistability when \(\tilde{c} > 0\) and \(F(1) > \tilde{c}\). Setting \(F(1) = \tilde{c}\) and solving for \(\beta\) defines the bistability threshold:

\[ \beta_{\text{bist}} \;=\; \frac{c_L + R_0 - A}{A \cdot \lambda_L + \psi_L}, \qquad A = \frac{(\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_0}{1 + \kappa_G} \]

With baseline parameters: \(A = (1.8 + 0.5) \cdot 10 / (1 + 0.15) = 20\), so \(\beta_{\text{bist}} = (14 - 20)/(20 + 35) = -6/55 \approx -0.109\). Since \(\beta_{\text{bist}} < 0\), the \(L\)–\(R\) edge is bistable for all \(\beta > 0\) below \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^L\): All-Leverage is locally stable on that edge even in Phase I, separated from All-Restrict by a tipping point \(x^*\).

The Leverage dominance threshold is simply \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^L = (c_L + R_0)/\psi_L\), the \(\beta\) at which the standalone efficiency of Leverage offsets its costs and the tipping point vanishes (\(\tilde{c} = 0\)).

Theorem 8.1 (Business Model Phase Transitions)

Assumption: \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T < \beta_{\text{inv}}^L\) (Transform invades All-Restrict at a lower \(\beta\) than Leverage). With baseline parameters: \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T \approx 0.357 < 0.400 = \beta_{\text{inv}}^L\).

As the anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\) increases from 0, the business model landscape undergoes the following phase transitions:

Phase I (\(\beta < \beta_{\text{inv}}^T\)): All-Restrict is locally stable at the simplex vertex (both Jacobian eigenvalues at \((0,0)\) are negative). Neither Transform nor Leverage can invade through the standalone channel. However, on the \(L\)–\(R\) edge the system is bistable: since \(\beta_{\text{bist}} < 0\) with baseline parameters, \(F(1) > \tilde{c}\) holds for all \(\beta > 0\), so All-Leverage is also stable on the \(L\)–\(R\) edge, separated from All-Restrict by a tipping point \(x^*\). The tipping point is positive, so leverage adoption still requires ecosystem coordination; its quantitative size depends on parameterization.

Phase II (\(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T \leq \beta < \beta_{\text{inv}}^L\)): Transform can invade the all-Restrict state (the Jacobian eigenvalue \(\beta\psi_T - c_T - R_0 > 0\) in the \(T\)-direction), so all-Restrict is a saddle—stable against Leverage but unstable against Transform. Transform-present states (including All-Transform, if it is locally stable) become attracting from the \(T\)-invasion direction. On the \(L\)–\(R\) edge, bistability persists but the effective threshold \(\tilde{c} = c_L + R_0 - \beta\psi_L\) shrinks, the tipping point \(x^*_L(\beta)\) decreases, and the basin of attraction for All-Leverage expands along the \(L\)–\(R\) edge. Multiple stable states (All-Transform, All-Leverage) may coexist on their respective edges; full-simplex stability of each vertex additionally requires negative transverse eigenvalues (Proposition 3.2). The saddle at All-Restrict mediates between them.

Phase III (\(\beta \geq \beta_{\text{inv}}^L\)): The standalone efficiency of both strategies exceeds their respective cost thresholds. All-Restrict is unstable in both directions (both Jacobian eigenvalues positive). The \(L\)–\(R\) tipping point vanishes (\(\tilde{c} \leq 0\)). All-Leverage is stable on the \(L\)–\(R\) edge (and in the full simplex when the transverse T-eigenvalue at \((1,0)\) is negative; see Proposition 3.2) and dominates through the combination of standalone efficiency and stronger ecosystem returns from tighter coupling. All-Transform may remain stable on the T–R edge but with a shrinking basin of attraction.

In what follows, “phase transition” refers to changes in local stability structure and basin configuration, not necessarily to a discontinuous jump in the equilibrium state as a function of \(\beta\).

Remark 8.0 (The Bistability Threshold)

If the model parameters were such that \(\beta_{\text{bist}} > 0\), a potential fourth sub-phase would appear: for \(\beta < \beta_{\text{bist}}\), the \(L\)–\(R\) edge would have \(F(1) < \tilde{c}\), making All-Restrict globally stable on that edge (no bistability). Above \(\beta_{\text{bist}}\), bistability would emerge. With baseline parameters, \(\beta_{\text{bist}} \approx -0.109 < 0\), so this sub-phase is absent: the \(L\)–\(R\) edge is bistable for all positive \(\beta\) below \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^L\). The sign of \(\beta_{\text{bist}}\) depends on whether the full-adoption ecosystem value \(A = (\varphi_L + \delta) V_0 / (1 + \kappa_G)\) exceeds the cost-plus-displacement threshold \(c_L + R_0\); when it does (\(A = 20 > 14 = c_L + R_0\)), the ecosystem channel alone can sustain All-Leverage, making bistability universal on the edge.

Remark 8.1 (Empirical Correlates)

These phases have observable empirical correlates. Phase I is analogous to sectors or historical periods in which anti-rival properties were weak relative to coordination costs and rival bottlenecks: all-Restrict is locally stable at the simplex vertex, though in principle the \(L\)–\(R\) edge may already be bistable, with a positive and parameter-dependent coordination threshold. Phase II describes the current era: Transform strategies have become viable (de-transactionalizing through subscription models, digitalizing physical offerings), while Leverage strategies are emerging with decreasing tipping points as \(\beta\) grows (community-driven development where shared contributions optimize each participant’s resource usage). Multiple equilibria coexist, with ecosystem coordination determining the outcome. Phase III describes the emerging future where anti-rivalry is strong enough that standalone efficiency alone destabilises All-Restrict, the \(L\)–\(R\) tipping point vanishes, and the symbiosis-seeking approach dominates. The transition from Phase II to Phase III exhibits hysteresis when bistability is present on the L–R edge—once the tipping point is crossed, the shift to leverage-dominant markets is rapid. Stability claims in the phase theorem refer to edge dynamics and invasion eigenvalues at vertices; full-simplex local stability of each vertex depends additionally on transverse eigenvalues computed in Proposition 3.2.

8.3 The Transform-to-Leverage Transition

A particularly important dynamic occurs within the anti-rival-friendly strategies: the competitive interaction between synergy-seeking (Transform) and symbiosis-seeking (Leverage) approaches. On the \(L\)–\(T\) edge of the simplex (where \(x_R = 0\), \(x_T = 1 - x_L\)), the fitness difference is:

\[ \pi_L - \pi_T = (s_L - s_T) + (\varphi_L - \varphi_T) \cdot V_a \cdot h(x_L, 1-x_L) - (c_L - c_T) \]

At the All-Transform vertex (\(x_T = 1, x_L = 0\)), governance is bootstrapped at \(h(0, 1) = \omega/(\omega + \kappa_G) > 0\). Leverage can now invade when:

\[ s_L - s_T + (\varphi_L - \varphi_T) \cdot V_0(1 + a_T)^\eta \cdot \frac{\omega}{\omega + \kappa_G} > c_L - c_T \]

This is a genuine possibility because the unified governance function allows Transform to provide baseline governance. Conversely, at the All-Leverage vertex (\(x_L = 1, x_T = 0\)), Transform can invade when the cost advantage of Transform exceeds the combined standalone and value advantages of Leverage. For strongly anti-rival goods (\(\beta\) large), the standalone difference \(s_L - s_T = \beta(\psi_L - \psi_T)\) grows and Transform cannot invade. For weakly anti-rival goods, the cost advantage dominates. This generates a second bifurcation within the anti-rival strategy space.

9.2 Tipping Point Calculation

First, compute the phase boundaries. With \(\psi_L = 35\), \(\psi_T = 28\), \(c_L = 8\), \(c_T = 4\), \(R_0 = 6\):

\[ \beta_{\text{inv}}^T = \frac{c_T + R_0}{\psi_T} = \frac{10}{28} \approx 0.357, \qquad \beta_{\text{inv}}^L = \frac{c_L + R_0}{\psi_L} = \frac{14}{35} = 0.400 \]

At the baseline \(\beta = 0.4\), we are exactly at the Phase II/III boundary: \(\beta = \beta_{\text{inv}}^L\), so the standalone term for Leverage exactly equals the cost threshold (\(s_L = 0.4 \times 35 = 14 = c_L + R_0\)). The effective threshold is \(\tilde{c} = 14 - 14 = 0\), meaning the tipping point vanishes and any positive foothold of Leverage grows even without additional ecosystem coordination.

For a more interesting bistable case, consider \(\beta = 0.36\) (just above \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T \approx 0.357\), i.e., Phase II). Since \(a_L = \beta \lambda_L\), we have \(a_L = 0.36 \times 1.0 = 0.36\). Then \(s_L = 0.36 \times 35 = 12.6\), so \(\tilde{c} = 14 - 12.6 = 1.4\). The tipping point equation on the \(L\)–\(R\) edge becomes:

\[ F(x) = (1.8 + 0.5) \cdot 10 \cdot (1 + 0.36x) \cdot \frac{x}{x + 0.15} = 1.4 \] \[ 23 \cdot (1 + 0.36x) \cdot \frac{x}{x + 0.15} = 1.4 \]

Expanding: \(23x + 8.28x^2 = 1.4x + 0.21\), hence \(8.28x^2 + 21.6x - 0.21 = 0\). By the quadratic formula:

\[ x^* = \frac{-21.6 + \sqrt{466.56 + 6.96}}{16.56} = \frac{-21.6 + \sqrt{473.52}}{16.56} = \frac{-21.6 + 21.76}{16.56} \approx 0.010 \]

The tipping point is approximately 1.0%—very low because the standalone efficiency has already done most of the work. Even a tiny amount of ecosystem coordination suffices.

For comparison, at \(\beta = 0.25\) (Phase I, since \(\beta < \beta_{\text{inv}}^T \approx 0.357\)): \(a_L = 0.25 \times 1.0 = 0.25\), \(s_L = 8.75\), \(\tilde{c} = 5.25\):

\[ 23 \cdot (1 + 0.25x) \cdot \frac{x}{x + 0.15} = 5.25 \]

Expanding: \(5.75x^2 + 17.75x - 0.7875 = 0\), giving \(x^* \approx 0.044\). Still positive, so some ecosystem coordination is required, though under this baseline parameterization the threshold is quantitatively modest.

9.4 Governance Trap Numerical Example

For the community governance model (Section 4), consider an open-source platform with:

  • \(\beta = 0.3\), \(V_0 = 10\), \(\varphi = 1.5\), \(\kappa = 0.1\)
  • \(c_C = 2\), \(c_G = 5\), \(\sigma = 1\)

Since \(\sigma = 1 < c_G - c_C = 3\), the governance trap condition is satisfied. Under this parameterization, any interior state with both governance and content contribution is dynamically undermined rather than stably sustained. The governance-to-content ratio \(y/x\) declines monotonically (Theorem 4.1), and the system follows the four-phase trajectory:

  1. Growth Phase: Community grows, both content and governance contributions appear, \(V(x,y)\) increases.
  2. Success Phase: High content quality attracts free-riders, content contribution remains viable (\(0.5 \cdot V > 2\) when \(V > 4\)).
  3. Burnout Phase: Governance contributors exit (\(y\) declining), quality begins to erode. The project appears healthy by surface metrics (user count, content volume) but governance debt accumulates.
  4. Crisis Phase: \(y\) falls below the threshold where \(h(y) \approx 0\), \(V \to 0\), content contribution is no longer viable, community collapses.

The minimum governance premium to avoid the trap is \(\sigma_{\min} = c_G - c_C = 3\). Achieving this requires stacking premiums from multiple BMPs (e.g., community governance providing power \(\sigma = 1\), revenue sharing providing financial \(\sigma = 1\), stewardship providing reputational \(\sigma = 1\)).

10. Discussion

10.1 Summary of Analytical Results

Our formalization yields several results that sharpen the original manuscript’s qualitative findings:

Result 1: Phase-Dependent Stability and Critical Mass (Theorem 3.2, Theorem 8.1). The stability of the all-Restrict state depends on the anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\). At low \(\beta\) (Phase I), all-Restrict is locally stable at the simplex vertex, but the \(L\)–\(R\) edge is already bistable: All-Leverage is also stable on the \(L\)–\(R\) edge, separated from All-Restrict by a positive tipping point \(x^*\), whose magnitude depends on parameterization. At intermediate \(\beta\) (Phase II), Transform can invade and all-Restrict becomes a saddle; the \(L\)–\(R\) tipping point decreases as standalone efficiency grows. At high \(\beta\) (Phase III), all-Restrict is unstable in both directions and anti-rival strategies dominate. This formalizes the intuition that anti-rival business model innovation is an ecosystem-level challenge at intermediate anti-rivalry levels, while at sufficiently high anti-rivalry the transition occurs through standalone efficiency gains.

Result 2: Governance Trap Conditions (Theorem 4.1). Community-based business models (Mechanism 1, 15 BMPs) require a governance premium exceeding the cost differential between governance and content contribution. The 5 governance-oriented BMPs in Mechanism 1 can be interpreted as mechanisms for building this premium through different channels (power, reputation, financial, strategic, social).

Result 3: Incompatibility of Mixed Approaches (Theorem 7.1). Under the shared-interface coupling-allocation model, for all \(n > 1\), business models mixing leverage-seeking and synergy-seeking patterns within a single product line are dominated by pure approaches. The structural incompatibility arises because Leverage requires tight coupling between rival and anti-rival components (connecting them for mutual optimization), while Transform requires loose coupling (minimizing bidirectional interference). These opposing interface requirements create an endogenous incompatibility cost proportional to \((\beta_0 \ln n)^2\) that grows with scale.

Result 4: Phase Transitions (Theorem 8.1). Increasing anti-rivalry \(\beta\) drives qualitative changes in the stability of equilibria. The phase structure has explicit thresholds: \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T = (c_T + R_0)/\psi_T\) for Transform invasion and \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^L = (c_L + R_0)/\psi_L\) for Leverage invasion. Transform strategies become viable at lower thresholds than Leverage strategies because their lower costs are offset by lower standalone efficiency gains. Both approaches remain independently viable as distinct equilibria; the dominance of Leverage at high \(\beta\) reflects the combination of stronger standalone efficiency and stronger ecosystem returns from tighter coupling.

10.2 Prescriptive Implications

For business model innovators, these results suggest several prescriptions:

  1. Choose a pure interface approach. The Incompatibility Theorem warns against attempting to simultaneously amplify and reduce the coupling between rival and anti-rival components within a single product line. A firm cannot both tightly connect rival and anti-rival sides for mutual optimization (Leverage) and minimize their bidirectional interference (Transform) on the same interface. The payoff difference between mixed and pure strategies is proportional to \((\beta_0 \ln n)^2\), so it is negligible near \(n = 1\) but grows rapidly with scale. Firms should plan a transition to a pure approach as they grow.
  2. Stack governance premiums. Community-based business models (Mechanism 1) should not rely on a single source of governance premium. The minimum viable premium \(\sigma_{\min} = c_G - c_C\) is typically larger than any single premium source can provide. Combining community governance (power), revenue sharing (financial), and stewardship roles (reputational) increases the total premium and reduces governance trap risk.
  3. Coordinate ecosystem entry. At intermediate anti-rivalry levels (Phases I–II), the \(L\)–\(R\) edge bistability implies that individual firm entry may be insufficient for Leverage adoption. Firms should seek industry-level coordination—through consortia, standards bodies, or platform ecosystems—to achieve the critical mass \(x^*\) required on the \(L\)–\(R\) edge for leverage strategies to become self-sustaining. At higher \(\beta\) (Phase III), standalone efficiency alone suffices.
  4. Monitor the governance ratio. The governance trap is insidious because it is invisible in surface metrics (user count, content volume remain high during the burnout phase). Business model designers should track the governance-to-content ratio \(y/x\) and intervene when it falls below a sustainability threshold.
  5. Use Transform as a stepping stone to Leverage. Since \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T < \beta_{\text{inv}}^L\) (Theorem 8.1), Transform strategies become viable at lower anti-rivalry levels than Leverage strategies. Moreover, on the \(L\)–\(T\) edge, Transform bootstraps ecosystem governance: the governance quality at the all-Transform vertex is \(h(0,1) = \omega/(\omega + \kappa_G) > 0\) (Section 8.3), which lowers the Leverage invasion barrier relative to the \(L\)–\(R\) edge. At intermediate \(\beta\), firms should adopt Transform to build ecosystem governance capacity, then transition to Leverage as anti-rivalry increases and the tighter coupling becomes sustainable.
  6. Separate interfaces to escape the incompatibility constraint. The Incompatibility Theorem (Theorem 7.1) applies under the shared-interface model, where Leverage and Transform patterns compete over a single interface parameter \(\theta\) (Critical Assessment 7.1). When patterns target distinct rival–anti-rival interfaces, each operates on its own \(\theta\), and mixed strategies are no longer dominated by the coupling-allocation trade-off. Multi-product firms can therefore pursue both Leverage and Transform simultaneously by isolating them on separate interfaces, avoiding the endogenous incompatibility cost.
  7. Manage transparency as a strategic variable. The Subsidization–Transparency game (Section 5.2) shows that the effective market for opaque strategies shrinks with the transparent fraction via \(n_O(p) = n_0 \cdot \alpha \cdot (1 - \lambda p)\), creating negative frequency dependence on opacity. The tipping-point structure at \(p^*\) means early transparency adoption may be disadvantageous, but once the market crosses \(p^*\), opacity becomes self-undermining. Firms should time transparency moves to market composition: as the transparency norm builds, opaque holdouts face accelerating user loss.
  8. Prioritize de-transactionalization for high-anti-rivalry goods. The de-transactionalization value ratio \((1 + \eta p)^{\beta_0}\) (Proposition 6.1) scales with both per-use price friction \(\eta p\) and intrinsic anti-rivalry \(\beta_0\). For goods with high \(\beta_0\) facing significant per-use friction, removing transaction barriers is transformative; for low \(\beta_0\) or low \(\eta p\), the gains are marginal. Furthermore, the viability band \(n_{\min} < n < n_{\max}\) means subscription or flat-rate models require critical mass to sustain. Firms should assess \(\beta_0\) and \(\eta p\) before committing to de-transactionalization strategies.
  9. Diagnose your phase position before choosing strategy. The phase structure of Theorem 8.1 has qualitatively different strategic implications in each phase. In Phase I (\(\beta < \beta_{\text{inv}}^T\)), neither anti-rival strategy can invade independently, and coordination is the binding constraint. In Phase II (\(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T \leq \beta < \beta_{\text{inv}}^L\)), Transform is viable without coordination, while Leverage requires a positive but shrinking tipping point on the \(L\)–\(R\) edge. In Phase III (\(\beta \geq \beta_{\text{inv}}^L\)), standalone efficiency suffices for both strategies. Firms should identify where \(\beta\) sits relative to \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^T\) and \(\beta_{\text{inv}}^L\) to determine which constraint—coordination, governance, or neither—is binding.

10.3 Limitations and Critical Assessment

We identify several limitations that constrain the applicability of our results:

Limitation 1: Homogeneous Agents. The model treats all firms within each strategy type as identical. In practice, firms differ dramatically in resources, capabilities, market position, and strategic intent. Heterogeneous-agent extensions would allow for richer dynamics, including the possibility that large incumbents and small entrants adopt different strategies optimally.

Limitation 2: Assumed Functional Forms. The power-law anti-rivalry function \(V(n) = V_0 n^{\beta}\), the Michaelis–Menten quality modulator, and the linear enclosure dampening are mathematically convenient but empirically unvalidated. Alternative functional forms (logistic saturation for anti-rivalry, threshold effects for governance quality) could yield qualitatively different results. We conjecture that the bistability and incompatibility results are robust to functional form, but the specific phase transition thresholds are not.

Limitation 3: Static versus Dynamic Strategies. The replicator dynamics assume firms are committed to fixed strategies. In practice, firms pivot, experiment, and learn. Agent-based models allowing strategy switching would better capture the messy reality of business model innovation.

Limitation 4: Exogenous Anti-Rivalry. We treat \(\beta\) as exogenous. In reality, firms invest in technologies and practices that increase or decrease the anti-rivalry of their offerings. Endogenizing \(\beta\) would create a co-evolutionary model where the technological parameter and the business model distribution evolve jointly.

Limitation 5: Neglect of Market Structure. The model assumes a well-mixed population of competing firms. Real markets have network effects, switching costs, multi-homing, geographic segmentation, and regulatory heterogeneity. These factors can create local optima that differ from the global analysis.

Limitation 6: Measurement Challenges. The parameters \(\varphi_L\), \(\delta\), \(\kappa_G\), and \(\sigma\) are conceptually clear but empirically difficult to measure. Empirical validation of the model would require operationalizing these parameters through observable proxies, which is a non-trivial econometric challenge.

10.4 Connections to Existing Literature

Our formalization connects to several established research streams:

Evolutionary Economics. Nelson & Winter’s (1982) framework treats firms as carriers of organizational routines that evolve through variation, selection, and retention. Our model extends this to business model configurations, with the anti-rivalry parameter creating a novel selection pressure not present in traditional evolutionary economics.

Platform Economics. The bistability result resonates with the platform economics literature on network effects and tipping points (Katz & Shapiro, 1985; Eisenmann et al., 2006). Our contribution is to show that the tipping point applies not only to platform adoption by users but also to business model adoption by firms within an ecosystem.

Commons Governance. The governance trap formalization extends Ostrom’s (1990) design principles to digital business model communities. The key difference from natural commons is that the governance challenge is not preventing overuse (the good is anti-rival) but sustaining the rivalrous complement that enables anti-rival value creation.

Institutional Complexity. Our phase transition analysis provides a dynamical systems interpretation of the institutional complexity framework (Thornton et al., 2012; Battilana & Lee, 2014). The coexistence of complexity-avoiding (Restrict), complexity-decreasing (Transform, synergy-seeking through interface simplification), and complexity-leveraging (Leverage, symbiosis-seeking through interface amplification) approaches—and the conditions under which each dominates—corresponds to phase transitions driven by the technological evolution of anti-rivalry.

10.5 Future Research Directions

The formalization opens several avenues for future work:

  1. Empirical calibration: Estimating model parameters from longitudinal data on business model adoption in specific digital markets (e.g., software, media, education).
  2. Agent-based simulation: Extending the analytical model with heterogeneous agents, spatial structure, and adaptive learning to study the robustness of the phase transition and incompatibility results.
  3. Multi-level dynamics: Modeling the co-evolution of within-firm BMP composition (micro-level) and market-level business model distribution (macro-level) using multi-level selection frameworks.
  4. Policy analysis: Using the model to evaluate the effects of intellectual property policy, open data mandates, and platform regulation on the speed and direction of business model transitions.
  5. Endogenous anti-rivalry: Co-evolutionary models where firms invest in changing \(\beta\) (through technology choices, licensing decisions, API design) while simultaneously choosing business model strategies.

11. Conclusion

This paper has demonstrated that the qualitative findings of the original manuscript on anti-rival business model complexity management can be placed on rigorous analytical foundations through evolutionary game theory. The formalization is not merely a mathematical re-statement of existing insights; it generates new results—the bistability of business model landscapes, the threshold conditions governing governance trap risk and sustainability, the structural incompatibility, under a shared-interface coupling-allocation model, of coupling-amplifying (Leverage) and coupling-reducing (Transform) approaches within a single product line for all \(n > 1\), and the phase transition structure of market evolution—that go beyond what qualitative analysis alone can deliver.

At the same time, we have been candid about the limitations of the approach. Mathematical models impose assumptions that may not hold, parameters that may not be measurable, and functional forms that may not be accurate. The value of the formalization lies not in its predictive precision but in its structural clarity: it makes the logical relationships between variables, strategies, and outcomes explicit and falsifiable.

The most consequential finding, in our assessment, is the phase transition structure. At low to intermediate anti-rivalry levels, the all-Restrict state is locally stable or the system exhibits bistability on strategy edges, meaning that even in environments where anti-rival business models would outperform restrictive models once sufficient coordination is achieved, the transition may require coordinated entry exceeding a critical mass threshold. At high anti-rivalry, standalone efficiency gains destabilise all-Restrict and the transition occurs naturally. This has deep implications for both theory and practice: the persistence of traditional business models in increasingly anti-rival digital economies may reflect either a coordination failure (at intermediate \(\beta\)) or genuinely insufficient anti-rivalry (at low \(\beta\)). The tools for overcoming coordination failure—ecosystem orchestration, industry consortia, policy intervention—are most critical in the intermediate regime where Transform has already demonstrated viability but Leverage requires collective action.

Anti-rivalry is not a marginal modification of standard economic logic; it is a fundamental inversion. The evolutionary game-theoretic framework reveals just how deep this inversion runs: it transforms the selection pressures on business models, creates new instabilities and new equilibria, and demands institutional innovations that the market mechanism alone cannot supply.
