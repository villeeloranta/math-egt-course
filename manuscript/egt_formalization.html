An Evolutionary Game-Theoretic Formalization of Anti-Rival Business Model Complexity Management

Analytical Extension of “Managing the Complexities of Digital Goods’ Anti-Rivalry in Contemporary Business Model Innovation”

Working Paper — March 2026

Abstract

This paper develops a formal evolutionary game-theoretic (EGT) framework for analyzing the complexity management mechanisms and business model patterns (BMPs) identified in the study of anti-rival digital goods. We translate the seven complexity management mechanisms and three metamechanisms into explicit payoff functions, replicator dynamics, and stability conditions. Our formalization yields several analytical results: (i) a three-strategy replicator model demonstrating that symbiosis-seeking (complexity-leveraging) strategies are evolutionarily stable only above a critical anti-rivalry threshold, below which synergy-seeking (complexity-decreasing) strategies dominate; (ii) a formal incompatibility theorem showing that mixing leveraging and decreasing patterns within a single business model yields strictly lower fitness than pure approaches under broad conditions; (iii) an extension of the governance trap framework to business model communities, establishing necessary conditions for sustainability of community-based mechanisms; and (iv) a phase transition analysis revealing bifurcation points at which the market-dominant business model approach shifts discontinuously as technological anti-rivalry increases. These results provide analytical depth to the qualitative findings of the original study while generating testable predictions and design prescriptions for business model innovators operating with anti-rival digital goods.

Keywords: evolutionary game theory; replicator dynamics; anti-rival goods; business model patterns; institutional complexity; stability analysis; phase transitions

1. Introduction and Motivation

The original manuscript identifies seven complexity management mechanisms and 39 related business model patterns through which firms can benefit from the anti-rivalry of digital goods while managing the institutional complexity that arises from mixing rival and anti-rival resources. The study’s qualitative findings—organized into three metamechanisms and the symbiosis-seeking versus synergy-seeking distinction—represent a substantial contribution to the anti-rival goods discourse. However, the original analysis remains, by design, qualitative and classificatory. The present work responds to the original manuscript’s explicit call for “systems dynamics methods that provide tools for analyzing complex systems” by providing an evolutionary game-theoretic formalization.

Why evolutionary game theory? Three features of the business model pattern landscape make EGT the appropriate analytical framework. First, frequency dependence: the value of anti-rival-friendly business models depends critically on how many other firms adopt similar approaches, because anti-rival goods increase in value with use. A single firm adopting anti-rival-friendly complexity management practices in a market of proprietary competitors faces a different payoff landscape than one operating among many firms that leverage or transform institutional complexity for anti-rival benefits. Second, bounded rationality: firms do not solve global optimization problems when choosing business models; they imitate successful competitors, experiment incrementally, and respond to market signals—dynamics well captured by replicator dynamics (Hofbauer & Sigmund, 1998). Third, population-level selection: the organizational ecology literature (Hannan & Freeman, 1977) has long recognized that business model viability is determined by environmental selection as much as by deliberate design, and EGT provides the mathematical apparatus for analyzing such selection dynamics.

Our formalization makes four principal contributions:

  1. A three-strategy replicator model at the metamechanism level, establishing conditions under which leveraging versus decreasing approaches are evolutionarily stable (Section 3).
  2. An extension of the governance trap framework to community-based business model mechanisms, connecting the manuscript’s Mechanism 1 (15 BMPs) to precise sustainability conditions (Section 4).
  3. A formal Incompatibility Theorem demonstrating that mixed leveraging–decreasing strategies are evolutionarily dominated under realistic parameter regimes (Section 7).
  4. A phase transition analysis showing how increasing technological anti-rivalry drives discontinuous shifts in the market-dominant business model configuration (Section 8).

Throughout, we maintain a critical stance toward our own formalization. Mathematical models impose structural assumptions that may not hold in practice, and we identify these assumptions explicitly, assess their plausibility, and characterize the robustness of our results to their violation.

2. Theoretical Foundations

2.1 Anti-Rivalry as a Continuous Parameter

Following the mathematical framework developed in the course modules, we parameterize the rivalry–anti-rivalry spectrum through a single exponent \(\beta\). For a good with \(n\) users, the per-user value function takes the power-law form:

\[ V(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{\beta} \]

where \(\beta < 0\) corresponds to rival goods (value decreases with use), \(\beta = 0\) to non-rival goods (value invariant to use), and \(\beta > 0\) to anti-rival goods (value increases with use). The total social value \(W(n) = n \cdot V(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{1+\beta}\) is superlinear for \(\beta > 0\), capturing the increasingly positive-sum nature of anti-rival goods. This parameterization, while mathematically convenient, deserves scrutiny: real goods rarely exhibit pure power-law scaling across all ranges of \(n\), and many anti-rival goods exhibit bounded anti-rivalry (logistic saturation). We address robustness to functional form in Section 10.

2.2 The Coupled Value Function

A central insight from Module 10 is that anti-rivalry is never pure. Every anti-rivalrous resource depends on rivalrous complements—maintainer time, server infrastructure, editorial attention. The coupled value function captures this dependency:

Definition 2.1 (Coupled Value Function)

For an anti-rivalrous resource with \(n\) users depending on a rivalrous complement with total supply \(M\):

\[ V(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{\beta} \cdot h\!\left(\frac{M}{n^{\gamma}}\right), \qquad h(s) = \frac{s}{s + \kappa} \]

where \(\gamma > 0\) is the demand scaling exponent for the rivalrous complement and \(\kappa > 0\) is the half-saturation constant.

The function \(h\) (Michaelis–Menten or Monod form) acts as a quality modulator: when the rivalrous complement is abundant relative to demand (\(s \gg \kappa\)), quality approaches 1; when scarce (\(s \ll \kappa\)), quality collapses toward 0. The condition \(\gamma > \beta\) ensures that an optimal community size \(n^*\) exists, beyond which the rivalrous bottleneck dominates the anti-rivalrous benefit.

2.3 Business Model Patterns as Evolutionary Strategies

We treat the population of firms in a digital economy as the evolving population. Each firm adopts a business model configuration—a combination of BMPs—that determines its fitness (economic viability, growth rate, imitation attractiveness). The replicator dynamics then describe how the population distribution across business model configurations evolves over time.

Remark 2.1 (Validity of the Evolutionary Metaphor)

This mapping requires justification. Unlike biological organisms, firms can (i) switch strategies mid-life, (ii) combine multiple strategies simultaneously, and (iii) create genuinely novel strategies. The replicator dynamics strictly apply to fixed strategies with differential reproduction. We interpret the dynamics as describing market share evolution: the fraction of the market served by each business model type changes according to relative profitability. This interpretation is standard in evolutionary economics (Nelson & Winter, 1982) and organizational ecology (Hannan & Freeman, 1977), though it requires the assumption that more profitable business models expand (through growth or imitation) at rates proportional to their fitness advantage. This assumption is strongest in competitive markets with low switching costs and weakest in markets with significant lock-in or regulatory barriers.

2.4 The Manuscript’s Architecture: From BMPs to Metamechanisms

The original manuscript identifies 39 BMPs organized into 7 mechanisms and 3 metamechanisms. For the EGT formalization, we map these onto three strategy types corresponding to the three metamechanisms:

StrategyMetamechanismMechanismsBMPsApproach
\(L\) (Leverage)1: Large-Scale Collaboration
2: Anti-Rival Leverage		M1, M2, M3, M726Symbiosis-seeking
\(T\) (Transform)3: Asset & Offering TransformationM4, M5, M611Synergy-seeking
\(R\) (Restrict)Compartmentalization / Defiance—109Complexity-avoiding

The structural content of each strategy must be understood precisely:

Strategy \(L\) (Leverage) is a symbiosis-seeking approach that leverages institutional complexity for anti-rival benefits. It operates through two simultaneous structural moves: (1) Detaching rival resource usage from anti-rival digital good sharing, so that rival resource consumption does not rise in proportion to anti-rival sharing; and (2) Connecting the rival and anti-rival sides of the system, so that anti-rival sharing helps the rival part optimize its resource consumption. The combination of detachment and connection constitutes symbiosis: rival and anti-rival components coexist with their institutional complexity intact, but each benefits from the other. The two metamechanisms—large-scale collaboration (minimizing individuals’ rival costs through collective action) and seeking anti-rival leverage from rival investments (extracting anti-rival value from activities that also consume rival resources)—are the institutional mechanisms through which these structural moves are realized.

Strategy \(T\) (Transform) is a synergy-seeking approach that decreases institutional complexity for anti-rival benefits. It pursues two structural goals: (1) Minimizing (but not entirely removing) the limitations that rival resources set for anti-rival sharing; and (2) Minimizing (but not necessarily optimizing) the effect of anti-rival sharing on rival resource usage. Unlike Leverage, which maintains and exploits the coupling between rival and anti-rival components, Transform restructures the offerings to reduce the bidirectional interference between them. The metamechanism—asset and offering transformation toward anti-rivalry—achieves this through patterns that simplify the rival–anti-rival interface (de-transactionalization, digitalization, parallel offerings) rather than through building complex governance structures to manage the interface.

The critical distinction is qualitative, not merely quantitative: Leverage amplifies coupling between rival and anti-rival components (symbiosis), while Transform reduces coupling between them (synergy through simplification). These are structurally different approaches to the same challenge of managing institutional complexity, not simply more or less aggressive versions of the same approach.

Note that strategy \(R\) includes the 76 compartmentalization patterns, 33 defiance patterns excluded from the manuscript’s core analysis. These represent the traditional approach of either ignoring the rival–anti-rival tension or suppressing anti-rivalry through artificial scarcity (DRM, proprietary enclosure). Including them in the evolutionary model is essential: they constitute the incumbent population against which anti-rival-friendly strategies must compete.

3. Model I: Metamechanism-Level Population Dynamics

3.1 Strategy Space and Population State

Consider a population of firms in a digital economy. Each firm adopts one of three complexity management approaches: \(L\) (Leverage, symbiosis-seeking), \(T\) (Transform, synergy-seeking), or \(R\) (Restrict, complexity-avoiding). The population state is \((x_L, x_T, x_R)\) with \(x_L + x_T + x_R = 1\), living on the 2-simplex \(\Delta^2\).

3.2 Anti-Rival Ecosystem Value

The critical structural feature of this model is that anti-rival value is endogenous to the population state. The more firms adopt sharing-oriented strategies, the greater the anti-rival value available in the ecosystem:

Definition 3.1 (Ecosystem Anti-Rival Value)

\[ V_a(x_L, x_T) = V_0 \left(1 + \beta_L \cdot x_L + \beta_T \cdot x_T\right)^{\eta} \]

where \(\beta_L > \beta_T > 0\) are strategy-specific anti-rivalry contributions and \(\eta > 0\) controls the curvature of returns. The ordering \(\beta_L > \beta_T\) reflects the structural difference between the two approaches: Leverage strategies amplify coupling between rival and anti-rival components (connecting them so that anti-rival sharing optimizes rival resource consumption), thereby generating stronger ecosystem-level anti-rival value. Transform strategies reduce coupling (minimizing bidirectional interference between rival and anti-rival components), which preserves anti-rival value but at a lower intensity because the synergy-seeking simplification attenuates the network effects that full coupling would amplify.

We also incorporate the rivalrous complement through a governance quality function. The fraction of firms investing in governance and community infrastructure determines the quality modulator:

\[ h(x_L) = \frac{x_L}{x_L + \kappa_G} \]

where \(\kappa_G\) is the governance half-saturation constant. Only \(L\)-strategy firms contribute to ecosystem governance (community governance, stewardship, shared ownership patterns from Mechanisms 1 and 7), so governance quality depends solely on \(x_L\).

3.3 Payoff Functions

Definition 3.2 (Strategy Payoffs)

The fitness of each strategy is:

\[ \pi_L = \varphi_L \cdot V_a(x_L, x_T) \cdot h(x_L) - c_L \] \[ \pi_T = \varphi_T \cdot V_a(x_L, x_T) \cdot h_T(x_L, x_T) - c_T \] \[ \pi_R = R_0 - \delta \cdot V_a(x_L, x_T) \cdot h(x_L) \]

where:

  • \(\varphi_L > \varphi_T > 1\): differential access multipliers. Leverage firms capture disproportionate returns from anti-rival value because their symbiosis-seeking approach—connecting rival and anti-rival components—creates strategic positions (ecosystem centrality, data flows, integration advantages) that amplify value capture. Transform firms capture moderate returns because their synergy-seeking approach—minimizing bidirectional interference—yields a simpler but less strategically differentiated position.
  • \(c_L > c_T > 0\): cost ordering. Leverage strategies require higher investment because maintaining the dual structural moves (detaching rival costs from anti-rival sharing while connecting the sides for optimization) demands complex institutional infrastructure (governance mechanisms, community coordination, transparency systems). Transform strategies have lower costs because decreasing institutional complexity is inherently less infrastructure-intensive than leveraging it.
  • \(h_T(x_L, x_T) = \frac{x_L + \alpha_T x_T}{x_L + \alpha_T x_T + \kappa_G}\): governance quality accessible to \(T\)-firms. The coefficient \(0 < \alpha_T < 1\) reflects that Transform firms contribute to ecosystem governance through their own mechanisms (offering restructuring, interface simplification) but at lower intensity than Leverage firms, whose approach is specifically designed to build and maintain complex governance structures. Transform firms also benefit from the governance infrastructure that Leverage firms create, as the simplified interfaces they rely on presuppose some baseline ecosystem coordination.
  • \(R_0\): base returns from traditional rival goods business models.
  • \(\delta > 0\): competitive displacement coefficient. As the anti-rival ecosystem grows, traditional business models lose market share to firms offering anti-rival value.

Remark 3.1 (Justification of the Displacement Effect)

The negative term \(-\delta \cdot V_a \cdot h\) in \(\pi_R\) captures a key empirical regularity: when substantial anti-rival value exists in the ecosystem (e.g., high-quality open-source software), firms using restrictive strategies face competitive pressure. Customers can access anti-rival alternatives, reducing the market for proprietary offerings. This is not symmetric: \(L\)-firms do not suffer from the existence of \(R\)-firms, because restrictive strategies do not create comparable anti-rival value. The asymmetry is structural and reflects the positive-sum nature of anti-rival value creation.

3.4 Replicator Dynamics

The replicator equations on the 2-simplex (using \(x_R = 1 - x_L - x_T\)) are:

\[ \dot{x}_L = x_L \left(\pi_L - \bar{\pi}\right) \] \[ \dot{x}_T = x_T \left(\pi_T - \bar{\pi}\right) \]

where the average fitness is:

\[ \bar{\pi} = x_L \pi_L + x_T \pi_T + (1 - x_L - x_T) \pi_R \]

Substituting and expanding the fitness differences:

\[ \pi_L - \pi_R = \varphi_L \cdot V_a \cdot h(x_L) - c_L - R_0 + \delta \cdot V_a \cdot h(x_L) \] \[ = (\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_a(x_L, x_T) \cdot h(x_L) - (c_L + R_0) \] \[ \pi_T - \pi_R = \varphi_T \cdot V_a \cdot h_T - c_T - R_0 + \delta \cdot V_a \cdot h(x_L) \]

These expressions reveal the key structural feature: the fitness differences are state-dependent, increasing with the anti-rival ecosystem value \(V_a\) and governance quality \(h\). This creates positive feedback: more leverage firms → higher \(V_a\) and \(h\) → higher relative fitness of leveraging → more leverage firms. The dynamics are thus inherently nonlinear.

3.5 Fixed Point Analysis

Theorem 3.1 (Fixed Points of the Three-Strategy Model)

The system has the following fixed points:

  1. All-Restrict vertex \((0, 0)\): Always exists. Payoffs reduce to \(\pi_R = R_0\).
  2. All-Leverage vertex \((1, 0)\): Exists. Fitness is \(\pi_L = \varphi_L \cdot V_0(1+\beta_L)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{1}{1+\kappa_G} - c_L\).
  3. All-Transform vertex \((0, 1)\): Exists. Fitness is \(\pi_T = \varphi_T \cdot V_0(1+\beta_T)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{\alpha_T}{\alpha_T + \kappa_G} - c_T\).
  4. Interior fixed point \((x_L^*, x_T^*)\): Exists when \(\pi_L = \pi_T = \pi_R\), requiring specific parameter configurations.
  5. Edge fixed points: On the \(L\)–\(R\), \(T\)–\(R\), and \(L\)–\(T\) edges, defined by pairwise fitness equalities.

The stability of each vertex depends on whether the other strategies can invade:

Proposition 3.1 (Invasion Conditions)

(a) Leverage invades Restrict: Strategy \(L\) can invade an all-\(R\) population when:

\[ \pi_L\big|_{x_L=0, x_T=0} > \pi_R\big|_{x_L=0, x_T=0} \] \[ \Longleftrightarrow \quad (\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_0 \cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{\varepsilon + \kappa_G} > c_L + R_0 \]

where \(\varepsilon \to 0\) represents a vanishingly small fraction of leverage firms. Since \(h(\varepsilon) \to 0\) as \(\varepsilon \to 0\), leverage cannot invade an all-Restrict population through infinitesimal perturbation. The leverage strategy requires a critical mass to establish viable governance (\(h(x_L) > 0\) requires \(x_L > 0\)).

(b) Transform invades Restrict: Similarly, \(T\) cannot invade through infinitesimal perturbation because \(h_T(0, \varepsilon) \to 0\).

(c) Restrict invades Leverage: Strategy \(R\) can invade an all-\(L\) population when:

\[ R_0 > (\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_0(1+\beta_L)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{1}{1+\kappa_G} - c_L - \delta \cdot V_0(1+\beta_L)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{1}{1+\kappa_G} \] \[ \Longleftrightarrow \quad R_0 + c_L > \varphi_L \cdot V_0(1+\beta_L)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{1}{1+\kappa_G} \]

This fails (Restrict cannot invade) when the anti-rival ecosystem is sufficiently valuable.

Critical Observation 3.1

The inability of anti-rival strategies to invade an all-Restrict population through infinitesimal perturbation is a fundamental structural result. It implies that the transition from traditional to anti-rival business models cannot be gradual—it requires a critical mass. This is the business model analogue of the tipping point dynamics identified in Module 9 for anti-rivalrous contribution games. Mathematically, the all-Restrict vertex \((0,0)\) is always locally stable, making the system bistable when anti-rival strategies are also viable at higher frequencies.

This has a profound implication: in economies where traditional business models are entrenched, anti-rival business model innovation requires coordinated entry—a sufficient number of firms must simultaneously adopt leveraging or transforming strategies to create enough ecosystem value and governance quality to sustain themselves. This provides a formal justification for the ecosystem-level entrepreneurship and policy intervention that the anti-rival goods literature calls for.

3.6 Stability on the Leverage–Restrict Edge

The most analytically tractable reduction is the two-strategy game between \(L\) and \(R\) (setting \(x_T = 0\)). Let \(x = x_L\), so \(x_R = 1 - x\). The replicator equation becomes:

\[ \dot{x} = x(1-x) \left[\pi_L(x) - \pi_R(x)\right] \]

where:

\[ \pi_L(x) - \pi_R(x) = (\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_0(1 + \beta_L x)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{x}{x + \kappa_G} - (c_L + R_0) \]

Define \(F(x) = (\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_0(1 + \beta_L x)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{x}{x + \kappa_G}\). This function satisfies \(F(0) = 0\) and is strictly increasing for \(x > 0\) (both factors are increasing in \(x\)). Therefore:

Theorem 3.2 (Bistability on the \(L\)–\(R\) Edge)

On the \(L\)–\(R\) edge, the dynamics exhibit one of two regimes:

  1. If \(F(1) < c_L + R_0\): The fitness difference \(\pi_L - \pi_R < 0\) for all \(x \in (0,1]\). The all-Restrict state \(x = 0\) is globally stable. Anti-rival leverage strategies are not viable.
  2. If \(F(1) > c_L + R_0\): By the intermediate value theorem, there exists a unique \(x^* \in (0,1)\) such that \(F(x^*) = c_L + R_0\). The dynamics are bistable: \(x = 0\) is stable for \(x < x^*\), \(x = 1\) is stable for \(x > x^*\), and \(x^*\) is an unstable tipping point.

Proof

\(F(x)\) is continuous on \([0,1]\) with \(F(0) = 0\). Since \(F\) is strictly increasing (as a product of two strictly increasing positive functions for \(x > 0\)), if \(F(1) > c_L + R_0\), there exists exactly one crossing point \(x^*\) where \(F(x^*) = c_L + R_0\). For \(x < x^*\), \(F(x) < c_L + R_0\), so \(\pi_L - \pi_R < 0\) and \(\dot{x} < 0\). For \(x > x^*\), \(\pi_L - \pi_R > 0\) and \(\dot{x} > 0\). The monotonicity of \(F\) guarantees uniqueness of \(x^*\).

■

The tipping point \(x^*\) satisfies:

\[ (\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_0 (1 + \beta_L x^*)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{x^*}{x^* + \kappa_G} = c_L + R_0 \]

This equation does not admit a closed-form solution for general \(\eta\), but its qualitative behavior is clear: \(x^*\) decreases as \(\beta_L\) increases (stronger anti-rivalry lowers the tipping point), as \(V_0\) increases (higher base value), and as \(R_0\) decreases (weaker traditional alternatives). Conversely, \(x^*\) increases with \(c_L\) (higher leveraging costs) and \(\kappa_G\) (more demanding governance requirements).

4. Model II: The Community Governance Game

Mechanism 1 (“Using Communities to Minimize Rival Costs and Maximize Anti-Rival Value”) encompasses 15 of the 39 BMPs—the largest single mechanism. Its patterns span from crowdsourcing and open source to community governance and shared ownership. We formalize this mechanism by extending the three-strategy governance model from Module 10 to the business model context.

4.1 Mapping BMPs to Contribution Types

The 15 patterns in Mechanism 1 can be partitioned into content-oriented and governance-oriented contributions:

Contribution TypeBMPsRole in \(V(x,y)\)
Content (C)Crowdsourcing, Open source, Crowdsourced innovation, Review system, Increase quality through participation, Make more of itDrives \(g(x) = (1 + \beta x)^{\eta}\)
Governance (G)Community governance, Adopt a stewardship role, Revenue sharing, Open business model, Pay what you wantDrives \(h(y) = \frac{y}{y + \kappa}\)
Infrastructure (I)Peer-to-peer, Nodes, Crowdfunding, Repurpose for societyDetermines \(M\) (rivalrous complement supply)

4.2 The Three-Strategy Model Within Community Mechanisms

Within a community-based business ecosystem (e.g., an open-source project, a crowdsourced platform), individual participants choose among three strategies:

Definition 4.1 (Community Strategies)

  • Content Contributor (C): Creates anti-rival value (code, data, reviews). Pays cost \(c_C\).
  • Governance Contributor (G): Maintains the rivalrous complement (moderation, infrastructure, conflict resolution). Pays cost \(c_G > c_C\). Receives governance premium \(\sigma\).
  • Free-Rider (F): Consumes anti-rival value without contributing.

With differential access \(\varphi > 1\) for contributors (capturing reputational returns, learning-by-doing, community recognition effects embedded in patterns like “Review system” and “Pay what you want”):

\[ \pi_C = \varphi \cdot V(x,y) - c_C, \qquad \pi_G = \varphi \cdot V(x,y) - c_G + \sigma, \qquad \pi_F = V(x,y) \]

where \(V(x,y) = V_0 \cdot (1 + \beta x)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{y}{y + \kappa}\).

4.3 The Governance Trap in Business Model Communities

Theorem 4.1 (Business Model Governance Trap)

A community-based business model operating under Mechanism 1 is in a governance trap if and only if \(\sigma < c_G - c_C\). When this condition holds:

  1. The governance contribution fraction \(y(t)\) is monotonically decreasing.
  2. As \(y \to 0\), the commons value \(V(x,y) \to 0\) regardless of content contribution \(x\).
  3. When \(V \to 0\), content contribution is also dominated: \(\pi_C - \pi_F = (\varphi - 1) \cdot 0 - c_C < 0\).
  4. The system converges to the all-free-rider state: \((x, y) \to (0, 0)\).

Proof

The fitness difference between governance and content contribution is:

\[ \pi_G - \pi_C = \sigma - (c_G - c_C) \]

When \(\sigma < c_G - c_C\), this difference is negative and constant—independent of the population state. Therefore, governance contribution is strictly dominated by content contribution at every state. In the replicator dynamics, this implies:

\[ \dot{y} = y(\pi_G - \bar{\pi}) < y(\pi_C - \bar{\pi}) = \dot{x}/x \cdot y \]

More directly, in any population containing both \(G\) and \(C\) types, the ratio \(y/x\) satisfies \(\frac{d}{dt}\ln(y/x) = \pi_G - \pi_C = \sigma - (c_G - c_C) < 0\), so \(y/x\) decreases exponentially. The governance fraction shrinks relative to the content fraction, and since the quality modulator \(h(y) = y/(y + \kappa)\) requires positive governance for any value to be sustained, the commons eventually collapses.

■

4.4 Connecting BMPs to the Governance Premium

The governance trap condition \(\sigma < c_G - c_C\) provides a lens through which to evaluate the governance-oriented BMPs in Mechanism 1. Each pattern can be understood as a mechanism for increasing \(\sigma\):

BMPMechanism for Increasing \(\sigma\)Type of Premium
Community governanceDecision-making power over shared resourcePower \(\sigma\)
Adopt a stewardship roleReputational returns, career signalingReputational \(\sigma\)
Revenue sharingDirect monetary returns from governanceFinancial \(\sigma\)
Open business modelAccess to partner networks, business developmentStrategic \(\sigma\)
Pay what you wantCommunity validation, social recognitionSocial \(\sigma\)

Proposition 4.1 (Minimum Viable Governance Premium)

For a community-based business model to be sustainable, the aggregate governance premium must satisfy:

\[ \sigma_{\text{total}} = \sigma_{\text{power}} + \sigma_{\text{rep}} + \sigma_{\text{fin}} + \sigma_{\text{strat}} + \sigma_{\text{social}} \geq c_G - c_C \]

The practical implication is that Mechanism 1 requires stacking multiple governance-oriented BMPs to achieve the minimum viable premium. A community relying on social recognition alone (\(\sigma_{\text{social}}\)) will fail if \(\sigma_{\text{social}} < c_G - c_C\); it must combine recognition with revenue sharing, decision-making power, or reputational returns.

Critical Assessment 4.1

This formalization assumes that different types of governance premiums are additively separable. In practice, they may be complementary (revenue sharing becomes more attractive when combined with decision-making power) or substitutive (financial premiums may crowd out intrinsic motivation). The additive model is a first approximation that should be tested empirically. Furthermore, the costs \(c_G\) and \(c_C\) are treated as homogeneous across participants, when in reality they vary substantially with individual capabilities, opportunity costs, and organizational affiliation.

4.5 Interior Equilibrium Analysis

When \(\sigma = c_G - c_C\) exactly, an interior fixed point exists where \(\pi_C = \pi_G = \pi_F\). From \(\pi_C = \pi_F\):

\[ (\varphi - 1) \cdot V(x^*, y^*) = c_C \] \[ \Longrightarrow V(x^*, y^*) = \frac{c_C}{\varphi - 1} \]

This defines an iso-value curve in the \((x, y)\) space along which the interior equilibrium must lie. The equation \(V_0(1 + \beta x)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{y}{y+\kappa} = \frac{c_C}{\varphi - 1}\) implicitly defines a relationship between \(x^*\) and \(y^*\). For the interior equilibrium to be meaningful, we additionally need the constraint \(x^* + y^* < 1\) (positive free-rider fraction) and the iso-value curve must intersect the simplex interior.

Corollary 4.1 (Minimum Community Value)

A necessary condition for sustained content contribution is \(V(x,y) > \frac{c_C}{\varphi - 1}\). Substituting the coupled value function:

\[ V_0 (1 + \beta x)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{y}{y + \kappa} > \frac{c_C}{\varphi - 1} \]

This establishes a joint threshold on content activity \(x\), governance quality \(y\), base value \(V_0\), and anti-rivalry strength \(\beta\). Higher anti-rivalry (\(\beta\)) lowers the threshold, making community-based business models viable at smaller scales. This explains why strongly anti-rival goods (software, data) more readily support open-source business models than weakly anti-rival goods.

5. Model III: The Subsidization–Transparency Game

Mechanism 2 (“Subsidizing Sharing-Related Rival Costs to Allow Anti-Rival Sharing”) encompasses 8 BMPs including “Hidden revenue,” “Free,” “Sponsorship,” and three social mission patterns. This mechanism creates an asymmetric game between platforms and users.

5.1 The Platform–User Asymmetric Game

Definition 5.1 (Subsidization Game)

A platform (\(P\)) subsidizes anti-rival sharing and extracts information rent. Users (\(U\)) choose their level of engagement. The platform chooses a transparency level \(\tau \in [0, 1]\).

  • \(\tau = 1\): Full transparency about data extraction and monetization.
  • \(\tau = 0\): Complete opacity (hidden revenue).

The payoffs are:

\[ \pi_P(\tau) = (1 - \tau) \cdot I(n) + \tau \cdot T(n) - c_{\text{sub}} \cdot n \]

where \(I(n)\) is information rent (decreasing in \(\tau\) because transparency reduces extractable surplus), \(T(n)\) is trust-based revenue (increasing in \(\tau\)), and \(c_{\text{sub}}\) is the per-user subsidy cost.

User participation depends on perceived fairness:

\[ n(\tau) = n_0 \cdot \left[\alpha + (1 - \alpha) \cdot \tau^{\psi}\right] \]

where \(\alpha \in (0,1)\) is the baseline participation rate (users who join regardless of transparency) and \(\psi > 0\) controls transparency sensitivity. The anti-rival value is:

\[ V_a(n(\tau)) = V_0 \cdot n(\tau)^{\beta} \]

The platform faces a tradeoff: higher transparency increases participation \(n\) (boosting anti-rival value) but reduces information rent per user. The manuscript’s transparency challenge is thus formalized as an optimization problem:

\[ \tau^* = \arg\max_{\tau \in [0,1]} \; \pi_P(\tau) = \arg\max_{\tau \in [0,1]} \; \left[(1-\tau) \cdot I(n(\tau)) + \tau \cdot T(n(\tau)) - c_{\text{sub}} \cdot n(\tau)\right] \]

5.2 Evolutionary Dynamics of Transparency

In a population of platforms choosing different transparency levels, we discretize into two strategies: \(H\) (High transparency, \(\tau_H\)) and \(O\) (Opaque, \(\tau_O\)). Let \(p\) be the fraction of high-transparency platforms.

\[ \pi_H = \tau_H \cdot T(n_H) + (1-\tau_H) \cdot I(n_H) - c_{\text{sub}} \cdot n_H \] \[ \pi_O = \tau_O \cdot T(n_O) + (1-\tau_O) \cdot I(n_O) - c_{\text{sub}} \cdot n_O \]

where \(n_H > n_O\) (transparent platforms attract more users). The key interaction effect is that as more platforms become transparent (\(p\) increases), users develop higher expectations, so the participation penalty for opaque platforms increases:

\[ n_O(p) = n_0 \cdot \alpha \cdot (1 - \lambda p), \qquad \lambda \in (0, 1) \]

This creates negative frequency dependence for opaque strategies when transparency becomes the norm: opaque platforms lose users as the market becomes more transparency-conscious. Under these dynamics, the system can exhibit a tipping point at \(p^*\), below which opacity dominates and above which transparency dominates.

Remark 5.1 (Connection to the Original Manuscript)

The manuscript identifies the “transparency challenge” as the central tension in Metamechanism 2: how to maintain fair and transparent business practices without jeopardizing the leverage mechanics. Our model shows that this is not merely an ethical concern but a strategic one: the optimal transparency level is endogenous to the market composition, and there exist parameter regimes where full transparency is evolutionarily stable (when trust-based revenue sufficiently exceeds information rent) and regimes where opacity persists (when information rent extraction is sufficiently profitable relative to trust effects).

6. Model IV: De-Transactionalization and Anti-Rivalry Amplification

Mechanism 5 (“Enabling Anti-Rivalry through De-Transactionalization”) contains 4 BMPs: flat rate, subscription, membership, and license. These patterns exemplify Transform’s first structural goal—minimizing the limitations that rival resources set for anti-rival sharing. They achieve this by decoupling individual consumption events from payment, thereby removing per-use friction (a rival-resource limitation) that inhibits anti-rival sharing. Simultaneously, they address Transform’s second structural goal—minimizing the effect of anti-rival sharing on rival resource usage—by converting variable costs into fixed fees, so that increased sharing does not proportionally increase the rival resource (payment) burden on users.

6.1 Transactional vs. De-Transactional Value Capture

Definition 6.1 (Anti-Rivalry Amplification through De-Transactionalization)

Under a transactional model, each use incurs a price \(p\), creating an effective anti-rivalry parameter:

\[ \beta_{\text{trans}} = \beta_0 - \frac{p}{V_0} \]

where \(\beta_0\) is the intrinsic anti-rivalry of the good and the subtracted term captures the friction that per-use pricing introduces (each transaction is a micro-enclosure that reduces the effective sharing effect). Under a de-transactional model (subscription/flat-rate), the per-use price is zero and the full anti-rivalry is expressed:

\[ \beta_{\text{sub}} = \beta_0 \]

The effective anti-rivalry determines the value function experienced by users:

\[ V_{\text{trans}}(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{\beta_{\text{trans}}}, \qquad V_{\text{sub}}(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{\beta_0} \]

Since \(\beta_{\text{sub}} > \beta_{\text{trans}}\) (for \(p > 0\)), subscription models unlock strictly more anti-rival value. However, they also shift revenue risk: the firm must ensure sufficient subscribers to cover costs, whereas transactional models generate revenue proportional to usage.

6.2 Critical Subscription Mass

A subscription-based firm charges a flat fee \(f\) and serves \(n\) subscribers. Its revenue is \(n \cdot f\) while its cost is \(C_0 + c_m \cdot n^{\gamma}\) (fixed costs plus marginal costs that scale superlinearly due to infrastructure demands). The firm is viable when:

\[ n \cdot f > C_0 + c_m \cdot n^{\gamma} \]

For \(\gamma > 1\) (realistic for digital platforms with infrastructure costs), this defines a viability band \(n_{\min} < n < n_{\max}\) rather than a simple minimum. The lower bound \(n_{\min}\) is the critical subscription mass; below it, revenue fails to cover costs. The upper bound \(n_{\max}\) reflects infrastructure limits. This connects directly to the coupled value function: at \(n_{\max}\), the rivalrous complement (infrastructure) becomes the binding constraint.

Proposition 6.1 (De-Transactionalization as a Bifurcation Parameter)

In the two-strategy game between Transactional (\(T\)) and De-Transactional (\(D\)) business models, the effective anti-rivalry shift \(\Delta\beta = \beta_0 - \beta_{\text{trans}} = p/V_0\) acts as a bifurcation parameter. For small \(\Delta\beta\) (low per-use prices or high base value), the competitive advantage of de-transactionalization is marginal. As \(\Delta\beta\) increases, the coordination game between transactional and de-transactional models exhibits a declining tipping point, making de-transactional models viable at smaller critical masses.

7. The Incompatibility Theorem

The original manuscript contains a crucial design insight, stated qualitatively: business model patterns from different complexity management approaches (leveraging vs. decreasing) “should be combined into a single business model only with caution” because patterns seeking complexity leverage combined with those seeking complexity decrease can result in a system that “loses the ability to seek complexity leverage.” We now provide a formal statement and proof.

7.1 The Interface Decoupling Effect

Transform strategies (Metamechanism 3) manage the rival–anti-rival interface by reducing the coupling between rival and anti-rival components. De-transactionalization decouples payment from consumption; digitalization decouples physical constraints from digital sharing; parallel offerings decouple premium rival services from the anti-rival base. This decoupling achieves Transform’s dual goals—minimizing rival limitations on anti-rival sharing and minimizing anti-rival effects on rival resource usage—but it also attenuates the network amplification that full coupling would enable. By reducing the interface between rival and anti-rival sides, Transform strategies limit the channels through which anti-rival sharing can generate superlinear returns.

Definition 7.1 (Interface Decoupling Coefficient)

Let \(d \in [0, 1]\) represent the degree to which a Transform-type BMP decouples the rival and anti-rival components of a business model. The effective anti-rivalry under decoupling is:

\[ \beta_{\text{eff}}(d) = \beta_0 \cdot (1 - d) \]

Full decoupling (\(d = 1\)) eliminates the network amplification channel, reducing the good to non-rivalrous (\(\beta_{\text{eff}} = 0\)); no decoupling (\(d = 0\)) preserves full anti-rivalry but also full bidirectional interference. Note that Transform strategies operate at intermediate values of \(d\): they seek to minimize (not eliminate) bidirectional interference, so \(d \in (0, 1)\) rather than \(d = 1\). This linear attenuation is a simplification; the qualitative results hold for any monotonically decreasing relationship between decoupling and effective anti-rivalry.

The crucial distinction from simple enclosure is that decoupling is bidirectional: it reduces both (i) the limitations rival resources impose on anti-rival sharing, and (ii) the effects of anti-rival sharing on rival resource usage. This bidirectional reduction is structurally different from merely gating access (which would be a Restrict strategy). Transform strategies are not seeking to exclude users; they are seeking to simplify the interface so that rival and anti-rival components can coexist with less institutional friction.

7.2 Formal Statement

Theorem 7.1 (Incompatibility of Leveraging and Decreasing Patterns)

Consider a business model that combines a fraction \(w\) of leverage-seeking BMPs and a fraction \((1-w)\) of synergy-seeking BMPs, where \(w \in [0, 1]\). The synergy-seeking patterns impose an interface decoupling coefficient \(d > 0\) that attenuates the coupling between rival and anti-rival components. The effective payoff of the mixed configuration is:

\[ \pi_{\text{mix}}(w) = w \cdot \varphi_L \cdot V_0 \cdot n^{\beta_0(1-d(1-w))} \cdot h(y) + (1-w) \cdot \varphi_T \cdot V_0 \cdot n^{\beta_0(1-d(1-w))} \cdot h_T - w \cdot c_L - (1-w) \cdot c_T \]

The decoupling coefficient applies to the shared interface because within a single product line, the degree of coupling between rival and anti-rival components cannot be simultaneously high (as Leverage requires) and low (as Transform requires). The decoupling introduced by Transform patterns attenuates the network amplification channels that Leverage patterns depend on for superlinear returns.

Define the incompatibility cost:

\[ I(w) = w \cdot \varphi_L \cdot V_0 \cdot n^{\beta_0} \cdot h(y) - w \cdot \varphi_L \cdot V_0 \cdot n^{\beta_0(1-d(1-w))} \cdot h(y) \] \[ = w \cdot \varphi_L \cdot V_0 \cdot h(y) \cdot \left[n^{\beta_0} - n^{\beta_0(1-d(1-w))}\right] \]

For \(n > 1\), \(\beta_0 > 0\), and \(d > 0\), we have \(n^{\beta_0} > n^{\beta_0(1-d(1-w))}\) whenever \(w < 1\). Therefore \(I(w) > 0\) for all \(w \in (0, 1)\), and \(I(0) = I(1) = 0\).

The source of incompatibility is structural: Leverage requires tight coupling (connecting rival and anti-rival sides for mutual optimization), while Transform requires loose coupling (minimizing bidirectional interference). The Leverage component suffers a value loss because the decoupling introduced by the Transform component attenuates the network amplification that the Leverage strategy depends on. Symmetrically, the Transform component suffers because the coupling amplified by the Leverage component increases the bidirectional interference that Transform seeks to minimize. This loss is zero only at the pure strategies (\(w = 0\) or \(w = 1\)).

Proof (Strict Dominance of Pure Strategies)

We show that for sufficiently large \(n\) (i.e., for business models at scale), the mixed strategy is dominated by at least one pure strategy. Consider the payoff difference between pure leverage (\(w = 1\)) and mixed (\(w \in (0,1)\)):

\[ \pi_L - \pi_{\text{mix}}(w) = \varphi_L V_0 h(y) \left[n^{\beta_0} - n^{\beta_0(1-d(1-w))}\right] \cdot (1) + (1-w)(c_T - c_L) - (1-w) \varphi_T V_0 n^{\beta_0(1-d(1-w))} h_T \]

The first term (value gain from restoring full coupling) grows as \(n^{\beta_0}\), while the third term (lost transform-component revenue) grows as \(n^{\beta_0(1-d(1-w))}\) with a strictly smaller exponent. Therefore, for sufficiently large \(n\):

\[ \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\pi_L - \pi_{\text{mix}}(w)}{n^{\beta_0}} = \varphi_L h(y) \left[1 - n^{-\beta_0 d(1-w)}\right] > 0 \]

Hence \(\pi_L > \pi_{\text{mix}}(w)\) for sufficiently large \(n\). A symmetric argument shows that for sufficiently small \(n\) (below the critical mass for leverage strategies), \(\pi_T > \pi_{\text{mix}}(w)\) because the leverage component incurs coupling costs without sufficient scale for the network amplification to justify them.

■

Corollary 7.1 (Evolutionary Elimination of Mixed Strategies)

In the replicator dynamics with a continuum of strategies indexed by \(w \in [0,1]\), interior strategies (\(0 < w < 1\)) are driven to extinction. The population converges to a mixture of pure leveraging (\(w = 1\)) and pure transforming (\(w = 0\)) firms, with the equilibrium mixture determined by the anti-rivalry level \(\beta_0\) and the community scale \(n\).

Critical Assessment 7.1

This theorem relies on the assumption that the decoupling effect is global within a product line—that the interface simplification pursued by Transform patterns is incompatible with the interface amplification pursued by Leverage patterns when they share the same digital good. This assumption is defensible when the patterns target the same rival–anti-rival interface (e.g., a firm attempting to both tightly couple community development with infrastructure optimization and decouple consumption from infrastructure costs within the same product). It is less applicable when the patterns target different interfaces within a diversified business model, or when the rival and anti-rival components are sufficiently separable that one interface can be coupled while another is decoupled. The theorem should therefore be interpreted as applying within a single product line sharing a common rival–anti-rival interface, not necessarily across an entire conglomerate.

A further limitation: the theorem is asymptotic (it holds “for sufficiently large \(n\)”). At intermediate scales, the mixed strategy may be locally optimal as a transitional configuration. This suggests that firms may rationally adopt mixed strategies during the growth phase before converging to a pure approach at scale—a prediction that could be tested empirically.

Finally, note that the linear attenuation model \(\beta_{\text{eff}}(d) = \beta_0(1-d)\) is a simplification of Transform’s dual minimization goals. In practice, Transform strategies may achieve their goals asymmetrically—minimizing rival limitations on anti-rival sharing more effectively than minimizing anti-rival effects on rival usage, or vice versa. A richer model would use separate parameters for each direction of interference, potentially revealing parameter regimes where partial mixing is viable.

8. Phase Transitions in Business Model Landscapes

Module 9 demonstrates that the anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\) acts as a bifurcation parameter in contribution games, driving phase transitions from Prisoner’s Dilemma through Coordination Game to Harmony Game as \(\beta\) increases. We extend this analysis to the business model landscape.

8.1 \(\beta\) as a Technology-Driven Bifurcation Parameter

The anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\) is not a firm-level choice variable—it is determined by the nature of the digital good and the technological infrastructure. As technology evolves (cloud computing, open APIs, blockchain, AI), the intrinsic anti-rivalry of digital goods tends to increase: more goods become shareable, replicable, and value-additive with use. We can therefore treat \(\beta\) as an exogenous, secularly increasing parameter and study the induced transitions in the business model landscape.

8.2 Phase Structure

Consider the two-strategy game between Leverage (\(L\)) and Restrict (\(R\)) from Theorem 3.2. The fitness difference at the all-Leverage state is:

\[ \Delta\pi(1; \beta) \equiv \pi_L(1; \beta) - \pi_R(1; \beta) = (\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_0 (1+\beta_L)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{1}{1+\kappa_G} - (c_L + R_0) \]

Define \(\beta^*\) as the critical anti-rivalry level at which \(\Delta\pi(1; \beta^*) = 0\):

\[ (1 + \beta_L^*)^{\eta} = \frac{(c_L + R_0)(1 + \kappa_G)}{(\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_0} \] \[ \beta_L^* = \left[\frac{(c_L + R_0)(1 + \kappa_G)}{(\varphi_L + \delta) \cdot V_0}\right]^{1/\eta} - 1 \]

Theorem 8.1 (Business Model Phase Transitions)

As the anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\) increases from 0, the business model landscape undergoes the following phase transitions:

Phase I (\(\beta < \beta_{\min}\)): Anti-rivalry is too weak to sustain any anti-rival-friendly strategy. All-Restrict is the unique stable equilibrium. The business landscape is dominated by traditional proprietary business models (compartmentalization and defiance patterns).

Phase II (\(\beta_{\min} < \beta < \beta^*\)): Transform strategies become viable but require critical mass. The dynamics are bistable: both All-Restrict and All-Transform are locally stable, separated by an unstable tipping point \(x^*_T(\beta)\) that decreases with \(\beta\). Markets can support either traditional or transform-based business models depending on history.

Phase III (\(\beta^* < \beta < \beta_{\max}\)): Leverage strategies become viable with sufficient critical mass. The landscape is now tri-stable: All-Restrict, All-Transform, and All-Leverage can each be locally stable. The tipping point for Leverage, \(x^*_L(\beta)\), decreases with \(\beta\), while the All-Restrict basin of attraction shrinks.

Phase IV (\(\beta > \beta_{\max}\)): Anti-rivalry is sufficiently strong that All-Leverage dominates. The all-Restrict state becomes unstable. All-Transform remains locally stable but with a shrinking basin of attraction relative to All-Leverage.

Remark 8.1 (Empirical Correlates)

These phases have observable empirical correlates. Phase I describes the pre-internet economy where most goods were rival. Phase II describes the early internet era: firms began restructuring their offerings to minimize the interference between rival and anti-rival components (Transform strategies—e.g., de-transactionalizing through subscription models, digitalizing physical offerings), but traditional models remained dominant. Phase III describes the current era: firms are building complex governance structures that leverage the coupling between rival and anti-rival components (Leverage strategies—e.g., community-driven development where shared contributions optimize each participant’s resource usage), but these approaches still require ecosystem coordination to sustain governance quality. Phase IV describes a hypothetical future where the symbiosis-seeking approach dominates because anti-rivalry is strong enough that the returns from tight coupling justify the governance costs. The phase transition analysis predicts that the transition from Phase III to Phase IV is discontinuous—once the tipping point is reached, the shift to leverage-dominant markets is rapid.

8.3 The Transform-to-Leverage Transition

A particularly important dynamic occurs within the anti-rival-friendly strategies: the competitive interaction between synergy-seeking (Transform) and symbiosis-seeking (Leverage) approaches, each representing a structurally distinct way of managing the rival–anti-rival interface. On the \(L\)–\(T\) edge of the simplex, the fitness difference is:

\[ \pi_L - \pi_T = \varphi_L \cdot V_a \cdot h(x_L) - c_L - \varphi_T \cdot V_a \cdot h_T(x_L, x_T) + c_T \]

At the All-Transform vertex (\(x_T = 1, x_L = 0\)), Leverage can invade only if governance can be bootstrapped—but \(h(0) = 0\), so Leverage cannot invade through infinitesimal perturbation. Conversely, at the All-Leverage vertex (\(x_L = 1, x_T = 0\)), Transform can invade when:

\[ \varphi_T \cdot V_0(1+\beta_L)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{\alpha_T}{\alpha_T + \kappa_G} - c_T > \varphi_L \cdot V_0(1+\beta_L)^{\eta} \cdot \frac{1}{1+\kappa_G} - c_L \]

This simplifies to:

\[ c_L - c_T > V_0(1+\beta_L)^{\eta} \left[\frac{\varphi_L}{1+\kappa_G} - \frac{\varphi_T \alpha_T}{\alpha_T + \kappa_G}\right] \]

Transform invades the All-Leverage state when the cost advantage of Transform (\(c_L - c_T\)) exceeds the value advantage of Leverage’s stronger governance and anti-rival capture. For strongly anti-rival goods (\(\beta_L\) large), the right-hand side dominates and Transform cannot invade. For weakly anti-rival goods, the cost advantage dominates and Transform can invade. This generates a second bifurcation within the anti-rival strategy space.

On the \(L\)–\(R\) edge with these parameters, the tipping point equation becomes:

\[ F(x) = (1.8 + 0.5) \cdot 10 \cdot (1 + 0.4x) \cdot \frac{x}{x + 0.15} = 8 + 6 = 14 \] \[ 23 \cdot (1 + 0.4x) \cdot \frac{x}{x + 0.15} = 14 \]

9.4 Governance Trap Numerical Example

For the community governance model (Section 4), consider an open-source platform with:

  • \(\beta = 0.3\), \(V_0 = 10\), \(\varphi = 1.5\), \(\kappa = 0.1\)
  • \(c_C = 2\), \(c_G = 5\), \(\sigma = 1\)

Since \(\sigma = 1 < c_G - c_C = 3\), the governance trap condition is satisfied. No interior equilibrium exists. The governance fraction \(y\) declines monotonically, and the system follows the four-phase trajectory:

  1. Growth Phase: Community grows, both content and governance contributions appear, \(V(x,y)\) increases.
  2. Success Phase: High content quality attracts free-riders, content contribution remains viable (\(0.5 \cdot V > 2\) when \(V > 4\)).
  3. Burnout Phase: Governance contributors exit (\(y\) declining), quality begins to erode. The project appears healthy by surface metrics (user count, content volume) but governance debt accumulates.
  4. Crisis Phase: \(y\) falls below the threshold where \(h(y) \approx 0\), \(V \to 0\), content contribution is no longer viable, community collapses.

The minimum governance premium to avoid the trap is \(\sigma_{\min} = c_G - c_C = 3\). Achieving this requires stacking premiums from multiple BMPs (e.g., community governance providing power \(\sigma = 1\), revenue sharing providing financial \(\sigma = 1\), stewardship providing reputational \(\sigma = 1\)).

10. Discussion

10.1 Summary of Analytical Results

Our formalization yields several results that sharpen the original manuscript’s qualitative findings:

Result 1: Bistability and Critical Mass (Theorem 3.2). The all-Restrict state is always locally stable, implying that transitions to anti-rival business models require coordinated entry exceeding a critical mass threshold. This result formalizes the intuition that anti-rival business model innovation is an ecosystem-level challenge, not merely a firm-level one.

Result 2: Governance Trap Conditions (Theorem 4.1). Community-based business models (Mechanism 1, 15 BMPs) require a governance premium exceeding the cost differential between governance and content contribution. The 5 governance-oriented BMPs in Mechanism 1 can be interpreted as mechanisms for building this premium through different channels (power, reputation, financial, strategic, social).

Result 3: Incompatibility of Mixed Approaches (Theorem 7.1). At scale, business models mixing leverage-seeking and synergy-seeking patterns within a single product line are dominated by pure approaches. The structural incompatibility arises because Leverage requires tight coupling between rival and anti-rival components (connecting them for mutual optimization), while Transform requires loose coupling (minimizing bidirectional interference). These opposing interface requirements create an endogenous incompatibility cost that grows with scale.

Result 4: Phase Transitions (Theorem 8.1). Increasing anti-rivalry drives discontinuous shifts in the market-dominant business model. The phase structure reveals that Transform strategies (synergy-seeking, interface simplification) become viable at lower anti-rivalry thresholds than Leverage strategies (symbiosis-seeking, interface amplification), because the governance costs and coordination requirements of tight coupling are only justified when anti-rival returns are sufficiently strong. Both approaches remain independently viable as distinct equilibria; the dominance of Leverage at high anti-rivalry reflects the superlinear returns from full coupling, not an inherent inferiority of the Transform approach.

10.2 Prescriptive Implications

For business model innovators, these results suggest several prescriptions:

  1. Choose a pure interface approach at scale. The Incompatibility Theorem warns against attempting to simultaneously amplify and reduce the coupling between rival and anti-rival components within a single product line. A firm cannot both tightly connect rival and anti-rival sides for mutual optimization (Leverage) and minimize their bidirectional interference (Transform) on the same interface. Mixed strategies may be viable during the growth phase (when scale \(n\) is small and the asymptotic dominance of pure strategies does not yet bind), but firms should plan a transition to a pure approach as they scale.
  2. Stack governance premiums. Community-based business models (Mechanism 1) should not rely on a single source of governance premium. The minimum viable premium \(\sigma_{\min} = c_G - c_C\) is typically larger than any single premium source can provide. Combining community governance (power), revenue sharing (financial), and stewardship roles (reputational) increases the total premium and reduces governance trap risk.
  3. Coordinate ecosystem entry. The bistability result implies that individual firm entry is insufficient. Firms should seek industry-level coordination—through consortia, standards bodies, or platform ecosystems—to achieve the critical mass \(x^*\) required for leverage strategies to become self-sustaining.
  4. Monitor the governance ratio. The governance trap is insidious because it is invisible in surface metrics (user count, content volume remain high during the burnout phase). Business model designers should track the governance contribution fraction \(y\) and intervene when it falls below a sustainability threshold.

10.3 Limitations and Critical Assessment

We identify several limitations that constrain the applicability of our results:

Limitation 1: Homogeneous Agents. The model treats all firms within each strategy type as identical. In practice, firms differ dramatically in resources, capabilities, market position, and strategic intent. Heterogeneous-agent extensions would allow for richer dynamics, including the possibility that large incumbents and small entrants adopt different strategies optimally.

Limitation 2: Assumed Functional Forms. The power-law anti-rivalry function \(V(n) = V_0 n^{\beta}\), the Michaelis–Menten quality modulator, and the linear enclosure dampening are mathematically convenient but empirically unvalidated. Alternative functional forms (logistic saturation for anti-rivalry, threshold effects for governance quality) could yield qualitatively different results. We conjecture that the bistability and incompatibility results are robust to functional form, but the specific phase transition thresholds are not.

Limitation 3: Static versus Dynamic Strategies. The replicator dynamics assume firms are committed to fixed strategies. In practice, firms pivot, experiment, and learn. Agent-based models allowing strategy switching would better capture the messy reality of business model innovation.

Limitation 4: Exogenous Anti-Rivalry. We treat \(\beta\) as exogenous. In reality, firms invest in technologies and practices that increase or decrease the anti-rivalry of their offerings. Endogenizing \(\beta\) would create a co-evolutionary model where the technological parameter and the business model distribution evolve jointly.

Limitation 5: Neglect of Market Structure. The model assumes a well-mixed population of competing firms. Real markets have network effects, switching costs, multi-homing, geographic segmentation, and regulatory heterogeneity. These factors can create local optima that differ from the global analysis.

Limitation 6: Measurement Challenges. The parameters \(\varphi_L\), \(\delta\), \(\kappa_G\), and \(\sigma\) are conceptually clear but empirically difficult to measure. Empirical validation of the model would require operationalizing these parameters through observable proxies, which is a non-trivial econometric challenge.

10.4 Connections to Existing Literature

Our formalization connects to several established research streams:

Evolutionary Economics. Nelson & Winter’s (1982) framework treats firms as carriers of organizational routines that evolve through variation, selection, and retention. Our model extends this to business model configurations, with the anti-rivalry parameter creating a novel selection pressure not present in traditional evolutionary economics.

Platform Economics. The bistability result resonates with the platform economics literature on network effects and tipping points (Katz & Shapiro, 1985; Eisenmann et al., 2006). Our contribution is to show that the tipping point applies not only to platform adoption by users but also to business model adoption by firms within an ecosystem.

Commons Governance. The governance trap formalization extends Ostrom’s (1990) design principles to digital business model communities. The key difference from natural commons is that the governance challenge is not preventing overuse (the good is anti-rival) but sustaining the rivalrous complement that enables anti-rival value creation.

Institutional Complexity. Our phase transition analysis provides a dynamical systems interpretation of the institutional complexity framework (Thornton et al., 2012; Battilana & Lee, 2014). The coexistence of complexity-avoiding (Restrict), complexity-decreasing (Transform, synergy-seeking through interface simplification), and complexity-leveraging (Leverage, symbiosis-seeking through interface amplification) approaches—and the conditions under which each dominates—corresponds to phase transitions driven by the technological evolution of anti-rivalry.

10.5 Future Research Directions

The formalization opens several avenues for future work:

  1. Empirical calibration: Estimating model parameters from longitudinal data on business model adoption in specific digital markets (e.g., software, media, education).
  2. Agent-based simulation: Extending the analytical model with heterogeneous agents, spatial structure, and adaptive learning to study the robustness of the phase transition and incompatibility results.
  3. Multi-level dynamics: Modeling the co-evolution of within-firm BMP composition (micro-level) and market-level business model distribution (macro-level) using multi-level selection frameworks.
  4. Policy analysis: Using the model to evaluate the effects of intellectual property policy, open data mandates, and platform regulation on the speed and direction of business model transitions.
  5. Endogenous anti-rivalry: Co-evolutionary models where firms invest in changing \(\beta\) (through technology choices, licensing decisions, API design) while simultaneously choosing business model strategies.

11. Conclusion

This paper has demonstrated that the qualitative findings of the original manuscript on anti-rival business model complexity management can be placed on rigorous analytical foundations through evolutionary game theory. The formalization is not merely a mathematical re-statement of existing insights; it generates new results—the bistability of business model landscapes, the precise conditions for governance trap avoidance, the structural incompatibility of coupling-amplifying (Leverage) and coupling-reducing (Transform) approaches within a single product line at scale, and the phase transition structure of market evolution—that go beyond what qualitative analysis alone can deliver.

At the same time, we have been candid about the limitations of the approach. Mathematical models impose assumptions that may not hold, parameters that may not be measurable, and functional forms that may not be accurate. The value of the formalization lies not in its predictive precision but in its structural clarity: it makes the logical relationships between variables, strategies, and outcomes explicit and falsifiable.

The most consequential finding, in our assessment, is the bistability result. The all-Restrict state is always locally stable, meaning that even in environments where anti-rival business models would be globally optimal, the transition cannot occur through gradual, decentralized entry. This has deep implications for both theory and practice: it suggests that the persistence of traditional business models in increasingly anti-rival digital economies is not evidence of their optimality but evidence of a coordination failure. The tools for overcoming this coordination failure—ecosystem orchestration, industry consortia, policy intervention—are themselves the subject of further analysis.

Anti-rivalry is not a marginal modification of standard economic logic; it is a fundamental inversion. The evolutionary game-theoretic framework reveals just how deep this inversion runs: it transforms the selection pressures on business models, creates new instabilities and new equilibria, and demands institutional innovations that the market mechanism alone cannot supply.
