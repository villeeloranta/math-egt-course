The Anti-Rivalry Paradox: Scarcity Displacement, Business Model Disclosure, and Innovation Policy

Working Paper — Prepared for Research Policy

March 2026

Abstract

Digital goods that increase in value with use—anti-rival goods—present a fundamental paradox: the very property that makes them valuable (anti-rivalry) inevitably generates new scarcities when embedded in real-world business models. We identify this scarcity displacement as the core challenge of anti-rival goods and show that organizations face three directions for managing it. Restricting anti-rivalry (through DRM, paywalls, proprietary licensing) creates artificial scarcity of the anti-rival resource itself, generating societal welfare loss by forfeiting superlinear value creation. Enhancing anti-rivalry with firm subsidies (free services, ad-supported models, platform subsidization) creates dependency scarcity—scarcity of user agency, transparency, and control, as the subsidizing firm captures disproportionate leverage. Enhancing anti-rivalry with community organization (open source, commons governance, peer production) creates governance scarcity—scarcity of governance labor, curation capacity, and contribution incentives, risking tragedy of the commons. Drawing on a systematic analysis of 175 business model patterns (39 of which actively manage anti-rivalry), evolutionary game theory, and real-world cases, we formalize each direction’s viability conditions, failure modes, and welfare implications. We show that different contexts call for different directions, and that the most productive policy does not mandate a single path but ensures all three remain available as mutual checks. As a central policy contribution, we propose business model disclosure: requiring organizations to disclose the business model patterns they employ, the dependency structures these create, and the anti-rival compliance of their value creation logic. Such disclosure enables users, communities, and policymakers to identify scarcity displacement, assess dependency risks, and design targeted interventions. We develop criteria for anti-rival compliant business model patterns and show how disclosure creates the informational precondition for effective innovation policy.

Keywords: anti-rival goods; scarcity displacement; business model disclosure; innovation policy; platform governance; business model patterns; evolutionary game theory

1. Introduction

Digitalization has produced a class of goods that defy the logic of scarcity. Shared datasets improve with more contributors. Open-source software gains from every patch. Collaborative knowledge bases become more useful as they grow. These anti-rival goods—goods whose per-user value increases with the number of users (Cooper, 2006; Weber, 2004)—create value that is superlinear in participation. The economic logic points toward maximal sharing.

But sharing is never free. Every anti-rivalrous resource depends on rivalrous complements—server infrastructure, editorial curation, conflict resolution, maintainer attention—that must be paid for (Nikander, Eloranta, Karhu, & Hiekkanen, 2020). Markets, designed to allocate rival goods through price equilibrium, fail with anti-rival goods: artificial scarcity leads to underprovision, while zero marginal cost drives compensation toward zero (Bliss & Egler, 2020). This creates what we call the anti-rivalry paradox: the property that makes these goods most valuable—increasing returns to sharing—is precisely the property that generates new and often hidden forms of scarcity when organizations attempt to harness it.

This paper argues that the paradox is structural, not contingent. Anti-rivalry does not eliminate scarcity; it displaces it. Any organizational arrangement that engages with anti-rival goods must choose how to manage the rival costs that sharing creates, and each choice generates a distinct form of new scarcity. We identify three directions:

  1. Restrict anti-rivalry (per-unit pricing, DRM, paywalls): This avoids the rival-cost problem but creates artificial scarcity of the anti-rival resource itself—a societal welfare loss from forfeited superlinear value creation.
  2. Enhance anti-rivalry with firm subsidies (ad-supported free services, platform subsidization, freemium): The firm covers rival costs and extracts information rent, creating dependency scarcity—scarcity of user agency, transparency, and control.
  3. Enhance anti-rivalry with community organization (open source, commons governance, peer production): The community collectively covers rival costs, creating governance scarcity—scarcity of governance labor, contribution incentives, and curation capacity, with the attendant risk of tragedy of the commons.

Each direction has legitimate applications, and each generates real costs. The question is not which direction is universally superior but which direction fits which context—and what policy interventions are needed to ensure that the choice is made transparently rather than by default.

We develop this argument in four steps. First, drawing on a systematic review of 175 business model patterns (BMPs) from the literature (Eloranta & [Authors], 2026), of which 39 actively manage the rival–anti-rival tension, we map the empirical landscape onto the three-direction framework. Second, using evolutionary game theory and the coupled resource model from the anti-rivalry literature (Nikander et al., 2020), we formalize the viability conditions, failure modes, and welfare implications of each direction. Third, we analyze real-world cases—Android, Wikipedia, OpenSSL, Spotify, Linux, Mastodon, and others—to illustrate how scarcity displacement operates in practice, with both benefits and costs. Fourth, and as the paper’s central policy contribution, we propose business model disclosure: a regulatory framework requiring organizations to disclose the business model patterns they employ, the dependency structures these create, and the anti-rival compliance of their value creation logic.

The paper contributes to three literatures. To the emerging discourse on anti-rival goods (Bliss & Egler, 2020; Cooper, 2006; Kubiszewski, Farley, & Costanza, 2010; Weber, 2004), we offer a formal analysis of scarcity displacement as the mechanism by which anti-rival value creation generates new scarcities rather than eliminating scarcity altogether. To business model innovation research (Gassmann, Frankenberger, & Csik, 2014; Massa, Tucci, & Afuah, 2017), we provide a framework that explains why certain pattern combinations succeed or fail and introduces anti-rival compliance as a design criterion. To innovation policy (Blind, 2012; Edler & Fagerberg, 2017), we introduce business model disclosure as a policy instrument and identify context-dependent conditions under which each direction is welfare-maximizing.

2. Theoretical Background

2.1 Anti-rival goods and the failure of markets

The traditional economic taxonomy classifies goods along two dimensions: excludability and subtractability (Ostrom, 1990). Digital goods challenge this framework because they exhibit negative subtractability: rather than diminishing with consumption, they may increase in value when used (Weber, 2004). Cooper (2006) formalized this as anti-rivalry, and subsequent work has established a three-way classification: rival (value decreases with use), non-rival (value unchanged), and anti-rival (value increases with use) (Kubiszewski et al., 2010; Nikander et al., 2020).

We capture anti-rivalry with a single parameter \(\beta\) in the per-user value function \(V(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{\beta}\), where \(n\) is the number of users. When \(\beta < 0\), the good is rival; when \(\beta = 0\), non-rival; when \(\beta > 0\), anti-rival. This parameterization treats anti-rivalry as a continuous spectrum and allows us to study how organizational viability changes as \(\beta\) increases—which it does secularly as technology evolves (cloud infrastructure, open APIs, AI).

Markets allocate anti-rival goods poorly because price-based equilibrium requires scarcity (Bliss & Egler, 2020; Haeringer, 2018). Two market-compatible approaches exist, and both fail. First, artificial scarcity (DRM, paywalls) makes the good market-compatible but leads to underprovision and forfeits the superlinear value that anti-rivalry creates. Second, zero marginal reproduction cost drives the market-clearing price toward zero, under-compensating producers and threatening sustainability (Olleros, 2018). The increasing role of anti-rival goods in economic systems thus requires organizing structures beyond markets—what the institutional logic literature calls hybrid systems (Battilana & Lee, 2014).

2.2 Institutional complexity in hybrid systems

Hybrid systems that combine anti-rival and rival goods face institutional complexity: the logics governing their rival and anti-rival components are fundamentally incompatible (Greenwood, Raynard, Kodeih, Micelotta, & Lounsbury, 2011). Organizing rival goods follows the logic of scarcity—Pareto-optimal allocation through markets. Organizing anti-rival goods concerns the logic of abundance: marginal cost is not only zero but potentially negative (Daoud, 2018; Kubiszewski et al., 2010).

The institutional logic literature identifies four approaches to managing this complexity (Battilana & Lee, 2014; Kraatz & Block, 2008): compartmentalization (structural separation of conflicting logics), defiance (forcing one logic to dominate), aggregation (negotiating coexistence), and integration (creating a new shared logic). Daoud (2018) mapped these to social strategies for managing the tension between scarcity and abundance logics, establishing a direct theoretical bridge between institutional complexity management and the anti-rival goods discourse.

Our framework builds on this bridge but reframes the strategic landscape. The compartmentalization and defiance approaches correspond to what we call restriction—they suppress anti-rivalry to avoid the institutional tension. The aggregation and integration approaches correspond to enhancement—they embrace anti-rivalry and accept scarcity displacement. Within enhancement, we distinguish two paths based on who absorbs the rival costs: a subsidizing firm or an organizing community. This distinction is not merely organizational but generates qualitatively different scarcities, as we demonstrate below.

2.3 Business model patterns as the unit of analysis

We adopt the business model pattern (BMP) perspective, which treats business models as combinations of reproducible, configurable building blocks (Alexander, Ishikawa, & Silverstein, 1977; Gassmann et al., 2014). This approach, most comprehensively developed in Gassmann, Frankenberger, and Csik’s (2013) identification of 55 universal BMPs, allows detailed analysis of how organizations manage institutional complexity at the operational level (Ocasio & Radoynovska, 2016; Remane, Hanelt, Tesch, & Kolbe, 2017).

An earlier study by the present authors (Eloranta & [Authors], 2026) systematically reviewed BMP literature, gathering 175 patterns from 24 studies. The study identified seven complexity management mechanisms organized into three metamechanisms for managing the rival–anti-rival tension. Of the 175 patterns, 39 actively manage anti-rivalry through aggregation or integration approaches, while 109 use compartmentalization (76 patterns) or defiance (33 patterns)—suppressing anti-rivalry rather than harnessing it. The seven mechanisms were:

  1. Using communities to minimize rival costs (14 patterns: peer-to-peer, open source, crowdsourcing, community governance, crowdfunding, review systems, etc.)
  2. Subsidizing sharing-related rival costs (8 patterns: hidden revenue, free/ad-supported, sponsorship, social mission patterns, etc.)
  3. Shifting revenue focus to anti-rival results (3 patterns: performance-based contracting, guaranteed availability, result as a service)
  4. Creating parallel offerings (4 patterns: freemium, dual licensing, charging for changes, demand-oriented platform)
  5. De-transactionalization (4 patterns: subscription, flat rate, membership, license)
  6. Converting the resource for anti-rival fit (3 patterns: digitalization, sharing business, unbundling)
  7. Facilitating shared ownership (2 patterns: fractional ownership, no dividends)

The study also identified that each metamechanism faced a distinctive challenge: an accountability challenge for community-based mechanisms (preventing free-riding and ensuring active contribution), a transparency challenge for firm-subsidy mechanisms (maintaining fairness when the firm captures disproportionate anti-rival value), and a valuation dilemma for transformation mechanisms (balancing free and paid tiers). These challenges are precisely the displaced scarcities that our framework formalizes.

2.4 The coupled resource model

Anti-rivalry is never pure. Every anti-rivalrous resource depends on complementary resources that remain rivalrous. We formalize this through the coupled value function:

\[ V(n) = V_a(n) \cdot h(S_r(n)) \]

where \(V_a(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{\beta}\) is the anti-rivalrous value component (\(\beta > 0\)), \(S_r(n) = M / n^{\gamma}\) is the per-user availability of the rivalrous complement (\(M\) = total supply, \(\gamma > 0\) = demand scaling), and \(h(s) = s/(s + \kappa)\) is a quality modulator capturing how the rivalrous complement constrains effective value. The tension is immediate: \(dV_a/dn > 0\) but \(dS_r/dn < 0\)—anti-rivalrous value rises with participation while rivalrous complement availability falls.

This coupled model yields a carrying capacity result: per-user value is maximized at an optimal community size \(n^*\), beyond which the rivalrous drain outpaces the anti-rivalrous gain. The effective value of any commons is determined not by its anti-rivalrous potential alone but by the interaction between its anti-rivalrous value and the governance of its rivalrous complements. The three directions in our framework represent different strategies for managing this interaction.

3. Methods: Business Model Pattern Discovery

The business model patterns that form the empirical foundation for this paper were identified through a two-stage process: a systematic literature review (to gather BMPs from studies) and a qualitative content analysis (targeting the gathered BMPs). The review built a comprehensive set of BMPs related to hybrid systems involving anti-rival digital goods (Denyer & Tranfield, 2009; Page et al., 2021). We then applied qualitative content analysis to the results of the review (BMPs and their descriptions) to recognize the institutional complexity management approaches, mechanisms, and metamechanisms used in the patterns to benefit from the anti-rivalry of digital goods (Elo & Kyngäs, 2008; Hsieh & Shannon, 2005).

3.1 Literature review

Search strategy. Our review followed a predefined process to maintain transparency and rigor (Page et al., 2021). First, we conducted a literature search on existing BMP studies by using SciVerse Scopus and EBSCOhost Business Source Complete (results included until March 31, 2022). The terms “pattern” and “archetype” are used synonymously in the business model literature; hence, we used “business model patterns” and “business model archetypes” (and the same phrases in singular form), targeting the abstracts, titles, and keywords (in EBSCOhost: subject terms) of articles. We included only publications in peer-reviewed academic journals to ensure the reliability of our results. Furthermore, we included only articles written in English. The searches resulted in 73 studies from SciVerse Scopus and 47 from EBSCOhost. There were 40 duplicates.

Article selection process. We excluded the duplicates and screened the remaining abstracts (n = 80). Based on the abstracts, we excluded papers that clearly did not recognize BMPs (n = 13). We also excluded studies to which we had no access through our host university’s databases or publicly available platforms (n = 5). Altogether, abstract-based screening excluded 18 articles, and we were left with 62 papers. The second screening involved full-text articles. We excluded papers that: 1) did not focus on BMPs related to digital goods at all (n = 15); 2) did analyze digital goods-related BMPs but applied them solely to rival resource contexts (n = 7); 3) did not describe BMPs in enough detail to be further analyzed (n = 6); or 4) defined BMPs entirely differently than us (n = 11). In total, we excluded in the second screening 39 papers and were left with 23 studies. The screening was made by two independently working researchers, and the results were compared and revised if needed (Denyer & Tranfield, 2009).

Finally, we added Gassmann et al.’s (2013) article to the literature set. The 55-pattern set presented in that paper is the “most comprehensive collection of business model patterns” within the literature in the BMP research and practitioner domain (Remane et al., 2017: 7). Gassmann et al. (2013) also explicitly used the modular-combination approach to patterns that is appropriate for our study. After the entire selection process, we were left with 24 articles for synthesis.

Synthesizing the results. As the last stage of the literature review, each included article was further analyzed, aiming to form an overall view of the BMPs appearing in them. Altogether, we recognized 227 patterns (including duplicates). We removed potentially duplicate patterns as follows: 1) We extracted all the patterns to a table with pattern names and the author descriptions of them; and 2) we compared the results with the original patterns recognized in Gassmann et al.’s (2013) study by reading BMP descriptions and evaluating whether they correspond to Gassmann et al.’s (2013) patterns. Two researchers made the comparisons independently; results were compared and carefully revised as needed (Denyer & Tranfield, 2009). We noted i) patterns that were already included in (or were the same as) Gassmann et al.’s patterns, ii) patterns that were incrementally developed from the original Gassmann et al. patterns, and iii) entirely new patterns. Patterns duplicating Gassmann et al.’s (2013) (n = 46) were grouped and counted as one each. In the process, we also noted that the pattern set had duplicates with the patterns of Bocken, Short, Rana, and Evans (2014) (n = 6); those duplicates were also removed. The synthesis and removal of duplicates resulted in 175 patterns.

3.2 Qualitative content analysis

First stage. We used deductive and inductive qualitative content analyses to proceed with the detailed description of the recognized BMPs (Elo & Kyngäs, 2008; Hsieh & Shannon, 2005). We focused on how the description explains the institutional complexity management approach used in the BMP (in practice: how the BMP tries, in broad and specific levels, to benefit from the anti-rivalry of digital goods by managing institutional complexity).

Our analysis began by coding all the 175 patterns with predetermined codes (Elo & Kyngäs, 2008). Existing theory on institutional logic alignment approaches (Battilana & Lee, 2014) was used deductively to set a directed starting point (Elo & Kyngäs, 2008; Potter & Levine-Donnerstein, 1999). The data were thus analyzed from the perspective of institutional complexity management approaches: 1) compartmentalization: strive for logic avoidance, 2) defiance: seek defiance over other logic, 3) aggregation: find an adaptive solution, and 4) integration: innovate a shared logic (Battilana & Lee, 2014). All listed BMP descriptions were read through. Each pattern was assigned a code that tells the complexity management approach used with a short explanation. As the focus of our research was systems that want to intentionally benefit from the anti-rivalry of digital goods while acknowledging the finiteness of rival goods, we only focused on patterns that used the “aggregation” (n = 16) or “integration” (n = 23) approach. Thus, we omitted the patterns that we recognized to be seeking institutional complexity “compartmentalization” (not considering the interaction of anti-rival and rival goods) (n = 76) or “defiance” of one logic (forcing artificial scarcity onto digital goods or not considering rival good finiteness) (n = 33). We also excluded patterns that were focused solely on non-digital goods (n = 31).

Second stage. We then focused on the underlying concrete mechanisms and metamechanisms (mechanism categories) that instantiate the complexity management approach used in the BMP. At this stage, we moved toward inductive content analysis, allowing the mechanisms to flow from the BMP descriptions (Elo & Kyngäs, 2008). We used open coding to track the mechanisms from each BMP description. Each mechanism was given a code that best describes its operation. New mechanisms similar to each other were combined. The recognized mechanisms were further grouped into metamechanisms based on how they approach the rival–anti-rival dilemma at a broad level (Coffey & Atkinson, 1996; Elo & Kyngäs, 2008). Two researchers conducted the analysis, and disagreements were elaborated.

3.3 Methodological limitations

As in any research, this approach has certain limitations. First, our reliance on the classifications and descriptions of BMPs being provided by the literature might lead to misinterpretation or oversimplification. The implementations of the identified BMPs in business contexts may include nuances not fully captured in the academic descriptions. Second, our approach to identifying and categorizing institutional complexity management mechanisms and metamechanisms was inductive, relying on the depth and quality of descriptions provided in the original articles and on our subjective interpretation. We employed multiple researchers and an iterative method in our analysis to mitigate this issue. Third, as in any literature-based study, some relevant studies may have been inadvertently omitted due to our selection criteria, leading to significant BMPs not being captured in the analysis.

4. Findings: Mechanisms and Patterns for Managing Anti-Rivalry

The analysis revealed seven complexity management mechanisms and 39 related business model patterns that actively seek to benefit from the anti-rivalry of digital goods. We present each mechanism and its constituent patterns below, organized into three metamechanisms. The remaining 109 patterns (76 compartmentalization, 33 defiance) correspond to the “restrict” direction in our scarcity displacement framework (Section 5).

4.1 Metamechanism I: Large-scale collaboration to minimize individuals’ rival costs

This metamechanism facilitates the strategic utilization of communities to minimize the costs associated with rivalry while concurrently maximizing the value derived from sharing anti-rival goods. It ensures that the benefits accrued from anti-rival goods are equitably distributed among members of the community and proactively engenders an environment favorable to sharing and cooperation. The measures taken also instigate a shift toward decentralized governance structures, mitigating the risk of centralized coercion and inequalities regarding value sharing in the long run.

A prevalent challenge in large-scale collectives is how to ensure active contribution from community members to the rival resource pool and prevent free-riding on the contributions of others or else polluting or congesting the community with low-quality contributions (Mindel, Mathiassen, & Rai, 2018; Ostrom, 2009). This brings to light an accountability challenge: the development and implementation of coherent and consistent accounting systems for both rival resource usage and anti-rival output.

Mechanism 1: Using communities to minimize rival costs and maximize anti-rival value

Several BMPs sought anti-rivalry benefits by increasing community involvement in the digital good’s design, production, or governance and decentralizing the necessary rival resource costs. The scope of community involvement varied from networks of individuals to networks of firms. At heart, the idea most often was to organize as indicated in the peer-to-peer (Gassmann et al., 2013) and nodes (Curtis, 2021) patterns, in which actors collectively structure for a common purpose. The repurpose for society pattern highlighted how an existing business model can transition toward a community orientation (Bocken et al., 2014). Two patterns directly connected increased anti-rival digital good sharing in communities to improved quality of the good: increase quality through participation (Zeleti, Ojo, & Curry, 2016) and review system (Curtis, 2021) seek positive network effects through goods’ anti-rival characteristics and consider that such effects completely outweigh the related rival resource usage.

The crowdsourced innovation pattern (Weking, Stocker, Kowalkiewicz, Böhm, & Krcmar, 2020) focuses on using the power of crowds to come up with innovation-related contributions. The crowdsourcing pattern is more generally about relying on crowds for operational contributions (Curtis, 2021; Gassmann et al., 2013; Lüdeke-Freund et al., 2018; Yablonsky, 2017). Open source is a particular instantiation of crowdsourcing in technical development (Curtis, 2021; Gassmann et al., 2013; Zeleti et al., 2016), and crowdfunding (Curtis, 2021; Gassmann et al., 2013; Lüdeke-Freund et al., 2018) in rival resource cost decentralization.

The adopt a stewardship role (Bocken et al., 2014) and community governance (Curtis, 2021) patterns extend the community use to decision-making related to digital goods. Such governance arrangements can give actors the possibility of defining the product’s value themselves without inhibiting anti-rivalry, as the pattern pay what you want (Curtis, 2021; Gassmann et al., 2013) particularly highlights.

In the open business model, crowd collaboration reaches the industrial level; here, cocreating companies actively seek ways to partner to extend their business (Gassmann et al., 2013). In such constellations, revenue sharing is a prevailing pattern to enhance symbiotic effects (Gassmann et al., 2013). Further, know-how and available assets are shared in line with the pattern make more of it (Gassmann et al., 2013).

Mechanism 7: Facilitating shared ownership

The fractional ownership pattern aims to diminish rival resource constraints on anti-rival sharing by converting the ownership of a rival asset to a shared one among a group of stakeholders (Curtis, 2021; Gassmann et al., 2013; Lüdeke-Freund et al., 2018; Yablonsky, 2017). The no dividends pattern (Lüdeke-Freund et al., 2018) also enables investing economic surplus back into the jointly developed resource base. While ownership is a rival concept, this community-based ownership approach can help avoid scarcity-oriented issues, thereby managing rival–anti-rival tensions. In this multi-stakeholder approach, the assets provided by the business are subsequently “owned and managed by cooperative members”—where members can, for example, “be retail consumers, users of services, employees, suppliers, or the local community” (Lüdeke-Freund et al., 2018).

4.2 Metamechanism II: Seeking anti-rival leverage from rival investments

This metamechanism allows the capture of disproportionate benefits of rival resource investments through maximizing the sharing of anti-rival goods and the value generated by anti-rivalry. The category’s emphasis on leverage seeking taps into the inherent characteristics of anti-rival goods, such as their ability to be used simultaneously by multiple entities, usually without depletion (Weber, 2004). In result-focused models, rather than charging users based on their consumption of rival goods, a business is able to charge based on the anti-rival value or results the user derives from the resource’s usage.

The sought leverage creates tensions between providers and consumers regarding the perceived fairness of this approach. Consumers may question the fairness of the trade-off they are making. In the context of social media platforms, for instance, users might weigh the utility of the free networking service against the substantial amount of personal data they are offering in return. This metamechanism faces a transparency challenge: How can providers maintain transparent business practices without jeopardizing the mechanics of anti-rival utilization, which inherently thrive on disproportionate returns?

Mechanism 2: Subsidizing sharing-related rival costs to allow anti-rival sharing

In eight patterns, free sharing of anti-rival digital goods was allowed using rival resources of some other business unit or external actor. The motivation for such subsidization is that the party giving rival resources gains disproportionate benefits from the anti-rival digital good sharing. In the free as branded advertising (Zeleti et al., 2016), sponsorship (Zeleti et al., 2016), hidden revenue (Gassmann et al., 2013; Yablonsky, 2017), and free (Curtis, 2021) patterns, this means gaining information (an anti-rival digital good) related to customer sentiment or to product or service usage or to building a better brand image. In the one-sided social mission, two-sided social mission, and commercially utilized social mission patterns (Lüdeke-Freund et al., 2018), the anti-rival returns were connected to the social mission of the parties providing rival resources. A particular instance of these generic patterns was the experience broker, who accumulates actor know-how and mediates it to the social target groups who need such information (Lüdeke-Freund et al., 2018).

Mechanism 3: Shifting the revenue focus from rival resource usage to anti-rival results

Three patterns manage institutional complexity by shifting the revenue source focus from the rival resource usage to the actual business benefit created (which, in these cases, is perceived as anti-rival). Involved actors decide on what is mutually valuable; this functions as a jointly agreed metric of success. Such a logic leads to sustainable use of rival resources and allows maximizing the sharing of anti-rival resources. This approach was directly visible in the performance-based contracting (Gassmann et al., 2013; Yablonsky, 2017), guaranteed availability (Gassmann et al., 2013), and result as a service (Weking et al., 2020) patterns.

4.3 Metamechanism III: Asset and offering transformation toward anti-rivalry

With this metamechanism, the target is to improve the anti-rival compatibility of traditional business models through transforming offerings, revenue models, delivery channels, and the resources themselves. Such approaches allow anti-rivalry through an arrangement where the provision of goods or services is not coupled with each individual transaction. Businesses can also consider options for limited sharing, through offering both exclusive and free access to the product or service. Finally, the approach considers converting the resources and offerings at least partially to digital form to reach the anti-rival characteristics.

Business models built on this metamechanism allow only limited anti-rivalry. The models usually utilize tiered pricing schemes in which one version of a product or service is free of charge, increasing anti-rival dissemination, while the economic sustainability of the providers relies on customers paying for some added-value services. This creates a valuation dilemma for both customers and providers: customers consider whether the value of paid access is high enough in comparison to the free version, while providers must ensure that the free version does not cannibalize income from the paid version—but, at the same time, provides enough leeway for the anti-rival benefits to emerge.

Mechanism 4: Creating parallel offerings

Four BMPs aimed to decrease rival–anti-rival tension by developing parallel offerings that enable businesses to at least partially benefit from the anti-rival nature of digital goods while supporting rival resource costs. In the dual-licensing pattern (Zeleti et al., 2016), the business offered free data for noncommercial uses, along with a limited availability of useful free data to the public while charging for the commercial use of data (often of a higher quality and perceived value). Access to data can thereby be democratized, as the pattern demand-oriented platform highlights (Zeleti et al., 2016). A slight variation is the charging for changes pattern, in which some basic data and data-oriented services were provided freely to all customers, while customization and changes requested by the customer were charged for (Zeleti et al., 2016). More generically, this mechanism is also exhibited by the freemium BMP (Gassmann et al., 2013; Lüdeke-Freund et al., 2018; Weinberger, Bilgeri, & Fleisch, 2016; Yablonsky, 2017; Zeleti et al., 2016), in which a “generally smaller volume of paying ‘premium customers’ generate the revenue, which also cross-finances the free offering” (Gassmann et al., 2013).

Mechanism 5: Enabling anti-rivalry through de-transactionalization

We identified four BMPs that switched from transactional (exchange) models to those that support the additive utilization (sharing) of anti-rival digital goods. This was best elucidated by the flat rate BMP, which states that “a single fixed fee for a product or service is charged, regardless of actual usage or time restrictions on it” (Gassmann et al., 2013). In a broadly analogous manner, the subscription pattern (Curtis, 2021; Gassmann et al., 2013; Lüdeke-Freund et al., 2018; Yablonsky, 2017) deploys a similar logic: users are charged a “rolling fee typically on a monthly basis or annual basis,” irrespective of use (Lüdeke-Freund et al., 2018). Other BMPs that adopted this mechanism were membership (Curtis, 2021) and license (Gassmann et al., 2013).

Mechanism 6: Converting the resource for better fit with anti-rivalry

Three BMPs addressed rival–anti-rival tensions by the conversion of assets offered by the business to enable limited (excludable) forms of sharing. In these patterns, rival assets are developed into (somewhat) shareable assets that support anti-rival behavior. This is achieved through 1) converting physical assets into partially or entirely virtual ones and 2) providing value to users through allowing sharing, as opposed to exclusive ownership, of the assets. The conversion of physical assets is exemplified by the digitalization pattern (Gassmann et al., 2013), which relies on an organization’s ability “to turn existing products or services into digital variants” that can provide “advantages over tangible products, e.g., easier and faster distribution”. In turn, the sharing business (Lüdeke-Freund et al., 2018) and unbundling (Curtis, 2021) patterns highlight the possibilities of providing multi-party access to resources, and sharing them, as opposed to exclusive ownership.

The seven mechanisms and three metamechanisms provide the empirical foundation for the scarcity displacement framework developed in the following sections. Each metamechanism’s distinctive challenge—accountability (Metamechanism I), transparency (Metamechanism II), and valuation (Metamechanism III)—maps directly onto the three types of displaced scarcity that we formalize below.

5. The Core Paradox: Anti-Rivalry Creates New Scarcities

The central claim of this paper is that anti-rivalry does not eliminate scarcity but displaces it. Regardless of which organizational direction is chosen, new scarcities emerge as a direct consequence of engaging with anti-rival value creation. This is not a failure of organizational design but a structural property of anti-rival goods.

5.1 Why scarcity displacement is inevitable

The logic is straightforward. An anti-rival good becomes more valuable with more users (\(\beta > 0\)), which attracts more users, which increases the demand on rivalrous complements (infrastructure, curation, governance). These complements have finite supply and often face superlinear demand scaling (\(\gamma > 1\), as in Brooks’s Law for coordination overhead). The organization must respond to this growing demand for rivalrous complements, and every response creates scarcity somewhere:

  • If the organization restricts access to limit demand on complements, the anti-rival good itself becomes artificially scarce.
  • If a firm subsidizes the complements, users become dependent on the firm’s continued willingness and terms.
  • If a community organizes to provide complements, governance labor and contribution incentives become scarce.

There is no fourth option. The rivalrous costs are real, and someone must bear them. The choice of who bears them determines which scarcity is created.

5.2 Three types of displaced scarcity

Scarcity Displacement Framework

Direction 1 — Restrict: Artificial scarcity of the anti-rival resource. Welfare loss = \(\Delta W = V_0 \cdot N^{1+\beta} \cdot (1 - p^{k(1+\beta)})\), where \(N\) is the potential user population, \(k\) is the number of exclusion mechanisms, and \(p\) is the access probability per mechanism. For \(\beta > 0\), this loss is superlinear in \(N\): excluding users from an anti-rival good destroys not only their direct access but the value they would have added for everyone else.

Direction 2 — Firm subsidy: Dependency scarcity. The subsidizing firm chooses transparency \(\tau \in [0,1]\) and users trade agency for access. Scarcity manifests as opacity of data practices, lock-in through switching costs, algorithmic control of user experience, and unilateral terms changes. The displaced scarcity is concentrated—one entity holds leverage.

Direction 3 — Community: Governance scarcity. Governance is costlier than content creation (\(c_G > c_C\)). If the governance premium \(\sigma < c_G - c_C\), the governance-to-content ratio declines exponentially and the community enters a governance trap—growing content and users masking deteriorating governance. The displaced scarcity is diffuse—burnout, quality decline, and infrastructure decay.

The three scarcities differ in their visibility. Artificial scarcity (Direction 1) is the most visible—users directly experience paywalls and access restrictions. Dependency scarcity (Direction 2) is semi-visible—users experience it gradually as lock-in accumulates and alternatives diminish. Governance scarcity (Direction 3) is the least visible—surface metrics (user count, content volume, download statistics) remain healthy during the decline phase while governance erodes underneath. This asymmetry in visibility has profound implications for policy, which we develop in Sections 9 and 10.

5.3 The superlinear welfare loss of restriction

When an anti-rival resource is restricted through \(k\) exclusion mechanisms, each with access probability \(p\), the welfare loss has two components that distinguish it from the standard rival-good case:

  1. Direct loss: Excluded users lose access to the existing resource. This is the standard welfare loss from under-provision, familiar from rival goods.
  2. Anti-rivalry loss: Excluded users would have increased the resource’s value for all remaining users. This component is unique to anti-rival goods and, for strongly anti-rival resources (\(\beta\) large), dominates the direct loss.

Consider a gene-editing technique with \(\beta = 0.4\), fragmented by \(k = 12\) overlapping patents, each granting access with probability \(p = 0.9\). Only 28% of researchers gain full access (\(0.9^{12} \approx 0.28\)). The welfare ratio is \(0.28^{1.4} \approx 0.17\)—meaning 83% of potential value is destroyed. This superlinear loss is the fundamental argument against restriction for strongly anti-rival goods: the welfare cost of artificial scarcity grows faster than the resource’s potential value.

6. Three Directions for Managing the Paradox

6.1 Direction 1: Restrict anti-rivalry

Restriction treats the anti-rival good as if it were rival. Per-unit pricing, digital rights management, paywalls, proprietary licensing, and artificial scarcity all fall under this category. These correspond to the 109 BMPs identified in the compartmentalization and defiance categories from the original BMP study—they avoid the rival–anti-rival tension by suppressing anti-rivalry.

When restriction is appropriate. Restriction is viable and even welfare-maximizing when \(\beta\) is low—when the good is only weakly anti-rival. In this regime, the superlinear value from open sharing is modest, and the welfare cost of artificial scarcity is small relative to the benefit of stable financing through per-unit revenue. Restriction is also appropriate when the rivalrous complements are expensive and no feasible subsidy or community mechanism exists to cover them—such as early-stage pharmaceutical development, where the rivalrous costs (clinical trials, regulatory compliance) vastly exceed any anti-rival value from shared access to compound data.

When restriction fails. As \(\beta\) increases, the welfare cost of restriction grows superlinearly while the cost of enhancement mechanisms may remain constant or decline (due to technology-driven reductions in coordination costs). Beyond an invasion threshold \(\beta_{\text{inv}}\), enhancement strategies can invade the all-restrict equilibrium unilaterally—a single firm or community adopting enhancement captures enough anti-rival value to compensate for the rival costs. The practical implication is that restriction becomes increasingly unsustainable as technology makes sharing cheaper and more valuable.

Case: Academic Publishing

Academic research articles are strongly anti-rival (\(\beta\) high): each citation, replication, and application increases the value of the original finding. Yet the dominant business model restricts access through paywalls and subscription fees. The welfare loss is substantial: publicly funded research is locked behind private access barriers, and the anti-rivalry loss (foregone citations, replications, and applications by excluded researchers) compounds over time. The open access movement represents an attempt to shift from Direction 1 to Directions 2 or 3—with Article Processing Charges (author pays) representing the firm-subsidy path and preprint archives (arXiv, bioRxiv) representing the community path. The persistence of the restriction model despite its welfare costs illustrates how institutional inertia and established revenue streams can sustain suboptimal equilibria.

Pros of restriction

  • Stable revenue for publishers
  • Quality assurance via peer review bundled with access
  • Clear intellectual property rights

Cons of restriction

  • Massive welfare loss from excluding researchers
  • Publicly funded research privately gated
  • Anti-rivalry loss compounds over time
  • Widening research inequality between institutions

6.2 Direction 2: Enhance with firm subsidies — dependency scarcity

In this direction, a firm subsidizes rival costs, maximizes participation in the anti-rival resource, and extracts value through mechanisms other than per-unit access fees. This corresponds to the metamechanism “seeking anti-rival leverage from rival investments” from the original BMP study, which includes the mechanisms of subsidizing sharing (hidden revenue, free/ad-supported, sponsorship) and shifting revenue toward anti-rival results (performance-based contracting, result as a service).

The original study identified a “transparency challenge” for this path. Our framework formalizes this challenge. The subsidizing firm faces a structural tension between two revenue sources. Information rent \(I(n)\) requires opacity—the firm’s ability to extract value from user data, attention, and behavior depends on users not fully understanding or being able to replicate the extraction mechanism. Trust-based revenue \(T(n)\) requires transparency—users participate more when they understand and trust the platform. The firm chooses transparency \(\tau \in [0,1]\), and participation responds:

\[ n(\tau) = n_0[\alpha + (1-\alpha)\tau^{\psi}] \]

where \(\alpha\) is the captive user fraction (users who remain regardless of transparency, due to network effects or switching costs) and \(\psi\) governs the transparency-responsiveness of the remaining users.

The dependency scarcity. Users in the firm-subsidy path trade agency for access. The displaced scarcity manifests in several forms: opacity of data collection and monetization practices; algorithmic curation that serves the firm’s extraction objectives rather than user interests; platform-specific lock-in through proprietary formats, accumulated data, and social graph control; and unilateral terms changes that users cannot meaningfully resist. This scarcity is concentrated—a single entity holds the leverage—which makes it qualitatively different from the diffuse governance scarcity of the community path.

Case: Google Android

Android exemplifies the firm-subsidy direction at massive scale. Google provides the Android operating system as an open-source project (AOSP), subsidizing the substantial rival costs of OS development, security maintenance, and hardware compatibility testing. The anti-rival value is immense: every app developed for Android benefits all Android users, and every user attracts more developers. Google covers the rival costs and extracts value through the Google Mobile Services (GMS) suite—mandatory licensing of Play Store, Search, Maps, and other services that embed Google’s data collection and advertising business model. The dependency scarcity is concrete: device manufacturers who want GMS must accept Google’s terms (the Mobile Application Distribution Agreement), including default search engine placement and pre-installed apps. Users gain access to a massive anti-rival ecosystem but cede control over their data practices and default services to Google.

Pros of firm subsidy

  • Massive participation: 3+ billion active devices
  • Rich anti-rival ecosystem: millions of apps
  • Low entry cost for users and manufacturers
  • Continuous investment in infrastructure

Cons (dependency scarcity)

  • Manufacturers locked into GMS terms
  • User data monetized with limited transparency
  • Google controls app distribution and payment terms
  • Forked alternatives (e.g., Huawei) lose ecosystem value
  • EU antitrust action (2018: €4.34B fine) over bundling practices

Case: Spotify

Spotify subsidizes music streaming through a freemium model: the ad-supported free tier maximizes anti-rival participation (more listeners improve recommendation algorithms, playlist curation, and artist discovery), while the premium tier generates subscription revenue. The rival costs are substantial—licensing fees, server infrastructure, content delivery networks. The dependency scarcity affects multiple stakeholders: artists become dependent on Spotify’s opaque per-stream payment calculations; listeners become dependent on Spotify’s algorithmic recommendations, which optimize for engagement rather than musical diversity; and the music industry becomes structurally dependent on streaming revenue that Spotify controls.

Pros of firm subsidy

  • 600+ million users accessing vast music catalog
  • Discovery algorithms create anti-rival value (each listener improves recommendations for others)
  • Reduced piracy through convenient access

Cons (dependency scarcity)

  • Opaque per-stream payment methodology
  • Artists receive fraction of a cent per stream
  • Algorithmic curation shapes cultural consumption
  • Industry-wide dependency on single platform’s terms

6.3 Direction 3: Enhance with community organization — governance scarcity

In the community path, the community collectively covers rival costs. This corresponds to the metamechanism “large-scale collaboration to minimize individuals’ rival costs,” which includes using communities, shared ownership, and decentralized governance. The BMPs—crowdsourcing, open source, community governance, stewardship, peer-to-peer, revenue sharing—eliminate dependency on any single firm.

The original study identified an “accountability challenge”: how to ensure active contribution and prevent free-riding. Our framework reveals a deeper structural problem. Within a community, participants specialize: some create anti-rival value (content, code, data), others maintain the rivalrous complement (governance, moderation, infrastructure). Governance is costlier than content creation (\(c_G > c_C\)): reviewing a pull request is harder than writing code; moderating a discussion is harder than posting. The governance contributor must receive a premium \(\sigma\) for this additional cost.

The Governance Trap

If the governance premium falls below the cost differential, \(\sigma < c_G - c_C\), the governance-to-content ratio declines exponentially and the community enters a governance trap. Content grows (the anti-rival resource appears healthy), but governance erodes (the rivalrous complement deteriorates). Surface metrics—user count, stars, downloads, content volume—remain positive during the decline phase, creating a dangerous illusion of health. The community collapses not when content stops growing but when governance can no longer sustain the infrastructure that content depends on.

The governance premium must be stacked from multiple sources to reach the threshold: decision-making power (\(\sigma_{\text{power}}\)), reputational returns (\(\sigma_{\text{rep}}\)), financial compensation (\(\sigma_{\text{fin}}\)), strategic access (\(\sigma_{\text{strat}}\)), and social recognition (\(\sigma_{\text{social}}\)). A community relying on a single premium source is fragile; one relying on a diversified portfolio is robust.

A paradox of falling content costs. The governance trap condition depends on the differential \(c_G - c_C\). When content creation becomes easier (through AI-generated code, low-code tools, generative AI), \(c_C\) falls, which widens the gap \(c_G - c_C\) and makes the governance trap harder to avoid. Communities must respond by increasing \(\sigma\) or reducing \(c_G\), not by celebrating content growth. This has immediate policy relevance: the current wave of generative AI makes governance investment more urgent, not less.

Case: OpenSSL and the Heartbleed Pattern

OpenSSL is perhaps the starkest illustration of the governance trap. The software library provided encryption for approximately 66% of all web servers—a maximally anti-rival resource where every user benefit from every security patch and every compatibility improvement. Yet the rivalrous complement (security review, code maintenance, vulnerability testing) was provided by effectively one full-time developer. The maintenance ratio \(\rho = S(n)/D(n)\) approached zero: demand for security review scaled with the millions of users, while supply remained constant. The 2014 Heartbleed vulnerability was the direct consequence—a critical flaw that went undetected for two years because the governance infrastructure was catastrophically under-resourced. The community appeared maximally healthy by every surface metric (downloads, deployments, citations) right up until catastrophic failure.

Pros of community path

  • Universal access to critical encryption
  • No single firm controlling internet security
  • Open source enables auditing and trust

Cons (governance scarcity)

  • No governance premium attracted sufficient maintainers
  • Millions of free-riders, near-zero contributors
  • Catastrophic vulnerability went undetected for 2 years

Case: Wikipedia

Wikipedia exemplifies both the promise and the governance challenge of the community path. Articles are anti-rival (\(\beta \approx 0.2\) for quality improvement with editor base growth), and the community has built the largest encyclopedia in human history. But the rivalrous complements—editor attention, content curation, dispute resolution, vandalism patrol—face increasing demand with scale. The signal-to-noise ratio declines as \(n^{-0.8}\): content volume grows linearly with users while content quality improves sublinearly. Wikipedia has responded with elaborate governance institutions (featured article process, deletion policies, arbitration committees, barnstars for recognition), which represent successful premium stacking: governance contributors receive power (admin rights), reputation (community recognition), and social standing. However, the community faces ongoing challenges: declining active editor counts since 2007, gender and geographic bias in coverage, and increasing bureaucratic overhead that discourages new contributors.

  • Community quality control (featured articles, peer review)

  • Systemic coverage biases (gender, geography)

  • Vandalism patrol consumes disproportionate governance resources

6.4 The coupling dial: transform as mitigation

Within either enhancement path, a coupling dial is available. Transform strategies—de-transactionalization (subscription, flat rate), parallel offerings (freemium, dual licensing), and resource conversion (digitalization, unbundling)—reduce the coupling between rival and anti-rival components. This simultaneously reduces both the anti-rival benefit and the displaced scarcity.

Transform is not a fourth direction but a parameter within Directions 2 and 3. Within the firm path, de-transactionalization (moving from per-use data extraction to flat-rate subscription) reduces the extraction opportunity and the dependency. Within the community path, modularization and interface simplification reduce governance demands. In both cases, the coupling parameter \(\lambda\) moves from high (full enhancement, high scarcity displacement) to low (partial enhancement, low scarcity displacement).

A fundamental constraint applies: on a shared interface, the mixed payoff is convex in \(\lambda\)—any interior coupling is a minimum, not a maximum. The optimum is at a corner: commit to full enhancement or full transform on each interface. This explains why hybrid strategies often fail unless they separate interfaces architecturally (e.g., a freemium model with clearly distinct free and premium tiers rather than a sliding scale).

8. Real-World Cases: Scarcity Displacement in Practice

The following cases illustrate how scarcity displacement operates across different sectors and organizational forms, showing that each direction has genuine advantages and genuine costs. The purpose is not to rank directions but to demonstrate that the choice between them is context-dependent and that each creates real, identifiable scarcities.

8.1 Linux: the governance success story

Direction 3 (Community) with partial Direction 2 (firm co-investment)

Linux represents the most successful large-scale community-governed anti-rival resource. The kernel codebase is maximally anti-rival: every driver, security patch, and optimization benefits all users. The community path’s governance scarcity has been addressed through a sophisticated multi-layered approach: the Linux Foundation provides institutional infrastructure (\(\sigma_{\text{strat}}\) and \(\sigma_{\text{fin}}\)), maintainer hierarchies provide decision-making authority (\(\sigma_{\text{power}}\)), and the Linus Torvalds model of “benevolent dictator for life” provides reputational anchoring (\(\sigma_{\text{rep}}\)). Corporate contributors (Red Hat, Google, Intel, Microsoft) represent a hybrid element: they subsidize rival costs (developer salaries, testing infrastructure) in exchange for strategic influence, introducing elements of Direction 2 within a predominantly Direction 3 framework.

Key insight: Linux’s success depends on a hybrid arrangement where corporate firm-subsidy patterns co-exist with community governance. The governance premium is adequately stacked, but the community faces ongoing dependency risks: corporate contributors can shift priorities, and the maintainer pipeline is perpetually thin relative to demand. Recent maintainer burnout incidents illustrate that even successful community governance faces persistent governance scarcity.

8.2 The npm/left-pad incident: invisible governance scarcity

Direction 3 (Community) — governance trap in action

In March 2016, developer Azer Koçulu unpublished an 11-line JavaScript package called “left-pad” from the npm registry. The package was a dependency of thousands of other packages, and its removal broke build processes across the JavaScript ecosystem, including at Facebook, Netflix, and other major companies. The npm ecosystem exhibited extremely high \(\beta\)—each package added value to all other packages through the dependency graph—but governance quality \(h(y)\) for supply-chain security was approximately zero. No review process existed for package dependencies; no governance mechanism prevented a single developer from breaking thousands of downstream projects; and no community infrastructure tracked the criticality of individual packages within the dependency graph.

Key insight: The npm ecosystem appeared maximally healthy by surface metrics (exponential package growth, millions of downloads) right up until a governance failure caused systemic disruption. The case demonstrates the governance trap’s most dangerous feature: anti-rival success metrics can grow monotonically while governance metrics deteriorate, because the metrics organizations track (downloads, stars, user counts) proxy the anti-rivalrous dimension and hide governance quality.

8.3 Meta/Facebook: dependency scarcity at platform scale

Direction 2 (Firm subsidy) — dependency concentration

Facebook/Meta provides the canonical case of firm-subsidized anti-rivalry at scale. The social graph is intensely anti-rival: every connection, post, and interaction increases the platform’s value for all users. Meta subsidizes the massive rival costs (data centers, content moderation, security infrastructure) and extracts value through advertising revenue based on user data—the “hidden revenue” BMP. The dependency scarcity is multi-layered: users cannot export their social graph or content history in interoperable formats; advertisers depend on Meta’s opaque auction and targeting systems; news publishers became dependent on algorithmic distribution that Meta could unilaterally change (as demonstrated by the news feed algorithm changes and the 2023 withdrawal of news content in Canada and Australia).

The Cambridge Analytica scandal (2018) revealed the depth of dependency scarcity: users had no meaningful visibility into how their data was shared with third parties, and the platform’s business model pattern (hidden revenue + social mission) created structural incentives for data maximization over data protection. Regulatory responses (GDPR, DSA) represent attempts to impose transparency floors on the firm-subsidy path.

8.4 Mastodon: the infrastructure challenge of community organization

Direction 3 (Community) — infrastructure scarcity variant

Mastodon, the federated social network, represents a deliberate choice of Direction 3 to avoid Direction 2’s dependency scarcity. No single firm controls the network; instances are independently operated and federate through the ActivityPub protocol. The anti-rival value is preserved: content shared across instances benefits all users. But the governance scarcity manifests specifically in infrastructure: volunteer instance operators bear the rival costs of hosting, moderation, and maintenance. During the November 2022 Twitter migration, several large Mastodon instances experienced capacity crises—not because the software failed but because volunteer-operated infrastructure was exhausted by sudden demand growth. This is the digital Jevons paradox: making the anti-rival resource better (through network growth) increases adoption, which increases infrastructure demand beyond the community’s capacity to provide.

The case illustrates that community organization does not eliminate scarcity; it replaces dependency on a single firm with dependency on distributed volunteer labor. The question is which dependency structure is more sustainable and more amenable to correction.

8.5 AI/ML commons: the new frontier

Directions 2 and 3 in tension

Open AI models (LLaMA, Mistral, Stable Diffusion) exhibit strong anti-rivalry: shared weights, fine-tuning techniques, and benchmarks become more valuable with more users and contributors. But the rivalrous complement—compute for training and inference—is intensely concentrated. Training a frontier model requires hundreds of millions of dollars in GPU compute, concentrating the ability to create foundational anti-rival resources among a handful of firms. This creates a hybrid structure where the anti-rival resource (model weights) follows Direction 3 (open release, community fine-tuning) while the rivalrous complement (compute) follows Direction 2 (firm-subsidized, with the releasing firm capturing strategic advantage through ecosystem lock-in, API dependency, and fine-tuning infrastructure control).

The dependency scarcity is subtle but real: communities can use and adapt open models but cannot produce new foundational models without access to compute concentrated in a few firms. The “open” label may thus overstate the community’s autonomy while obscuring the dependency structure. This case motivates the business model disclosure framework we develop in Section 10.

9. Context-Dependent Policy: Matching Directions to Contexts

The framework implies that no single direction is universally optimal. Instead, the welfare-maximizing direction depends on context—specifically on the anti-rivalry strength \(\beta\), the cost structure of rivalrous complements, the availability of governance infrastructure, and the existing institutional landscape. We identify four context-dependent policy prescriptions.

9.1 When to accept restriction

Restriction is welfare-maximizing when:

  • \(\beta\) is low (weakly anti-rival goods): the superlinear value from open sharing is modest, so the welfare cost of artificial scarcity is small.
  • Rivalrous complements are expensive and non-substitutable (e.g., clinical trials, physical manufacturing), so neither firm subsidy nor community organization can feasibly cover them.
  • The good is in an early development phase where investment recovery requires temporary exclusion (pharmaceutical patents, early-stage R&D).

Policy action: Ensure that restriction mechanisms are time-limited and scope-appropriate. Patent terms for strongly anti-rival goods (software, data, algorithms) should be shorter than for weakly anti-rival goods (physical inventions). Compulsory licensing provisions should activate when \(\beta\) demonstrably exceeds the original estimates at the time of patent granting—as has occurred with many biotechnology patents where the anti-rival value of shared research data was initially underestimated.

9.2 When to enable firm subsidies (with safeguards)

Firm subsidies are welfare-maximizing when:

  • \(\beta\) is high but community governance capacity is insufficient (\(\sigma < c_G - c_C\)): the anti-rival value justifies enhancement but no viable community path exists.
  • Rivalrous complements require scale investment that exceeds community capacity (e.g., large-scale cloud infrastructure, content moderation at billion-user scale).
  • Rapid deployment matters (public health information, emergency communication) and firm infrastructure already exists.

Policy action: Accept firm subsidies but constrain the dependency scarcity they create. The policy instruments are:

  1. Transparency requirements. Mandatory disclosure of data practices, algorithmic decision-making, and business model patterns (developed in Section 10). The formal analysis shows that the marginal welfare gain from transparency is highest at low transparency levels—the first unit of disclosure has the largest effect.
  2. Interoperability and data portability. Ensure users can exit the firm-subsidy arrangement. Without mobility, the community alternative is notional rather than credible. The EU Digital Markets Act’s interoperability requirements for messaging services exemplify this approach.
  3. The credible-threat mechanism. Maintain viable community alternatives so that firm-subsidy platforms face competitive discipline. When a community path exists, the captive user fraction \(\alpha\) decreases, the transparency tipping point \(p^*\) drops, and firms must compete on transparency: \[ \underbrace{\text{Lower } c_G, \text{ increase } \sigma}_{\text{governance policy}} \;\longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\sigma \geq c_G - c_C}_{\text{community viable}} \;\longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\alpha \downarrow}_{\text{outside option}} \;\longrightarrow\; \underbrace{p^* \downarrow}_{\text{tipping point drops}} \;\longrightarrow\; \underbrace{\tau \uparrow}_{\text{firms compete on transparency}} \]

9.3 When to invest in community organization

Community organization is welfare-maximizing when:

  • \(\beta\) is high and governance capacity is sufficient or can be built (\(\sigma \geq c_G - c_C\) or the governance premium can be raised through institutional design).
  • The good serves public-interest functions where concentrated dependency is unacceptable (critical infrastructure, democratic discourse, scientific knowledge).
  • Long-term sustainability matters more than rapid deployment: community governance is slower to establish but more resilient once established.

Policy action: Invest in governance infrastructure as a public good.

  1. Lower \(c_G\): Fund open-source governance tooling (moderation tools, dispute resolution platforms, automated compliance checking, contribution tracking systems). This is analogous to funding physical infrastructure that enables market competition.
  2. Increase \(\sigma\): Create institutional conditions for governance premium stacking. Tax treatment that recognizes governance labor as a contribution; portability of reputation across platforms; legal frameworks for community revenue sharing; antitrust policy that preserves strategic value of community participation.
  3. Address the falling-content-cost paradox: As generative AI reduces \(c_C\), the governance gap \(c_G - c_C\) widens. Policy must anticipate this by proactively investing in governance capacity before the gap becomes unsustainable.
  4. Support federated governance models: Federation (as in Mastodon, Matrix, or the Fediverse more broadly) reduces governance costs through parallelization. If governance demand scales as \(D(n) = d_0 \cdot n^{\gamma}\) with \(\gamma > 1\), splitting the community into \(K\) federated sub-communities reduces total governance demand by factor \(K^{\gamma-1}\).

9.4 The phase transition and policy timing

The anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\) increases secularly as technology evolves. This drives phase transitions in business model landscapes:

Phase Transitions and Policy Windows

Phase I (\(\beta\) low): Restriction is stable. Enhancement requires coordinated adoption (consortium, standards body, dominant platform). Policy focus: ensure restriction mechanisms are time-limited; fund basic research into community governance models.

Phase II (\(\beta\) intermediate): Transform strategies invade the all-restrict equilibrium. Full enhancement still requires a tipping point, but the point is shrinking. This is the critical policy window: extraction norms are forming, path dependence has not yet locked in, and the choices made here shape long-run equilibria. Policy focus: invest in governance infrastructure; impose transparency floors; mandate interoperability.

Phase III (\(\beta\) high): Full enhancement dominates. Restriction is unstable. Policy focus: ensure both firm-subsidy and community paths remain viable so that competition between them constrains scarcity displacement.

The secular increase in \(\beta\) means that industries move through these phases over time. What was Phase I a decade ago may be Phase II now. Many digital goods are currently in Phase II, making governance infrastructure investment the most time-sensitive policy lever.

9.5 The private–social divergence

The formal payoffs in the evolutionary model measure private fitness. They do not account for user participation. This creates a wedge between private and social optima. Define the participation ratio \(P = n_T/n_L > 1\) (transparent/community paths attract more users) and the per-user value ratio \(Q = E_L/E_T > 1\) (full firm-subsidy enhancement creates more per-user value through tight coupling). Social welfare depends on the product of participation and per-user value:

Private–Social Divergence

A less extractive direction (community or transparent firm) is socially superior despite being privately dominated when \(P > Q - (c_L - c_T)/(n_L \cdot E_T)\). The divergence is widest at intermediate \(\beta\) (Phase II): both directions are viable, per-user value differences are moderate, and the widest parameter region exists where extraction is privately dominant but community organization is socially superior. This is precisely the regime where business model disclosure (Section 8) has its highest marginal impact.

10. Business Model Disclosure: A Transparency Solution

The preceding analysis reveals a common challenge across all three directions: scarcity displacement is difficult to detect, its welfare consequences are difficult to assess, and the dependencies it creates are difficult to mitigate—because the business model logic that generates them is opaque. We propose business model disclosure as a structural solution.

10.1 The information asymmetry problem

Users, communities, and policymakers face a fundamental information asymmetry regarding the business model patterns embedded in digital products and services. When a user adopts a “free” platform, they rarely understand whether the business model is:

  • Hidden revenue (data extraction): creating dependency scarcity through opacity of data practices
  • Sponsorship (third-party subsidy): creating dependency on sponsor’s continued support
  • Freemium (tiered access): creating partial restriction of the anti-rival resource
  • Open source with corporate backing: creating a hybrid dependency on the backing firm’s strategic interests

Each of these patterns creates different dependencies, different scarcities, and different risks for the user. Yet the surface presentation may be identical: a free service with a user-friendly interface. The business model pattern determines the direction of scarcity displacement, and without visibility into the pattern, users cannot assess the dependencies they are accepting.

This information asymmetry is compounded for stacked business model patterns. Real organizations rarely employ a single BMP; they combine multiple patterns, often from different directions. A platform might use open source (Direction 3) for its core software, hidden revenue (Direction 2) for its data practices, and subscription (transform) for premium features. Each layer creates a different dependency, and the interaction between layers may create dependencies that no single layer would generate alone. Without disclosure, neither users nor policymakers can trace the full dependency structure.

10.2 The disclosure framework

We propose that organizations employing anti-rival goods be required to disclose three elements:

Business Model Disclosure Requirements

1. Pattern identification. The organization must identify which of the recognized business model patterns it employs, using a standardized taxonomy (such as the 175-pattern framework from the BMP literature). This includes both the primary revenue-generating patterns and ancillary patterns that shape the user relationship.

2. Dependency structure. For each identified pattern, the organization must disclose the dependencies it creates: what data is collected and how it is monetized (for hidden revenue patterns); what terms govern access and how they can change unilaterally (for platform-subsidy patterns); what switching costs exist and whether data is portable (for lock-in patterns); and what governance mechanisms exist and how users can influence them (for community patterns).

3. Anti-rival compliance assessment. The organization must assess whether its business model patterns are anti-rival compliant—whether they allow the full anti-rival potential of the resource to be realized, or whether they restrict it artificially. This assessment follows the criteria defined in Section 10.3.

The disclosure is analogous to financial reporting requirements: just as investors require standardized financial statements to assess a company’s economic position, users and policymakers require standardized business model statements to assess the dependency structures and scarcity displacement that a company’s practices create.

10.3 Anti-rival compliance criteria

We define anti-rival compliance as the degree to which a business model pattern allows the full anti-rival potential of its underlying resource to be realized without creating unnecessary scarcity. The criteria are:

CriterionDescriptionAssessment indicators
Access maximization Does the BMP allow the maximum feasible number of users to access and contribute to the anti-rival resource? User access restrictions; price barriers; geographic limitations; format compatibility
Value additivity Does each additional user’s participation increase the resource’s value for existing users? Network effect measurement; contribution mechanisms; whether the BMP captures or suppresses user contributions
Dependency transparency Are the dependencies created by the BMP visible and understandable to users? Clarity of data practices; predictability of terms; availability of dependency mapping
Exit viability Can users exit the arrangement without forfeiting accumulated value? Data portability; interoperability with alternatives; switching cost assessment
Governance accountability Are governance decisions that affect the anti-rival resource made through accountable processes? Decision-making transparency; user representation; appeal mechanisms; governance premium structure
Complement sustainability Is the provision of rivalrous complements sustainably funded? Governance-to-content ratio; maintainer capacity metrics; infrastructure funding durability

A business model pattern that scores high on all six criteria is fully anti-rival compliant. Most real-world BMPs are partially compliant, with their specific deficiencies mapping to the direction of scarcity they create. Restriction patterns fail on access maximization. Firm-subsidy patterns often fail on dependency transparency and exit viability. Community patterns may fail on complement sustainability if the governance trap condition is not met.

10.4 How disclosure enables policy and community action

Business model disclosure creates the informational precondition for three types of response:

User response. With visible dependency structures, users can make informed choices about which scarcities they are willing to accept. A user who values autonomy over convenience may choose a community-organized alternative (accepting governance scarcity) over a firm-subsidized platform (avoiding dependency scarcity). Currently, this choice is effectively impossible because the business model patterns are opaque.

Community response. Disclosure enables communities to identify where their efforts would have the most impact. If a firm-subsidized platform discloses that its core dependency is algorithmic curation control, a community initiative can target exactly that function—building an open curation algorithm that serves as a credible alternative for the specific dependency identified. Without disclosure, community efforts are unfocused and often redundant. The disclosed anti-rival compliance assessment also enables communities to identify which of the firm’s business model patterns could be replaced with community-governed alternatives and which genuinely require firm-scale investment.

Policy response. Disclosure enables policymakers to target interventions precisely. Rather than broad platform regulation, policy can address specific dependency structures: if disclosure reveals that a platform’s primary extraction mechanism is opaque data monetization (hidden revenue BMP, failing on dependency transparency), the targeted intervention is data practice transparency requirements—not blanket content regulation. If disclosure reveals that a community project is failing on complement sustainability (governance trap condition approaching), the targeted intervention is governance infrastructure investment—not content subsidies.

10.5 The recognized anti-rival compliant patterns as guidance

The 39 anti-rival-engaging BMPs from the original study can serve as a reference library for disclosure and compliance assessment. Each pattern has known properties regarding which scarcities it creates and which anti-rival compliance criteria it satisfies. This creates a practical tool for policy implementation:

  • Pattern matching: When a company discloses its business model patterns, each pattern can be matched against the reference library to immediately identify expected dependency structures and scarcity displacement directions. Novel or proprietary patterns require additional scrutiny.
  • Combination assessment: The framework identifies which pattern combinations are coherent (within-direction combinations) and which create internal tensions (cross-direction combinations). Disclosure of pattern combinations enables regulators to flag structurally problematic configurations—e.g., a firm claiming community governance (Direction 3) while simultaneously operating hidden revenue extraction (Direction 2) on the same user base.
  • Compliance pathways: For each non-compliant pattern, the framework identifies a compliance pathway—specific modifications or complementary patterns that would bring the BMP into anti-rival compliance. For example, a hidden revenue pattern becomes conditionally compliant when combined with data portability and algorithmic transparency.
  • Sector-specific templates: Different sectors face different \(\beta\) values and different rivalrous complement structures. Disclosure templates can be sector-specific: a social media platform’s disclosure focuses on data practices and algorithmic curation; an open-source project’s disclosure focuses on governance premium structure and maintainer sustainability; an AI model provider’s disclosure focuses on compute dependency and model licensing terms.

10.6 Implementation considerations

Business model disclosure faces several implementation challenges that must be addressed for it to be effective:

Standardization. Disclosure requires a standardized taxonomy of business model patterns. The BMP literature provides a starting point (175 patterns from 24 studies), but this taxonomy must be validated and maintained by an institutional body—analogous to accounting standards bodies (IFRS, FASB) for financial reporting. The anti-rival compliance criteria (Section 10.3) similarly require standardized assessment methodologies.

Dynamic updating. Business models evolve, and disclosure must be updated accordingly. A platform that starts with a subscription model (Direction 2, transform) and gradually introduces data monetization (Direction 2, hidden revenue) should be required to update its disclosure. This mirrors the requirement for material event disclosure in financial reporting.

Enforcement. Disclosure without enforcement is ineffective. Enforcement mechanisms could include: regulatory audits (analogous to financial audits); civil liability for materially inaccurate disclosure; integration with existing regulatory frameworks (GDPR data processing inventories, EU Digital Services Act transparency requirements); and market-based enforcement through consumer rating agencies that assess business model disclosures.

Proportionality. Disclosure requirements should be proportional to the platform’s reach and the \(\beta\) of its core resource. A small open-source project with 50 users faces different disclosure obligations than a platform with a billion users. The EU Digital Markets Act’s concept of “gatekeeper” platforms provides a model for threshold-based disclosure requirements.

Avoiding gaming. Companies may attempt to game disclosure by formally adopting compliant patterns while substantively operating non-compliant ones—e.g., labeling a hidden revenue model as “community-supported” by establishing a nominal community governance structure. Anti-rival compliance assessment must include substantive criteria (actual data flows, actual governance authority, actual switching costs) rather than formal criteria alone. The dependency structure disclosure (element 2) is designed to prevent this: it requires disclosure of actual practices, not just pattern labels.

11. Discussion

11.1 Contributions

To the anti-rival goods literature. We introduce scarcity displacement as the central mechanism governing anti-rival value creation and show that it is structural rather than contingent. Prior work established that anti-rival goods challenge market allocation (Bliss & Egler, 2020; Cooper, 2006) and identified the need for alternative organizing structures (Kubiszewski et al., 2010; Nikander et al., 2020). Our contribution is threefold: we formalize the three-direction choice that organizations face (restrict, firm subsidize, community organize); we characterize the distinct scarcity that each direction creates (artificial scarcity, dependency scarcity, governance scarcity); and we show that each scarcity has different properties regarding visibility, concentration, and policy amenability.

To business model innovation research. We provide a framework that explains why certain BMP combinations succeed or fail, grounding the observation in scarcity displacement rather than abstract compatibility. The framework explains the metamechanism-level findings from the original BMP study—why each faces a distinctive challenge—and introduces anti-rival compliance as a design criterion that connects BMP choice to welfare outcomes. For practitioners, the framework provides a diagnostic: identify which direction your business model occupies, understand which scarcity you are creating, and assess whether the scarcity is sustainable and acceptable.

To innovation policy. We make three policy contributions. First, we show that the welfare-maximizing direction depends on context, challenging universal prescriptions for either open access or market-based allocation. Second, we identify Phase II (intermediate \(\beta\)) as the critical policy window where private-social divergence is widest and intervention has the highest marginal impact. Third, and most centrally, we propose business model disclosure as a new policy instrument that creates the informational precondition for effective context-dependent intervention. The anti-rival compliance criteria provide a practical assessment framework for evaluating whether business model patterns serve or undermine anti-rival value creation.

11.2 Competition between directions as mutual discipline

The three directions are not merely alternatives but mutual checks. The firm path’s dependency scarcity is constrained when a viable community path exists, because the community alternative reduces the captive user base and lowers the transparency tipping point. Conversely, the community path’s governance scarcity is partially alleviated when firm-subsidized alternatives set a quality floor that communities must match, preventing governance complacency. Even restriction serves a disciplinary role: the existence of a viable restrict path (paid, per-unit, but well-functioning) prevents both enhancement paths from taking user participation for granted.

The healthiest ecosystem is one where multiple directions are viable—each direction’s displaced scarcity is constrained by the credible threat of defection to another. Business model disclosure enables this competition by making the scarcity trade-offs visible to all participants.

11.3 Limitations

The model assumes homogeneous agents within each strategy type and exogenous \(\beta\) (in reality, firms invest in changing anti-rivalry). The functional forms (power-law value, Michaelis–Menten governance quality) are mathematically convenient but empirically unvalidated. We conjecture that the qualitative results (governance trap, phase transitions, scarcity displacement) are robust to functional form, but specific threshold values are not.

The framework treats the three directions as distinct categories. In practice, many ecosystems involve hybrid arrangements (as illustrated by the Linux and AI/ML cases). The framework applies to hybrids by analyzing each interface separately: the open-source component operates on the community path, the firm’s proprietary services on the firm path, and each interface can be independently assessed for anti-rival compliance.

The business model disclosure proposal is conceptual. Practical implementation requires standardized taxonomies, assessment methodologies, and enforcement mechanisms that do not yet exist. The analogy to financial reporting is instructive but imperfect: business model patterns are more fluid than financial transactions, and compliance assessment is more subjective than accounting standards.

The real-world cases are illustrative rather than systematic. A rigorous empirical test of the framework would require systematic measurement of \(\beta\), governance premiums, and dependency structures across a representative sample of ecosystems—which we identify as the most important direction for future research.

11.4 Future research

Five directions are particularly promising. First, empirical estimation of \(\beta\) and the governance premium components in specific ecosystems, using panel data on user growth, per-user value metrics, and governance activity indicators. Second, endogenizing \(\beta\)—modeling how firms invest in technologies that change the anti-rivalry of their goods, and the strategic dynamics this creates. Third, developing standardized business model disclosure taxonomies and compliance assessment methodologies through multi-stakeholder processes, analogous to the development of accounting standards. Fourth, testing whether the governance trap’s exponential decay prediction matches observed patterns in open-source projects, using the governance-to-content ratio as a leading indicator. Fifth, analyzing how generative AI changes the \(c_G - c_C\) gap across sectors and what governance investments are needed to prevent the resulting governance trap expansion.

12. Conclusion

Anti-rival goods create value that scales with use, but this scaling inevitably displaces scarcity. The choice facing organizations is not whether to create scarcity but which kind. Restricting anti-rivalry creates artificial scarcity of the resource itself, with superlinear welfare loss. Firm subsidization creates dependency scarcity, concentrating leverage in the subsidizing entity. Community organization creates governance scarcity, diffusing the burden but risking under-provision of the rivalrous complements that the anti-rival resource depends on.

Each direction has legitimate applications, and the welfare-maximizing choice depends on context: the strength of anti-rivalry, the cost structure of rivalrous complements, the availability of governance capacity, and the public-interest stakes involved. The most effective innovation policy does not mandate a single direction but ensures that multiple directions remain viable as mutual checks, with competition between directions constraining the scarcity that each creates.

The central contribution of this paper is the proposal that business model disclosure is the enabling precondition for effective context-dependent policy. Without visibility into the business model patterns that organizations employ, the dependencies these patterns create, and the scarcity they displace, neither users nor policymakers can make informed choices about which scarcities to accept. Business model disclosure, assessed against anti-rival compliance criteria and grounded in the recognized library of anti-rival-engaging business model patterns, provides this visibility.

The framework, grounded in a systematic analysis of 175 business model patterns and formalized with evolutionary game theory and the coupled resource model, yields three results with direct policy relevance. First, the business model landscape undergoes phase transitions as anti-rivalry increases, and Phase II is the critical policy window. Second, private and social optima diverge most widely in Phase II, making governance infrastructure investment and business model disclosure the most time-sensitive interventions. Third, a credible community alternative disciplines firm-path dependency scarcity through market competition rather than direct regulation—but only when users have the information (through disclosure) and the ability (through interoperability) to exercise their outside option.

Scarcity displacement cannot be avoided—only navigated. Business model disclosure turns navigation from an opaque, firm-controlled process into a transparent, collectively informed one.
