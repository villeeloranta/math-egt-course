1. Social Value Orientations (SVO)

Throughout this course, we have assumed that players maximize their own payoff \(\pi_{\text{self}}\). But decades of experimental research show that people systematically differ in how much weight they place on their own versus others' outcomes. The Social Value Orientation (SVO) framework captures this heterogeneity mathematically.

Definition — Social Value Orientation

A player's Social Value Orientation is a stable preference for particular distributions of outcomes between self and others. It characterizes how an individual weighs their own payoff relative to the payoff of interaction partners.

1.1 The SVO Framework (Van Lange, Murphy & Ackermann)

The SVO framework, developed by Van Lange (1999) and refined by Murphy & Ackermann (2014), identifies four primary orientation types based on how a person allocates resources between themselves and another person:

Altruistic

Maximizes the other's payoff, even at cost to self. Willing to sacrifice own outcomes to benefit others.

Prosocial

Maximizes joint payoff (self + other) and seeks equality. The most common orientation, found in roughly 60% of participants.

Individualistic

Maximizes own payoff with no regard for the other. This is the "rational actor" assumption of classical game theory.

Competitive

Maximizes the difference between own and other's payoff. Wants to "win" relative to the other person.

1.2 The SVO Ring Measure

The SVO Slider Measure (Murphy, Ackermann & Handgraaf, 2011) asks participants to make a series of resource allocation decisions. Each choice corresponds to a point on a circle centered at (50, 50) in payoff space, where the x-axis represents payoff to self and the y-axis represents payoff to other. The participant's choices trace out an angle on this ring.

The SVO Angle

Given a person's mean allocation \((\bar{A}_s, \bar{A}_o)\), where \(\bar{A}_s\) is the average payoff to self and \(\bar{A}_o\) is the average payoff to other across their choices, the SVO angle is:

\[ \theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{\bar{A}_o - 50}{\bar{A}_s - 50}\right) \]

The four SVO types correspond to angle ranges:

  • Altruistic: \(\theta > 57.15^\circ\)
  • Prosocial: \(22.45^\circ < \theta \leq 57.15^\circ\)
  • Individualistic: \(-12.04^\circ < \theta \leq 22.45^\circ\)
  • Competitive: \(\theta \leq -12.04^\circ\)
Intuition

Think of the SVO angle as the direction you "lean" in payoff space. At \(\theta = 0^\circ\), you move purely along the self-payoff axis (individualistic). At \(\theta = 45^\circ\), you move equally in both directions (prosocial). At \(\theta = 90^\circ\), you move purely along the other-payoff axis (altruistic). At negative angles, increasing your own payoff comes with decreasing the other's (competitive).

1.3 Mathematical Formalization: The SVO Utility Function

The key insight is that we can formalize SVO as a transformation of the utility function. Rather than each player simply maximizing \(\pi_{\text{self}}\), a player with SVO angle \(\theta\) maximizes:

SVO-Weighted Utility Function

\[ U_i(\theta_i) = \cos(\theta_i) \cdot \pi_i + \sin(\theta_i) \cdot \pi_j \]

where \(\pi_i\) is player \(i\)'s material payoff, \(\pi_j\) is the other player's material payoff, and \(\theta_i\) is player \(i\)'s SVO angle.

Let us verify this formula captures the four SVO types correctly:

Example — Checking the SVO Utility Function
  • Individualistic (\(\theta = 0^\circ\)): \(U = \cos(0)\cdot\pi_i + \sin(0)\cdot\pi_j = 1\cdot\pi_i + 0\cdot\pi_j = \pi_i\). Pure self-interest.
  • Prosocial (\(\theta = 45^\circ\)): \(U = \cos(45^\circ)\cdot\pi_i + \sin(45^\circ)\cdot\pi_j = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\pi_i + \pi_j)\). Maximizes joint payoff.
  • Altruistic (\(\theta = 90^\circ\)): \(U = \cos(90^\circ)\cdot\pi_i + \sin(90^\circ)\cdot\pi_j = 0\cdot\pi_i + 1\cdot\pi_j = \pi_j\). Pure other-regard.
  • Competitive (\(\theta = -45^\circ\)): \(U = \cos(-45^\circ)\cdot\pi_i + \sin(-45^\circ)\cdot\pi_j = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\pi_i - \pi_j)\). Maximizes relative advantage.

1.4 How SVO Transforms the Payoff Matrix

Consider a standard Prisoner's Dilemma with material payoff matrix:

Cooperate Defect
Cooperate 3, 3 0, 5
Defect 5, 0 1, 1

For an individualistic player (\(\theta = 0^\circ\)), this is the standard PD and defection dominates. But for a prosocial player (\(\theta = 45^\circ\)), the perceived payoffs transform:

Example — Prosocial Transformation of the Prisoner's Dilemma

Using \(U = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\pi_{\text{self}} + \pi_{\text{other}})\), the row player's transformed payoff matrix becomes:

C D
C \(\frac{3+3}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{6}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 4.24\) \(\frac{0+5}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{5}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 3.54\)
D \(\frac{5+0}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{5}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 3.54\) \(\frac{1+1}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 1.41\)

Now cooperation is the best response to cooperation (\(4.24 > 3.54\)), and defection is the best response to defection (\(1.41 < 3.54\)). The game has been transformed from a Prisoner's Dilemma into an Assurance Game (Stag Hunt), where mutual cooperation is a Nash equilibrium.

This is a profound result: social preferences can solve social dilemmas.

Key Insight

The SVO framework shows that whether a game is a "dilemma" depends not just on the material payoff structure, but on the psychological orientations of the players. The same material payoff matrix can represent entirely different strategic situations for players with different SVO angles.

2. SVO and Evolutionary Game Theory

Now we connect SVO to the evolutionary framework from Module 7. The central question becomes: if people have different social value orientations, which orientations survive and spread through evolutionary dynamics?

2.1 SVO Types as Strategies

In a standard evolutionary model, strategies are actions (Cooperate, Defect). In an SVO-evolutionary model, the "strategy" is the player's orientation — their SVO angle \(\theta\). Two players with different SVO angles facing the same material game will behave differently because they perceive different payoff matrices.

Definition — SVO-Conditional Strategy

A player with SVO angle \(\theta\) plays the best response in the transformed game. Given material payoff matrix \(A\), the player perceives the game with payoff matrix:

\[ A^{(\theta)} = \cos(\theta) \cdot A + \sin(\theta) \cdot A^T \]

where \(A^T\) is the transpose of \(A\) (the opponent's payoff matrix in a symmetric game).

2.2 Replicator Dynamics with SVO-Dependent Payoffs

Consider a population with \(k\) distinct SVO types, each with angle \(\theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots, \theta_k\) and frequency \(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k\) where \(\sum x_i = 1\). Each type plays their best response in their perceived game.

The fitness of type \(i\) when interacting with a random opponent is:

\[ f_i = \sum_{j=1}^{k} x_j \cdot \pi\!\left(s^*(\theta_i),\, s^*(\theta_j)\right) \]

where \(s^*(\theta_i)\) is the best-response strategy of an individual with SVO angle \(\theta_i\), and \(\pi(\cdot, \cdot)\) gives the material payoff (since evolutionary fitness depends on material outcomes, not subjective utility).

Critical Distinction

SVO determines behavior (which action is chosen), but evolutionary fitness depends on material payoffs. A prosocial player cooperates because they value joint outcomes, but their reproductive fitness depends on actual resources obtained, not on subjective satisfaction.

2.3 When Do Prosocial Types Survive?

Consider a simplified model with two SVO types in a Prisoner's Dilemma (with payoffs \(T > R > P > S\)):

  • Individualistic (\(\theta = 0^\circ\)): always defects (since D dominates in the standard PD)
  • Prosocial (\(\theta = 45^\circ\)): cooperates if the perceived game makes C a best response

If prosocial types cooperate with everyone and individualistic types defect with everyone, the fitness matrix for material payoffs is:

Prosocial (C) Individualist (D)
Prosocial (C) \(R\) \(S\)
Individualist (D) \(T\) \(P\)

This is just the original Prisoner's Dilemma at the population level. By the replicator dynamics analysis from Module 7, defection (individualism) is the only ESS. Prosocial types go extinct.

Theorem — Conditions for Prosocial Survival

Prosocial types can survive under the replicator dynamics if one or more of the following conditions hold:

  1. Assortative matching: prosocial types are more likely to interact with other prosocial types (e.g., through network structure, group selection, or tag-based recognition).
  2. Conditional strategies: prosocial types cooperate only with other cooperators (transforming the game from PD to an assurance game at the population level).
  3. Institutional mechanisms: punishment, monitoring, or reputation systems change the effective payoff matrix (Ostrom's contribution, which we develop below).
  4. Group selection: groups with more prosocial types outcompete groups with fewer (Boyd & Richerson's model, Section 6).

2.4 Assortative Matching: The Mathematical Rescue of Cooperation

Let \(r\) be the coefficient of assortment: the probability that a prosocial type interacts with another prosocial type, above and beyond what random matching would predict. With assortative matching, the fitness of a cooperator (prosocial) in a population where cooperators have frequency \(x\) is:

\[ f_C = \bigl[x + r(1-x)\bigr] \cdot R + \bigl[(1-x)(1-r)\bigr] \cdot S \]

\[ f_D = \bigl[x(1-r)\bigr] \cdot T + \bigl[(1-x) + rx\bigr] \cdot P \]

Cooperation is favored when \(f_C > f_D\). At the boundary \(x = 0\) (cooperation can invade), this simplifies to:

\[ r \cdot R + (1 - r) \cdot S > (1-r) \cdot T + r \cdot P \]

Solving for \(r\):

\[ r > \frac{T - S}{(T - S) + (R - P)} = \frac{T - S}{T + R - S - P} \]

Example — Critical Assortment in the PD

Using \(T = 5, R = 3, P = 1, S = 0\):

\[ r > \frac{5 - 0}{5 + 3 - 0 - 1} = \frac{5}{7} \approx 0.714 \]

Prosocial types need a fairly high degree of assortment to invade — they must interact with fellow prosocials at least 71.4% more often than random matching would predict. This is a quantitative prediction that connects SVO theory to population structure.

3. Ostrom's Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) Framework

Elinor Ostrom (Nobel Prize in Economics, 2009) showed that real communities often solve commons dilemmas without privatization or state control — through self-organized institutions. Her Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) framework provides a systematic way to analyze how institutional rules modify the structure of strategic interaction.

Definition — The IAD Framework

The IAD framework identifies three categories of exogenous variables that shape an action arena (the situation where actors interact):

  1. Biophysical conditions: the nature of the resource or good (rivalrous? excludable? regenerative?)
  2. Community attributes: norms, trust, shared knowledge, SVO distribution
  3. Rules-in-use: the actual operating rules that participants follow (which may differ from formal laws)

These three factors jointly determine the action situation: who participates, what actions are available, what information is available, how actions map to outcomes, and how outcomes map to payoffs.

3.1 Formalizing the IAD: Institutions as Game Modifiers

From a game-theoretic perspective, each element of the IAD framework corresponds to a formal component of a game:

IAD Component Game-Theoretic Formalization
Biophysical conditions Determines the payoff function \(\pi(a_1, \ldots, a_n; \mathbf{R})\) where \(\mathbf{R}\) is the resource state
Community attributes (norms, SVO) Determines the utility transformation \(U_i = \cos(\theta_i)\pi_i + \sin(\theta_i)\pi_j\)
Rules: Boundary Defines the player set \(N = \{1, 2, \ldots, n\}\)
Rules: Choice Defines the strategy space \(S_i\) for each player
Rules: Information Defines the information partition (who knows what before choosing)
Rules: Payoff Modifies the payoff function (e.g., adding fines, rewards)
Rules: Scope Constrains the set of allowable outcomes
Intuition

Ostrom's fundamental insight, in mathematical terms, is this: when a game has a socially undesirable equilibrium (like mutual defection in a commons), you don't need to change the players — you can change the game. Institutions are rules that transform the game \(\Gamma\) into a new game \(\Gamma'\) with a better equilibrium. The mathematical question becomes: which rule changes transform the payoff matrix in a way that makes cooperation an equilibrium?

4. Common-Pool Resource (CPR) Problems

A common-pool resource is one that is rivalrous (one person's use diminishes availability for others) but non-excludable (it is difficult to prevent access). Fisheries, forests, groundwater, and shared irrigation systems are classic examples. We now build a complete mathematical model of a CPR game.

4.1 The Mathematical Model

Definition — CPR Game Setup

Players: \(n\) harvesters, indexed \(i = 1, \ldots, n\).

Resource: A shared resource stock of size \(R\), with maximum carrying capacity \(K\).

Actions: Each player \(i\) chooses harvest level \(h_i \geq 0\). Total harvest \(H = \sum_{i=1}^{n} h_i\).

Yield function: The per-unit value of harvest depends on total extraction. A common specification:

\[ v(H) = a - bH \]

where \(a > 0\) is the base value and \(b > 0\) captures congestion/depletion effects. Player \(i\)'s payoff is:

\[ \pi_i(h_i, H_{-i}) = h_i \cdot v(H) = h_i(a - b(h_i + H_{-i})) \]

where \(H_{-i} = \sum_{j \neq i} h_j\) is the total harvest of all other players.

4.2 Resource Dynamics

In a dynamic (multi-period) model, the resource stock evolves according to:

\[ \frac{dR}{dt} = g(R) - H \]

where \(g(R)\) is the natural growth function. The standard logistic growth model gives:

\[ g(R) = rR\!\left(1 - \frac{R}{K}\right) \]

This is precisely the kind of differential equation we studied in Module 4. The resource is sustainable when \(H \leq g(R)\), i.e., harvest does not exceed natural regeneration.

Connection to Module 4

The equation \(\frac{dR}{dt} = rR(1 - R/K) - H\) is a modified logistic equation with a constant harvesting term. From our dynamical systems analysis, we know this has fixed points where \(g(R^*) = H\), and these fixed points may or may not be stable. If \(H\) exceeds the maximum sustainable yield \(g_{\max} = rK/4\), no stable positive equilibrium exists — the resource collapses to zero.

4.3 The Tragedy of the Commons: Nash Equilibrium Analysis

Using the static CPR game, we find the Nash equilibrium by computing each player's best response. Player \(i\) maximizes:

\[ \pi_i = h_i(a - b(h_i + H_{-i})) \]

Taking the first-order condition (Module 3):

\[ \frac{\partial \pi_i}{\partial h_i} = a - 2bh_i - bH_{-i} = 0 \]

Solving for \(h_i\):

\[ h_i^* = \frac{a - bH_{-i}}{2b} \]

By symmetry in Nash equilibrium, all players harvest the same amount \(h^*\), so \(H_{-i} = (n-1)h^*\):

\[ h^* = \frac{a - b(n-1)h^*}{2b} \]

\[ 2bh^* = a - b(n-1)h^* \]

\[ h^*(2b + b(n-1)) = a \]

\[ h^* = \frac{a}{b(n+1)} \]

Theorem — Nash Equilibrium of the CPR Game

In the symmetric CPR game with \(n\) players and payoff \(\pi_i = h_i(a - bH)\), the symmetric Nash equilibrium harvest is:

\[ h^*_{\text{Nash}} = \frac{a}{b(n+1)}, \qquad H^*_{\text{Nash}} = \frac{na}{b(n+1)} \]

Each player's equilibrium payoff is:

\[ \pi^*_{\text{Nash}} = \frac{a^2}{b(n+1)^2} \]

4.4 Social Optimum vs. Nash Equilibrium

The social optimum maximizes total payoff \(\Pi = \sum_i \pi_i = H(a - bH)\):

\[ \frac{d\Pi}{dH} = a - 2bH = 0 \implies H^*_{\text{social}} = \frac{a}{2b} \]

By symmetry, each player should harvest \(h^*_{\text{social}} = \frac{a}{2bn}\), giving each player:

\[ \pi^*_{\text{social}} = \frac{a}{2bn} \cdot \frac{a}{2} = \frac{a^2}{4bn} \]

Theorem — The Mathematics of Overexploitation

Comparing Nash and social optimum:

\[ \frac{H^*_{\text{Nash}}}{H^*_{\text{social}}} = \frac{na/(b(n+1))}{a/(2b)} = \frac{2n}{n+1} \]

As \(n \to \infty\), this ratio approaches \(2\): the Nash equilibrium harvests approximately twice the socially optimal amount.

The ratio of individual payoffs:

\[ \frac{\pi^*_{\text{Nash}}}{\pi^*_{\text{social}}} = \frac{a^2/(b(n+1)^2)}{a^2/(4bn)} = \frac{4n}{(n+1)^2} \]

For large \(n\), this approaches \(4/n\) — each player earns a vanishing fraction of what they could earn under optimal management. This is the tragedy of the commons, made precise.

Example — Numerical CPR Calculation

Let \(a = 120\), \(b = 1\), \(n = 5\):

  • \(h^*_{\text{Nash}} = 120 / (1 \cdot 6) = 20\), so \(H^*_{\text{Nash}} = 100\)
  • \(h^*_{\text{social}} = 120 / (2 \cdot 1 \cdot 5) = 12\), so \(H^*_{\text{social}} = 60\)
  • \(\pi^*_{\text{Nash}} = 20 \cdot (120 - 100) = 400\) per player
  • \(\pi^*_{\text{social}} = 12 \cdot (120 - 60) = 720\) per player

Each player gets only \(400/720 \approx 55.6\%\) of their optimal payoff in the Nash equilibrium. Collectively, the group loses \(5 \times (720 - 400) = 1600\) units of value due to overexploitation.

Exercise 8.3 — CPR with Heterogeneous Costs

Suppose \(n = 3\) harvesters face a CPR with \(a = 60\), \(b = 1\), but player 1 has harvesting cost \(c_1 = 2\) per unit while players 2 and 3 have \(c_2 = c_3 = 0\). Payoffs become \(\pi_i = h_i(a - bH) - c_i h_i\). Find the Nash equilibrium harvest for each player.

Player \(i\)'s FOC: \(\frac{\partial \pi_i}{\partial h_i} = (a - c_i) - 2bh_i - bH_{-i} = 0\).

So \(h_i = \frac{(a - c_i) - bH_{-i}}{2b}\).

For player 1: \(h_1 = \frac{58 - (h_2 + h_3)}{2}\).

For players 2, 3 (symmetric): \(h_2 = h_3 = \frac{60 - h_1 - h_j}{2}\) where \(j\) is the other low-cost player.

By symmetry \(h_2 = h_3 = h\). From player 2's FOC: \(h = \frac{60 - h_1 - h}{2}\), so \(3h = 60 - h_1\), giving \(h = \frac{60 - h_1}{3}\).

Substituting into player 1's FOC: \(h_1 = \frac{58 - 2 \cdot \frac{60 - h_1}{3}}{2} = \frac{58 - \frac{120 - 2h_1}{3}}{2} = \frac{\frac{174 - 120 + 2h_1}{3}}{2} = \frac{54 + 2h_1}{6}\).

So \(6h_1 = 54 + 2h_1\), giving \(h_1 = 13.5\) and \(h = \frac{60 - 13.5}{3} = 15.5\).

The higher-cost player harvests less. Total harvest: \(H = 13.5 + 2(15.5) = 44.5\).

5. Ostrom's Design Principles as Game Modifications

Ostrom identified eight "design principles" characteristic of long-enduring common-pool resource institutions, based on comparative case studies spanning centuries and continents. We now show how each principle can be understood as a specific modification to the game structure.

5.1 The Eight Principles, Formalized

Principle 1: Clearly Defined Boundaries

Game Modification — Defining the Player Set

Qualitative: Who has the right to withdraw resource units must be clearly defined, as must the boundaries of the resource itself.

Formal: Defines the player set \(N = \{1, \ldots, n\}\) and establishes excludability. Without clear boundaries, the game has \(n \to \infty\), and from our CPR analysis, the overexploitation ratio \(\frac{2n}{n+1} \to 2\) and per-person payoff ratio \(\frac{4n}{(n+1)^2} \to 0\). Bounding \(n\) is a necessary condition for efficiency.

Principle 2: Proportional Equivalence Between Benefits and Costs

Game Modification — Payoff Structure

Qualitative: Rules should ensure that contributions to maintaining the resource are proportional to benefits received.

Formal: Modifies the payoff function from \(\pi_i = h_i \cdot v(H)\) to \(\pi_i = h_i \cdot v(H) - c(h_i)\) where cost \(c(h_i)\) is proportional to harvest \(h_i\). This shifts the best-response function inward, reducing the Nash equilibrium harvest toward the social optimum.

Principle 3: Collective-Choice Arrangements

Game Modification — Endogenous Rule Formation

Qualitative: Most individuals affected by the operational rules can participate in modifying them.

Formal: Introduces a constitutional-level game \(\Gamma^C\) in which players choose the rules of the operational game \(\Gamma^O\). This is a two-level game: players first choose rules, then play the resource game under those rules. The constitutional game can select payoff-dominant equilibria of the resulting operational game.

Principle 4: Monitoring

Game Modification — Information Structure

Qualitative: Monitors who actively audit CPR conditions and appropriator behavior are at least partially accountable to the appropriators.

Formal: Transforms the game from imperfect information (where deviations are unobserved) to perfect monitoring (where actions are observed with probability \(p\)). This enables conditional strategies. Without monitoring, punishment is impossible; with monitoring, trigger strategies and graduated sanctions become feasible.

Principle 5: Graduated Sanctions

Game Modification — Punishment Payoffs

Qualitative: Appropriators who violate operational rules are likely to be assessed graduated sanctions by other appropriators or by officials accountable to the appropriators.

Formal: Adds a punishment function \(F(h_i)\) to the payoff: \(\pi_i^{\text{new}} = \pi_i^{\text{old}} - F(h_i)\) where \(F(h_i) = 0\) if \(h_i \leq \bar{h}\) (the agreed-upon limit) and \(F(h_i) = \beta(h_i - \bar{h})^\alpha\) for \(h_i > \bar{h}\), with \(\alpha \geq 1\) capturing the graduated nature of sanctions.

Principle 6: Conflict-Resolution Mechanisms

Game Modification — Meta-Game

Qualitative: Rapid access to low-cost local arenas to resolve disputes.

Formal: When a rule violation is contested, the dispute enters a meta-game — a game about interpreting the rules of the game. Low-cost conflict resolution reduces the transaction costs of enforcement, making punishment credible by reducing the cost parameter \(\gamma\) for enforcers.

Principle 7: Minimal Recognition of Rights to Organize

Game Modification — External Enforcement

Qualitative: External governmental authorities do not challenge the rights of appropriators to devise their own institutions.

Formal: Ensures that the two-level game (constitutional + operational) is binding: the rules chosen at the constitutional level are enforceable and not overridden by external players. Formally, the strategy space of external authorities is constrained: \(S_{\text{ext}} \subseteq \{s : s \text{ does not override local rules}\}\).

Principle 8: Nested Enterprises

Game Modification — Multi-Level Games

Qualitative: Governance activities are organized in multiple layers of nested enterprises.

Formal: The overall game is decomposed into a hierarchy of games: \(\Gamma = \Gamma^1 \times \Gamma^2 \times \cdots \times \Gamma^L\) where each level \(\Gamma^\ell\) governs the rules of level \(\ell - 1\). This hierarchical structure allows for specialization and economies of scale in governance.

5.2 Mathematical Analysis: Monitoring + Graduated Sanctions

We now show rigorously how Principles 4 and 5 (monitoring and graduated sanctions) together change the Nash equilibrium and replicator dynamics.

Consider the CPR game with an institutional rule: each player should harvest no more than \(\bar{h} = h^*_{\text{social}} = \frac{a}{2bn}\). Violations are detected with probability \(p\) (monitoring) and punished with fine \(\beta\) per unit of over-harvest (graduated sanctions).

The modified payoff function for player \(i\) becomes:

\[ \pi_i^{\text{inst}} = h_i(a - bH) - p \cdot \beta \cdot \max(0, h_i - \bar{h}) \]

The first-order condition for \(h_i > \bar{h}\):

\[ \frac{\partial \pi_i^{\text{inst}}}{\partial h_i} = a - 2bh_i - bH_{-i} - p\beta = 0 \]

Solving with symmetry (\(h_i = h^*\) for all \(i\)):

\[ h^*_{\text{inst}} = \frac{a - p\beta}{b(n+1)} \]

Theorem — Institutions Shift Nash Equilibrium Toward Social Optimum

With monitoring probability \(p\) and fine \(\beta\), compliance with the social optimum becomes a Nash equilibrium when the expected fine makes over-harvesting unprofitable. This requires:

\[ p\beta \geq a - \frac{a(n+1)}{2n} = \frac{a(n-1)}{2n} \]

That is, the expected fine \(p\beta\) must be at least \(\frac{a(n-1)}{2n}\). As the number of players grows, the required deterrent approaches \(\frac{a}{2}\), but for small groups the requirement is modest.

Example — Required Monitoring for Cooperation

Using \(a = 120\), \(b = 1\), \(n = 5\):

Required: \(p\beta \geq \frac{120 \cdot 4}{10} = 48\).

With monitoring probability \(p = 0.8\), the required fine is \(\beta \geq 60\). With \(p = 0.5\), the required fine is \(\beta \geq 96\).

This gives Ostrom's qualitative insight quantitative content: higher monitoring reduces the sanctions needed, and vice versa. Communities can trade off between monitoring intensity and punishment severity.

5.3 Replicator Dynamics with Institutions

Now consider an evolutionary model where some agents follow the institutional rules (Compliers, C) and others do not (Free-riders, F). The material payoffs depend on the proportion \(x\) of compliers:

Without institutions: this is equivalent to a Prisoner's Dilemma. Free-riders always have higher fitness, and compliance goes extinct.

With monitoring and sanctions: Let \(p\) be the probability of detecting a free-rider and \(\beta\) the fine. The modified fitness functions become:

\[ f_C(x) = R_C(x) \quad \text{(complier's material payoff)} \]

\[ f_F(x) = R_F(x) - p\beta \quad \text{(free-rider's material payoff minus expected fine)} \]

The replicator equation for complier frequency \(x\) becomes:

\[ \dot{x} = x(1 - x)\bigl[f_C(x) - f_F(x)\bigr] = x(1 - x)\bigl[R_C(x) - R_F(x) + p\beta\bigr] \]

If \(p\beta\) is large enough that \(R_C(x) - R_F(x) + p\beta > 0\) for all \(x\), then \(\dot{x} > 0\) whenever \(0 < x < 1\), and full compliance (\(x = 1\)) is the only stable equilibrium. Monitoring and sanctions transform the dynamics from convergence to all-defection to convergence to all-cooperation.

Exercise 8.4 — Optimal Monitoring

Monitoring is costly: it costs the community \(c_m \cdot p\) to maintain monitoring at level \(p\). The community's net benefit from institutions is the gain from cooperation minus monitoring costs. For the CPR game with \(a = 120\), \(b = 1\), \(n = 5\), and fine \(\beta = 100\):

(a) What is the minimum monitoring level \(p_{\min}\) that sustains cooperation? (b) If monitoring costs \(c_m = 200\) per unit of monitoring probability, what is the net benefit of institutions at \(p = p_{\min}\)?

(a) We need \(p\beta \geq \frac{a(n-1)}{2n} = \frac{120 \cdot 4}{10} = 48\).

With \(\beta = 100\): \(p_{\min} = 48/100 = 0.48\).

(b) Gain from cooperation: per-player payoff increases from \(\pi_{\text{Nash}} = 400\) to \(\pi_{\text{social}} = 720\), a gain of \(320\) per player, or \(5 \times 320 = 1600\) total.

Monitoring cost: \(c_m \cdot p = 200 \times 0.48 = 96\).

Net benefit: \(1600 - 96 = 1504\). Institutions are highly worthwhile — the gains from cooperation far exceed monitoring costs.

6. Evolutionary Models of Institutional Formation

We have shown that institutions can sustain cooperation, but how do institutions emerge in the first place? This is where evolutionary models of cultural evolution, developed by Boyd & Richerson and others, become essential.

6.1 Boyd & Richerson's Cultural Group Selection

Boyd & Richerson (1985, 2005) proposed that human cooperation evolved through a process of cultural group selection: groups with cooperative norms outcompete groups without them, even if cooperation is not individually advantageous within a group.

Definition — Cultural Group Selection Model

Consider \(m\) groups, each of size \(n\). Within each group, a fraction \(x_k\) are cooperators and \(1 - x_k\) are defectors. The model has two levels:

  1. Within-group selection: Defectors have higher fitness within each group (since cooperation is a PD). \(x_k\) decreases over time within each group.
  2. Between-group selection: Groups with more cooperators are more productive and grow faster, or are imitated by other groups. Groups with higher \(x_k\) spread.

Cooperation persists when between-group selection is strong enough to counteract within-group selection.

Formally, the change in the global frequency of cooperation \(\bar{x}\) across all groups is given by the Price equation (from Module 5's probability theory):

\[ \Delta\bar{x} = \underbrace{\text{Cov}(w_k, x_k)}_{\text{between-group selection}} + \underbrace{E[w_k \Delta x_k]}_{\text{within-group selection}} \]

where \(w_k\) is group \(k\)'s relative fitness and \(\Delta x_k\) is the change in cooperator frequency within group \(k\). The first term is positive (groups with more cooperators grow faster), and the second is negative (cooperators decline within each group). Cooperation increases globally when the first term dominates.

6.2 Punishment and the Second-Order Free Rider Problem

A key mechanism for sustaining cooperation is punishment: individuals who detect defectors impose costs on them. But punishment is itself costly to the punisher, creating a second-order free rider problem: why pay the cost of punishing when others can do it?

Definition — Second-Order Free Rider Problem

In a population with three types — Cooperators (C), Defectors (D), and Punishers (P, who cooperate and also punish defectors) — punishers bear an extra cost \(\gamma\) for each defector they punish, while cooperators who don't punish benefit from the deterrence without paying the cost. Non-punishing cooperators free-ride on punishers' enforcement effort.

6.3 Replicator Dynamics with Cooperators, Defectors, and Punishers

Consider a public goods game in a group of \(n\) players. Each type's behavior:

  • Cooperator (C): Contributes \(c\) to the public good, does not punish.
  • Defector (D): Does not contribute, does not punish.
  • Punisher (P): Contributes \(c\) to the public good, and pays cost \(\gamma\) to impose fine \(\beta\) on each detected defector.

Let \(x_C, x_D, x_P\) be the frequencies of each type (\(x_C + x_D + x_P = 1\)). In a well-mixed population where a random group of \(n\) is formed, the expected payoffs are approximately:

\[ f_C = \frac{rc(x_C + x_P)n}{n} - c = rc(x_C + x_P) - c \]

\[ f_D = rc(x_C + x_P) - \beta \cdot x_P \cdot n \]

\[ f_P = rc(x_C + x_P) - c - \gamma \cdot x_D \cdot n \]

where \(r\) is the multiplication factor of the public good. The replicator equations are:

\[ \dot{x}_C = x_C\bigl(f_C - \bar{f}\bigr), \quad \dot{x}_D = x_D\bigl(f_D - \bar{f}\bigr), \quad \dot{x}_P = x_P\bigl(f_P - \bar{f}\bigr) \]

where \(\bar{f} = x_C f_C + x_D f_D + x_P f_P\) is the average fitness.

Key Result — Dynamics of Punishment

The pairwise fitness differences reveal the dynamics:

\[ f_P - f_D = -c + \beta x_P n - \gamma x_D n + \beta x_P n = -c + \beta x_P n(1) + ... \]

More precisely:

\[ f_C - f_D = -c + \beta x_P n \quad \text{(cooperators vs defectors)} \]

\[ f_P - f_D = -c + \beta x_P n - \gamma x_D n + \beta x_P n \]

Wait — let us be more careful. Computing differences directly:

\[ f_C - f_D = -c + \beta x_P n \]

\[ f_P - f_C = -\gamma x_D n \]

\[ f_P - f_D = -c + \beta x_P n - \gamma x_D n \]

From these, the dynamics work in stages:

  1. When punishers are common (\(x_P\) large), \(f_C - f_D > 0\): cooperating is better than defecting because defectors get punished.
  2. But \(f_P - f_C = -\gamma x_D n < 0\) always: cooperators who don't punish always outperform punishers (the second-order free rider problem).
  3. Once defectors are eliminated (\(x_D = 0\)), \(f_P = f_C\): punishers and cooperators are selectively neutral. Drift can then erode the punisher population, eventually allowing defectors to reinvade.
Intuition — The Cycle of Punishment

Punishment faces a fundamental challenge: it works too well. Once punishers eliminate defectors, there's no one left to punish, so the cost of being a punisher is zero — but so is the benefit. Non-punishing cooperators drift into the population. As the fraction of punishers declines, the deterrent weakens, and defectors can reinvade. This creates a cyclic dynamic in finite populations.

Ostrom's insight: real institutions solve this through cheap punishment (graduated sanctions reduce \(\gamma\)), role-based monitoring (not everyone needs to punish), and intrinsic motivation (prosocial SVO types may punish even without a material incentive).

Exercise 8.5 — Punisher Invasion

In the C-D-P model above, let \(n = 10\), \(c = 2\), \(r = 3\), \(\beta = 4\), \(\gamma = 1\). Can punishers invade a population of all-defectors? Find the condition on initial punisher frequency \(x_P\) needed for punishers to have higher fitness than defectors.

In a population of mostly defectors (\(x_D \approx 1 - x_P\), \(x_C = 0\)):

\(f_P - f_D = -c + \beta x_P n - \gamma x_D n = -2 + 4 \cdot 10 \cdot x_P - 1 \cdot 10 \cdot (1 - x_P)\)

\(= -2 + 40x_P - 10 + 10x_P = -12 + 50x_P\)

For punishers to have higher fitness: \(-12 + 50x_P > 0\), so \(x_P > 12/50 = 0.24\).

Punishers need at least 24% of the population to successfully invade. A lone mutant punisher cannot invade — this requires group invasion, which is where cultural group selection plays a role: if a group of punishers migrates together, they can establish a beachhead large enough to tip the dynamics in favor of cooperation.

7. Public Goods Games with Punishment

The public goods game (PGG) with punishment has become one of the most important models in the study of human cooperation. We now develop the full mathematical framework.

7.1 The Standard Public Goods Game

Definition — Public Goods Game

Players: \(n\) individuals.

Endowment: Each player has endowment \(e\).

Action: Each player \(i\) chooses contribution \(g_i \in [0, e]\).

Public good: Total contributions are multiplied by \(r > 1\) and divided equally:

\[ \pi_i = (e - g_i) + \frac{r \sum_{j=1}^n g_j}{n} \]

The social dilemma arises when \(1 < r < n\): contributing benefits the group (\(r > 1\)) but costs the individual more than their share of the return (\(r/n < 1\)).

In the standard PGG with \(r < n\), the dominant strategy is \(g_i = 0\) (contribute nothing), since \(\frac{\partial \pi_i}{\partial g_i} = -1 + r/n < 0\). The Nash equilibrium is zero contributions, even though the social optimum is full contribution.

7.2 Adding Punishment

After contributions are observed, each player can punish other players. The extended game has two stages:

  1. Contribution stage: Player \(i\) contributes \(g_i\).
  2. Punishment stage: Player \(i\) can spend cost \(\gamma\) per punishment point to impose fine \(\beta\) per punishment point on any other player.

With three strategies (Cooperator, Defector, Punisher as defined in Section 6), the payoff functions in a well-mixed population with frequencies \((x_C, x_D, x_P)\) are:

Payoff Functions in the PGG with Punishment

For a randomly formed group of size \(n\) from a large population:

\[ f_C = e - c + \frac{rc(x_C + x_P)(n-1) + rc}{n} \approx e - c + rc(x_C + x_P) \]

\[ f_D = e + \frac{rc(x_C + x_P)(n-1)}{n} - \beta(n-1)x_P \approx e + rc(x_C + x_P) \cdot\frac{n-1}{n} - \beta(n-1)x_P \]

\[ f_P = e - c + \frac{rc(x_C + x_P)(n-1) + rc}{n} - \gamma(n-1)x_D \approx e - c + rc(x_C + x_P) - \gamma(n-1)x_D \]

where we use the approximation for large populations. We set \(e = 0\) without loss of generality (shifting all payoffs by a constant doesn't affect replicator dynamics).

7.3 Analysis of the Replicator Dynamics

Setting \(e = 0\) and writing \(x_C + x_P = 1 - x_D\), the simplified pairwise fitness differences are:

\[ f_C - f_D = -c + \beta(n-1)x_P + \frac{rc}{n} \]

For cooperation to be favored over defection, we need:

\[ x_P > \frac{c(1 - r/n)}{\beta(n-1)} = \frac{c(n - r)}{\beta n(n-1)} \]

And the second-order free rider problem:

\[ f_C - f_P = \gamma(n-1)x_D \geq 0 \]

This is always non-negative: cooperators who don't punish always weakly outperform punishers (strictly so when defectors exist).

Theorem — When Punishment Sustains Cooperation

In the PGG with punishment, the all-cooperator-and-punisher state (\(x_D = 0\), arbitrary mix of C and P) is stable against invasion by defectors if and only if:

\[ \beta(n-1)x_P > c\!\left(1 - \frac{r}{n}\right) \]

However, this state is not stable against drift between C and P when \(x_D = 0\), since cooperators and punishers earn the same payoff when there are no defectors to punish.

Full cooperation is stabilized when:

  1. The fine-to-cost ratio \(\beta/\gamma\) is sufficiently large (punishment is efficient)
  2. Group size \(n\) is not too large (punishment is more effective in smaller groups)
  3. Some mechanism prevents drift from eroding the punisher population (e.g., cultural transmission, institutional roles, or SVO-based intrinsic motivation to punish)
Worked Example — PGG with Punishment Equilibrium

Let \(n = 5\), \(c = 2\), \(r = 3\), \(\beta = 3\), \(\gamma = 1\).

Without punishment (C vs D only): \(f_C - f_D = -c + rc/n = -2 + 6/5 = -0.8 < 0\). Defection dominates. The replicator dynamics drive cooperation to extinction.

With punishment: For a population of only P and D, \(f_P - f_D\) becomes:

\(f_P - f_D = -c + rc/n + \beta(n-1)x_P - \gamma(n-1)x_D\)

\(= -0.8 + 3 \cdot 4 \cdot x_P - 1 \cdot 4 \cdot (1-x_P) = -0.8 + 12x_P - 4 + 4x_P = -4.8 + 16x_P\)

Setting \(f_P = f_D\): \(x_P^* = 4.8/16 = 0.3\). This is an interior fixed point.

For \(x_P > 0.3\), punishers grow; for \(x_P < 0.3\), defectors grow. So \(x_P = 1\) (all-punisher) is stable, and \(x_P = 0.3\) is an unstable threshold. Punishment can sustain cooperation, but punishers must start above 30% of the population.

Exercise 8.6 — The Effectiveness Ratio

Define the "punishment effectiveness ratio" as \(\rho = \beta / \gamma\) (fine imposed per unit cost to the punisher). Show that in the two-type (P vs D) model, the critical punisher frequency for invasion decreases as \(\rho\) increases. What happens in the limit \(\rho \to \infty\) (costless punishment)?

The condition \(f_P > f_D\) gives us (with \(x_C = 0\), so \(x_D = 1 - x_P\)):

\(-c + rc/n + \beta(n-1)x_P - \gamma(n-1)(1-x_P) > 0\)

\(-c(1-r/n) + (n-1)x_P(\beta + \gamma) - \gamma(n-1) > 0\)

\(x_P > \frac{c(1-r/n) + \gamma(n-1)}{(n-1)(\beta + \gamma)} = \frac{c(1-r/n)/\!\left((n-1)\right) + \gamma}{\beta + \gamma}\)

Dividing numerator and denominator by \(\gamma\):

\(x_P^* = \frac{c(1-r/n)/\!\left(\gamma(n-1)\right) + 1}{\rho + 1}\)

As \(\rho \to \infty\): \(x_P^* \to 0\). With costless punishment, even a tiny fraction of punishers can deter defection. This is why institutional punishment (which leverages collective resources, reducing the per-capita cost \(\gamma\)) is so powerful.

8. Bringing It All Together

This capstone section integrates the three main threads of this module — Social Value Orientations, Evolutionary Game Theory, and Ostrom's Institutional Analysis — into a unified mathematical framework.

8.1 The Integrated Model

Consider a population of agents who differ along two dimensions:

  1. SVO type (their angle \(\theta\)): determines how they weigh self vs. other payoffs
  2. Institutional strategy (comply or free-ride): determines their behavior in a CPR game

The key insight is that SVO affects the threshold for institutional compliance. A prosocial agent (\(\theta = 45^\circ\)) requires a smaller expected punishment to comply, because cooperation already has subjective value for them:

\[ U_i^{\text{comply}} = \cos(\theta_i)\cdot\pi_i^{\text{comply}} + \sin(\theta_i)\cdot\pi_{-i}^{\text{comply}} \]

\[ U_i^{\text{free-ride}} = \cos(\theta_i)\cdot\left(\pi_i^{\text{free-ride}} - p\beta\right) + \sin(\theta_i)\cdot\pi_{-i}^{\text{free-ride}} \]

Agent \(i\) complies when \(U_i^{\text{comply}} > U_i^{\text{free-ride}}\):

\[ \cos(\theta_i)\left(\pi_i^C - \pi_i^F + p\beta\right) + \sin(\theta_i)\left(\pi_{-i}^C - \pi_{-i}^F\right) > 0 \]

Theorem — SVO-Dependent Compliance Threshold

An agent with SVO angle \(\theta\) complies with institutional rules even when the expected punishment alone is insufficient (\(p\beta < \pi_i^F - \pi_i^C\)) if:

\[ \tan(\theta) > \frac{\pi_i^F - \pi_i^C - p\beta}{\pi_{-i}^C - \pi_{-i}^F} \]

Since \(\pi_{-i}^C > \pi_{-i}^F\) (others benefit from compliance) and \(\tan(\theta) > 0\) for prosocial types, there is a range of punishment levels that are insufficient for individualists but sufficient for prosocials. This means:

  1. Communities with more prosocial members need less institutional enforcement
  2. SVO and institutions are complements, not substitutes
  3. The evolutionary viability of prosocial types depends on the institutional environment

8.2 Co-evolution of SVO and Institutions

In the most general model, both SVO types and institutional rules evolve simultaneously:

  • Fast dynamics: Given a fixed SVO distribution and institutional rules, the population plays the CPR/PGG game and strategy frequencies evolve via replicator dynamics (behavioral dynamics).
  • Medium dynamics: SVO types evolve through cultural learning and socialization. In populations where prosocial behavior is rewarded (due to institutions), prosocial SVO spreads.
  • Slow dynamics: Institutional rules evolve through collective choice (Principle 3). Groups with better institutions outcompete groups with worse ones (cultural group selection).

8.3 Future Directions and Open Questions

The integration of SVO, EGT, and institutional analysis opens many research frontiers:

Open Research Questions
  1. Endogenous SVO: Can SVO itself evolve through cultural or genetic evolution? What fitness landscapes favor prosociality?
  2. Institutional diversity: Why do different communities develop different institutional solutions to the same dilemma? Can we predict institutional forms from game structure?
  3. Network effects: How does population structure (social networks) interact with SVO and institutions? (Extension of the assortment analysis from Section 2.4.)
  4. Scale transitions: How do institutions that work for small groups scale to larger populations? (Related to Ostrom's Principle 8 on nested enterprises.)
  5. Climate commons: Can the CPR framework be applied to global commons like the atmosphere? What are the mathematical barriers to cooperation at global scale?
  6. Digital commons: How do these models apply to open-source software, Wikipedia, and other knowledge commons where the resource is non-rivalrous?
The Big Picture

Ostrom's lasting contribution was showing that the "tragedy of the commons" is not inevitable. Communities can and do craft institutions that solve social dilemmas. The mathematics of evolutionary game theory explains when and how: institutions work by modifying the payoff matrix, changing the information structure, and altering the dynamics of cultural evolution. Social value orientations provide the individual-level foundation: people are not purely self-interested, and this heterogeneity is precisely what allows institutional entrepreneurs to build cooperative norms.

The mathematical toolkit you have built in this course — from algebra through differential equations, from Nash equilibria through replicator dynamics — is exactly what is needed to formalize these insights and push them further. The equations are not just abstract exercises; they are lenses for understanding how cooperation and institutions work in the real world.

Exercise 8.7 — Capstone Problem

Consider a community of \(n = 10\) fishers sharing a fishery modeled as a CPR game with \(a = 100\), \(b = 1\). The community has 60% prosocial (\(\theta = 45^\circ\)) and 40% individualistic (\(\theta = 0^\circ\)) members. They are considering an institution with monitoring probability \(p = 0.5\) and fine \(\beta\).

(a) Calculate the Nash equilibrium harvest and social optimum without institutions.
(b) Find the minimum fine \(\beta\) needed to make individualistic types comply.
(c) Do prosocial types need any fine at all to cooperate? Explain using the SVO utility function.
(d) What is the net gain to the community from the institution (assuming compliance)?

(a) Nash: \(h^*_N = \frac{100}{11} \approx 9.09\), \(H^*_N = \frac{1000}{11} \approx 90.9\). Social optimum: \(h^*_S = \frac{100}{20} = 5\), \(H^*_S = 50\).

\(\pi^*_N = 9.09 \times (100 - 90.9) \approx 82.6\) per player. \(\pi^*_S = 5 \times 50 = 250\) per player.

(b) For individualistic types (\(\theta = 0\)), compliance requires the fine to make over-harvesting unprofitable. The gain from deviating (harvesting \(h^*_N\) instead of \(h^*_S = 5\)) when others comply is:

Best-response to \(H_{-i} = 9 \times 5 = 45\): \(h_i^{BR} = \frac{100 - 45}{2} = 27.5\). Payoff from deviating: \(27.5 \times (100 - 72.5) = 756.25\). Payoff from complying: \(5 \times (100 - 50) = 250\).

Expected fine needed: \(p \cdot \beta \cdot (h^{BR} - \bar{h}) \geq 756.25 - 250\). \(0.5 \cdot \beta \cdot 22.5 \geq 506.25\), so \(\beta \geq 44.96 \approx 45\).

(c) Prosocial types (\(\theta = 45^\circ\)) evaluate: \(U = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\pi_i + \pi_{-i})\). When they comply and others comply: \(U^C = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(250 + 9 \times 250/9) = \frac{500}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 353.6\).

If they deviate: \(\pi_i = 756.25\) but \(\pi_{-i}\) drops because total harvest increases. With \(H = 72.5\), each other player gets \(5 \times (100 - 72.5) = 137.5\), so total others' payoff \(= 9 \times 137.5 = 1237.5\). \(U^D = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(756.25 + 1237.5/9 \times ...) \) — more carefully: \(U^D = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(756.25 + \bar{\pi}_{-i})\) where we use the average other's payoff \(= 137.5\). So \(U^D = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(756.25 + 137.5) = \frac{893.75}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 632.0\).

Hmm, the deviation is still profitable for the prosocial type in this case (632 > 354), but the temptation is reduced. With the fine: \(U^D = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(756.25 - p\beta(22.5) + 137.5)\). The prosocial type needs a fine \(p\beta \cdot 22.5 > 893.75 - 500 = 393.75\), so \(\beta > 393.75 / (0.5 \times 22.5) = 35\). Lower than the 45 needed for individualists.

(d) Without institutions: total payoff = \(10 \times 82.6 = 826\). With institutions: total payoff = \(10 \times 250 = 2500\). Net gain: \(2500 - 826 = 1674\).
