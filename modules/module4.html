Why This Module Matters

This is arguably the most important mathematical module in the entire course. The replicator equation — the central equation of evolutionary game theory — is a differential equation. Everything we study here applies directly: finding equilibria means finding equilibrium strategy mixes, stability analysis determines whether an equilibrium is evolutionarily stable, and phase portraits show us how populations of strategies evolve over time.

If you understand this module well, Module 7 (Replicator Dynamics) will feel like a natural continuation rather than a leap.

1. What is a Differential Equation?

In Module 3 (Calculus), we learned that a derivative \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) represents the rate of change of \(x\) with respect to time \(t\). A differential equation is simply an equation that tells us how this rate of change depends on the current state.

Definition — Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE)

An ordinary differential equation (ODE) is an equation of the form:

\[\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x, t)\]

It tells us: given the current value of \(x\) (and possibly the current time \(t\)), what is the rate at which \(x\) is changing?

The word "ordinary" distinguishes these from partial differential equations, which involve multiple independent variables. For evolutionary game theory, ordinary differential equations are what we need.

The Key Idea: A Rule for Change

Think of a differential equation as a recipe: "Given where you are right now, here is how fast you should move and in which direction." If you follow this recipe starting from some initial position, you trace out a path through time — that path is the solution.

Intuition — Navigation Analogy

Imagine you are in a river with a current. At every point in the river, the current has a certain strength and direction. The differential equation is like a map of the current: it tells you, at every position, which way and how fast the water pushes you. Your actual path through the river — that depends on where you start (your initial condition).

Solutions Are Functions

Unlike algebraic equations where the solution is a number (e.g., \(x^2 = 4\) gives \(x = \pm 2\)), the solution to a differential equation is a function. We seek \(x(t)\) — a function that tells us the value of \(x\) at every time \(t\).

Definition — Initial Value Problem (IVP)

An initial value problem consists of a differential equation together with an initial condition:

\[\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x), \quad x(0) = x_0\]

The initial condition \(x(0) = x_0\) specifies where the system starts. Given a well-behaved function \(f\), there is a unique solution \(x(t)\) for each choice of \(x_0\).

Example 1: Exponential Growth

The simplest and most important differential equation:

Example — Exponential Growth
\[\frac{dx}{dt} = rx\]

This says: "The rate of growth is proportional to the current size." The constant \(r\) is the growth rate.

  • If \(r > 0\): the population grows — the bigger it is, the faster it grows.
  • If \(r < 0\): the population shrinks — exponential decay.
  • If \(r = 0\): no change — the population stays constant.

The solution (which we will derive shortly) is:

\[x(t) = x_0 e^{rt}\]

where \(x_0 = x(0)\) is the initial population size.

Example 2: Logistic Growth

Example — Logistic Growth

Exponential growth cannot continue forever — resources are finite. The logistic equation adds a correction term:

\[\frac{dx}{dt} = rx\left(1 - \frac{x}{K}\right)\]

where:

  • \(r\) is the intrinsic growth rate
  • \(K\) is the carrying capacity — the maximum sustainable population

When \(x\) is small compared to \(K\), the term \((1 - x/K) \approx 1\) and growth is approximately exponential. As \(x\) approaches \(K\), the growth rate slows. At \(x = K\), growth stops entirely.

Connection to EGT

The replicator equation from evolutionary game theory has the form:

\[\frac{dx_i}{dt} = x_i \left( f_i(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{f}(\mathbf{x}) \right)\]

This is a differential equation! It says: the rate of change of strategy \(i\)'s frequency is proportional to its current frequency times (its fitness minus the average fitness). We will study this in detail in Module 7. For now, notice the structural similarity to the logistic equation — both have the form "frequency times something."

2. Solving Simple ODEs

Not all differential equations can be solved analytically (i.e., with a formula). But many important ones can, and the technique of separation of variables is the most fundamental method.

Separation of Variables

Method — Separation of Variables

For an ODE of the form \(\frac{dx}{dt} = g(x) \cdot h(t)\), we can separate the variables:

\[\frac{1}{g(x)}\,dx = h(t)\,dt\]

Then integrate both sides:

\[\int \frac{1}{g(x)}\,dx = \int h(t)\,dt\]

The key idea: move all \(x\) terms to one side, all \(t\) terms to the other, then integrate.

Worked Example: Solving the Exponential Growth Equation

Worked Example — Solving \(\frac{dx}{dt} = rx\)

Step 1: Separate variables. Divide both sides by \(x\):

\[\frac{1}{x}\frac{dx}{dt} = r\]

Rewrite as:

\[\frac{1}{x}\,dx = r\,dt\]

Step 2: Integrate both sides:

\[\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \int r\,dt\]
\[\ln|x| = rt + C\]

where \(C\) is the constant of integration.

Step 3: Solve for \(x\). Exponentiate both sides:

\[|x| = e^{rt + C} = e^C \cdot e^{rt}\]

Let \(A = e^C\) (a positive constant):

\[x(t) = A e^{rt}\]

Step 4: Apply the initial condition \(x(0) = x_0\):

\[x(0) = A e^{0} = A = x_0\]

Solution:

\[\boxed{x(t) = x_0 e^{rt}}\]

Worked Example: Solving the Logistic Equation

Worked Example — Solving \(\frac{dx}{dt} = rx\left(1 - \frac{x}{K}\right)\)

Step 1: Separate variables:

\[\frac{dx}{x(1 - x/K)} = r\,dt\]

Step 2: Use partial fractions on the left side. Write:

\[\frac{1}{x(1 - x/K)} = \frac{1}{x} + \frac{1/K}{1 - x/K}\]

You can verify this by combining the fractions on the right.

Step 3: Integrate both sides:

\[\int\left(\frac{1}{x} + \frac{1/K}{1 - x/K}\right)dx = \int r\,dt\]
\[\ln|x| - \ln|1 - x/K| = rt + C\]
\[\ln\left|\frac{x}{1 - x/K}\right| = rt + C\]

Step 4: Exponentiate and solve for \(x(t)\). After applying the initial condition \(x(0) = x_0\), we get:

\[\boxed{x(t) = \frac{K}{1 + \left(\frac{K - x_0}{x_0}\right) e^{-rt}}}\]

Behavior:

  • As \(t \to 0\): \(x(0) = x_0\) (initial condition satisfied).
  • As \(t \to \infty\): the exponential term \(e^{-rt} \to 0\), so \(x(t) \to K\) (approaches carrying capacity).
  • The solution traces out an S-shaped (sigmoid) curve.
Intuition — Why Solutions Methods Matter for EGT

In many cases, we will not solve the replicator equation exactly. Instead, we will study it qualitatively — asking "where are the equilibria?" and "are they stable?" rather than "what is the exact formula?" But understanding how solutions work (exponential growth, sigmoidal saturation) gives us deep intuition about the dynamics. When we see eigenvalues with negative real parts, we know the system decays exponentially toward the equilibrium — because we understand the exponential solution.

A Note on Autonomous ODEs

Definition — Autonomous ODE

An ODE is called autonomous if the right-hand side does not depend explicitly on time:

\[\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x)\]

Compare this to \(\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x, t)\), where the rule for change itself changes over time. Almost all equations in evolutionary game theory are autonomous — the rules of the game do not change as time passes. This makes the analysis considerably simpler.

3. Systems of ODEs

So far, we have tracked a single quantity \(x(t)\). But in evolutionary game theory, we typically track multiple strategy frequencies simultaneously. If there are \(n\) strategies, we need \(n\) equations.

Definition — System of ODEs

A system of ordinary differential equations is a collection of coupled equations:

\[\begin{aligned} \frac{dx}{dt} &= f(x, y) \\[6pt] \frac{dy}{dt} &= g(x, y) \end{aligned}\]

Here \(x\) and \(y\) change simultaneously, and the rate of change of each can depend on both variables. The system is coupled because the equation for \(x\) involves \(y\) and vice versa.

Intuition — Why Systems?

In a two-strategy game (e.g., Hawk vs. Dove), we need to track the fraction of Hawks \(x\) and the fraction of Doves \(y = 1 - x\). In a three-strategy game (Rock-Paper-Scissors), we need \(x_1, x_2, x_3\) with \(x_1 + x_2 + x_3 = 1\). The replicator dynamics for \(n\) strategies is a system of \(n\) coupled ODEs.

Vector Notation

We can write a system compactly using vectors. Define:

\[\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{pmatrix} f(x,y) \\ g(x,y) \end{pmatrix}\]

Then the system becomes:

\[\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x})\]

This is exactly the same as the single-variable case, just with bold vectors.

Linear Systems

A particularly important class of systems is linear systems, where \(f\) and \(g\) are linear functions of \(x\) and \(y\):

Definition — Linear System
\[\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt} = A\mathbf{x}\]

where \(A\) is a constant matrix. Written out:

\[\begin{pmatrix} \dot{x} \\ \dot{y} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}\]

(Here we use the dot notation \(\dot{x} = \frac{dx}{dt}\) for brevity.)

Example — A Linear System

Consider the system:

\[\begin{aligned} \dot{x} &= -2x + y \\ \dot{y} &= x - 2y \end{aligned}\]

In matrix form: \(\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A\mathbf{x}\) where

\[A = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\]

The eigenvalues of \(A\) determine the qualitative behavior. Recall from Module 2 that eigenvalues \(\lambda\) satisfy \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\):

\[(-2 - \lambda)^2 - 1 = 0 \implies \lambda^2 + 4\lambda + 3 = 0 \implies \lambda = -1, -3\]

Both eigenvalues are negative, so the origin is a stable node — all trajectories converge to \((0, 0)\). We will formalize this in Section 5.

The Solution of Linear Systems

For the linear system \(\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A\mathbf{x}\), if \(A\) has eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\) with corresponding eigenvectors \(\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2\), the general solution is:

\[\mathbf{x}(t) = c_1 e^{\lambda_1 t} \mathbf{v}_1 + c_2 e^{\lambda_2 t} \mathbf{v}_2\]

where \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are constants determined by the initial conditions. Notice that each term involves an exponential \(e^{\lambda t}\) — exactly the solution form we found for the single-variable case! This is why eigenvalues control the behavior:

  • If \(\lambda < 0\): the term \(e^{\lambda t}\) decays to 0 — the component shrinks.
  • If \(\lambda > 0\): the term \(e^{\lambda t}\) grows — the component explodes.
  • If \(\lambda\) is complex: the exponential produces oscillations (spirals).
Connection to EGT

The multi-strategy replicator equation is a system of ODEs. Near an equilibrium, we can approximate it as a linear system \(\dot{\mathbf{x}} \approx J\mathbf{x}\) where \(J\) is the Jacobian matrix. The eigenvalues of \(J\) then tell us whether the equilibrium is evolutionarily stable. This is the core technique we will use in Module 7.

4. Fixed Points (Equilibria)

A fixed point (also called an equilibrium or rest point) is a state where the system stays put — nothing changes.

Definition — Fixed Point

A point \(x^*\) is a fixed point of the ODE \(\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x)\) if:

\[f(x^*) = 0\]

For a system \(\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x})\), a fixed point \(\mathbf{x}^*\) satisfies \(\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{0}\) — all components of the rate of change are zero simultaneously.

Intuition — Ball on a Landscape

Think of a ball rolling on a hilly landscape. Fixed points are where the ball can sit without moving: hilltops (unstable — any nudge sends it rolling away), valley bottoms (stable — nudges lead to return), and saddle points (stable in one direction, unstable in another).

Finding Fixed Points: One Variable

Worked Example — Fixed Points of the Logistic Equation

Find the fixed points of \(\frac{dx}{dt} = rx(1 - x/K)\).

Solution: Set \(f(x) = 0\):

\[rx\left(1 - \frac{x}{K}\right) = 0\]

This is zero when:

  • \(x^* = 0\) (extinction), or
  • \(1 - x^*/K = 0 \implies x^* = K\) (carrying capacity)

So there are two fixed points. Which one is stable? We will answer this in Section 5, but intuitively: if you start with a small population, it grows toward \(K\); if you start above \(K\), it decreases toward \(K\). So \(x^* = K\) is stable and \(x^* = 0\) is unstable (for \(r > 0\)).

Finding Fixed Points: Two Variables

Worked Example — Fixed Points of a 2D System

Find the fixed points of the system:

\[\begin{aligned} \dot{x} &= x(3 - x - 2y) \\ \dot{y} &= y(2 - x - y) \end{aligned}\]

Solution: We need both \(\dot{x} = 0\) and \(\dot{y} = 0\) simultaneously.

From \(\dot{x} = 0\): either \(x = 0\) or \(3 - x - 2y = 0\).

From \(\dot{y} = 0\): either \(y = 0\) or \(2 - x - y = 0\).

Combining all cases:

Case 1: \(x = 0\) and \(y = 0\): Fixed point \((0, 0)\).

Case 2: \(x = 0\) and \(2 - 0 - y = 0\): Fixed point \((0, 2)\).

Case 3: \(3 - x - 0 = 0\) and \(y = 0\): Fixed point \((3, 0)\).

Case 4: \(3 - x - 2y = 0\) and \(2 - x - y = 0\). Subtracting: \(1 - y = 0\), so \(y = 1\), then \(x = 1\). Fixed point \((1, 1)\).

The four fixed points are: \((0,0)\), \((0,2)\), \((3,0)\), and \((1,1)\).

Connection to EGT

In the replicator equation, fixed points correspond to equilibrium strategy distributions. These include:

  • Pure strategy equilibria (corners of the simplex): only one strategy survives.
  • Mixed equilibria (interior of the simplex): multiple strategies coexist at specific frequencies.
  • Edge equilibria: some strategies present, others extinct.

Finding fixed points of the replicator equation is equivalent to finding Nash equilibria of the underlying game. This is one of the deep connections between dynamics and static game theory.

5. Stability Analysis

Finding fixed points tells us where the system can rest. Stability analysis tells us whether it actually will — do small perturbations die out (stable) or grow (unstable)?

Stability in One Dimension

For a one-dimensional ODE \(\dot{x} = f(x)\), we can determine stability by looking at the derivative \(f'(x)\) evaluated at the fixed point.

Theorem — Stability Criterion (1D)

Let \(x^*\) be a fixed point of \(\dot{x} = f(x)\). Then:

  • If \(f'(x^*) < 0\): \(x^*\) is stable (attracting).
  • If \(f'(x^*) > 0\): \(x^*\) is unstable (repelling).
  • If \(f'(x^*) = 0\): inconclusive (need higher-order analysis).
Worked Example — Stability of Logistic Fixed Points

For \(f(x) = rx(1 - x/K)\), we found fixed points \(x^* = 0\) and \(x^* = K\).

Compute \(f'(x)\):

\[f'(x) = r - \frac{2rx}{K}\]

At \(x^* = 0\): \(f'(0) = r\).

  • If \(r > 0\): \(f'(0) > 0\) — unstable. A small population will grow away from 0.

At \(x^* = K\): \(f'(K) = r - 2r = -r\).

  • If \(r > 0\): \(f'(K) = -r < 0\) — stable. The population is attracted to \(K\).

This confirms our intuition: populations grow toward the carrying capacity.

Stability in Higher Dimensions: The Jacobian

For systems with multiple variables, we generalize the derivative \(f'(x^*)\) to the Jacobian matrix.

Definition — Jacobian Matrix

For a system \(\dot{x} = f(x,y)\), \(\dot{y} = g(x,y)\), the Jacobian matrix at a point \((x_0, y_0)\) is:

\[J = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \\[6pt] \frac{\partial g}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial g}{\partial y} \end{pmatrix}\Bigg|_{(x_0, y_0)}\]

The Jacobian captures how the system behaves for small perturbations around the point \((x_0, y_0)\). It is the "best linear approximation" to the nonlinear system near that point.

Linearization

Near a fixed point \(\mathbf{x}^*\), let \(\boldsymbol{\xi} = \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}^*\) be a small perturbation. Then:

\[\frac{d\boldsymbol{\xi}}{dt} \approx J\big|_{\mathbf{x}^*} \boldsymbol{\xi}\]

This is a linear system! We have reduced the problem of understanding a nonlinear system near its equilibrium to understanding a linear system. And we know how to analyze linear systems: compute eigenvalues.

Eigenvalue Classification

Theorem — Stability via Eigenvalues (Hartman-Grobman)

Let \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\) be the eigenvalues of the Jacobian \(J\) at a fixed point \(\mathbf{x}^*\). If no eigenvalue has zero real part, then the local behavior is determined by the eigenvalues:

Eigenvalues Type Behavior
\(\lambda_1, \lambda_2 < 0\) (both real, negative) Stable node All trajectories approach \(\mathbf{x}^*\)
\(\lambda_1, \lambda_2 > 0\) (both real, positive) Unstable node All trajectories move away from \(\mathbf{x}^*\)
\(\lambda_1 < 0 < \lambda_2\) (real, opposite signs) Saddle point Attracts along one direction, repels along another
\(\alpha \pm i\beta\) with \(\alpha < 0\) Stable spiral Spirals inward toward \(\mathbf{x}^*\)
\(\alpha \pm i\beta\) with \(\alpha > 0\) Unstable spiral Spirals outward away from \(\mathbf{x}^*\)
\(\pm i\beta\) (purely imaginary) Center Closed orbits around \(\mathbf{x}^*\) (neutrally stable)
Worked Example — Classifying the Fixed Point (1,1)

Return to our earlier system:

\[\dot{x} = x(3 - x - 2y), \quad \dot{y} = y(2 - x - y)\]

Compute the Jacobian. Let \(f = x(3 - x - 2y)\), \(g = y(2 - x - y)\):

\[J = \begin{pmatrix} 3 - 2x - 2y & -2x \\ -y & 2 - x - 2y \end{pmatrix}\]

At the fixed point \((1, 1)\):

\[J\big|_{(1,1)} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 - 2 - 2 & -2 \\ -1 & 2 - 1 - 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & -2 \\ -1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\]

Find eigenvalues from \(\det(J - \lambda I) = 0\):

\[(-1 - \lambda)^2 - 2 = 0\]
\[\lambda^2 + 2\lambda - 1 = 0 \implies \lambda = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{4 + 4}}{2} = -1 \pm \sqrt{2}\]

So \(\lambda_1 = -1 + \sqrt{2} \approx 0.414\) and \(\lambda_2 = -1 - \sqrt{2} \approx -2.414\).

One eigenvalue is positive and one is negative: the fixed point \((1, 1)\) is a saddle point. It attracts trajectories from some directions and repels them in others.

Lyapunov Stability

Definition — Lyapunov Stability

There are several levels of stability:

  • Lyapunov stable: trajectories starting near \(\mathbf{x}^*\) stay near \(\mathbf{x}^*\) forever (they do not necessarily converge, just stay close).
  • Asymptotically stable: trajectories starting near \(\mathbf{x}^*\) converge to \(\mathbf{x}^*\) as \(t \to \infty\). This is stronger than Lyapunov stability.
  • Unstable: there exist trajectories starting arbitrarily close to \(\mathbf{x}^*\) that move far away.

A center (purely imaginary eigenvalues) is Lyapunov stable but not asymptotically stable — orbits circle around the equilibrium forever without approaching it.

Connection to EGT

In evolutionary game theory, asymptotic stability of a fixed point of the replicator equation is closely related to the concept of an evolutionarily stable strategy (ESS). Specifically:

  • Every ESS is an asymptotically stable fixed point of the replicator dynamics.
  • A Nash equilibrium is a fixed point, but it may not be stable.
  • Saddle points correspond to equilibria that are evolutionarily unstable — the population evolves away from them after any perturbation.

This is why stability analysis is so central: it is the mathematical mechanism that distinguishes robust evolutionary outcomes from fragile ones.

6. Phase Portraits

A phase portrait is a visual representation of how a system evolves. Instead of plotting \(x\) versus time, we plot the state space — the space of all possible values of \((x, y)\) — and draw arrows showing the direction and speed of change at each point.

Definition — Phase Portrait

A phase portrait of the system \(\dot{x} = f(x,y)\), \(\dot{y} = g(x,y)\) is a plot in the \((x, y)\)-plane showing:

  • The vector field: arrows at each point showing the direction and magnitude of \((f(x,y), g(x,y))\).
  • Trajectories: curves that solutions trace out from various initial conditions.
  • Fixed points: marked where the vector field vanishes.

Types of Phase Portraits

The eigenvalue classification from the previous section determines the shape of the phase portrait near each fixed point. Here is a summary of what each type looks like:

Stable Node

All trajectories flow straight toward the fixed point. If the eigenvalues have different magnitudes, trajectories first align with the slower eigenvector, then approach along it.

Unstable Node

The reverse of a stable node: all trajectories flow away from the fixed point. The direction of the faster-growing eigenvector dominates at large distances.

Saddle Point

There are two special directions: the stable manifold (trajectories approach) and the unstable manifold (trajectories depart). All other trajectories approach along the stable manifold and then veer away along the unstable manifold.

Center

Trajectories are closed loops (ellipses for linear systems) circling the fixed point forever. The system is conservative — orbits neither grow nor shrink.

Stable Spiral

Trajectories spiral inward toward the fixed point. The rotation comes from the imaginary part of the eigenvalues; the inward motion from the negative real part.

Unstable Spiral

Trajectories spiral outward from the fixed point. Same rotation, but the positive real part causes the spiral to expand.

Intuition — Phase Portraits in EGT

In evolutionary game theory, the state space is the simplex — the set of all probability distributions over strategies. For two strategies, it is the interval \([0, 1]\). For three strategies, it is a triangle. Phase portraits on the simplex show us the flow of strategy evolution: which strategies grow, which shrink, and where the population ultimately settles.

In Module 7, we will draw replicator dynamics phase portraits on the simplex. The concepts you learn here — vector fields, trajectories, fixed points, stable and unstable manifolds — carry over directly.

Nullclines

Definition — Nullclines

The \(x\)-nullcline is the set of points where \(\dot{x} = 0\) (i.e., \(f(x,y) = 0\)).

The \(y\)-nullcline is the set of points where \(\dot{y} = 0\) (i.e., \(g(x,y) = 0\)).

Fixed points occur where nullclines intersect (both \(\dot{x} = 0\) and \(\dot{y} = 0\)).

Nullclines are incredibly useful for sketching phase portraits by hand. On the \(x\)-nullcline, the flow is purely vertical (only \(y\) changes). On the \(y\)-nullcline, the flow is purely horizontal (only \(x\) changes). Between nullclines, you can determine the direction of flow by checking the signs of \(\dot{x}\) and \(\dot{y}\).

Worked Example — Nullclines for a Competitive System

For the system \(\dot{x} = x(3 - x - 2y)\), \(\dot{y} = y(2 - x - y)\):

\(x\)-nullclines (\(\dot{x} = 0\)): \(x = 0\) or \(x + 2y = 3\).

\(y\)-nullclines (\(\dot{y} = 0\)): \(y = 0\) or \(x + y = 2\).

The nullclines divide the first quadrant into regions. In each region, we can determine the signs of \(\dot{x}\) and \(\dot{y}\), which tells us the qualitative direction of the flow. Fixed points are where the nullcline curves cross: \((0,0)\), \((3,0)\), \((0,3/2)\), and \((1,1)\).

7. The Logistic Equation as a Preview of Replicator Dynamics

We now arrive at the conceptual bridge between this module and Module 7. The logistic-like equation and the replicator equation share the same mathematical structure. Understanding this connection will make the transition seamless.

The Replicator Form

Consider a population with two strategies. Let \(x\) be the frequency of strategy 1 (so \(1 - x\) is the frequency of strategy 2). Suppose strategy 1 has fitness \(a\) and strategy 2 has fitness \(b\). The replicator equation says:

\[\frac{dx}{dt} = x \left( f_1 - \bar{f} \right)\]

where \(f_1 = a\) is the fitness of strategy 1 and \(\bar{f} = xa + (1-x)b\) is the average fitness. Substituting:

\[\frac{dx}{dt} = x \Big( a - \big[xa + (1-x)b\big] \Big) = x \Big( a - xa - b + xb \Big)\]
\[= x(a - b)(1 - x)\]
Key Result — The Two-Strategy Replicator Equation

For two strategies with constant fitnesses \(a\) and \(b\), the replicator equation reduces to:

\[\boxed{\frac{dx}{dt} = x(1-x)(a-b)}\]

This is exactly a logistic equation! Compare with \(\dot{x} = rx(1 - x/K)\): set \(K = 1\) and \(r = a - b\), and they are the same equation.

Analyzing the Structure

Let us analyze \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)(a-b)\) using the tools from this module:

Worked Example — Complete Analysis of \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)(a-b)\)

Step 1: Find fixed points. Set \(\dot{x} = 0\):

\[x(1-x)(a-b) = 0\]

This gives us: \(x^* = 0\) or \(x^* = 1\) (or \(a = b\), which is a degenerate case where everything is a fixed point).

Step 2: Determine stability. Let \(f(x) = x(1-x)(a-b)\). Then:

\[f'(x) = (1-x)(a-b) + x(-1)(a-b) = (a-b)(1 - 2x)\]

At \(x^* = 0\): \(f'(0) = (a - b)\).

  • If \(a > b\): \(f'(0) > 0\) — unstable. Strategy 1 is fitter, so any small amount of it will grow.
  • If \(a < b\): \(f'(0) < 0\) — stable. Strategy 2 dominates; strategy 1 goes extinct.

At \(x^* = 1\): \(f'(1) = -(a - b)\).

  • If \(a > b\): \(f'(1) < 0\) — stable. Strategy 1 takes over.
  • If \(a < b\): \(f'(1) > 0\) — unstable. Strategy 1 does not survive.

Conclusion:

  • If \(a > b\): strategy 1 is fitter. \(x^* = 0\) is unstable, \(x^* = 1\) is stable. The population evolves toward 100% strategy 1. Strategy 1 is the ESS.
  • If \(a < b\): strategy 2 is fitter. \(x^* = 0\) is stable, \(x^* = 1\) is unstable. The population evolves toward 0% strategy 1 (100% strategy 2).
  • If \(a = b\): every point is a fixed point. No selection pressure.

When Fitness Depends on Frequency

The example above used constant fitnesses. In real evolutionary games, fitness depends on what everyone else is doing (frequency-dependent selection). For example, in the Hawk-Dove game, the fitness of Hawks depends on how many Hawks there are. This makes the equation:

\[\frac{dx}{dt} = x(1-x)\big[f_1(x) - f_2(x)\big]\]

where the fitness functions \(f_1(x)\) and \(f_2(x)\) depend on \(x\). Now interior fixed points (where \(0 < x^* < 1\)) become possible: they occur where \(f_1(x^*) = f_2(x^*)\) — where both strategies have equal fitness. This is the heart of evolutionary game theory, and we will explore it fully in Module 7.

Exercises

Exercise 1: Solving an ODE

Solve the initial value problem:

\[\frac{dx}{dt} = -3x, \quad x(0) = 5\]

What is \(x(t)\)? What happens as \(t \to \infty\)?

Using separation of variables (or recognizing this as exponential decay with \(r = -3\)):

\[x(t) = 5e^{-3t}\]

As \(t \to \infty\): \(e^{-3t} \to 0\), so \(x(t) \to 0\). The solution decays exponentially to zero. The fixed point \(x^* = 0\) is stable (since \(f'(0) = -3 < 0\)).

Exercise 2: Finding Fixed Points

Find all fixed points of the following ODE:

\[\frac{dx}{dt} = x^2 - 4x + 3\]

Determine the stability of each fixed point.

Finding fixed points: Set \(x^2 - 4x + 3 = 0\):

\[(x - 1)(x - 3) = 0 \implies x^* = 1 \text{ or } x^* = 3\]

Stability: Compute \(f'(x) = 2x - 4\).

  • At \(x^* = 1\): \(f'(1) = 2 - 4 = -2 < 0\) — stable.
  • At \(x^* = 3\): \(f'(3) = 6 - 4 = 2 > 0\) — unstable.

Solutions starting between 1 and 3 converge to \(x^* = 1\). Solutions starting above 3 diverge to \(+\infty\). Solutions starting below 1 converge to \(x^* = 1\).

Exercise 3: Jacobian and Eigenvalues

Consider the system:

\[\begin{aligned} \dot{x} &= y \\ \dot{y} &= -x - 0.5y \end{aligned}\]

(a) Find the fixed point(s). (b) Compute the Jacobian. (c) Find the eigenvalues and classify the fixed point.

(a) Set \(\dot{x} = 0\) and \(\dot{y} = 0\):

\[y = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad -x - 0.5(0) = 0 \implies x = 0\]

The only fixed point is \((0, 0)\).

(b) The Jacobian is:

\[J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & -0.5 \end{pmatrix}\]

(This is already a linear system, so \(J\) is the same at every point.)

(c) Eigenvalues: \(\det(J - \lambda I) = 0\):

\[-\lambda(-0.5 - \lambda) - (-1) = 0\]
\[\lambda^2 + 0.5\lambda + 1 = 0\]
\[\lambda = \frac{-0.5 \pm \sqrt{0.25 - 4}}{2} = \frac{-0.5 \pm \sqrt{-3.75}}{2} = -0.25 \pm i\frac{\sqrt{3.75}}{2}\]

The eigenvalues are complex with negative real part \(\alpha = -0.25 < 0\). The fixed point is a stable spiral. Trajectories spiral inward toward the origin with damped oscillations.

Exercise 4: Two-Strategy Replicator Analysis

Two strategies have frequency-dependent fitnesses:

\[f_1(x) = 3 - 2x, \quad f_2(x) = 1 + x\]

where \(x\) is the frequency of strategy 1.

(a) Write the replicator equation \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)[f_1(x) - f_2(x)]\).

(b) Find all fixed points (including interior ones).

(c) Determine the stability of each fixed point. Which strategy (or mix) is the ESS?

(a) The fitness difference is:

\[f_1(x) - f_2(x) = (3 - 2x) - (1 + x) = 2 - 3x\]

So the replicator equation is:

\[\dot{x} = x(1-x)(2 - 3x)\]

(b) Fixed points: set \(\dot{x} = 0\):

  • \(x^* = 0\) (all strategy 2)
  • \(x^* = 1\) (all strategy 1)
  • \(2 - 3x = 0 \implies x^* = 2/3\) (interior fixed point — mixed population)

(c) Let \(h(x) = x(1-x)(2-3x)\). Compute \(h'(x)\):

\[h(x) = (2x - 3x^2)(1-x)(2-3x) \text{ ... but it is easier to use the product rule carefully.}\]

Actually, expand first: \(h(x) = x(1-x)(2-3x) = (x - x^2)(2-3x) = 2x - 3x^2 - 2x^2 + 3x^3 = 2x - 5x^2 + 3x^3\).

\[h'(x) = 2 - 10x + 9x^2\]
  • At \(x^* = 0\): \(h'(0) = 2 > 0\) — unstable.
  • At \(x^* = 1\): \(h'(1) = 2 - 10 + 9 = 1 > 0\) — unstable.
  • At \(x^* = 2/3\): \(h'(2/3) = 2 - 20/3 + 9(4/9) = 2 - 20/3 + 4 = 6 - 20/3 = -2/3 < 0\) — stable.

The interior fixed point \(x^* = 2/3\) is the ESS. The population evolves to a mix of 2/3 strategy 1 and 1/3 strategy 2. At this mix, both strategies have equal fitness: \(f_1(2/3) = 3 - 4/3 = 5/3\) and \(f_2(2/3) = 1 + 2/3 = 5/3\). This is a stable polymorphism.

Exercise 5: Phase Portrait Sketching

Consider the linear system:

\[\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \mathbf{x}\]

(a) Find the eigenvalues. (b) What type of fixed point is the origin? (c) Describe the trajectories qualitatively. (d) This system models a famous physical system — which one?

(a) Eigenvalues: \(\det(A - \lambda I) = -\lambda(-\lambda) - (-1) = \lambda^2 + 1 = 0\), so \(\lambda = \pm i\).

(b) Purely imaginary eigenvalues: the origin is a center.

(c) Trajectories are closed ellipses (in fact, circles) around the origin. The system is:

\[\dot{x} = y, \quad \dot{y} = -x\]

which gives \(\ddot{x} = \dot{y} = -x\), i.e., \(\ddot{x} + x = 0\). The solution is \(x(t) = A\cos(t + \phi)\) — simple harmonic oscillation.

(d) This is the simple harmonic oscillator (undamped spring, frictionless pendulum for small angles). In EGT, centers arise in games like Rock-Paper-Scissors under the replicator dynamics, producing cyclic oscillations in strategy frequencies.

Exercise 6: Nullclines and Qualitative Analysis

For the Lotka-Volterra predator-prey system:

\[\begin{aligned} \dot{x} &= x(2 - y) \\ \dot{y} &= y(x - 1) \end{aligned}\]

(a) Find the nullclines. (b) Find the fixed points. (c) Compute the Jacobian at the interior fixed point and find the eigenvalues. (d) What is the behavior near this fixed point?

(a) Nullclines:

  • \(x\)-nullclines: \(x = 0\) or \(y = 2\).
  • \(y\)-nullclines: \(y = 0\) or \(x = 1\).

(b) Fixed points (intersections of nullclines):

  • \((0, 0)\): trivial (no prey, no predator).
  • \((1, 2)\): interior fixed point (coexistence).

(c) Jacobian:

\[J = \begin{pmatrix} 2 - y & -x \\ y & x - 1 \end{pmatrix}\]

At \((1, 2)\):

\[J\big|_{(1,2)} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 2 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\]

Eigenvalues: \(\lambda^2 + 2 = 0\), so \(\lambda = \pm i\sqrt{2}\).

(d) Purely imaginary eigenvalues: the interior fixed point is a center. Trajectories form closed loops around \((1, 2)\) — the predator and prey populations oscillate forever without damping. This is the classic Lotka-Volterra cycle.

In EGT, similar cyclic dynamics appear in Rock-Paper-Scissors games under the replicator equation.

Exercise 7: From Payoff Matrix to Replicator Equation

Consider a symmetric two-player game with payoff matrix:

\[A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\]

Strategy 1's fitness against a population with frequency \(x\) of strategy 1 is \(f_1(x) = 2x + 4(1-x)\) and strategy 2's fitness is \(f_2(x) = 1 \cdot x + 3(1-x)\).

(a) Simplify \(f_1(x)\) and \(f_2(x)\). (b) Write the replicator equation \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)(f_1 - f_2)\). (c) Find and classify all fixed points. (d) Interpret the result: which strategy wins?

(a)

\[f_1(x) = 2x + 4(1-x) = 4 - 2x\]
\[f_2(x) = x + 3(1-x) = 3 - 2x\]

(b) Fitness difference: \(f_1 - f_2 = (4 - 2x) - (3 - 2x) = 1\). So:

\[\dot{x} = x(1-x)(1) = x(1-x)\]

(c) Fixed points: \(x^* = 0\) and \(x^* = 1\). No interior fixed point (since \(f_1 - f_2 = 1 \neq 0\) for all \(x\)).

Let \(h(x) = x(1-x) = x - x^2\). Then \(h'(x) = 1 - 2x\).

  • At \(x^* = 0\): \(h'(0) = 1 > 0\) — unstable.
  • At \(x^* = 1\): \(h'(1) = -1 < 0\) — stable.

(d) Strategy 1 always has higher fitness than strategy 2 (the difference is a constant 1, independent of \(x\)). Strategy 1 is strictly dominant. No matter the initial mix, the population evolves to 100% strategy 1. Strategy 1 is the ESS.

Module Summary

In this module, you learned:

  1. Differential equations describe how systems change over time via \(\dot{x} = f(x)\).
  2. Separation of variables solves equations like \(\dot{x} = rx\) to give \(x(t) = x_0 e^{rt}\).
  3. Systems of ODEs track multiple interacting quantities; linear systems have the form \(\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A\mathbf{x}\).
  4. Fixed points are where \(f(x^*) = 0\) — the system is at rest. These correspond to equilibrium strategies in EGT.
  5. Stability analysis uses the Jacobian and its eigenvalues to classify fixed points as stable nodes, unstable nodes, saddle points, spirals, or centers.
  6. Phase portraits visualize the dynamics: vector fields, trajectories, and the qualitative behavior of solutions.
  7. The logistic/replicator connection: the two-strategy replicator equation \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)(a-b)\) is a logistic equation, providing a direct bridge to Module 7.
