Module 10

Advanced Anti-Rivalry in Digital Commons

The central paradox of digital commons: anti-rivalrous goods create scarcity. When a resource becomes more valuable with use, it attracts more users, more contributions, and more demands — overwhelming the rivalrous complements it depends on. This module formalizes the coupling between anti-rivalrous and rivalrous resources, derives the game-theoretic consequences, and explores institutional responses.

1. The Anti-Rivalry Paradox: Success Breeds Scarcity

Prerequisites. This module builds directly on Module 9’s formalization of anti-rivalry. We use the value function \(V(n) = V_0 \cdot g(n)\) with rivalry parameter \(\beta\) throughout. If you haven’t worked through Module 9, Section 5, do so first.

In Module 9 we established that anti-rivalrous goods have the remarkable property that their value increases with the number of users:

\[ V'(n) > 0 \quad \text{(anti-rivalrous)} \]

We showed how this transforms social dilemmas: under sufficiently strong anti-rivalry, the Prisoner’s Dilemma becomes a Coordination Game or even a Harmony Game, making cooperation self-sustaining.

This picture, however, is incomplete. It treats the anti-rivalrous good in isolation, as if it exists in a vacuum. In practice, every digital commons depends on complementary resources that remain rivalrous:

  • Wikipedia articles are anti-rivalrous (more readers → more editors → better articles), but editor attention and curation capacity are strictly rivalrous.
  • Open-source software is anti-rivalrous (more users → more bug reports, patches, and libraries), but maintainer time and review bandwidth are rivalrous.
  • AI training datasets become more valuable with broader contribution, but data quality curation and compute resources are rivalrous.
  • Online forums become more valuable with participants, but moderation capacity and signal-to-noise ratio degrade.
Definition 10.1 (The Anti-Rivalry Paradox). The success of an anti-rivalrous resource generates demand that exhausts its rivalrous complements. Formally, if the anti-rivalrous resource \(R_a\) has value \(V_a(n)\) increasing in users \(n\), and depends on a rivalrous complement \(R_r\) whose per-user availability \(S_r(n)\) is decreasing in \(n\), then there exists a tension: \[ \frac{dV_a}{dn} > 0 \quad \text{but} \quad \frac{dS_r}{dn} < 0 \] The effective value of the commons is determined by both.
Intuition: The Restaurant Analogy. Imagine a restaurant whose food gets better the more customers it serves (anti-rivalrous quality — perhaps through crowd-sourced recipe improvement). Word spreads, and ever more diners arrive. But the kitchen has finite capacity, the waitstaff are overworked, and tables are limited. The food quality rises, but the dining experience collapses. The anti-rivalrous dimension (recipe quality) creates a crisis in the rivalrous dimensions (seating, service, ingredients).

This is not merely a theoretical curiosity. It is arguably the central governance challenge of digital commons. The open-source sustainability crisis, Wikipedia’s editor retention problem, and the moderation challenges of online communities are all instances of this paradox.

2. Coupled Resource Model

2.1 Formal Framework

We model a digital commons as depending on two coupled resources:

  • Anti-rivalrous resource \(R_a\): the core digital good (code, knowledge, data). Its value to each user increases with the number of active users.
  • Rivalrous complement \(R_r\): the scarce input the commons requires (attention, maintenance, infrastructure, moderation). Its per-user availability decreases with the number of users.
Definition 10.2 (Coupled Value Function). The effective per-user value of a digital commons with \(n\) users is: \[ V(n) \;=\; V_a(n) \cdot h\!\bigl(S_r(n)\bigr) \] where:
  • \(V_a(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{\beta}\) is the anti-rivalrous component (\(\beta > 0\)), as defined in Module 9
  • \(S_r(n)\) is the per-user availability of the rivalrous complement
  • \(h: \mathbb{R}_+ \to [0,1]\) is a quality modulator: \(h(0) = 0\), \(h'(s) > 0\), \(h(s) \to 1\) as \(s \to \infty\)

The simplest model for the rivalrous complement assumes a fixed total supply \(M\) (e.g., total maintainer-hours available) shared among \(n\) users plus overhead:

\[ S_r(n) = \frac{M}{n^\gamma} \]

where \(\gamma > 0\) measures how fast demand for the rivalrous resource scales with users. If each user creates exactly one unit of demand, \(\gamma = 1\) (linear scaling). But often demand is superlinear (\(\gamma > 1\)) because coordination overhead grows faster than linearly (cf. Brooks’s Law).

For the quality modulator, a natural choice is:

\[ h(s) = \frac{s}{s + \kappa} \]

where \(\kappa > 0\) is the half-saturation constant (when \(s = \kappa\), quality is at 50%). This is a Michaelis–Menten / Monod-type function, widely used in ecology and biochemistry for exactly this kind of saturation dynamics.

Example 10.1: Open-Source Project. Consider an open-source library with:
  • Anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta = 0.3\) (moderate ecosystem effects)
  • Base value \(V_0 = 10\)
  • Total maintainer capacity \(M = 100\) hours/month
  • Demand exponent \(\gamma = 1.2\) (mildly superlinear)
  • Half-saturation \(\kappa = 5\)

The coupled value function is:

\[ V(n) = 10 \cdot n^{0.3} \cdot \frac{100/n^{1.2}}{100/n^{1.2} + 5} \]

For small \(n\), the anti-rivalry term dominates and value rises. But as \(n\) grows, the maintainer bottleneck term \(S_r(n) = 100/n^{1.2}\) shrinks, pulling quality down. At some point, value peaks and then declines — the commons enters crisis.

2.2 Carrying Capacity of the Commons

A crucial quantity is the peak of the coupled value function — the number of users at which the commons delivers maximum per-user value.

Theorem 10.1 (Optimal Community Size). For the coupled value function \(V(n) = V_0 \, n^{\beta} \cdot \frac{M/n^{\gamma}}{M/n^{\gamma} + \kappa}\), the per-user value is maximized at: \[ n^* = \left(\frac{\beta \, M}{(\gamma - \beta)\,\kappa}\right)^{1/\gamma} \] provided \(\gamma > \beta\) (the rivalrous drain outpaces the anti-rivalrous gain).
Proof

Let \(s(n) = M/n^{\gamma}\). Write \(V(n) = V_0 \, n^{\beta} \cdot s/(s+\kappa)\). Taking the log-derivative:

\[ \frac{V'(n)}{V(n)} = \frac{\beta}{n} + \frac{s'(n)}{s(n)} \cdot \frac{\kappa}{s(n)+\kappa} \]

Since \(s'(n) = -\gamma M n^{-\gamma-1}\), we have \(s'(n)/s(n) = -\gamma/n\). Setting \(V'(n) = 0\):

\[ \frac{\beta}{n} = \frac{\gamma}{n} \cdot \frac{\kappa}{s(n) + \kappa} \] \[ \beta \bigl(s(n) + \kappa\bigr) = \gamma \kappa \] \[ s(n^*) = \frac{(\gamma - \beta)\kappa}{\beta} \]

Substituting \(s(n) = M/n^{\gamma}\) and solving for \(n\) gives the result. \(\square\)

Interpreting \(n^*\).
  • Higher \(M\) (more maintainer capacity) → larger optimal community. This is intuitive: more governance resources support more users.
  • Higher \(\beta\) (stronger anti-rivalry) → larger optimal community. The anti-rivalrous benefit justifies tolerating more strain.
  • Higher \(\gamma\) (faster demand scaling) → smaller optimal community. When each new user creates disproportionate governance burden, the community saturates sooner.
  • Higher \(\kappa\) (quality more sensitive to scarcity) → smaller optimal community.

Beyond \(n^*\), per-user value declines. The commons hasn’t failed — total welfare \(W(n) = n \cdot V(n)\) may still increase — but the experience degrades. This is precisely the point at which communities report “the project isn’t what it used to be.”

Definition 10.3 (Carrying Capacity of the Commons). The carrying capacity \(n_K\) is the community size at which total welfare \(W(n) = n \cdot V(n)\) is maximized. For \(\gamma > \beta + 1\), this is finite; for \(\gamma \le \beta + 1\), total welfare grows without bound (though per-user value may still peak and decline).

The analogy to ecological carrying capacity is deliberate. Just as an ecosystem can support a maximum population given finite resources, a digital commons can support a maximum community given finite governance resources — even though the core resource itself is anti-rivalrous.

3. Attention Scarcity in Information Commons

The oldest and most fundamental case of anti-rivalry-induced scarcity: information is anti-rivalrous, but attention is rivalrous.

Herbert Simon observed in 1971: “A wealth of information creates a poverty of attention.” We can now state this precisely in our framework.

Definition 10.4 (Attention-Bounded Information Commons). An information commons with \(n\) contributors produces content at rate \(C(n)\) (increasing in \(n\)), while each user has fixed attention budget \(A\). The signal-to-noise ratio experienced by a user is: \[ \text{SNR}(n) = \frac{q(n) \cdot A}{C(n)} \] where \(q(n)\) is average content quality (which may increase with \(n\) due to peer review and competition) and \(C(n)\) is total content volume.

The core tension: more contributors increase both \(q(n)\) (quality, anti-rivalrous) and \(C(n)\) (volume). When volume grows faster than quality, the signal-to-noise ratio falls.

Example 10.2: Wikipedia’s Quality–Volume Tradeoff.

Wikipedia’s article quality improves with editors (anti-rivalrous): \(q(n) \approx q_0 \cdot n^{0.2}\) (diminishing returns as easy improvements are made first). But content volume grows nearly linearly: \(C(n) \approx c_0 \cdot n\).

The signal-to-noise ratio is: \[ \text{SNR}(n) = \frac{q_0 \, n^{0.2} \cdot A}{c_0 \, n} = \frac{q_0 A}{c_0} \cdot n^{-0.8} \]

This declines as \(n^{-0.8}\) — sharply. Without curation institutions (featured articles, review processes, deletion policies), the commons would drown in its own abundance. Wikipedia’s elaborate governance structure is precisely a response to this anti-rivalry-induced attention scarcity.

More generally, the attention scarcity problem applies to any information commons:

Commons Anti-Rivalrous Dimension Attention Bottleneck
Wikipedia Article quality (more editors → better coverage) Reader/editor attention to find & fix errors
Stack Overflow Answer quality (voting, competition among answerers) Expert time to answer; reader time to find answers
Academic preprints Knowledge grows with contributions Peer review capacity; reader time to assess quality
Social media Content diversity and network value Algorithmic filtering needed; moderation overwhelmed
Proposition 10.1 (Attention Scarcity Threshold). In an information commons where quality scales as \(q(n) = q_0 \, n^{\alpha}\) and volume as \(C(n) = c_0 \, n^{\delta}\) with \(\delta > \alpha\) (volume outpaces quality), the signal-to-noise ratio crosses a critical threshold \(\text{SNR}_{\min}\) at community size: \[ n_{\text{crisis}} = \left(\frac{q_0 \, A}{c_0 \, \text{SNR}_{\min}}\right)^{1/(\delta - \alpha)} \] Beyond \(n_{\text{crisis}}\), the commons requires institutional intervention (curation, filtering, editorial policies) to remain functional.

This result connects directly to Ostrom: the institutional rules governing an information commons are not optional niceties — they are mathematically necessary responses to anti-rivalry-induced attention scarcity.

4. Maintainer Scarcity in Open Source

Perhaps the most vivid instance of the anti-rivalry paradox occurs in open-source software. The code is anti-rivalrous: more users generate more bug reports, more patches, more integrations, and a richer ecosystem. But the people who review pull requests, triage issues, make architectural decisions, and cut releases are finite in number and energy.

Definition 10.5 (Maintainer Load Model). Let \(n\) be the number of active users of an open-source project. The maintenance demand generated is: \[ D(n) = d_0 \, n^{\gamma} \] where \(\gamma \ge 1\) reflects how demand scales (issues, PRs, questions, security patches). The maintenance supply is: \[ S(n) = m_0 + s \cdot n^{\alpha} \] where \(m_0\) is core maintainer capacity, \(s \cdot n^{\alpha}\) captures community contributions (\(\alpha < 1\) because only a fraction of users contribute and coordination costs grow), and the maintenance ratio is: \[ \rho(n) = \frac{S(n)}{D(n)} = \frac{m_0 + s \cdot n^{\alpha}}{d_0 \, n^{\gamma}} \]

When \(\gamma > \alpha\) (demand outpaces community supply), the maintenance ratio declines with scale. This is the open-source sustainability crisis in a single equation.

Example 10.3: The Heartbleed Pattern.

OpenSSL was used by roughly 66% of all web servers (\(n \approx\) millions) but maintained by effectively one full-time developer (\(m_0 = 1\), \(s \approx 0\)). The maintenance ratio was:

\[ \rho \approx \frac{1}{d_0 \cdot n^{\gamma}} \to 0 \]

The Heartbleed vulnerability (2014) was a direct consequence: a critical security bug went unreviewed because \(\rho\) was far below any reasonable threshold. The code was maximally anti-rivalrous (used everywhere), which created maximal scarcity of the rivalrous complement (security review).

Brooks’s Law in the Coupled Model. Fred Brooks observed that “adding manpower to a late software project makes it later.” In our framework, this means \(\gamma > 1\): the coordination overhead of additional maintainers can increase demand faster than it increases supply. When both \(\gamma > 1\) and \(\alpha < 1\), the maintenance ratio declines especially fast. New contributors help, but with diminishing returns.

The maintenance ratio \(\rho(n)\) can be incorporated into our coupled value function:

\[ V(n) = V_0 \, n^{\beta} \cdot h\!\bigl(\rho(n)\bigr) \]

where \(h(\rho) = \rho/(\rho + \kappa)\) as before. Projects with \(\rho \gg \kappa\) are well-maintained and their users enjoy full anti-rivalrous benefits. Projects with \(\rho \ll \kappa\) are effectively abandoned despite being widely used — the anti-rivalry exists in principle but the rivalrous bottleneck destroys the experienced value.

5. Infrastructure Scarcity

A third type of anti-rivalry-induced scarcity arises when the anti-rivalrous good runs on rivalrous physical infrastructure.

Software can be copied at zero marginal cost (anti-rivalrous). But it must execute on hardware: servers, bandwidth, storage, and compute cycles — all of which are rivalrous. The more users the anti-rivalrous software attracts, the more infrastructure is consumed.

Definition 10.6 (Infrastructure-Bounded Anti-Rivalry). Let \(V_a(n) = V_0 \, n^{\beta}\) be the anti-rivalrous value and let the infrastructure cost per user be \(I(n)\). In many systems, infrastructure cost per user increases with \(n\) due to congestion, coordination, and the need for redundancy: \[ I(n) = I_0 \cdot n^{\delta - 1} \quad (\delta \ge 1) \] The net value to each user is: \[ V_{\text{net}}(n) = V_0 \, n^{\beta} - I_0 \, n^{\delta - 1} \]

When \(\delta > \beta + 1\), infrastructure costs eventually dominate, and net value peaks at:

\[ n^*_{\text{infra}} = \left(\frac{\beta \, V_0}{(\delta - 1) \, I_0}\right)^{1/(\delta - 1 - \beta)} \]
Example 10.4: The Jevons Paradox of Digital Commons.

William Stanley Jevons observed in 1865 that improvements in coal efficiency increased total coal consumption. The digital analog: making software better (anti-rivalrous improvement) increases adoption, which increases infrastructure demand, which can exceed the original resource savings.

A federated social network (e.g., Mastodon) has anti-rivalrous content (more users → more diverse feeds), but each instance operator bears rivalrous server costs that scale with users. The network’s anti-rivalry drives adoption, but adoption exhausts volunteer-operated infrastructure. Many instances shut down not because the software failed but because the infrastructure complement was exhausted.

The three types of induced scarcity — attention, maintenance, and infrastructure — often occur simultaneously, compounding the challenge:

Scarcity Type What Runs Out Scaling Examples
Attention Human cognitive capacity to process, filter, and evaluate Fixed per user; total content grows Wikipedia, forums, preprint servers
Maintenance Developer/curator time to govern, review, and fix Demand superlinear; supply sublinear Open-source projects, community wikis
Infrastructure Compute, storage, bandwidth Per-user cost may increase with congestion Federated services, P2P networks, CDNs

6. Game Theory of Coupled Systems

In Module 9, we analyzed a two-strategy game (Contribute vs. Free-Ride) for the anti-rivalrous good. But once we recognize that anti-rivalry induces scarcity in a rivalrous complement, we need a richer strategy space. The key insight: not all contributions are alike.

6.1 Two-Role Contribution Game

We now distinguish two types of contribution:

  • Content contribution (type C): Adding to the anti-rivalrous resource (writing code, creating articles, submitting data).
  • Governance contribution (type G): Maintaining the rivalrous complement (reviewing PRs, moderating content, running infrastructure, curating quality).
Definition 10.7 (Three-Strategy Model). Consider a population where each agent adopts one of three strategies:
  • \(\mathbf{C}\) (Content Contributor): Contributes to the anti-rivalrous resource at personal cost \(c_C\).
  • \(\mathbf{G}\) (Governance Contributor): Maintains the rivalrous complement at personal cost \(c_G\) (typically \(c_G > c_C\), as governance is often less rewarding and more demanding).
  • \(\mathbf{F}\) (Free-Rider): Consumes the commons without contributing. Cost \(= 0\).

Let \(x\), \(y\), and \(z = 1 - x - y\) be the population fractions playing C, G, and F respectively. The per-user value depends on both content (driven by \(x\)) and governance (driven by \(y\)):

\[ V(x, y) = V_0 \cdot g(x) \cdot h(y) \] where \(g(x) = (1 + \beta x)^{\eta}\) captures anti-rivalrous content effects and \(h(y) = y / (y + \kappa)\) captures governance quality.

The payoffs for each strategy are:

\[ \begin{aligned} \pi_C &= V(x,y) - c_C \\ \pi_G &= V(x,y) - c_G + \sigma \\ \pi_F &= V(x,y) \end{aligned} \]

where \(\sigma \ge 0\) is a governance premium — social status, decision-making power, or institutional incentives given to maintainers. This premium is often crucial for attracting governance contributors.

The Governance Dilemma. Even if everyone recognizes the need for governance, individual incentives push toward content contribution (more visible, more rewarding) or free-riding. Governance is the “less glamorous” contribution: code reviewers are thanked less than code authors, Wikipedia admins are appreciated less than prolific editors. Without institutional mechanisms to reward governance (\(\sigma > 0\)), the rivalrous complement is systematically under-supplied.

6.2 Three-Strategy Replicator Dynamics

The replicator dynamics on the 2-simplex (with \(z = 1-x-y\)) are:

\[ \begin{aligned} \dot{x} &= x \,(\pi_C - \bar{\pi}) \\ \dot{y} &= y \,(\pi_G - \bar{\pi}) \\ \end{aligned} \]

where \(\bar{\pi} = x\pi_C + y\pi_G + (1-x-y)\pi_F\) is the population average payoff. Substituting:

\[ \begin{aligned} \pi_C - \pi_F &= -c_C \\ \pi_G - \pi_F &= -c_G + \sigma \\ \pi_C - \pi_G &= c_G - c_C - \sigma \end{aligned} \]
Key observation. The fitness differences \(\pi_C - \pi_F = -c_C\) and \(\pi_G - \pi_F = -c_G + \sigma\) are constant (independent of \(x\) and \(y\)) if the value function \(V(x,y)\) enters all payoffs equally. This is the standard public goods result: when everyone gets the same value regardless of their own strategy, contributing is always dominated.

But this changes when we incorporate differential access: contributors may receive more of the anti-rivalrous benefit than free-riders (e.g., GitHub contributors get better reputation, job prospects, and community standing).

Let us introduce differential access: contributors receive a multiplier \(\phi > 1\) on the value function (capturing reputational returns, learning-by-doing, and community recognition), while free-riders receive the base value:

\[ \begin{aligned} \pi_C &= \phi \cdot V(x,y) - c_C \\ \pi_G &= \phi \cdot V(x,y) - c_G + \sigma \\ \pi_F &= V(x,y) \end{aligned} \]

Now the fitness differences become state-dependent:

\[ \begin{aligned} \pi_C - \pi_F &= (\phi - 1) \, V(x,y) - c_C \\ \pi_G - \pi_F &= (\phi - 1) \, V(x,y) - c_G + \sigma \end{aligned} \]

Contributing is favored when the commons is valuable enough that \((\phi - 1)\,V(x,y) > c_C\). The anti-rivalry effect matters: as the commons grows, \(V(x,y)\) increases, making contribution more attractive. But the governance bottleneck constrains \(V\) through \(h(y)\), creating a coupling between the two types of contribution.

Theorem 10.2 (Governance-Content Coupling). In the three-strategy model with differential access \(\phi > 1\), the interior fixed point \((x^*, y^*)\) satisfies: \[ V(x^*, y^*) = \frac{c_C}{\phi - 1} = \frac{c_G - \sigma}{\phi - 1} \] An interior equilibrium therefore requires \(c_C = c_G - \sigma\), i.e., the governance premium must exactly compensate the extra cost of governance: \(\sigma = c_G - c_C\).

When \(\sigma < c_G - c_C\) (insufficient governance premium), the system has no interior equilibrium. Instead, governance contributors are driven to extinction (\(y \to 0\)), which collapses \(h(y) \to 0\), which collapses the commons value, which then also eliminates content contributors. The commons fails because governance failed first.
Proof sketch

At an interior fixed point, \(\pi_C = \pi_G = \pi_F = \bar{\pi}\). From \(\pi_C = \pi_F\): \((\phi-1)V(x^*,y^*) = c_C\). From \(\pi_G = \pi_F\): \((\phi-1)V(x^*,y^*) = c_G - \sigma\). Equating gives \(c_C = c_G - \sigma\). If \(\sigma < c_G - c_C\), then at any state \(\pi_G < \pi_C\) (governance is dominated by content contribution), so \(y\) decreases monotonically. As \(y \to 0\), \(h(y) \to 0\), \(V \to 0\), and then \(\pi_C - \pi_F = (\phi-1) \cdot 0 - c_C < 0\), so content contribution is also dominated. \(\square\)

6.3 The Governance Trap

Definition 10.8 (The Governance Trap). A digital commons is in a governance trap when:
  1. The anti-rivalrous resource is valuable and growing (\(V_a\) increasing).
  2. The governance premium is insufficient (\(\sigma < c_G - c_C\)).
  3. Governance contributor fraction \(y\) is declining.
The system appears healthy (growing user base, growing content) but is on a trajectory toward collapse: the rivalrous complement is being depleted at a rate hidden by the surface-level growth of the anti-rivalrous resource.
Example 10.5: The Governance Trap in Open Source.

Consider a successful open-source project with parameters: \(\beta = 0.3\), \(\phi = 1.5\), \(c_C = 2\), \(c_G = 5\), \(\sigma = 1\), \(\kappa = 0.1\).

Since \(\sigma = 1 < c_G - c_C = 3\), the governance premium is insufficient. Even though content contribution can be sustained (when the project is popular enough that \(0.5 \cdot V > 2\)), governance contribution is never individually rational.

The project follows a characteristic trajectory:

  1. Growth phase: Founders provide governance; content contributors join; the project grows; anti-rivalry kicks in.
  2. Success phase: The project becomes popular; anti-rivalrous value is high; many content contributors; governance still handled by founders.
  3. Burnout phase: Governance demand exceeds founder capacity; founders burn out; governance quality \(h(y)\) drops; but anti-rivalrous value masks the problem temporarily.
  4. Crisis phase: A critical event (security vulnerability, breaking change, community conflict) reveals the governance deficit; rapid collapse.

The governance trap is insidious because growth metrics (users, stars, downloads) are all proxies for the anti-rivalrous dimension and hide the deteriorating rivalrous dimension. The project looks healthier than ever right up until it collapses.

7. The Tragedy of the Anti-Commons

Michael Heller (1998) identified a phenomenon symmetric to the tragedy of the commons: the tragedy of the anti-commons, where multiple parties hold rights that can exclude others, leading to underuse of a resource.

Definition 10.9 (Tragedy of the Anti-Commons). An anti-commons arises when an anti-rivalrous resource is fragmented by exclusion rights (patents, licenses, proprietary APIs), so that no single user can access the full resource. If there are \(k\) right-holders, each of whom can block access, the probability of gaining access is: \[ P_{\text{access}} = \prod_{i=1}^{k} p_i \] where \(p_i\) is the probability of obtaining permission from right-holder \(i\). Even if each \(p_i\) is close to 1, the product shrinks exponentially in \(k\).

This is a particularly perverse interaction with anti-rivalry. The resource should become more valuable with use, but the exclusion rights prevent the use that would generate the value. The social loss has two components:

  1. Direct loss: underuse of the existing resource.
  2. Anti-rivalry loss: the foregone value increase that would have occurred if more users had access. This second loss is unique to anti-rivalrous goods and can dwarf the first.
Proposition 10.2 (Anti-Commons Welfare Loss). For an anti-rivalrous good with value \(V(n) = V_0 \, n^{\beta}\) and potential user population \(N\), fragmented into \(k\) exclusion rights with uniform access probability \(p\), the expected number of users is \(n = N \cdot p^k\) and the welfare loss relative to open access is: \[ \Delta W = N \cdot V_0 \cdot N^{\beta} - N p^k \cdot V_0 \cdot (Np^k)^{\beta} = V_0 N^{1+\beta}\bigl(1 - p^{k(1+\beta)}\bigr) \] For \(\beta > 0\), this loss grows superlinearly in \(N\): the larger the potential community, the more destructive the anti-commons.
Example 10.6: Patent Thickets in Biotechnology.

A gene-editing technique has potential value \(V(n) = V_0 \, n^{0.4}\) (anti-rivalrous through shared protocols, reagents, and training data). But 12 overlapping patents (\(k = 12\)) each require licensing (\(p = 0.9\) probability of access). The expected fraction of researchers with full access:

\[ p^{12} = 0.9^{12} \approx 0.28 \]

Only 28% of potential users gain access, and the realized value is: \(V(0.28N) = V_0 \cdot (0.28N)^{0.4}\) instead of \(V_0 \cdot N^{0.4}\). The welfare ratio is \(0.28^{1.4} \approx 0.17\) — 83% of the value is destroyed by the anti-commons, most of it from the lost anti-rivalry effect.

The tragedy of the anti-commons shows that the interaction between anti-rivalry and institutional design cuts both ways: good institutions can amplify anti-rivalry, but bad institutions (excessive fragmentation of rights) can destroy it.

8. Institutional Design for Coupled Systems

Given the anti-rivalry paradox, how should institutions be designed to manage the coupling between anti-rivalrous and rivalrous resources? We revisit Ostrom’s design principles (Module 8) through this new lens.

The Design Challenge. The institution must simultaneously:
  1. Maximize the anti-rivalrous effect (keep the commons open, encourage use).
  2. Protect the rivalrous complement (prevent governance collapse, manage infrastructure).
These goals are in tension. Making the commons more open accelerates the anti-rivalrous benefit but also accelerates the depletion of rivalrous complements.

We identify four institutional mechanisms that address this tension:

8.1 Governance Premiums (\(\sigma\)-Mechanisms)

To escape the governance trap (Theorem 10.2 requires \(\sigma \ge c_G - c_C\)), institutions must reward governance contribution. Real-world mechanisms include:

  • Formal roles: Apache Foundation’s “committer” and “PMC member” status (social \(\sigma\)).
  • Financial incentives: Linux Foundation, Mozilla, and corporate sponsorship paying maintainers (monetary \(\sigma\)).
  • Decision-making power: Governance contributors get voting rights, veto power, or agenda-setting ability (power \(\sigma\)).
  • Career signaling: Maintainer roles that signal competence to employers (reputational \(\sigma\)).

8.2 Graduated Contribution Expectations

An analog of Ostrom’s “graduated sanctions”: as users benefit more from the anti-rivalrous resource, expectations for governance contribution increase.

  • Wikipedia: Experienced editors are expected to participate in deletion discussions, arbitration, and policy development.
  • Open-source: Heavy corporate users are expected to “give back” through maintainer funding, dedicated engineers, or infrastructure support.
  • Academic commons: Researchers who benefit from peer review are expected to review for others.

8.3 Attention Management Institutions

To combat attention scarcity (Section 3), commons develop filtering and curation institutions:

  • Quality hierarchies: Wikipedia’s featured article process, Stack Overflow’s voting system, academic peer review.
  • Algorithmic curation: Recommendation systems that increase the effective signal-to-noise ratio (raising \(q(n)/C(n)\) in our model).
  • Scope boundaries: Rules about what belongs in the commons and what doesn’t (Wikipedia’s notability criteria, subreddit topic rules).

8.4 Federated Governance

When a single governance structure cannot scale, federating into semi-autonomous sub-communities can help. Each sub-community manages its own rivalrous complement while sharing the anti-rivalrous resource:

\[ V_{\text{federated}} = V_0 \cdot g(n_{\text{total}}) \cdot \frac{1}{K} \sum_{j=1}^K h(y_j) \]

where \(K\) sub-communities each maintain governance quality \(h(y_j)\) independently. The anti-rivalrous benefit \(g(n_{\text{total}})\) is shared globally, while the governance burden is distributed locally. This is the pattern of Linux distributions, Mastodon instances, and Wikipedia’s language editions.

Proposition 10.3 (Federation Advantage). If governance cost per sub-community scales as \(D_j(n_j) = d_0 \, n_j^{\gamma}\) and total users are split equally across \(K\) sub-communities (\(n_j = n/K\)), the total governance demand is: \[ D_{\text{fed}} = K \cdot d_0 \left(\frac{n}{K}\right)^{\gamma} = d_0 \, n^{\gamma} \, K^{1-\gamma} \] For \(\gamma > 1\) (superlinear scaling), federation reduces total governance demand by a factor of \(K^{\gamma - 1}\). The larger the coordination overhead exponent, the greater the benefit of federation.

9. Case Studies

9.1 Wikipedia: The Attention–Anti-Rivalry Spiral

Wikipedia illustrates the full anti-rivalry paradox lifecycle:

Phase Anti-Rivalrous Dimension Induced Scarcity Institutional Response
2001–2006 Rapid article growth; quality improves with editors Vandalism outpaces editor attention Bots, semi-protection, admin tools
2007–2012 Comprehensive coverage; reliable source Editor burnout; newbie-hostile culture Mentorship programs, WikiProjects
2013–present Dominant knowledge resource; high citation value Systemic bias; gender gap among editors Outreach, grants, structured data (Wikidata)

Each phase shows the same pattern: anti-rivalry success → induced scarcity → institutional innovation. Wikipedia survives because it continuously invents new governance mechanisms to address each new form of induced scarcity.

9.2 The npm/left-pad Incident: Fragile Anti-Rivalry

In March 2016, a single developer unpublished a trivial 11-line JavaScript package (“left-pad”) from the npm registry. This broke thousands of projects worldwide, including major systems at Facebook and Airbnb.

In our framework:

  • Anti-rivalrous dimension: The npm ecosystem had extremely high \(\beta\) — each package made the ecosystem more valuable, driving adoption.
  • Induced scarcity: No one governed the dependency graph. The rivalrous complement — dependency curation and risk assessment — was effectively zero (\(y \approx 0\)).
  • Governance quality: \(h(y) \approx h(0) = 0\) for supply-chain security.

The anti-rivalrous success of npm (thousands of micro-packages, easy reuse) created a hidden scarcity in supply-chain governance. The system looked maximally healthy (extreme reuse, rapid growth) right up until it catastrophically failed.

9.3 AI/ML Commons: Compute as the Rivalrous Bottleneck

Open AI/ML models exhibit strong anti-rivalry: a model trained on diverse data and fine-tuned by many users improves for everyone. But the compute needed for training, inference, and evaluation is intensely rivalrous.

  • Anti-rivalrous: model weights, training techniques, evaluation benchmarks, datasets (with curation).
  • Rivalrous complements: GPU time for training, inference costs for deployment, human evaluator attention for alignment.

The result: open-weight models create enormous anti-rivalrous value, but the compute requirements concentrate power among those who can afford the rivalrous complement. The anti-rivalry of the knowledge coexists with intense rivalry for the infrastructure to use it.

10. Synthesis

Let us collect the key results of this module.

Central Thesis. Anti-rivalry is never pure. Every anti-rivalrous resource depends on rivalrous complements, and the success of the anti-rivalrous resource accelerates the depletion of those complements. The governance challenge of digital commons is not just about managing the anti-rivalrous resource — it is about managing the coupling between anti-rivalrous and rivalrous dimensions.

The mathematical framework we developed captures this through:

  1. Coupled value function: \(V(n) = V_a(n) \cdot h(S_r(n))\), where anti-rivalry and rivalrous depletion interact multiplicatively.
  2. Carrying capacity: Even with unbounded anti-rivalry, finite rivalrous complements create an optimal community size \(n^*\).
  3. Three-strategy dynamics: Content contribution, governance contribution, and free-riding produce qualitatively different dynamics than the two-strategy model.
  4. The governance trap: Without sufficient governance premiums, the rivalrous complement is systematically under-supplied, leading to hidden fragility.
  5. The anti-commons: Exclusion rights destroy anti-rivalrous value superlinearly.

Module 9 View (Isolated Anti-Rivalry)

  • More users → more value
  • Anti-rivalry transforms PD → Coordination → Harmony
  • Key question: is \(\beta\) large enough?
  • Cooperation can be self-sustaining

Module 10 View (Coupled Systems)

  • More users → more value but rivalrous bottleneck
  • Success of anti-rivalry creates new dilemmas
  • Key question: is governance sustainable?
  • Cooperation requires institutional design

This synthesis connects directly back to Ostrom (Module 8): the design principles for governing commons are not just about preventing overuse of rivalrous resources — they are about sustaining the rivalrous complements that make anti-rivalrous resources function. The digital commons is not a solved problem once anti-rivalry is established; it is a continuous institutional challenge of managing coupled systems.

11. Exercises

  1. (Coupled Value Function) Consider a digital commons with \(V_0 = 10\), \(\beta = 0.25\), \(M = 50\), \(\gamma = 1.5\), and \(\kappa = 3\).
    1. Write out the coupled value function \(V(n)\) explicitly.
    2. Calculate \(V(n)\) for \(n = 1, 10, 50, 100, 500\).
    3. Find the optimal community size \(n^*\) using Theorem 10.1.
    4. Plot the per-user value \(V(n)\) and total welfare \(W(n) = nV(n)\). At what \(n\) does per-user value peak? Does total welfare continue to grow after that?
  2. (Attention Scarcity) A Q&A forum has quality \(q(n) = 5n^{0.15}\), volume \(C(n) = 2n^{0.9}\), and user attention budget \(A = 10\).
    1. Derive the signal-to-noise ratio \(\text{SNR}(n)\).
    2. If the minimum usable SNR is \(\text{SNR}_{\min} = 1\), find \(n_{\text{crisis}}\).
    3. The forum introduces an upvoting system that effectively raises quality to \(q'(n) = 5n^{0.35}\) by surfacing better answers. How does \(n_{\text{crisis}}\) change?
    4. Interpret: what does the upvoting system do in terms of the coupled resource model?
  3. (Maintainer Sustainability) An open-source project has \(m_0 = 3\) (three core maintainers), community supply \(s = 0.5\), \(\alpha = 0.6\), and demand \(d_0 = 0.1\), \(\gamma = 1.3\).
    1. Find the community size at which the maintenance ratio \(\rho(n) = 1\) (demand equals supply). This is the sustainability threshold.
    2. A corporate sponsor adds 2 paid maintainers (\(m_0 = 5\)). How does the sustainability threshold change?
    3. Alternatively, the project invests in tooling that reduces demand exponent to \(\gamma = 1.1\). Compare this to adding paid maintainers.
  4. (Three-Strategy Dynamics) In the three-strategy model (C, G, F) with differential access \(\phi = 1.3\), costs \(c_C = 1\), \(c_G = 4\), governance premium \(\sigma\), and value function \(V(x,y) = 10(1+2x) \cdot y/(y+0.1)\):
    1. What is the minimum governance premium \(\sigma\) for an interior equilibrium to exist?
    2. For \(\sigma = 2\), show that governance is dominated and find the long-run outcome.
    3. For \(\sigma = 3\), find the fixed point conditions and verify the interior equilibrium.
    4. Discuss: what real-world institution corresponds to increasing \(\sigma\) from 2 to 3?
  5. (Anti-Commons) A research tool has potential users \(N = 1000\), anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta = 0.5\), and base value \(V_0 = 1\).
    1. Calculate total welfare under open access.
    2. Now suppose the tool requires licenses from \(k = 5\) patent holders, each granting access with probability \(p = 0.85\). Calculate expected users and total welfare.
    3. What fraction of potential welfare is destroyed?
    4. A patent pool reduces the situation to a single license (\(k = 1\)) with \(p = 0.8\). Compare welfare. Is the patent pool beneficial despite the lower individual access probability?
  6. (Federation) A digital commons has \(n = 10{,}000\) users, governance demand exponent \(\gamma = 1.4\), and a fixed total governance supply.
    1. Compare total governance demand for \(K = 1\) (centralized), \(K = 10\), and \(K = 100\) sub-communities.
    2. Discuss the tradeoff: federation reduces governance demand but may reduce the anti-rivalrous benefit if sub-communities become too isolated. Under what conditions is federation optimal?
  7. (Case Study Analysis) Choose a real digital commons (e.g., a specific open-source project, a wiki, a dataset).
    1. Identify the anti-rivalrous dimension(s) and the rivalrous complement(s).
    2. Estimate (qualitatively) the parameters \(\beta\), \(\gamma\), and \(\kappa\).
    3. Has the commons encountered the anti-rivalry paradox? What institutional mechanisms (if any) were developed in response?
    4. Using the framework of this module, propose one institutional change that could improve governance sustainability.
