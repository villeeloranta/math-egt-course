1. The Idea of a Limit

Before we can talk about rates of change, we need to understand one of the most fundamental ideas in mathematics: the limit. Every definition in calculus — derivatives, integrals, continuity — is built on limits. But the core idea is surprisingly intuitive.

Approaching a Value

Imagine you are walking toward a wall. At each step, you cover half the remaining distance. You get closer and closer: 1 meter away, then 0.5 meters, then 0.25, then 0.125... You never quite reach the wall in a finite number of steps, but you can get as close as you want. The wall is your limit.

Intuition

A limit describes what value a function approaches as its input approaches some target. We don't need the function to actually equal that value at the target — only that it gets arbitrarily close.

Definition — Limit (Informal)

We write \[ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \] and say "the limit of \(f(x)\) as \(x\) approaches \(a\) is \(L\)" if we can make \(f(x)\) as close to \(L\) as we wish by taking \(x\) sufficiently close to \(a\) (but not equal to \(a\)).

Example — A Simple Limit

Consider \(f(x) = 2x + 1\). What is \(\lim_{x \to 3} f(x)\)?

As \(x\) gets closer and closer to 3, \(f(x)\) gets closer and closer to \(2(3) + 1 = 7\). There is no surprise here — for continuous functions, the limit is simply the function value:

\[ \lim_{x \to 3}(2x + 1) = 7 \]
Example — A More Interesting Limit

Consider \(f(x) = \dfrac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}\). What is \(\lim_{x \to 2} f(x)\)?

Notice that plugging in \(x = 2\) gives \(\frac{0}{0}\), which is undefined. But the limit asks what happens as we approach 2, not what happens at 2. We can factor:

\[ \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} = \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = x + 2 \quad \text{(when } x \neq 2\text{)} \]

So \(\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} = 2 + 2 = 4\).

The function has a "hole" at \(x = 2\), but the limit tells us where the function is heading.

The Formal Definition (Epsilon-Delta)

The informal definition is enough for most of our work, but the rigorous version gives calculus its logical foundation.

Definition — Limit (Formal, Epsilon-Delta)

We say \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\) if for every \(\varepsilon > 0\), there exists a \(\delta > 0\) such that whenever \(0 < |x - a| < \delta\), we have \(|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon\).

In words: no matter how tight a tolerance \(\varepsilon\) someone demands around \(L\), we can always find a sufficiently small neighborhood \(\delta\) around \(a\) such that all function values in that neighborhood fall within the tolerance.

Important Limit Laws

Limits obey natural algebraic rules. If \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\) and \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = M\), then:

Theorem — Limit Laws
  • Sum: \(\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) + g(x)] = L + M\)
  • Product: \(\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] = L \cdot M\)
  • Quotient: \(\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{L}{M}\), provided \(M \neq 0\)
  • Scalar: \(\lim_{x \to a} [c \cdot f(x)] = c \cdot L\) for any constant \(c\)
  • Power: \(\lim_{x \to a} [f(x)]^n = L^n\) for any positive integer \(n\)

A Crucial Limit for Calculus

One limit appears over and over in calculus. It is the foundation of the derivative:

\[ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h} \]

This is the difference quotient. It measures the average rate of change of \(f\) over a tiny interval of width \(h\), and asks what happens as that interval shrinks to zero. We will explore this fully in the next section.

Why This Matters for EGT

The replicator equation \(\dot{x}_i = x_i(f_i - \bar{f})\) is written in terms of \(\dot{x}_i = \frac{dx_i}{dt}\), which is a derivative — defined as a limit of a difference quotient. Without limits, we cannot rigorously define what "rate of change of strategy frequency" means.

2. Derivatives: The Rate of Change

The derivative is arguably the single most important concept in this entire course. It tells us how fast a quantity is changing at any given instant. In evolutionary game theory, the quantity we most care about is the frequency of a strategy in a population, and the derivative tells us whether that strategy is growing, shrinking, or at equilibrium.

The Difference Quotient and the Definition of the Derivative

Consider a function \(f(x)\). The average rate of change between \(x = a\) and \(x = a + h\) is:

\[ \text{Average rate of change} = \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h} \]

Geometrically, this is the slope of the secant line connecting the points \((a, f(a))\) and \((a+h, f(a+h))\). As \(h\) gets smaller and smaller, this secant line rotates toward the tangent line at \(x = a\).

Definition — The Derivative

The derivative of \(f\) at \(x = a\) is:

\[ f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a + h) - f(a)}{h} \]

provided this limit exists. We also write \(\frac{df}{dx}\Big|_{x=a}\) or \(\frac{d}{dx}f(x)\Big|_{x=a}\).

When we want the derivative as a function (not just at one point), we write:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \]
Example — Derivative from the Definition

Let \(f(x) = x^2\). Compute \(f'(x)\) from the definition.

\[ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x + h)^2 - x^2}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2xh + h^2}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} (2x + h) \\ &= 2x \end{aligned} \]

So the derivative of \(x^2\) is \(2x\). At \(x = 3\), the slope of the tangent line is \(f'(3) = 6\). At \(x = -1\), the slope is \(f'(-1) = -2\).

Example — Another from the Definition

Let \(f(x) = 3x + 5\). Compute \(f'(x)\).

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{[3(x+h) + 5] - [3x + 5]}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{3h}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} 3 = 3 \]

The derivative of a linear function \(mx + b\) is the constant \(m\) — its slope. This makes perfect sense: a straight line has the same steepness everywhere.

Geometric Meaning: The Slope of the Tangent Line

Intuition

The derivative \(f'(a)\) is the slope of the tangent line to the curve \(y = f(x)\) at the point \((a, f(a))\). A positive derivative means the function is increasing; a negative derivative means it is decreasing; a zero derivative means the function is momentarily flat (a potential maximum, minimum, or inflection point).

Derivative Rules

Computing derivatives from the definition every time would be tedious. Fortunately, we have powerful rules that let us differentiate almost any function quickly.

Theorem — Power Rule

If \(f(x) = x^n\) for any real number \(n\), then:

\[ f'(x) = nx^{n-1} \]

Examples: \(\frac{d}{dx}x^3 = 3x^2\),   \(\frac{d}{dx}x^{-1} = -x^{-2}\),   \(\frac{d}{dx}\sqrt{x} = \frac{d}{dx}x^{1/2} = \frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2}\).

Theorem — Constant Multiple Rule

If \(c\) is a constant, then:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[c \cdot f(x)] = c \cdot f'(x) \]
Theorem — Sum Rule

The derivative of a sum is the sum of the derivatives:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[f(x) + g(x)] = f'(x) + g'(x) \]
Theorem — Product Rule

For two functions multiplied together:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[f(x) \cdot g(x)] = f'(x) \cdot g(x) + f(x) \cdot g'(x) \]

Mnemonic: "derivative of the first times the second, plus the first times the derivative of the second."

Theorem — Quotient Rule

For a ratio of two functions:

\[ \frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right] = \frac{f'(x) \cdot g(x) - f(x) \cdot g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2} \]
Theorem — Chain Rule

For composed functions \(f(g(x))\):

\[ \frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \]

In Leibniz notation: if \(y = f(u)\) and \(u = g(x)\), then \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}\).

Example — Using the Rules

Differentiate \(f(x) = 5x^3 - 2x^2 + 7x - 4\).

Applying the power rule, constant multiple rule, and sum rule term by term:

\[ f'(x) = 5 \cdot 3x^2 - 2 \cdot 2x + 7 \cdot 1 - 0 = 15x^2 - 4x + 7 \]
Example — Product Rule

Differentiate \(f(x) = x^2 \cdot e^x\).

Let \(u = x^2\) and \(v = e^x\). Then \(u' = 2x\) and \(v' = e^x\).

\[ f'(x) = u'v + uv' = 2x \cdot e^x + x^2 \cdot e^x = e^x(2x + x^2) = e^x \cdot x(2 + x) \]
Example — Chain Rule

Differentiate \(f(x) = (3x + 1)^5\).

The outer function is \(u^5\) and the inner function is \(u = 3x + 1\). The chain rule gives:

\[ f'(x) = 5(3x + 1)^4 \cdot 3 = 15(3x + 1)^4 \]

Derivatives of Common Functions

Here is a reference table of derivatives you will use frequently:

Function \(f(x)\) Derivative \(f'(x)\)
\(c\) (constant)\(0\)
\(x^n\)\(nx^{n-1}\)
\(e^x\)\(e^x\)
\(a^x\)\(a^x \ln a\)
\(\ln x\)\(\dfrac{1}{x}\)
\(\log_a x\)\(\dfrac{1}{x \ln a}\)
\(\sin x\)\(\cos x\)
\(\cos x\)\(-\sin x\)
\(\tan x\)\(\sec^2 x\)
Intuition — Why \(e^x\) is Special

The exponential function \(e^x\) is its own derivative: \(\frac{d}{dx}e^x = e^x\). This means \(e^x\) grows at a rate equal to its current value — the hallmark of exponential growth. In EGT, exponential functions arise naturally when modeling populations with constant per-capita growth rates.

Why This Matters for EGT

The replicator dynamics equation is:

\[ \dot{x}_i = \frac{dx_i}{dt} = x_i \big( f_i(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{f}(\mathbf{x}) \big) \]

This is a derivative — the rate of change of the frequency \(x_i\) of strategy \(i\). The right-hand side says the growth rate is proportional to how much better strategy \(i\) performs than the population average. Understanding derivatives lets us read this equation: positive derivative means the strategy is spreading; negative means it is declining; zero means the strategy frequency is at an equilibrium.

3. Higher Derivatives

If we can differentiate a function once to get the rate of change, we can differentiate again to get the rate of change of the rate of change. This is the second derivative, and it tells us about the curvature of a function.

Definition — Second Derivative

The second derivative of \(f\) is the derivative of the first derivative:

\[ f''(x) = \frac{d^2 f}{dx^2} = \frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{df}{dx}\right] \]

Similarly, the third derivative is \(f'''(x)\) or \(\frac{d^3 f}{dx^3}\), and so on. The \(n\)-th derivative is written \(f^{(n)}(x)\) or \(\frac{d^n f}{dx^n}\).

Example — Computing Higher Derivatives

Let \(f(x) = x^4 - 3x^2 + 2x\).

\[ \begin{aligned} f'(x) &= 4x^3 - 6x + 2 \\ f''(x) &= 12x^2 - 6 \\ f'''(x) &= 24x \\ f^{(4)}(x) &= 24 \\ f^{(5)}(x) &= 0 \end{aligned} \]

Concavity and the Second Derivative

Intuition

The first derivative tells us direction (increasing or decreasing). The second derivative tells us shape:

  • \(f''(x) > 0\): the graph is concave up (curves upward like a cup). The slope is increasing.
  • \(f''(x) < 0\): the graph is concave down (curves downward like a frown). The slope is decreasing.
  • \(f''(x) = 0\): possible inflection point where concavity changes.
Definition — Inflection Point

An inflection point is a point where the concavity of \(f\) changes — that is, where \(f''(x)\) changes sign. At an inflection point, \(f''(x) = 0\) (or is undefined), but not every point where \(f''(x) = 0\) is necessarily an inflection point.

Example — Concavity Analysis

Let \(f(x) = x^3 - 3x\). Find the concavity and any inflection points.

\[ f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3, \quad f''(x) = 6x \]
  • \(f''(x) = 0\) when \(x = 0\).
  • For \(x < 0\): \(f''(x) < 0\), so the function is concave down.
  • For \(x > 0\): \(f''(x) > 0\), so the function is concave up.

Since \(f''\) changes sign at \(x = 0\), the point \((0, 0)\) is an inflection point.

Why This Matters for EGT

Concavity is central to stability analysis. When we study whether an equilibrium is stable under the replicator dynamics, we often need to check whether a fitness function is concave or convex. A concave fitness function (negative second derivative) can indicate a stable interior equilibrium, while a convex one (positive second derivative) may indicate instability. The second-derivative test (discussed in the Optimization section) is directly used to classify equilibria.

4. Partial Derivatives

So far, our functions have had a single input variable. But in evolutionary game theory, fitness typically depends on multiple strategy frequencies. If there are three strategies with frequencies \(x_1, x_2, x_3\), then the fitness of each strategy is a function of all three variables. We need a way to measure how the output changes when we change one input while holding the others fixed. This is the partial derivative.

Definition — Partial Derivative

Given a function \(f(x, y)\) of two variables, the partial derivative with respect to \(x\) is:

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h, y) - f(x, y)}{h} \]

We differentiate with respect to \(x\) while treating \(y\) as a constant. Similarly:

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x, y + h) - f(x, y)}{h} \]

Alternative notations: \(f_x\), \(\partial_x f\), or \(D_x f\).

Example — Computing Partial Derivatives

Let \(f(x, y) = 3x^2 y + 2xy^3 - 5y + 7\).

Partial with respect to \(x\) (treat \(y\) as a constant):

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 6xy + 2y^3 \]

Partial with respect to \(y\) (treat \(x\) as a constant):

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 3x^2 + 6xy^2 - 5 \]
Example — Fitness Function

Suppose the fitness of strategy A in a two-strategy game is:

\[ f_A(x, y) = 3x + 2y \]

where \(x\) is the frequency of strategy A and \(y\) is the frequency of strategy B. Then:

\[ \frac{\partial f_A}{\partial x} = 3, \quad \frac{\partial f_A}{\partial y} = 2 \]

This tells us: increasing the frequency of A by a small amount raises A's fitness by 3 units per unit change; increasing B's frequency raises A's fitness by 2 units per unit change.

The Gradient Vector

Definition — Gradient

The gradient of a function \(f(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)\) is the vector of all its partial derivatives:

\[ \nabla f = \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \right) \]

The gradient points in the direction of steepest ascent of \(f\). Its magnitude \(\|\nabla f\|\) tells us how steeply \(f\) is increasing in that direction.

Example — Gradient Computation

Let \(f(x, y) = x^2 + 3xy + y^2\). The gradient is:

\[ \nabla f = \left( 2x + 3y, \; 3x + 2y \right) \]

At the point \((1, 2)\):

\[ \nabla f(1, 2) = (2 + 6, \; 3 + 4) = (8, 7) \]

The function increases most steeply in the direction of the vector \((8, 7)\).

Second-Order Partial Derivatives

We can take partial derivatives of partial derivatives. For a function \(f(x,y)\):

  • \(f_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}\): differentiate twice with respect to \(x\).
  • \(f_{yy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2}\): differentiate twice with respect to \(y\).
  • \(f_{xy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \, \partial x}\): differentiate first with respect to \(x\), then \(y\).
Theorem — Clairaut's Theorem (Symmetry of Mixed Partials)

If \(f_{xy}\) and \(f_{yx}\) are both continuous, then:

\[ f_{xy} = f_{yx} \]

The order of differentiation does not matter (for well-behaved functions).

Why This Matters for EGT

In a game with \(n\) strategies, the fitness of strategy \(i\) is a function of the full strategy profile \(\mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)\). When we analyze how the replicator dynamics changes near an equilibrium, we need the Jacobian matrix, which is the matrix of all partial derivatives \(\frac{\partial \dot{x}_i}{\partial x_j}\). This connects partial derivatives directly to the stability theory you will study in Module 4.

5. Optimization

Optimization — finding maxima and minima — is one of the most important applications of calculus. In evolutionary game theory, organisms effectively "maximize" fitness through natural selection, and rational agents maximize utility. The mathematics of finding these maxima and minima is built directly on derivatives.

Critical Points

Definition — Critical Point

A critical point of \(f(x)\) is a value \(x = c\) where either \(f'(c) = 0\) or \(f'(c)\) does not exist.

At a critical point, the tangent line is horizontal (slope zero) or there is no well-defined tangent. All local maxima and minima occur at critical points.

Example — Finding Critical Points

Let \(f(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x + 2\). Find all critical points.

\[ f'(x) = 3x^2 - 12x + 9 = 3(x^2 - 4x + 3) = 3(x - 1)(x - 3) \]

Setting \(f'(x) = 0\): \(x = 1\) and \(x = 3\) are the critical points.

The First Derivative Test

Theorem — First Derivative Test

Let \(c\) be a critical point of \(f\) where \(f'(c) = 0\).

  • If \(f'(x)\) changes from positive to negative at \(c\), then \(f\) has a local maximum at \(c\).
  • If \(f'(x)\) changes from negative to positive at \(c\), then \(f\) has a local minimum at \(c\).
  • If \(f'(x)\) does not change sign at \(c\), then \(c\) is neither a max nor a min (it is an inflection point).

The Second Derivative Test

Theorem — Second Derivative Test

Let \(c\) be a critical point where \(f'(c) = 0\).

  • If \(f''(c) < 0\), then \(f\) has a local maximum at \(c\) (concave down).
  • If \(f''(c) > 0\), then \(f\) has a local minimum at \(c\) (concave up).
  • If \(f''(c) = 0\), the test is inconclusive (use the first derivative test instead).
Example — Classifying Critical Points

Continuing with \(f(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x + 2\), we found critical points at \(x = 1\) and \(x = 3\). We compute:

\[ f''(x) = 6x - 12 \]
  • \(f''(1) = 6(1) - 12 = -6 < 0\) → local maximum at \(x = 1\).
  • \(f''(3) = 6(3) - 12 = 6 > 0\) → local minimum at \(x = 3\).

The maximum value is \(f(1) = 1 - 6 + 9 + 2 = 6\) and the minimum value is \(f(3) = 27 - 54 + 27 + 2 = 2\).

Optimization with Multiple Variables

For a function \(f(x, y)\) of two variables, a critical point occurs where both partial derivatives are zero:

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 0 \]
Theorem — Second Derivative Test (Multivariable)

At a critical point \((a, b)\) of \(f(x, y)\), define the discriminant:

\[ D = f_{xx}(a,b) \cdot f_{yy}(a,b) - [f_{xy}(a,b)]^2 \]
  • If \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx} < 0\): local maximum.
  • If \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx} > 0\): local minimum.
  • If \(D < 0\): saddle point (neither max nor min).
  • If \(D = 0\): inconclusive.
Example — Two-Variable Optimization

Find and classify the critical points of \(f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2 - 4x - 6y + 13\).

Step 1: Set partial derivatives to zero.

\[ f_x = 2x - 4 = 0 \implies x = 2, \qquad f_y = 2y - 6 = 0 \implies y = 3 \]

Critical point: \((2, 3)\).

Step 2: Compute second partials.

\[ f_{xx} = 2, \quad f_{yy} = 2, \quad f_{xy} = 0 \]

Step 3: Compute discriminant.

\[ D = (2)(2) - (0)^2 = 4 > 0 \]

Since \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx} = 2 > 0\), the point \((2, 3)\) is a local minimum.

The minimum value is \(f(2, 3) = 4 + 9 - 8 - 18 + 13 = 0\).

Why This Matters for EGT

In evolutionary game theory, natural selection drives organisms toward strategies that maximize fitness. Finding equilibria of the replicator dynamics is closely related to optimization: we look for strategy frequencies where the "pressure to change" is zero (analogous to \(f'(x) = 0\)). The second derivative test is analogous to checking stability: a maximum in the fitness landscape can correspond to a stable equilibrium (an Evolutionarily Stable Strategy), while a saddle point corresponds to an unstable one.

6. Integration Basics

Integration is the reverse of differentiation. If derivatives tell us rates of change, integrals tell us about accumulation — adding up infinitely many infinitesimal pieces. Integration lets us compute areas, total payoffs, and probabilities.

Antiderivatives

Definition — Antiderivative

An antiderivative (or indefinite integral) of \(f(x)\) is a function \(F(x)\) such that \(F'(x) = f(x)\). We write:

\[ \int f(x) \, dx = F(x) + C \]

where \(C\) is the constant of integration (since the derivative of any constant is zero, there are infinitely many antiderivatives differing by a constant).

Example — Basic Antiderivatives

Since \(\frac{d}{dx}x^3 = 3x^2\), the antiderivative of \(3x^2\) is \(x^3 + C\). More generally:

\[ \int x^n \, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \neq -1) \]

Some important antiderivatives:

  • \(\int e^x \, dx = e^x + C\)
  • \(\int \frac{1}{x} \, dx = \ln|x| + C\)
  • \(\int \cos x \, dx = \sin x + C\)
  • \(\int \sin x \, dx = -\cos x + C\)
Example — Finding an Antiderivative

Compute \(\int (4x^3 - 6x + 2) \, dx\).

Integrate term by term using the power rule for integrals:

\[ \int (4x^3 - 6x + 2) \, dx = \frac{4x^4}{4} - \frac{6x^2}{2} + 2x + C = x^4 - 3x^2 + 2x + C \]

We can verify: \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^4 - 3x^2 + 2x + C) = 4x^3 - 6x + 2\). Correct!

The Definite Integral as Area

Definition — Definite Integral

The definite integral of \(f(x)\) from \(a\) to \(b\) is:

\[ \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*) \, \Delta x \]

where \(\Delta x = \frac{b - a}{n}\) and \(x_i^*\) is a sample point in the \(i\)-th subinterval. Geometrically, this is the signed area between the curve \(y = f(x)\) and the \(x\)-axis, from \(x = a\) to \(x = b\).

Intuition

Think of the definite integral as slicing the region under a curve into very thin rectangles, computing the area of each, and adding them all up. As the rectangles get thinner and thinner (more and more of them), the sum converges to the exact area.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC) is one of the most profound results in mathematics. It connects the two seemingly different operations of differentiation and integration.

Theorem — Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Part 1)

If \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) and we define \(F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt\), then \(F\) is differentiable and:

\[ F'(x) = f(x) \]

In words: the derivative of the integral is the original function. Differentiation undoes integration.

Theorem — Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Part 2)

If \(f\) is continuous on \([a, b]\) and \(F\) is any antiderivative of \(f\), then:

\[ \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a) \]

This is the evaluation theorem: to compute a definite integral, find an antiderivative and subtract its values at the endpoints.

Example — Evaluating a Definite Integral

Compute \(\int_1^3 (2x + 1) \, dx\).

Step 1: Find an antiderivative: \(F(x) = x^2 + x\).

Step 2: Evaluate at the bounds:

\[ \int_1^3 (2x + 1) \, dx = F(3) - F(1) = (9 + 3) - (1 + 1) = 12 - 2 = 10 \]
Example — Area Under an Exponential

Compute \(\int_0^1 e^x \, dx\).

The antiderivative of \(e^x\) is \(e^x\):

\[ \int_0^1 e^x \, dx = e^1 - e^0 = e - 1 \approx 1.718 \]
Why This Matters for EGT

Integration appears in evolutionary game theory in several important contexts:

  • Cumulative payoffs: The total payoff a strategy accumulates over a time interval \([0, T]\) is \(\int_0^T f_i(t) \, dt\), where \(f_i(t)\) is the instantaneous payoff rate.
  • Probability distributions: In stochastic models of evolution, the probability of an event is computed as an integral of a probability density function: \(P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b p(x) \, dx\).
  • Average fitness: The mean fitness of a population with a continuous distribution of types is an integral: \(\bar{f} = \int f(x) \, p(x) \, dx\).

7. The Chain Rule in Detail

The chain rule is so important for evolutionary game theory that it deserves its own extended treatment. Whenever a quantity depends on another quantity that itself depends on a third, we need the chain rule. In EGT, fitness depends on strategy frequencies, which depend on time — a classic chain of dependencies.

The Chain Rule, Revisited

Theorem — The Chain Rule

If \(y = f(u)\) and \(u = g(x)\), then:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \]

In words: the rate of change of \(y\) with respect to \(x\) equals the rate of change of \(y\) with respect to \(u\), times the rate of change of \(u\) with respect to \(x\).

Intuition — Chain of Cause and Effect

If changing \(x\) by 1 unit causes \(u\) to change by 3 units, and changing \(u\) by 1 unit causes \(y\) to change by 5 units, then changing \(x\) by 1 unit causes \(y\) to change by \(3 \times 5 = 15\) units. The chain rule simply multiplies the rates along the chain.

Example — Chain Rule with Exponential

Differentiate \(y = e^{3x^2}\).

Let \(u = 3x^2\), so \(y = e^u\). Then \(\frac{dy}{du} = e^u\) and \(\frac{du}{dx} = 6x\).

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = e^u \cdot 6x = 6x \, e^{3x^2} \]
Example — Chain Rule with Logarithm

Differentiate \(y = \ln(x^2 + 1)\).

Let \(u = x^2 + 1\), so \(y = \ln u\). Then \(\frac{dy}{du} = \frac{1}{u}\) and \(\frac{du}{dx} = 2x\).

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \cdot 2x = \frac{2x}{x^2 + 1} \]
Example — Nested Chain Rule

Differentiate \(y = \sin(e^{2x})\).

This involves two layers of composition. Let \(v = 2x\), \(u = e^v\), and \(y = \sin u\).

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dv} \cdot \frac{dv}{dx} = \cos(u) \cdot e^v \cdot 2 = 2e^{2x} \cos(e^{2x}) \]

The Multivariable Chain Rule

When a function depends on several variables, each of which depends on a common parameter (like time \(t\)), the chain rule generalizes.

Theorem — Multivariable Chain Rule

If \(z = f(x, y)\) where \(x = x(t)\) and \(y = y(t)\) are both functions of \(t\), then:

\[ \frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \cdot \frac{dy}{dt} \]

The Chain Rule in Replicator Dynamics

Let us see exactly how the chain rule appears in a derivation relevant to EGT. This example is important preparation for Module 7.

Example — Average Fitness Rate of Change

In a two-strategy game, the average fitness of the population is:

\[ \bar{f}(x) = x \cdot f_A(x) + (1 - x) \cdot f_B(x) \]

where \(x = x(t)\) is the frequency of strategy A (a function of time). How does \(\bar{f}\) change over time?

Using the chain rule, \(\frac{d\bar{f}}{dt} = \frac{d\bar{f}}{dx} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt}\). The factor \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) is exactly what the replicator equation gives us, and \(\frac{d\bar{f}}{dx}\) tells us how sensitive the average fitness is to the strategy mix.

Suppose the fitness functions are linear: \(f_A(x) = ax + b(1-x)\) and \(f_B(x) = cx + d(1-x)\) for constants \(a, b, c, d\). Then:

\[ \bar{f}(x) = x[ax + b(1-x)] + (1-x)[cx + d(1-x)] \]

Taking the derivative with respect to \(x\):

\[ \frac{d\bar{f}}{dx} = f_A(x) + x \cdot f_A'(x) - f_B(x) + (1-x)\cdot(-f_B'(x)) \]

At this point, every step requires the chain rule and product rule working together. This is why fluency with these rules is essential before tackling the replicator dynamics in Module 7.

Example — Differentiating a Composed Fitness Function

Suppose the fitness of strategy A depends on a resource level \(R\), which itself depends on the strategy frequency \(x\):

\[ f_A(R(x)) = \ln(1 + R(x)), \quad R(x) = \frac{K}{1 + e^{-\alpha x}} \]

Here \(R(x)\) is a logistic function of \(x\) (modeling a saturating resource), and fitness is the log of available resources. To find \(\frac{df_A}{dx}\), we use the chain rule:

\[ \frac{df_A}{dx} = \frac{df_A}{dR} \cdot \frac{dR}{dx} \]

First: \(\frac{df_A}{dR} = \frac{1}{1 + R}\).

Second, for \(R(x) = K(1 + e^{-\alpha x})^{-1}\), we use the chain rule again:

\[ \frac{dR}{dx} = K \cdot (-1)(1 + e^{-\alpha x})^{-2} \cdot (-\alpha e^{-\alpha x}) = \frac{K \alpha \, e^{-\alpha x}}{(1 + e^{-\alpha x})^2} \]

Putting it together:

\[ \frac{df_A}{dx} = \frac{1}{1 + R(x)} \cdot \frac{K \alpha \, e^{-\alpha x}}{(1 + e^{-\alpha x})^2} \]

This is a realistic calculation you might encounter when analyzing how fitness responds to changes in strategy composition through a resource-mediated pathway.

Why This Matters for EGT

The chain rule is everywhere in evolutionary game theory because the quantities we care about are composed: fitness depends on strategy frequencies, which depend on time; payoffs depend on the population state, which depends on individual strategies, which evolve according to their payoffs. Every time we need to trace how a change propagates through these layers of dependence, we use the chain rule.

Exercise 1: Evaluating Limits

Evaluate the following limits:

  1. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x}\)
  3. \(\displaystyle \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(2 + h)^3 - 8}{h}\)

(a) Factor: \(\frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} = \frac{(x-1)(x+1)}{x-1} = x + 1\). So the limit is \(1 + 1 = \mathbf{2}\).

(b) This is a famous limit: \(\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = \mathbf{1}\). (This can be proved using the squeeze theorem.)

(c) Expand: \((2+h)^3 = 8 + 12h + 6h^2 + h^3\). So \(\frac{(2+h)^3 - 8}{h} = \frac{12h + 6h^2 + h^3}{h} = 12 + 6h + h^2\). As \(h \to 0\), this approaches \(\mathbf{12}\). (Note: this is \(f'(2)\) for \(f(x) = x^3\), and indeed \(3 \cdot 2^2 = 12\).)

Exercise 2: Differentiation Practice

Find the derivative of each function:

  1. \(f(x) = 4x^5 - 3x^3 + 2x - 7\)
  2. \(g(x) = x^2 e^x\)
  3. \(h(x) = \ln(x^3 + 1)\)
  4. \(p(x) = \dfrac{x}{x^2 + 1}\)

(a) Power rule: \(f'(x) = 20x^4 - 9x^2 + 2\).

(b) Product rule with \(u = x^2\), \(v = e^x\): \(g'(x) = 2xe^x + x^2 e^x = e^x(2x + x^2) = xe^x(2 + x)\).

(c) Chain rule: \(h'(x) = \frac{1}{x^3 + 1} \cdot 3x^2 = \frac{3x^2}{x^3 + 1}\).

(d) Quotient rule: \(p'(x) = \frac{(1)(x^2 + 1) - x(2x)}{(x^2 + 1)^2} = \frac{x^2 + 1 - 2x^2}{(x^2+1)^2} = \frac{1 - x^2}{(x^2+1)^2}\).

Exercise 3: Critical Points and Optimization

A strategy's fitness is modeled as \(f(x) = -2x^2 + 8x - 3\), where \(x \in [0, 1]\) is the strategy frequency. Find the value of \(x\) that maximizes fitness, and verify it is indeed a maximum.

Step 1: Find critical points. \(f'(x) = -4x + 8 = 0 \implies x = 2\).

Step 2: But \(x = 2\) is outside our domain \([0, 1]\)! So we need to check the endpoints.

\(f(0) = -3\), \(f(1) = -2 + 8 - 3 = 3\).

Since \(f'(x) = -4x + 8 > 0\) for all \(x \in [0, 1]\) (because \(-4x + 8 \geq -4(1) + 8 = 4 > 0\)), the function is increasing on the entire interval.

The maximum on \([0, 1]\) is at \(x = 1\) with \(f(1) = \mathbf{3}\).

EGT interpretation: the derivative is positive everywhere in \([0,1]\), meaning this strategy's fitness always increases with its own frequency — a situation that leads to fixation (the strategy takes over the population).

Exercise 4: Partial Derivatives and the Gradient

In a three-strategy game, suppose the fitness of strategy 1 is \(f_1(x, y) = 2x - xy + 3y^2\), where \(x\) and \(y\) are the frequencies of strategies 1 and 2 (the frequency of strategy 3 is \(1 - x - y\)).

  1. Compute \(\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x}\) and \(\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial y}\).
  2. Compute the gradient \(\nabla f_1\) at the point \((x, y) = (0.3, 0.3)\).
  3. Interpret the gradient in terms of how fitness changes with strategy frequencies.

(a)

\[ \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x} = 2 - y, \quad \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial y} = -x + 6y \]

(b) At \((0.3, 0.3)\):

\[ \nabla f_1(0.3, 0.3) = (2 - 0.3, \; -0.3 + 6(0.3)) = (1.7, \; 1.5) \]

(c) The gradient tells us: increasing strategy 1's frequency by a small amount raises its fitness by about 1.7 per unit, while increasing strategy 2's frequency raises strategy 1's fitness by about 1.5 per unit. Both components are positive, meaning strategy 1 benefits from increases in both its own frequency and strategy 2's frequency (at this particular population state).

Exercise 5: Integration

A strategy's payoff rate at time \(t\) is \(r(t) = 6t^2 - 4t + 1\). Compute the total payoff accumulated from \(t = 0\) to \(t = 3\).

The total payoff is the definite integral:

\[ \int_0^3 (6t^2 - 4t + 1) \, dt = \left[ 2t^3 - 2t^2 + t \right]_0^3 \] \[ = (2 \cdot 27 - 2 \cdot 9 + 3) - (0) = 54 - 18 + 3 = \mathbf{39} \]
Exercise 6: Chain Rule Application

In a model of frequency-dependent selection, the fitness of cooperators is \(f_C(x) = e^{-\beta(1 - x)}\), where \(x\) is the frequency of cooperators and \(\beta > 0\) is the selection intensity. The frequency changes over time: \(x = x(t)\).

  1. Find \(\frac{df_C}{dx}\) and interpret its sign.
  2. Using the chain rule, express \(\frac{df_C}{dt}\) in terms of \(\frac{dx}{dt}\).
  3. If the replicator dynamics gives \(\frac{dx}{dt} > 0\) (cooperators are increasing), what happens to cooperator fitness over time?

(a) Using the chain rule with \(u = -\beta(1-x)\):

\[ \frac{df_C}{dx} = e^{-\beta(1-x)} \cdot \beta = \beta \, e^{-\beta(1-x)} \]

Since \(\beta > 0\) and \(e^{(\cdot)} > 0\) always, we have \(\frac{df_C}{dx} > 0\). Cooperator fitness increases as cooperator frequency increases — there is positive frequency dependence.

(b) By the chain rule:

\[ \frac{df_C}{dt} = \frac{df_C}{dx} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} = \beta \, e^{-\beta(1-x)} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} \]

(c) If \(\frac{dx}{dt} > 0\), then \(\frac{df_C}{dt} > 0\) as well (since all factors are positive). Cooperator fitness is increasing over time. This creates a positive feedback loop: more cooperators → higher cooperator fitness → even more cooperators. This is characteristic of coordination games where cooperation is self-reinforcing once it reaches a critical mass.

Exercise 7: Second Derivative and Stability

A population's average fitness as a function of strategy frequency \(x\) is:

\[ \bar{f}(x) = -x^3 + 3x^2 - 2x + 1 \]
  1. Find all critical points of \(\bar{f}\) on \(\mathbb{R}\).
  2. Use the second derivative test to classify each critical point.
  3. Find the inflection point of \(\bar{f}\).

(a) \(\bar{f}'(x) = -3x^2 + 6x - 2\). Set equal to zero:

\[ 3x^2 - 6x + 2 = 0 \implies x = \frac{6 \pm \sqrt{36 - 24}}{6} = \frac{6 \pm \sqrt{12}}{6} = 1 \pm \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3} \]

So \(x_1 = 1 - \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \approx 0.423\) and \(x_2 = 1 + \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \approx 1.577\).

(b) \(\bar{f}''(x) = -6x + 6\).

  • \(\bar{f}''(x_1) = -6(0.423) + 6 \approx 3.46 > 0\): local minimum.
  • \(\bar{f}''(x_2) = -6(1.577) + 6 \approx -3.46 < 0\): local maximum.

(c) \(\bar{f}''(x) = -6x + 6 = 0 \implies x = 1\). Since \(\bar{f}''(x)\) changes from positive (for \(x < 1\)) to negative (for \(x > 1\)), the point \(x = 1\) is an inflection point.

Exercise 8: Putting It All Together

In a Hawk-Dove game, the fitness of Hawks when the frequency of Hawks is \(x\) is:

\[ f_H(x) = x \cdot \frac{V - C}{2} + (1 - x) \cdot V \]

where \(V\) is the resource value and \(C\) is the cost of fighting (\(C > V > 0\)).

  1. Simplify \(f_H(x)\) and find \(f_H'(x)\).
  2. Is Hawk fitness increasing or decreasing in \(x\)? Interpret this biologically.
  3. The average fitness is \(\bar{f}(x) = x \cdot f_H(x) + (1-x) \cdot f_D(x)\) where \(f_D(x) = x \cdot 0 + (1-x) \cdot \frac{V}{2}\) is Dove fitness. Compute \(\bar{f}'(x)\).

(a) Simplify:

\[ f_H(x) = x \cdot \frac{V - C}{2} + V - Vx = V + x\left(\frac{V - C}{2} - V\right) = V - x \cdot \frac{V + C}{2} \]

So \(f_H'(x) = -\frac{V + C}{2}\).

(b) Since \(V > 0\) and \(C > 0\), we have \(f_H'(x) = -\frac{V+C}{2} < 0\). Hawk fitness is decreasing in \(x\). This makes biological sense: the more Hawks there are, the more fights occur, and fights are costly (since \(C > V\)). Each additional Hawk in the population lowers the expected payoff for all Hawks.

(c) First, simplify \(f_D(x) = \frac{V}{2}(1-x)\). Then:

\[ \bar{f}(x) = x\left[V - x\frac{V+C}{2}\right] + (1-x)\left[\frac{V}{2}(1-x)\right] \] \[ = Vx - \frac{V+C}{2}x^2 + \frac{V}{2}(1 - 2x + x^2) \] \[ = Vx - \frac{V+C}{2}x^2 + \frac{V}{2} - Vx + \frac{V}{2}x^2 \] \[ = \frac{V}{2} - \frac{C}{2}x^2 \]

Therefore:

\[ \bar{f}'(x) = -Cx \]

Since \(C > 0\) and \(x \geq 0\), average fitness is always decreasing (or zero) as Hawk frequency increases. More Hawks means more wasteful fighting, reducing the population's overall fitness. This is an example of a social dilemma: individual Hawks may benefit (when rare), but collective welfare decreases as Hawks proliferate.
