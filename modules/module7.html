Evolutionary Game Theory & Replicator Dynamics

Where everything comes together. We abandon the assumption of rational players and instead ask: what happens when strategies evolve? The replicator equation — the central equation of evolutionary game theory — emerges from a beautifully simple principle: strategies that perform better than average grow; those that perform worse decline.

1. From Classical to Evolutionary Game Theory

In Module 6, we studied classical game theory: rational players, perfect information, utility maximization. Nash equilibrium answered the question "If everyone is perfectly rational, what strategies are self-consistent?" But this framework has a deep limitation: it requires players to reason their way to equilibrium. In many biological, social, and institutional settings, that assumption is unrealistic.

The Key Paradigm Shift

Classical game theory asks: "What should a rational player do?"
Evolutionary game theory asks: "What strategies survive and spread in a population?"

Players don't need to be rational — or even conscious. Strategies that happen to produce higher payoffs leave more copies of themselves in the next generation. This is selection, not calculation.

The evolutionary approach was pioneered by John Maynard Smith and George R. Price in the early 1970s, originally for biological applications. But the framework is far more general. Whenever you have:

  • A population of agents (organisms, firms, people, norms, institutions)
  • Each agent uses a strategy (behavioral rule, phenotype, convention)
  • Agents interact and receive payoffs (fitness, profit, reproductive success)
  • Strategies that earn higher payoffs spread (through reproduction, imitation, learning)

you are in the domain of evolutionary game theory.

Vocabulary Translation

Why This Matters for Social Science

In Ostrom's analysis of institutions (Module 8), communities don't arrive at cooperative norms through perfect rationality. Norms evolve over time: successful conventions spread through imitation and cultural learning, while unsuccessful ones fade. Evolutionary game theory provides the mathematical framework to model this process rigorously.

2. Populations and Strategy Frequencies

Instead of a single player choosing a mixed strategy, we now think about a large population where different individuals use different pure strategies. The population state describes how common each strategy is.

Definition — Population State

Suppose there are \(n\) pure strategies available. The population state is a vector

\[ \mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \]

where \(x_i\) is the fraction (proportion) of the population using strategy \(i\). These fractions must satisfy:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i = 1, \qquad x_i \geq 0 \quad \text{for all } i. \]
Definition — The Simplex

The set of all valid population states forms the \((n{-}1)\)-dimensional simplex:

\[ \Delta^{n-1} = \left\{ \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n \;\Big|\; \sum_{i=1}^n x_i = 1, \; x_i \geq 0 \;\text{for all } i \right\}. \]
Visualizing the Simplex

2 strategies (\(n = 2\)): The simplex is a line segment from \((1, 0)\) to \((0, 1)\). A single number \(x_1 \in [0, 1]\) describes the entire population state (since \(x_2 = 1 - x_1\)).

3 strategies (\(n = 3\)): The simplex is a triangle with vertices at \((1,0,0)\), \((0,1,0)\), and \((0,0,1)\). Each point inside the triangle represents a population mix of all three strategies. The vertices are monomorphic states (everyone plays the same strategy), edges are states where one strategy is absent.

General \(n\): An \((n{-}1)\)-dimensional geometric object. The vertices are the \(n\) monomorphic populations.

Interior vs. Boundary

Points on the interior of the simplex have \(x_i > 0\) for all \(i\) — every strategy is present. Points on the boundary have at least one \(x_i = 0\) — at least one strategy is absent. As we will see, the replicator dynamics preserve this distinction: extinct strategies stay extinct.

Example — Hawk-Dove Population

Consider a population with two strategies: Hawk (\(H\)) and Dove (\(D\)). The state \(\mathbf{x} = (0.3, 0.7)\) means 30% of the population plays Hawk and 70% plays Dove. The simplex is the interval \([0, 1]\), parameterized by \(x_H\) with \(x_D = 1 - x_H\).

3. Fitness and Expected Payoff

In evolutionary game theory, the payoff matrix \(A\) from classical game theory is reinterpreted: entry \(a_{ij}\) is the fitness gain to an individual playing strategy \(i\) when it encounters an opponent playing strategy \(j\). Since opponents are drawn randomly from the population, we compute expected fitness.

Definition — Fitness of Strategy \(i\)

Given payoff matrix \(A = (a_{ij})\) and population state \(\mathbf{x}\), the fitness (expected payoff) of strategy \(i\) is:

\[ f_i(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{ij} x_j = (A\mathbf{x})_i \]

This is the \(i\)-th component of the matrix-vector product \(A\mathbf{x}\). In compact notation, the fitness vector is:

\[ \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x} \]
Why Matrix Multiplication?

An individual playing strategy \(i\) faces opponents at random. With probability \(x_j\), the opponent uses strategy \(j\), yielding payoff \(a_{ij}\). So the expected payoff is the weighted average \(\sum_j a_{ij} x_j\) — exactly the \(i\)-th row of \(A\) dotted with \(\mathbf{x}\). This is precisely what matrix multiplication computes. The linear algebra from Module 2 now shows its purpose.

Definition — Average Population Fitness

The average fitness of the entire population is the weighted average of individual strategy fitnesses:

\[ \bar{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i f_i(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i \sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{ij} x_j = \mathbf{x}^{\!\top} A \mathbf{x} \]

This is a quadratic form (Module 2) — a single number summarizing overall population performance.

Worked Example — Hawk-Dove Fitness Calculation

Consider the Hawk-Dove game with payoff matrix (where \(V = 4\), \(C = 6\)):

\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{V - C}{2} & V \\ 0 & \frac{V}{2} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 4 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \]

Rows/columns: strategy 1 = Hawk, strategy 2 = Dove. Let \(x\) be the fraction of Hawks, so \(\mathbf{x} = (x, 1 - x)\).

Fitness of Hawks:

\[ f_H(x) = a_{11} x + a_{12}(1 - x) = -1 \cdot x + 4(1 - x) = 4 - 5x \]

Fitness of Doves:

\[ f_D(x) = a_{21} x + a_{22}(1 - x) = 0 \cdot x + 2(1 - x) = 2 - 2x \]

Average population fitness:

\[ \bar{f}(x) = x \cdot f_H(x) + (1 - x) \cdot f_D(x) = x(4 - 5x) + (1 - x)(2 - 2x) \] \[ = 4x - 5x^2 + 2 - 2x - 2x + 2x^2 = 2 - 3x^2 \]

At \(x = 0\) (all Doves): \(\bar{f} = 2\). At \(x = 1\) (all Hawks): \(\bar{f} = -1\). The population does worst when everyone is aggressive — a social dilemma emerges!

Connection to Module 5

The fitness \(f_i(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_j a_{ij} x_j\) is literally an expected value. The random variable is "payoff when meeting a random opponent," and the probabilities are the population frequencies \(x_j\). Everything we learned about expected value in Module 5 applies directly.

4. Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS)

The concept of an Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS) is one of the most important ideas in evolutionary biology and social science. It was introduced by Maynard Smith and Price (1973) to answer a fundamental question: which strategies can resist invasion by mutants?

The Invasion Thought Experiment

Imagine a population where everyone uses strategy \(\sigma^*\). Now a small group of mutants using a different strategy \(\sigma\) appears. The mutants are rare, so they mostly interact with the residents (\(\sigma^*\)-players). The question is: Can the mutants grow, or will they be driven to extinction?

If no mutant strategy can invade, then \(\sigma^*\) is evolutionarily stable — it is a strategy that, once established, will persist under evolutionary dynamics.

Definition — Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS)

A strategy \(\sigma^*\) is an Evolutionarily Stable Strategy if for every mutant strategy \(\sigma \neq \sigma^*\), at least one of the following holds:

  1. Strict Nash condition: \[ E(\sigma^*, \sigma^*) > E(\sigma, \sigma^*) \] The resident does strictly better against residents than the mutant does against residents.
  2. Stability condition: If \(E(\sigma^*, \sigma^*) = E(\sigma, \sigma^*)\), then \[ E(\sigma^*, \sigma) > E(\sigma, \sigma) \] When the mutant does equally well against residents, the resident must do strictly better against mutants than mutants do against themselves.

More compactly: \(\sigma^*\) is an ESS if it is a Nash equilibrium that satisfies the additional stability condition whenever the Nash inequality is not strict.

ESS is Stronger Than Nash Equilibrium

Every ESS is a Nash equilibrium, but not every Nash equilibrium is an ESS. The stability condition is the extra requirement. In fact, an ESS must be a symmetric Nash equilibrium that satisfies this invasion-resistance property.

ESS for 2-Strategy Games: The Matrix Test

For a 2-strategy symmetric game with payoff matrix \[ A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \] we can characterize ESS explicitly:

  • Pure strategy 1 is ESS if \(a > c\), OR if \(a = c\) and \(b > d\).
  • Pure strategy 2 is ESS if \(d > b\), OR if \(d = b\) and \(c > a\).
  • Mixed ESS at \(x^*\) may exist when \(a < c\) and \(d < b\) (the "Hawk-Dove" case). The ESS mix is: \[ x^* = \frac{d - b}{(a - c) + (d - b)} \]
Worked Example — Finding the ESS of Hawk-Dove

With our Hawk-Dove matrix \(A = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 4 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\):

Step 1: Is pure Hawk an ESS?

Check \(a > c\): Is \(-1 > 0\)? No. So pure Hawk is not an ESS.

Step 2: Is pure Dove an ESS?

Check \(d > b\): Is \(2 > 4\)? No. So pure Dove is not an ESS.

Step 3: Mixed ESS?

Since \(a = -1 < 0 = c\) and \(d = 2 < 4 = b\), the conditions for a mixed ESS are met. The ESS frequency of Hawks is:

\[ x^* = \frac{d - b}{(a - c) + (d - b)} = \frac{2 - 4}{(-1 - 0) + (2 - 4)} = \frac{-2}{-1 + (-2)} = \frac{-2}{-3} = \frac{2}{3} \]

So the ESS is a population with \(\frac{2}{3}\) Hawks and \(\frac{1}{3}\) Doves.

Verification: At \(x^* = 2/3\):

\[ f_H = 4 - 5 \cdot \tfrac{2}{3} = 4 - \tfrac{10}{3} = \tfrac{2}{3}, \qquad f_D = 2 - 2 \cdot \tfrac{2}{3} = 2 - \tfrac{4}{3} = \tfrac{2}{3}. \]

Both strategies have equal fitness at the ESS — as they must at any interior equilibrium. Neither strategy has a fitness advantage, so neither can invade.

The Bishop-Cannings Theorem

Theorem — Bishop-Cannings (1978)

If \(\sigma^*\) is a mixed ESS with support \(S = \{i : \sigma^*_i > 0\}\), then every pure strategy in the support has equal fitness against \(\sigma^*\):

\[ f_i(\sigma^*) = f_j(\sigma^*) = \bar{f}(\sigma^*) \qquad \text{for all } i, j \in S. \]

Furthermore, any pure strategy not in the support must have strictly lower fitness:

\[ f_k(\sigma^*) < \bar{f}(\sigma^*) \qquad \text{for all } k \notin S. \]
Why Equal Fitness?

If one strategy in the mix had higher fitness, selection would increase its frequency and decrease the others. The mix could only be stable if there is no fitness advantage to any component — they all perform equally. This is the indifference principle, the same logic behind mixed Nash equilibria in classical game theory (Module 6), but now with an evolutionary interpretation.

5. The Replicator Equation

We now arrive at the central equation of evolutionary game theory. The ESS is a static concept — it tells us what might be stable, but not how we get there. The replicator equation provides the dynamics: it tells us exactly how strategy frequencies change over time.

Derivation from First Principles

Consider a large population of size \(N\). Let \(n_i\) be the number of individuals using strategy \(i\), so \(x_i = n_i / N\). Each individual reproduces (or is imitated) at a rate proportional to its fitness \(f_i(\mathbf{x})\).

Step 1: Growth of strategy \(i\).

The number of strategy-\(i\) individuals grows according to:

\[ \dot{n}_i = f_i(\mathbf{x}) \cdot n_i \]

This says that the rate of increase is proportional to both the current number \(n_i\) and the fitness \(f_i\) — the hallmark of exponential-type growth (Module 4).

Step 2: Total population growth.

The total population \(N = \sum_i n_i\) grows as:

\[ \dot{N} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \dot{n}_i = \sum_{i=1}^{n} f_i(\mathbf{x}) \cdot n_i = N \sum_{i=1}^{n} f_i(\mathbf{x}) \cdot x_i = N \bar{f}(\mathbf{x}) \]

Step 3: Derive the frequency dynamics.

Since \(x_i = n_i / N\), we use the quotient rule (Module 3):

\[ \dot{x}_i = \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{n_i}{N}\right) = \frac{\dot{n}_i \cdot N - n_i \cdot \dot{N}}{N^2} \] \[ = \frac{f_i n_i \cdot N - n_i \cdot N\bar{f}}{N^2} = \frac{n_i}{N}\big(f_i - \bar{f}\big) = x_i\big(f_i(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{f}(\mathbf{x})\big) \]
The Replicator Equation

The replicator equation governs the evolution of strategy frequencies:

\[ \boxed{\dot{x}_i = x_i \Big[ f_i(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{f}(\mathbf{x}) \Big]} \] (R)

where \(f_i(\mathbf{x}) = (A\mathbf{x})_i\) is the fitness of strategy \(i\) and \(\bar{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^\top A \mathbf{x}\) is the average population fitness.

Reading the Replicator Equation

The equation says: the rate of change of \(x_i\) equals \(x_i\) times the excess fitness of strategy \(i\) over the population average.

  • If \(f_i > \bar{f}\): strategy \(i\) does better than average → \(\dot{x}_i > 0\) → frequency grows.
  • If \(f_i < \bar{f}\): strategy \(i\) does worse than average → \(\dot{x}_i < 0\) → frequency shrinks.
  • If \(f_i = \bar{f}\): strategy \(i\) is exactly average → \(\dot{x}_i = 0\) → frequency is stationary.
  • If \(x_i = 0\): strategy \(i\) is absent → \(\dot{x}_i = 0\) → extinct strategies stay extinct.

Fundamental Properties

Properties of the Replicator Equation
  1. Simplex invariance: If \(\mathbf{x}(0) \in \Delta^{n-1}\), then \(\mathbf{x}(t) \in \Delta^{n-1}\) for all \(t \geq 0\). Proof: \(\sum_i \dot{x}_i = \sum_i x_i(f_i - \bar{f}) = \bar{f} - \bar{f} = 0\).
  2. Boundary invariance: If \(x_i(0) = 0\), then \(x_i(t) = 0\) for all \(t\). Once a strategy goes extinct, it cannot reappear (there is no mutation in the basic model).
  3. Interior invariance: If \(x_i(0) > 0\) for all \(i\), then \(x_i(t) > 0\) for all \(i\) and all \(t\). Strategies present at the start remain present (though they may approach zero asymptotically).
  4. Fixed points: \(\mathbf{x}^*\) is a fixed point if and only if for every \(i\): either \(x_i^* = 0\) or \(f_i(\mathbf{x}^*) = \bar{f}(\mathbf{x}^*)\). Interior fixed points require all strategies to have equal fitness.
Where Did All the Math Come Together?

The replicator equation uses almost everything from the earlier modules:

  • Module 1: Functions and algebraic manipulation.
  • Module 2: Matrix-vector products for computing \(f_i = (Ax)_i\), quadratic forms for \(\bar{f} = \mathbf{x}^\top A \mathbf{x}\).
  • Module 3: The quotient rule in the derivation, derivatives for rates of change.
  • Module 4: This is a system of differential equations — fixed points, stability, phase portraits all apply.
  • Module 5: The fitness function is an expected value; the population state is a probability distribution.
  • Module 6: The payoff matrix, Nash equilibrium as a special case of fixed points.

The 2-Strategy Simplification

For two strategies, let \(x = x_1\) (so \(x_2 = 1 - x\)). The replicator equation simplifies to a single ODE:

\[ \dot{x} = x(1 - x)\big[f_1(\mathbf{x}) - f_2(\mathbf{x})\big] \]

Derivation:

\[ \dot{x}_1 = x_1\big[f_1 - \bar{f}\big] = x_1\big[f_1 - x_1 f_1 - x_2 f_2\big] = x_1\big[(1 - x_1)f_1 - x_2 f_2\big] = x_1\big[x_2 f_1 - x_2 f_2\big] = x_1 x_2 (f_1 - f_2) \]

Since \(x_2 = 1 - x_1\), this gives \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)(f_1 - f_2)\).

Structure of the 2-Strategy Equation

The factor \(x(1-x)\) ensures that \(x = 0\) and \(x = 1\) are always fixed points (boundary invariance). Interior dynamics are governed entirely by the sign of \(f_1 - f_2\): which strategy has higher fitness. The factor \(x(1-x)\) means changes are fastest at intermediate frequencies and slow near the boundaries — a logistic-type growth pattern (Module 4).

6. 2-Strategy Replicator Dynamics

Let us now analyze the replicator dynamics for all major types of 2×2 symmetric games. With payoff matrix \[ A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \] and \(x\) = fraction playing strategy 1, the fitnesses are:

\[ f_1(x) = ax + b(1 - x) = b + (a - b)x \] \[ f_2(x) = cx + d(1 - x) = d + (c - d)x \]

The fitness difference is:

\[ f_1 - f_2 = (b - d) + \big[(a - b) - (c - d)\big]x = (b - d) + (a - b - c + d)x \]

The replicator equation \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)(f_1 - f_2)\) always has fixed points at \(x = 0\) and \(x = 1\). An interior fixed point \(x^* \in (0, 1)\) exists when \(f_1(x^*) = f_2(x^*)\), giving:

\[ x^* = \frac{d - b}{(a - c) + (d - b)} = \frac{d - b}{a - b - c + d} \]

(provided the denominator is nonzero and \(x^* \in (0, 1)\)).

Case 1: Prisoner's Dilemma — Defection Dominates

Prisoner's Dilemma: \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 5 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\)

Strategy 1 = Cooperate, Strategy 2 = Defect. Here \(c = 5 > a = 3\) and \(d = 1 > b = 0\), so Defect strictly dominates Cooperate.

Fitness difference:

\[ f_C - f_D = (0 - 1) + (3 - 0 - 5 + 1)x = -1 - x \]

Since \(-1 - x < 0\) for all \(x \in [0, 1]\), cooperators always have lower fitness.

Dynamics: \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)(-1-x) < 0\) for all \(x \in (0, 1)\).

Result: \(x \to 0\). Cooperation goes extinct. The only stable equilibrium is \(x = 0\) (all Defect). This is the evolutionary tragedy — the population evolves to universal defection even though mutual cooperation (\(a = 3\)) gives a higher payoff than mutual defection (\(d = 1\)).

Case 2: Hawk-Dove — Polymorphic Coexistence

Hawk-Dove: \(A = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 4 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\)

Strategy 1 = Hawk, Strategy 2 = Dove. Here \(c = 0 > a = -1\) and \(b = 4 > d = 2\). Neither strategy dominates: Hawk beats Dove, but Dove beats Hawk (in a sense).

Fitness difference:

\[ f_H - f_D = (4 - 2) + (-1 - 4 - 0 + 2)x = 2 - 3x \]

Interior fixed point:

\[ f_H = f_D \implies 2 - 3x^* = 0 \implies x^* = \frac{2}{3} \]

Stability analysis:

  • For \(x < 2/3\): \(f_H - f_D = 2 - 3x > 0\), so \(\dot{x} > 0\) (Hawks increase).
  • For \(x > 2/3\): \(f_H - f_D = 2 - 3x < 0\), so \(\dot{x} < 0\) (Hawks decrease).

Result: \(x \to 2/3\) from any interior initial condition. The population converges to a stable polymorphism with 2/3 Hawks and 1/3 Doves. Both strategies coexist at equilibrium, and this is the ESS we calculated earlier.

Case 3: Coordination Game — Bistability

Coordination: \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\)

Strategy 1 and 2 are two conventions (e.g., driving on the left vs. right). Players get positive payoff only when they coordinate on the same convention. Here \(a = 3 > c = 0\) and \(d = 2 > b = 0\).

Fitness difference:

\[ f_1 - f_2 = (0 - 2) + (3 - 0 - 0 + 2)x = -2 + 5x \]

Interior fixed point:

\[ f_1 = f_2 \implies -2 + 5x^* = 0 \implies x^* = \frac{2}{5} = 0.4 \]

Stability analysis:

  • For \(x < 2/5\): \(f_1 - f_2 < 0\), so \(\dot{x} < 0\) → \(x \to 0\).
  • For \(x > 2/5\): \(f_1 - f_2 > 0\), so \(\dot{x} > 0\) → \(x \to 1\).

Result: The interior fixed point \(x^* = 2/5\) is unstable. It acts as a basin boundary. If the initial frequency of strategy 1 is above 2/5, the population converges to all-strategy-1. If below 2/5, it converges to all-strategy-2. This is bistability — history (initial conditions) determines the outcome.

Summary: Classification of 2×2 Games

Condition Game Type Dynamics Stable Outcomes
\(a > c, d > b\) Coordination Bistable \(x = 0\) and \(x = 1\) stable; interior unstable
\(a < c, d < b\) Hawk-Dove / Anti-coordination Coexistence Interior \(x^*\) stable; boundaries unstable
\(a > c, b > d\) Dominance (S1 dominates) \(x \to 1\) \(x = 1\) globally stable
\(a < c, b < d\) Dominance (S2 dominates) \(x \to 0\) \(x = 0\) globally stable
A Unifying Perspective

All four cases arise from one equation: \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)(f_1 - f_2)\). The only thing that differs is the sign pattern of \(f_1 - f_2\). When \(f_1 - f_2\) is always positive, strategy 1 dominates. When it's always negative, strategy 2 dominates. When it changes sign, there's an interior fixed point, and whether that fixed point is stable or unstable gives us the Hawk-Dove or coordination cases. The mathematics of Module 4 (fixed points and stability) is doing all the work.

7. Multi-Strategy Replicator Dynamics

With three or more strategies, the dynamics become richer — and more interesting. The population state lives on the simplex \(\Delta^{n-1}\), and we can observe phenomena impossible in 2-strategy games: cycles, heteroclinic orbits, and chaos (for \(n \geq 4\)).

The 3-Strategy Case: Rock-Paper-Scissors

The most famous 3-strategy game is Rock-Paper-Scissors (RPS), with payoff matrix:

\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \]

where rows/columns represent R, P, S respectively. Each strategy beats one other (\(+1\)), loses to another (\(-1\)), and ties with itself (\(0\)).

Let \(\mathbf{x} = (x_R, x_P, x_S)\) with \(x_R + x_P + x_S = 1\). The fitnesses are:

\[ f_R = -x_P + x_S, \qquad f_P = x_R - x_S, \qquad f_S = -x_R + x_P \]

Average fitness:

\[ \bar{f} = x_R f_R + x_P f_P + x_S f_S = x_R(-x_P + x_S) + x_P(x_R - x_S) + x_S(-x_R + x_P) \] \[ = -x_R x_P + x_R x_S + x_P x_R - x_P x_S - x_S x_R + x_S x_P = 0 \]

The average fitness is zero for all population states — this is because \(A\) is an antisymmetric (skew-symmetric) matrix (\(a_{ij} = -a_{ji}\)), so \(\mathbf{x}^\top A \mathbf{x} = 0\).

The replicator equations simplify to:

\[ \dot{x}_R = x_R(-x_P + x_S), \qquad \dot{x}_P = x_P(x_R - x_S), \qquad \dot{x}_S = x_S(-x_R + x_P) \]

Interior fixed point: Setting all excess fitnesses to zero:

\[ -x_P + x_S = 0, \quad x_R - x_S = 0, \quad -x_R + x_P = 0 \]

This gives \(x_R = x_P = x_S = 1/3\) — the center of the simplex.

Cyclic Dynamics and the Conserved Quantity

Theorem — Conserved Quantity for Standard RPS

For the standard Rock-Paper-Scissors game with antisymmetric payoffs, the function

\[ H(\mathbf{x}) = x_R \cdot x_P \cdot x_S \]

is a constant of motion (conserved quantity) along trajectories of the replicator dynamics. That is, \(\frac{d}{dt}H(\mathbf{x}(t)) = 0\).

Proof sketch:

\[ \frac{dH}{dt} = \dot{x}_R x_P x_S + x_R \dot{x}_P x_S + x_R x_P \dot{x}_S \] \[ = x_R x_P x_S \big[(-x_P + x_S) + (x_R - x_S) + (-x_R + x_P)\big] = x_R x_P x_S \cdot 0 = 0 \qquad \square \]
What Does This Mean?

Because \(H = x_R x_P x_S\) is conserved, trajectories are closed orbits around the center \((1/3, 1/3, 1/3)\). The system cycles endlessly: Rock increases, then Paper takes over, then Scissors, then Rock again. The interior fixed point is neutrally stable (Lyapunov stable but not asymptotically stable) — trajectories orbit it but never converge to it.

This is fundamentally different from 2-strategy games, where interior fixed points are either attracting or repelling. With 3+ strategies, new dynamical possibilities emerge.

Asymmetric RPS: Spiraling In or Out

The closed orbits of standard RPS are structurally unstable — they rely on exact symmetry. If we perturb the payoffs even slightly (e.g., making winning as Rock worth 1.1 instead of 1), the conserved quantity is destroyed. Depending on the perturbation, trajectories either spiral inward to the interior fixed point (stable focus) or spiral outward toward the boundary (unstable focus, leading to extinction of strategies). This sensitivity is ecologically important: in biological RPS-type systems, small asymmetries determine whether species coexist or go extinct.

8. Connection Between ESS and Replicator Dynamics

We now have two central concepts — the ESS (a static invasion-resistance concept) and the replicator dynamics (a dynamical system). How do they relate? The answer is one of the most elegant results in evolutionary game theory.

Theorem — The Folk Theorem of Evolutionary Game Theory

The following implications hold:

  1. ESS \(\Rightarrow\) Asymptotically Stable. If \(\mathbf{x}^*\) is an ESS, then it is an asymptotically stable fixed point of the replicator dynamics. That is, nearby trajectories converge to \(\mathbf{x}^*\).
  2. Asymptotically Stable \(\not\Rightarrow\) ESS. The converse is false: there exist asymptotically stable states in the replicator dynamics that are not ESS.
  3. Nash Equilibrium \(\Leftarrow\) Fixed Point (in interior). Every interior fixed point of the replicator dynamics corresponds to a (symmetric) Nash equilibrium. But not every Nash equilibrium is a rest point of the replicator dynamics (boundary Nash equilibria may not satisfy the replicator fixed-point condition in the required way).
  4. Strict Nash \(\Rightarrow\) Asymptotically Stable. Every strict Nash equilibrium (which must be a pure strategy) is asymptotically stable in the replicator dynamics.

We can summarize the relationship as a chain of implications:

\[ \text{ESS} \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{Asymptotically Stable} \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{Lyapunov Stable} \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{Nash Equilibrium} \]

None of these arrows reverse in general. Each level is strictly stronger than the next.

Example — Stable but Not ESS

Consider a 3-strategy game where strategies 1 and 2 have identical payoffs against everything, and both dominate strategy 3. The state \((1/2, 1/2, 0)\) can be asymptotically stable in the replicator dynamics (strategy 3 dies out, and 1 and 2 coexist neutrally on the boundary), but it fails the ESS stability condition because strategy 1 cannot "beat" a mutant of strategy 2 in the second condition — they are identical.

Why the Gap?

ESS requires resistance to all possible mutant invasions, including strategies not currently present. Replicator dynamics only tracks strategies that already exist in the population. A state can be dynamically stable (nearby perturbations are corrected) without being invasion-proof against novel strategies from outside the current population. This distinction matters enormously in biology (novel mutations) and social science (novel institutional innovations).

9. Stability Analysis in Replicator Dynamics

To rigorously determine stability of fixed points, we use the techniques from Module 4: linearization (Jacobian), eigenvalue analysis, and Lyapunov functions.

Jacobian of the Replicator Equation

For the replicator equation \(\dot{x}_i = x_i[f_i(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{f}(\mathbf{x})]\), the Jacobian matrix \(J\) has entries:

\[ J_{ij} = \frac{\partial \dot{x}_i}{\partial x_j} \]

For the linear fitness case \(f_i = (A\mathbf{x})_i\), computing the partial derivatives yields:

\[ J_{ij} = \begin{cases} x_i\Big[a_{ij} - \sum_k x_k a_{kj} - (A\mathbf{x})_j\Big] & \text{if } i \neq j \\[6pt] (f_i - \bar{f}) + x_i\Big[a_{ii} - \sum_k x_k a_{ki} - (A\mathbf{x})_i\Big] & \text{if } i = j \end{cases} \]

In practice, for 2-strategy games we can avoid the full Jacobian. But for 3+ strategies, it becomes essential. Let us work through a complete example.

Worked Example: Stability of the Hawk-Dove Interior Fixed Point

Jacobian Analysis for Hawk-Dove

For the 2-strategy case with \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)(f_1 - f_2)\), let \(g(x) = f_1(x) - f_2(x)\). Then \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)g(x)\), and the derivative is:

\[ \frac{d\dot{x}}{dx} = (1 - 2x)g(x) + x(1-x)g'(x) \]

For Hawk-Dove: \(g(x) = 2 - 3x\) and \(g'(x) = -3\). At the interior fixed point \(x^* = 2/3\) where \(g(x^*) = 0\):

\[ \left.\frac{d\dot{x}}{dx}\right|_{x^*} = (1 - 2 \cdot \tfrac{2}{3}) \cdot 0 + \tfrac{2}{3} \cdot \tfrac{1}{3} \cdot (-3) = -\frac{2}{3} < 0 \]

The derivative is negative, confirming that \(x^* = 2/3\) is asymptotically stable. (This is the 1D version of eigenvalue analysis: a negative "eigenvalue" means the fixed point attracts.)

Lyapunov Functions for the Replicator Equation

A Lyapunov function (Module 4) is a function that decreases along trajectories, proving stability without computing eigenvalues. For the replicator equation, several natural Lyapunov functions exist.

Theorem — Lyapunov Function for ESS

If \(\mathbf{x}^*\) is an ESS, then the function

\[ V(\mathbf{x}) = -\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^* \ln x_i \]

is a Lyapunov function for the replicator dynamics in a neighborhood of \(\mathbf{x}^*\). That is, \(\dot{V} \leq 0\) along trajectories, with equality only at \(\mathbf{x}^*\).

This function comes from information theory — it is related to the Kullback-Leibler divergence (relative entropy) from \(\mathbf{x}\) to \(\mathbf{x}^*\). Its derivative along replicator trajectories is:

\[ \dot{V} = -\sum_i x_i^* \frac{\dot{x}_i}{x_i} = -\sum_i x_i^*\big[f_i(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{f}(\mathbf{x})\big] = -\left[\sum_i x_i^* f_i(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{f}(\mathbf{x})\right] \] \[ = -\big[(\mathbf{x}^*)^\top A\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}^\top A\mathbf{x}\big] = -(\mathbf{x}^* - \mathbf{x})^\top A\mathbf{x} \]

The ESS condition guarantees that this is negative near \(\mathbf{x}^*\), completing the proof.

Practical Tip

For 2-strategy games, eigenvalue (derivative) analysis is simplest. For 3+ strategies, Lyapunov functions are often more powerful than direct Jacobian computation, especially when exact eigenvalues are hard to compute. The choice of method depends on the problem.

Linearization at the RPS Interior Fixed Point

Eigenvalue Analysis for Rock-Paper-Scissors

At the fixed point \(\mathbf{x}^* = (1/3, 1/3, 1/3)\), we can compute the Jacobian of the replicator dynamics restricted to the simplex (which reduces to a 2D system). After eliminating one variable using \(x_S = 1 - x_R - x_P\), the 2D Jacobian at the fixed point has eigenvalues:

\[ \lambda_{1,2} = \pm \frac{i}{\sqrt{3}} \]

These are purely imaginary eigenvalues, confirming that the fixed point is a center: trajectories orbit it in closed curves. The linearization correctly predicts the cyclic behavior we deduced from the conserved quantity \(H = x_R x_P x_S\).

For asymmetric RPS (e.g., slightly perturbed payoffs), the eigenvalues acquire nonzero real parts, turning the center into a stable or unstable spiral.

10. Extensions and Generalizations

Selection-Mutation Dynamics

The basic replicator equation has a limitation: extinct strategies stay extinct. In reality, new strategies can appear through mutation (in biology) or experimentation (in social systems). The replicator-mutator equation incorporates this:

\[ \dot{x}_i = \sum_{j=1}^{n} x_j f_j(\mathbf{x}) \, Q_{ji} - x_i \bar{f}(\mathbf{x}) \]

where \(Q_{ji}\) is the probability that an offspring of a type-\(j\) individual becomes type \(i\) (the mutation matrix). When \(Q = I\) (identity, no mutation), this reduces to the standard replicator equation.

With mutation, the boundary is no longer invariant — extinct strategies can reappear. This typically shifts fixed points slightly into the interior and can change stability properties. Small mutation rates produce dynamics close to the replicator equation but with regularizing effects.

Finite Populations and Stochastic Effects

The replicator equation assumes an infinite (or very large) population. In finite populations, random drift plays an important role:

  • Moran process: A stochastic model where at each time step, one individual is chosen to reproduce (proportional to fitness) and one dies randomly. The population size stays constant. Strategy frequencies perform a random walk with a drift determined by fitness differences.
  • Fixation probability: In a population of size \(N\), the probability that a single mutant eventually takes over the whole population. For a neutral mutant, this is \(1/N\). For a mutant with fitness advantage, the Moran process gives: \[ \rho = \frac{1 - 1/r}{1 - 1/r^N} \] where \(r\) is the relative fitness of the mutant.
  • Drift vs. selection: When population size \(N\) is small, random drift can overpower selection. A strategy that is slightly fitter may still go extinct by chance. As \(N \to \infty\), the stochastic dynamics converge to the deterministic replicator equation.
Why Finite Populations Matter

Ostrom's institutional analysis (Module 8) often involves small communities — a few dozen to a few hundred individuals. In these settings, the deterministic replicator equation is only an approximation. Stochastic effects can lead to the extinction of cooperative strategies that would survive in larger populations. Understanding this tension between deterministic and stochastic dynamics is crucial for institutional design.

Spatial and Network Games

The standard replicator equation assumes well-mixed populations: any individual can interact with any other. In reality, interactions are often local — individuals interact primarily with neighbors on a spatial lattice or social network.

Spatial structure can dramatically change evolutionary outcomes. Most famously, Nowak and May (1992) showed that cooperation can survive in spatial Prisoner's Dilemma games even though it is doomed in well-mixed populations. Cooperators can form clusters that protect each other. This is one of the "five rules for the evolution of cooperation" (Nowak, 2006): spatial selection.

Where This Leads

These extensions — mutation, finite populations, spatial structure — are active research areas. In Module 8, we will see how Ostrom's design principles for successful institutions relate to these mathematical frameworks, providing concrete mechanisms that enable cooperation in real-world settings where the basic theory predicts it should fail.

Exercise 7.1 — Fitness Calculation

Consider a 2-strategy game with payoff matrix \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 6 \\ 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\) and population state \(x = 0.4\) (40% play strategy 1).

(a) Compute \(f_1(x)\), \(f_2(x)\), and \(\bar{f}(x)\).

(b) Which strategy is currently fitter? Is \(x\) increasing or decreasing?

(c) Find the interior fixed point \(x^*\). Classify the game type.

(a)

\(f_1 = 2(0.4) + 6(0.6) = 0.8 + 3.6 = 4.4\)
\(f_2 = 3(0.4) + 1(0.6) = 1.2 + 0.6 = 1.8\)
\(\bar{f} = 0.4(4.4) + 0.6(1.8) = 1.76 + 1.08 = 2.84\)

(b)

\(f_1 = 4.4 > 1.8 = f_2\), so strategy 1 is fitter. Since \(f_1 > \bar{f}\), \(\dot{x} > 0\), so \(x\) is increasing — strategy 1 is growing.

(c)

\(f_1 = f_2\): \(2x + 6(1-x) = 3x + 1(1-x)\) gives \(6 - 4x = 1 + 2x\), so \(5 = 6x\), hence \(x^* = 5/6 \approx 0.833\).

Since \(a = 2 < 3 = c\) and \(d = 1 < 6 = b\), this is a Hawk-Dove (anti-coordination) type game. The interior fixed point \(x^* = 5/6\) is stable.

Exercise 7.2 — ESS Determination

For each payoff matrix, determine all ESS:

(a) \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\)

(b) \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\)

(c) \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\)

(a) \(a = 4 > 2 = c\) and \(d = 3 > 1 = b\). Both pure strategies satisfy the strict Nash condition: strategy 1 is ESS (since \(4 > 2\)) and strategy 2 is ESS (since \(3 > 1\)). This is a coordination game with two pure ESS. There is an unstable interior fixed point at \(x^* = (3-1)/((4-2)+(3-1)) = 2/4 = 1/2\).

(b) \(a = 1 < 2 = c\) and \(d = 0 < 3 = b\). Anti-coordination (Hawk-Dove type). Mixed ESS at \(x^* = (0-3)/((1-2)+(0-3)) = -3/(-1-3) = -3/-4 = 3/4\). The unique ESS is the mixed strategy \((3/4, 1/4)\).

(c) All payoffs are equal, so every strategy has equal fitness regardless of population state. No strategy can strictly outperform any other, so the ESS conditions are never strictly satisfied. No ESS exists — every population state is a fixed point but none is invasion-resistant.

Exercise 7.3 — Replicator Dynamics Derivation

For the game with payoff matrix \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 3 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\):

(a) Write out the full replicator equation \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)(f_1 - f_2)\) as a function of \(x\) alone.

(b) Find all fixed points.

(c) Determine the stability of each fixed point by evaluating \(\frac{d\dot{x}}{dx}\).

(d) Sketch the phase portrait on \([0, 1]\) and describe the long-term behavior.

(a)

\(f_1 = 0 \cdot x + 3(1-x) = 3 - 3x\)
\(f_2 = 1 \cdot x + 1(1-x) = 1\)
\(f_1 - f_2 = 3 - 3x - 1 = 2 - 3x\)
\(\dot{x} = x(1-x)(2 - 3x)\)

(b) Fixed points: \(x = 0\), \(x = 1\), and \(x^* = 2/3\).

(c) Let \(h(x) = x(1-x)(2-3x) = x(2 - 3x - 2x + 3x^2) = x(2 - 5x + 3x^2)\).

\(h'(x) = (1-2x)(2-3x) + x(1-x)(-3) = (1-2x)(2-3x) - 3x(1-x)\).

At \(x=0\): \(h'(0) = 1 \cdot 2 - 0 = 2 > 0\) → unstable.
At \(x=1\): \(h'(1) = (1-2)(2-3) - 3(0) = (-1)(-1) = 1 > 0\) → unstable.
At \(x=2/3\): \(h'(2/3) = (1-4/3)(0) - 3(2/3)(1/3) = 0 - 2/3 = -2/3 < 0\) → stable.

(d) This is an anti-coordination game. From any interior starting point, the population converges to \(x^* = 2/3\). The boundaries repel: if nearly everyone uses one strategy, the other strategy is fitter and grows. The ESS is the polymorphic state \((2/3, 1/3)\).

Exercise 7.4 — RPS Conserved Quantity

For the Rock-Paper-Scissors game with payoff matrix \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\), verify that \(H(\mathbf{x}) = x_R x_P x_S\) is conserved by computing \(\frac{dH}{dt}\) directly. (You may use the replicator equations \(\dot{x}_R = x_R(x_S - x_P)\), \(\dot{x}_P = x_P(x_R - x_S)\), \(\dot{x}_S = x_S(x_P - x_R)\).)

By the product rule:

\[ \frac{dH}{dt} = \dot{x}_R x_P x_S + x_R \dot{x}_P x_S + x_R x_P \dot{x}_S \]

Substituting:

\[ = x_R(x_S - x_P) \cdot x_P x_S + x_R \cdot x_P(x_R - x_S) \cdot x_S + x_R x_P \cdot x_S(x_P - x_R) \] \[ = x_R x_P x_S \Big[(x_S - x_P) + (x_R - x_S) + (x_P - x_R)\Big] \] \[ = x_R x_P x_S \cdot 0 = 0 \]

The terms in the bracket cancel: \(x_S - x_P + x_R - x_S + x_P - x_R = 0\). Therefore \(H\) is constant along every trajectory.

Exercise 7.5 — From Replicator to ESS

True or false? Justify each answer.

(a) Every ESS is a Nash equilibrium.

(b) Every Nash equilibrium is an ESS.

(c) If \(\mathbf{x}^*\) is asymptotically stable in the replicator dynamics, then it is an ESS.

(d) If \(\mathbf{x}^*\) is an ESS, then it is asymptotically stable in the replicator dynamics.

(e) In a 2-strategy game with a dominant strategy, the dominant strategy is always an ESS.

(a) True. The first condition of the ESS definition (\(E(\sigma^*, \sigma^*) \geq E(\sigma, \sigma^*)\)) is exactly the Nash equilibrium condition.

(b) False. A Nash equilibrium can fail the stability condition. For example, in a game where all payoffs are equal, every strategy is a Nash equilibrium but none is an ESS.

(c) False. Asymptotic stability in the replicator dynamics does not imply ESS. There are counterexamples in games with 3+ strategies (see Section 8).

(d) True. This is the key implication of the folk theorem: ESS implies asymptotic stability in the replicator dynamics.

(e) True. If strategy 1 dominates (\(a > c\) and \(b > d\)), then \(E(1, 1) = a > c = E(2, 1)\), satisfying the strict Nash condition. Since this holds strictly, the stability condition is not needed. So strategy 1 is ESS.

Exercise 7.6 — Institutional Evolution (Conceptual)

Consider a community managing a shared resource (like a fishery). Members can adopt one of two norms: "Sustainable Harvest" (S) or "Overexploit" (O). When both use S, both get payoff 4. When one uses S and the other O, the overexploiter gets 5 and the sustainable harvester gets 1. When both overexploit, both get 2.

(a) Write the payoff matrix.

(b) What type of game is this? Find all fixed points of the replicator dynamics.

(c) In the absence of institutions, what does the replicator equation predict will happen?

(d) How might Ostrom's design principles change the payoff matrix to support cooperation? (Preview of Module 8.)

(a) \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 5 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\) where row 1 = Sustainable, row 2 = Overexploit.

(b) Since \(c = 5 > a = 4\) and \(d = 2 > b = 1\), Overexploit dominates Sustainable. This is a Prisoner's Dilemma. Fixed points: \(x = 0\) (all Overexploit, stable), \(x = 1\) (all Sustainable, unstable). No interior fixed point.

(c) The replicator equation predicts \(x \to 0\): the community evolves toward universal overexploitation, a tragedy of the commons.

(d) Ostrom's principles suggest: (i) monitoring and sanctioning can reduce the payoff of overexploitation (lowering \(c\) and \(d\)), (ii) collective-choice arrangements can increase the payoff of mutual cooperation (raising \(a\)). If sanctions are strong enough that \(a > c\), the payoff matrix changes to a coordination game or dominance of cooperation, and the replicator dynamics can now support sustainable harvesting.
