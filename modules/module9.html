1. From Rivalrous to Non-Rivalrous Goods

Throughout this course, we have studied games where agents compete over scarce resources. In Module 6, we analyzed the Prisoner’s Dilemma and public goods problems. In Module 8, we studied common-pool resources (CPR) and Ostrom’s institutional design principles for managing them. All of those models rested on a fundamental economic assumption: rivalry in consumption. In this module, we challenge that assumption and explore what happens when goods are non-rivalrous — or even anti-rivalrous.

1.1 The Classical Taxonomy of Goods

Economists classify goods along two independent dimensions:

  • Rivalry: Does one person’s consumption reduce the amount available to others?
  • Excludability: Can non-payers be prevented from accessing the good?

These two binary distinctions produce the classic 2×2 matrix of good types:

In Module 8, we studied common-pool resources (CPR) — the upper-right cell. Ostrom’s design principles addressed the challenge of governing rivalrous, non-excludable goods. The mathematics assumed that total extraction must satisfy \(\sum_{i=1}^n c_i \leq R\), where \(R\) is the finite resource stock. This entire module asks: what happens when we remove or reverse that constraint?

1.2 Mathematical Formalization of Rivalry

Definition — Rivalrous Good

A good is rivalrous if it has a finite stock \(R > 0\) and the consumption amounts \(c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_n\) by \(n\) agents must satisfy the resource constraint:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n} c_i \leq R. \]

The amount available to agent \(i\) when all others have consumed is at most \(R - \sum_{j \neq i} c_j\). Each unit consumed by one agent is a unit unavailable to others.

Under rivalry, agent \(i\)’s utility from consumption is:

\[ u_i = v(c_i) \quad \text{subject to} \quad c_i \leq R - \sum_{j \neq i} c_j, \]

where \(v(\cdot)\) is an increasing, concave value function (e.g., \(v(c) = c^\alpha\) for \(0 < \alpha < 1\), as in Module 3). The critical feature is that the feasible set for each agent shrinks as others consume more. This creates the strategic tension at the heart of CPR problems.

1.3 Non-Rivalrous Goods: Removing the Constraint

Definition — Non-Rivalrous Good

A good is non-rivalrous if one agent’s consumption does not reduce the amount available to any other agent. Formally, each agent can consume the full good:

\[ c_i = R \quad \text{for all } i \in \{1, 2, \ldots, n\}, \]

regardless of what other agents do. There is no resource constraint linking the agents’ consumption.

The canonical examples of non-rivalrous goods are information goods:

  • A mathematical theorem: my knowing the Pythagorean theorem does not prevent you from knowing it.
  • A piece of software: copying a program does not degrade the original.
  • A musical recording (once digitized): the file can be replicated at near-zero marginal cost.
  • A scientific paper: reading it does not consume it.
Intuition — Why Non-Rivalry Changes Everything

In the CPR models of Module 8, the fundamental problem was overuse: each agent had an incentive to extract more than the socially optimal amount because they captured all the benefit of their extraction but shared the cost (resource depletion) with everyone. The tragedy of the commons arises from the negative externality of rivalry.

With non-rivalrous goods, there is no overuse problem — you literally cannot “overuse” an idea. Instead, the problem shifts to underprovision: who will bear the cost of creating the good in the first place, knowing that once created, everyone can use it for free?

1.4 The Game-Theoretic Implications

Let us contrast the payoff structures. In a rivalrous CPR game with \(n\) agents extracting from a common pool of size \(R\), the payoff for agent \(i\) choosing extraction level \(e_i\) is (recall Module 8):

\[ \pi_i^{\text{CPR}} = p \cdot e_i \cdot \frac{R - \sum_{j=1}^n e_j}{R} - w \cdot e_i, \]

where \(p\) is the price per unit of harvest and \(w\) is the cost per unit of effort. The key term is \(\frac{R - \sum_j e_j}{R}\): total effort by all agents degrades the resource, reducing everyone’s return. This creates a negative externality — more extraction by others hurts you.

For a non-rivalrous public good, the payoff structure is fundamentally different. Agent \(i\) choosing contribution level \(e_i\) receives:

\[ \pi_i^{\text{PG}} = b \cdot f\!\left(\sum_{j=1}^n e_j\right) - c \cdot e_i, \]

where \(b\) is the marginal benefit of the public good, \(f(\cdot)\) is a production function, and \(c\) is the marginal cost of contributing. Notice: there is no term that degrades with total usage. Everyone benefits from the total contributions \(\sum_j e_j\). The problem is not overuse but free-riding: each agent wants to enjoy \(b \cdot f(\sum_j e_j)\) while minimizing their own cost \(c \cdot e_i\).

Connection to Module 6

The public goods game is a generalized \(n\)-player Prisoner’s Dilemma. As we showed in Module 6, the dominant strategy is to contribute nothing (\(e_i = 0\)), even though everyone contributing a positive amount would make all players better off. The Nash equilibrium is Pareto-dominated by the social optimum. This is the classic underprovision problem for public goods.

The question that will drive this entire module is: given that digital goods are non-rivalrous, why are open-source software, Wikipedia, and other digital commons so successful? Standard theory predicts underprovision. Reality shows abundance. Resolving this puzzle requires new mathematical tools — and the concept of anti-rivalry.

2. The Digital Commons as a Game

A digital commons is a shared resource of information or software that is collectively created, maintained, and governed by a community. Key examples include:

  • Open-source software: Linux, Python, Apache, Firefox, TensorFlow.
  • Collaborative knowledge: Wikipedia, OpenStreetMap, Stack Overflow.
  • Creative Commons content: open textbooks, open access journals, public domain works.
  • Open data: government datasets, scientific repositories, genomic databases.
  • Open educational resources: MIT OpenCourseWare, Khan Academy materials, this very course.

What unites these is that they are non-rivalrous (copying costs essentially zero), non-excludable by design (open licenses ensure access), and collectively produced (many contributors, often volunteers).

2.1 The Contribution Game

Consider a community of \(n\) agents who can contribute to a digital commons. Each agent \(i\) chooses an effort level \(e_i \geq 0\). The total quality of the commons is determined by a production function applied to total effort:

\[ Q = f\!\left(\sum_{j=1}^{n} e_j\right), \]

where \(f\) is increasing and typically concave (diminishing returns to additional contributions). A natural choice is \(f(E) = E^\alpha\) for \(0 < \alpha \leq 1\), where \(E = \sum_j e_j\) is total effort.

Definition — Digital Commons Contribution Game

The digital commons contribution game is an \(n\)-player simultaneous game where:

  • Players: \(N = \{1, 2, \ldots, n\}\)
  • Strategies: Each player chooses effort \(e_i \in [0, \bar{e}]\)
  • Payoffs: \[ \pi_i = b \cdot f\!\left(\sum_{j=1}^{n} e_j\right) - c(e_i) \] where \(b > 0\) is the benefit parameter, \(f\) is the production function, and \(c(e_i)\) is the cost of contributing effort \(e_i\).

The critical feature: the benefit term \(b \cdot f(\sum_j e_j)\) is the same for every player, regardless of their individual contribution. This is because the good is non-rivalrous — everyone gets the full benefit of the commons.

2.2 Key Properties of the Digital Commons Game

The digital commons contribution game differs from classical public goods games in several important ways:

Persistence of contributions. In a one-shot public goods experiment, contributions are consumed. In a digital commons, contributions accumulate. Code committed to a repository stays there. A Wikipedia article, once written, persists. Mathematically, in a repeated version of the game, the quality at time \(t\) is:

\[ Q_t = f\!\left(\sum_{\tau=1}^{t} \sum_{j=1}^{n} e_j^{(\tau)}\right) = f(E_t), \]

where \(E_t\) is the cumulative total effort up to time \(t\). This is unlike a CPR where the stock \(R_t\) decreases with extraction: \(R_t = R_0 - \sum_{\tau=1}^{t} \sum_j c_j^{(\tau)}\).

Heterogeneous contributions. Not all contributions are equal. A core developer might contribute a critical feature; a casual user might fix a typo. We can model this by making the production function sensitive to contribution quality: \(Q = f(\sum_j \omega_j e_j)\), where \(\omega_j\) is the skill or quality weight of agent \(j\).

Modular structure. Large digital commons projects break work into small, independent modules. This dramatically reduces the cost of any individual contribution. Writing one function for Linux is far less costly than building an entire operating system. Mathematically, modularity means \(c(e_i)\) can be very small for each contribution.

2.3 Nash Equilibrium Analysis

Let us analyze the Nash equilibrium of the contribution game with linear costs: \(c(e_i) = c \cdot e_i\) and concave production: \(f(E) = E^\alpha\) for \(0 < \alpha < 1\).

Agent \(i\) maximizes:

\[ \pi_i = b \cdot \left(\sum_{j=1}^{n} e_j\right)^\alpha - c \cdot e_i. \]

Taking the first-order condition (Module 3):

\[ \frac{\partial \pi_i}{\partial e_i} = b \cdot \alpha \cdot \left(\sum_{j=1}^{n} e_j\right)^{\alpha - 1} - c = 0. \]

At a symmetric Nash equilibrium, \(e_i = e^*\) for all \(i\), so \(\sum_j e_j = n \cdot e^*\). Substituting:

\[ b \cdot \alpha \cdot (n \cdot e^*)^{\alpha - 1} = c \quad \Longrightarrow \quad e^* = \frac{1}{n} \left(\frac{b \alpha}{c}\right)^{\frac{1}{1-\alpha}}. \]

Total equilibrium effort is:

\[ E^{\text{NE}} = n \cdot e^* = \left(\frac{b \alpha}{c}\right)^{\frac{1}{1-\alpha}}. \]
Theorem — Underprovision in the Digital Commons

The socially optimal total effort \(E^{\text{SO}}\) satisfies:

\[ E^{\text{SO}} = \left(\frac{n \cdot b \cdot \alpha}{c}\right)^{\frac{1}{1-\alpha}}. \]

Therefore:

\[ \frac{E^{\text{NE}}}{E^{\text{SO}}} = \frac{1}{n^{1/(1-\alpha)}} < 1 \quad \text{for } n \geq 2. \]

The Nash equilibrium total effort is strictly less than the social optimum by a factor that decreases as the group gets larger. Larger communities underprovide more relative to the optimum.

Proof

The social planner maximizes total welfare \(W = n \cdot b \cdot E^\alpha - c \cdot E\) (since costs are borne by agents contributing total effort \(E\), and each of the \(n\) agents benefits from the commons). The first-order condition is:

\[ \frac{dW}{dE} = n \cdot b \cdot \alpha \cdot E^{\alpha - 1} - c = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad E^{\text{SO}} = \left(\frac{n \cdot b \cdot \alpha}{c}\right)^{\frac{1}{1-\alpha}}. \]

Comparing with \(E^{\text{NE}} = (b\alpha/c)^{1/(1-\alpha)}\):

\[ \frac{E^{\text{NE}}}{E^{\text{SO}}} = \left(\frac{b\alpha/c}{n \cdot b\alpha/c}\right)^{\frac{1}{1-\alpha}} = \left(\frac{1}{n}\right)^{\frac{1}{1-\alpha}} = n^{-\frac{1}{1-\alpha}}. \]

For \(n \geq 2\) and \(0 < \alpha < 1\), this ratio is strictly less than 1, and it decreases as \(n\) grows. ■

Intuition — The Free-Rider Problem in Digital Commons

Each individual captures only \(\frac{1}{n}\) of the social benefit of their contribution (they get benefit \(b\), but the total social benefit is \(n \cdot b\) since all \(n\) agents benefit from the non-rivalrous good). So each individual’s private incentive to contribute is only \(\frac{1}{n}\) of the social incentive. This gap between private and social incentives grows with group size — hence the worsening underprovision.

Yet Wikipedia has over 60 million articles across 300+ languages, and Linux runs on virtually every server on the internet. Something beyond the basic model must be at work. That is what we explore next.

Example — A Small Open-Source Project

Consider \(n = 20\) potential contributors, with \(b = 10\), \(c = 2\), and \(\alpha = 0.5\).

The Nash equilibrium total effort is: \[ E^{\text{NE}} = \left(\frac{10 \cdot 0.5}{2}\right)^{\frac{1}{0.5}} = (2.5)^2 = 6.25. \]

The socially optimal total effort is: \[ E^{\text{SO}} = \left(\frac{20 \cdot 10 \cdot 0.5}{2}\right)^{2} = (50)^2 = 2500. \]

The ratio is \(6.25 / 2500 = 0.0025\). The Nash equilibrium provides only 0.25% of the socially optimal effort. The underprovision problem is severe.

The Nash equilibrium quality is \(Q^{\text{NE}} = 6.25^{0.5} = 2.5\), while the socially optimal quality is \(Q^{\text{SO}} = 2500^{0.5} = 50\). The commons would be 20 times better under the social optimum.

3. Evolutionary Dynamics of Contribution

The Nash equilibrium analysis above treats agents as perfectly rational optimizers who play once. But real digital commons involve large populations interacting repeatedly over time, with strategies spreading through imitation and social learning. This is exactly the setting where evolutionary game theory (Module 7) applies.

3.1 Contributors vs. Free-Riders: The Binary Model

We simplify the continuous-effort model to a binary choice. Each agent is either a Contributor (C) who exerts effort \(e > 0\) at cost \(c\), or a Free-Rider (F) who exerts no effort and pays no cost. Let \(x\) denote the fraction of Contributors in the population.

In a population of size \(n\), the number of contributors is approximately \(k = xn\). The quality of the commons is \(Q = f(k \cdot e) = f(xne)\). Since the good is non-rivalrous, all agents enjoy the same benefit \(b \cdot Q\). The payoffs are:

\[ \pi_C = b \cdot f(x n e) - c, \qquad \pi_F = b \cdot f(x n e). \]

The fitness difference is:

\[ \pi_C - \pi_F = -c < 0. \]
Warning — The Dominance of Free-Riding

Under the basic model, free-riding is a strictly dominant strategy. The payoff to Contributors is always exactly \(c\) less than the payoff to Free-Riders, regardless of the population composition \(x\). The replicator dynamics (Module 7) give:

\[ \dot{x} = x(1-x)(\pi_C - \pi_F) = x(1-x)(-c) = -cx(1-x) < 0 \quad \text{for } x \in (0,1). \]

The fraction of Contributors monotonically decreases to zero. Free-riders always win. This is the evolutionary analogue of the underprovision result.

Connection to Module 7

Recall from Module 7 that when one strategy strictly dominates another, the dominated strategy goes extinct under replicator dynamics. The boundary \(x = 0\) (all Free-Riders) is a globally asymptotically stable equilibrium, while \(x = 1\) (all Contributors) is unstable. This is analogous to the Prisoner’s Dilemma dynamics.

Yet open-source software thrives. Wikipedia grows. Something is missing from our model. Let us now introduce the mechanisms that sustain contribution in practice.

3.2 Mechanism 1: Reputation and Signaling

Contributors to open-source projects and Wikipedia gain visible, verifiable reputations. GitHub profiles show contribution histories. Wikipedia user pages document editing records. This reputation has tangible value — it can lead to job offers, consulting opportunities, and social status within the community.

We model reputation as an additional payoff \(r > 0\) that contributors receive:

\[ \pi_C = b \cdot f(xne) - c + r, \qquad \pi_F = b \cdot f(xne). \]

The fitness difference becomes:

\[ \pi_C - \pi_F = r - c. \]
Proposition — Reputation Sustains Contribution

If the reputation benefit exceeds the cost of contributing (\(r > c\)), then Contributing becomes the dominant strategy. The replicator dynamics become:

\[ \dot{x} = x(1-x)(r - c) > 0 \quad \text{for } x \in (0,1), \]

and the population converges to \(x = 1\) (all Contributors). The threshold \(r^* = c\) is the critical reputation level for sustaining contribution.

However, this result gives us only two possibilities: all contribute or all free-ride. We cannot explain the commonly observed coexistence of contributors and free-riders. For richer dynamics, we need reputation to depend on the state of the commons.

3.3 Mechanism 2: Intrinsic Motivation (Warm Glow)

Many contributors to digital commons report that they contribute because they enjoy the work, feel satisfaction from helping others, or derive pleasure from solving problems. Economists call this “warm glow” utility. We model it as a term proportional to effort:

\[ U_i = \pi_i + w \cdot e_i, \]

where \(w > 0\) is the warm glow parameter. For contributors with \(e_i = e\):

\[ U_C = b \cdot f(xne) - c + w \cdot e = b \cdot f(xne) - (c - we). \]

This effectively reduces the cost from \(c\) to \(c - we\). If \(we > c\), contributing is dominant even without reputation. The warm glow model can explain contribution but, like constant reputation, does not generate frequency-dependent dynamics on its own.

3.4 Mechanism 3: Network Effects and Complementarities

In many digital commons, your contribution becomes more valuable when others also contribute. A Python library you write becomes more useful when there are thousands of other Python libraries to complement it. Your Wikipedia article is more valuable in an encyclopedia with millions of other articles to cross-reference. This is the feature of strategic complementarity.

Definition — Supermodular Game (Strategic Complements)

A game has strategic complementarities (is supermodular) if each player’s marginal benefit of increasing their action is increasing in the actions of other players. Formally:

\[ \frac{\partial^2 \pi_i}{\partial e_i \, \partial e_j} \geq 0 \quad \text{for all } i \neq j. \]

When others contribute more, the incentive for you to contribute also increases.

We model this by making the reputation benefit proportional to the quality of the commons:

\[ \pi_C = b \cdot Q - c + r \cdot Q, \qquad \pi_F = b \cdot Q, \]

where \(Q = f(xne)\) depends on the fraction of contributors. Now the fitness difference is:

\[ \pi_C - \pi_F = r \cdot Q - c = r \cdot f(xne) - c. \]

This is frequency-dependent: as the fraction of contributors \(x\) increases, \(Q\) increases, so the advantage of contributing grows. This creates much richer dynamics.

Theorem — Interior Equilibrium with Strategic Complementarities

With reputation proportional to commons quality and production function \(f(E) = E^\alpha\), the replicator equation is:

\[ \dot{x} = x(1-x)\bigl[r \cdot (xne)^\alpha - c\bigr]. \]

There exists an interior equilibrium at:

\[ x^* = \frac{1}{ne}\left(\frac{c}{r}\right)^{1/\alpha}. \]

This equilibrium is unstable (a threshold). For \(x > x^*\), the population converges to \(x = 1\) (all contribute). For \(x < x^*\), it converges to \(x = 0\) (all free-ride). The game is a coordination game.

Proof

At an interior equilibrium, \(\pi_C - \pi_F = 0\):

\[ r \cdot (x^* n e)^\alpha = c \quad \Longrightarrow \quad x^* = \frac{1}{ne}\left(\frac{c}{r}\right)^{1/\alpha}. \]

For stability, we check the derivative of the fitness function \(g(x) = r(xne)^\alpha - c\) with respect to \(x\):

\[ g'(x) = r \cdot \alpha \cdot (ne)^\alpha \cdot x^{\alpha - 1} > 0 \quad \text{for } x > 0. \]

Since \(g'(x) > 0\), the fitness of contributing increases with \(x\). This means:

  • For \(x < x^*\): \(g(x) < 0\), so \(\dot{x} < 0\) (contributors shrink).
  • For \(x > x^*\): \(g(x) > 0\), so \(\dot{x} > 0\) (contributors grow).

Therefore \(x^*\) is unstable, and the system is bistable with two stable equilibria: \(x = 0\) and \(x = 1\). ■

Intuition — Critical Mass and Tipping Points

This result explains a commonly observed pattern: digital commons projects either take off and become self-sustaining, or they die. If the initial community is large enough (above the threshold \(x^*\)), the project enters a virtuous cycle where contributions make the commons more valuable, which attracts more contributions. Below the threshold, the opposite vicious cycle leads to project abandonment.

This is precisely the coordination game dynamics from Module 6 (Stag Hunt), now arising endogenously from the structure of digital commons with network effects.

3.5 Mechanism 4: Low Costs Through Modularity

Open-source projects and wikis decompose large tasks into small, independent modules. Fixing a bug might take 30 minutes. Adding a missing Wikipedia sentence takes a few minutes. If the cost \(c\) is sufficiently small, the threshold \(x^*\) falls, making it easier for the commons to reach critical mass:

\[ x^* = \frac{1}{ne}\left(\frac{c}{r}\right)^{1/\alpha} \xrightarrow{c \to 0} 0. \]

In the limit of zero cost, any positive initial fraction of contributors leads to a thriving commons. This is a key insight: the design of digital collaboration tools (version control, wikis, package managers) that lower \(c\) is as important as the incentive structure.

3.6 Mechanism 5: Selective Incentives

Some contributors receive direct economic benefits that are conditional on contributing. Open-source developers get hired by companies that use their software. Academic researchers gain citations and career advancement from publishing open data. These selective incentives (in Olson’s terminology) create a subpopulation for whom \(r > c\) even without intrinsic motivation.

If a fraction \(\phi\) of the population has access to selective incentives with value \(s > c\), while the remaining \((1-\phi)\) fraction has no such incentives, the equilibrium analysis becomes richer. The population stratifies: the “incentivized” fraction always contributes, while the unincentivized fraction free-rides. The equilibrium contributor fraction is at least \(\phi\), regardless of the dynamics. If \(\phi > x^*\), the project takes off even if the general public free-rides.

4. Network Effects and Increasing Returns

Before we introduce anti-rivalry (the key concept of this module), we need to formalize network effects — the idea that the value of some goods increases with the number of users. Network effects are a precursor to, and distinct from, anti-rivalry, and understanding the distinction is important.

4.1 Direct and Indirect Network Effects

Definition — Network Effects

A good exhibits direct network effects if the value to each user increases with the total number of users. Formally, if \(V_i(n)\) is the value of the good to user \(i\) when there are \(n\) total users:

\[ \frac{dV_i}{dn} > 0. \]

A good exhibits indirect network effects if the number of users drives the production of complementary goods, which in turn increases the value of the original good.

Examples of direct network effects: telephones (more users = more people to call), social networks (more users = more content to consume), messaging apps. Examples of indirect network effects: operating systems (more users → more application developers → more apps → more valuable OS), game consoles, credit card networks.

4.2 Metcalfe’s Law and Its Refinements

The most famous model of network value is Metcalfe’s Law:

\[ V(n) = \kappa \cdot n^2, \]

where \(\kappa > 0\) is a constant. The justification is that in a network of \(n\) users, the number of possible pairwise connections is \(\binom{n}{2} = \frac{n(n-1)}{2} \approx \frac{n^2}{2}\) for large \(n\). If each connection has equal value, total network value grows quadratically.

However, Metcalfe’s Law is widely regarded as an overestimate. Not all connections are equally valuable — your 500th Facebook friend adds less value than your 5th. More realistic models include:

  • Odlyzko-Tilly model: \(V(n) = n \cdot \ln(n)\), reflecting Zipf-like diminishing value of additional connections.
  • Power-law model: \(V(n) = \kappa \cdot n^\gamma\) for \(1 < \gamma < 2\), a parameterized family between linear growth and Metcalfe’s Law.
  • Reed’s Law: For networks that support group formation, \(V(n) = 2^n\), counting the number of possible subgroups. This is generally too aggressive but may apply to some platform dynamics.
Connection to Module 3

Analyzing these value functions uses the calculus tools from Module 3. The marginal value of an additional user is \(V'(n)\): for Metcalfe’s Law, \(V'(n) = 2\kappa n\) (increasing — each additional user is more valuable); for the log model, \(V'(n) = \ln(n) + 1\) (still increasing, but slowly); for the power-law model, \(V'(n) = \kappa \gamma n^{\gamma-1}\).

4.3 Network Effects and Adoption Dynamics

Network effects create a natural connection to evolutionary game theory. Consider a population where each agent chooses whether to Adopt (A) a network good or Not Adopt (N). Let \(x\) be the fraction of adopters.

The payoff to adopting is:

\[ \pi_A(x) = V(xN) - p, \]

where \(V(xN)\) is the value with \(xN\) users out of a population \(N\), and \(p\) is the cost (price, learning effort). The payoff to not adopting is \(\pi_N = 0\) (or some outside option value).

With Metcalfe’s Law, \(V(xN) = \kappa(xN)^2\), so the payoff difference is:

\[ \pi_A - \pi_N = \kappa N^2 x^2 - p. \]

The replicator dynamics give:

\[ \dot{x} = x(1-x)(\kappa N^2 x^2 - p). \]

Setting \(\pi_A - \pi_N = 0\), the interior equilibrium is:

\[ x^* = \sqrt{\frac{p}{\kappa N^2}} = \frac{1}{N}\sqrt{\frac{p}{\kappa}}. \]
Theorem — Tipping Point Dynamics with Network Effects

Under network effects with value function \(V(xN) = \kappa(xN)^\gamma\) for \(\gamma > 1\):

  1. There exists an unstable interior equilibrium (tipping point) at \(x^* = \frac{1}{N}\left(\frac{p}{\kappa}\right)^{1/\gamma}\).
  2. For \(x < x^*\): adoption decreases toward \(x = 0\) (the good fails).
  3. For \(x > x^*\): adoption increases toward \(x = 1\) (universal adoption).

This is a coordination game with two stable equilibria (no adoption and full adoption) separated by an unstable tipping point.

Connection to Module 4

The tipping point \(x^*\) is an unstable fixed point of the differential equation \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)[\kappa(xN)^\gamma - p]\). Using the stability analysis tools from Module 4, we can verify that the eigenvalue (derivative of the right-hand side) at \(x^*\) is positive, confirming instability. Small perturbations away from \(x^*\) are amplified, pushing the system to one of the two stable states.

4.4 Critical Mass and the Chicken-and-Egg Problem

The tipping point result explains a key challenge for new digital commons: the chicken-and-egg problem. A new open-source project needs contributors to become valuable, but contributors are attracted by valuable projects. A new social platform needs users to be useful, but users join useful platforms.

Strategies to overcome the tipping point include:

  • Subsidized early adoption: Reduce \(p\) (or even make it negative through payment) to lower \(x^*\). Venture-capital-funded startups often give away services to build the user base.
  • Seeding with a core community: Start with a committed group of early contributors who ensure \(x > x^*\). Linux began with Linus Torvalds and a small group of kernel hackers. Wikipedia was seeded with content from Nupedia.
  • Compatibility with existing systems: Reduce the effective cost of switching, lowering \(p\).
Example — Wikipedia’s Growth Dynamics

When Wikipedia launched in January 2001, it had a tiny community of contributors. The project grew slowly at first (below the tipping point for broad adoption). But several factors lowered the threshold:

  • Extremely low contribution cost \(c\): anyone could edit, no credentials required.
  • Seeding from existing open content (Nupedia, public domain encyclopedias).
  • The wiki technology made the barrier to contribution nearly zero.

By late 2001, Wikipedia had crossed a critical mass. The positive feedback loop of more content → more readers → more editors → more content accelerated growth. By 2005, it had over 750,000 English articles and had become self-sustaining.

5. Anti-Rivalry — The Radical Concept

We now arrive at the central concept of this module. Standard economics recognizes two categories: rivalrous goods (your use reduces mine) and non-rivalrous goods (your use does not affect mine). We introduce a third category that fundamentally transforms the economics and game theory of digital commons: anti-rivalrous goods, where your use increases the value for me.

5.1 What is Anti-Rivalry?

The concept of anti-rivalry was articulated by Steven Weber in his 2004 book The Success of Open Source and has been further developed by scholars studying knowledge commons, open-source ecosystems, and digital infrastructure. The core insight is that some goods become more valuable precisely because more people use them.

Definition — Anti-Rivalrous Good

A good is anti-rivalrous if the value of the good to each user increases with the number of users. Formally, letting \(V_i(n)\) denote the value of the good to user \(i\) when there are \(n\) total users:

\[ \frac{\partial V_i}{\partial n} > 0 \quad \text{for all } i. \]

A good exhibits strong anti-rivalry (increasing returns to use) if additionally:

\[ \frac{\partial^2 V_i}{\partial n^2} > 0, \]

meaning each additional user adds more value than the previous one (at least over some range of \(n\)).

It is essential to distinguish anti-rivalry from mere network effects:

  • Network effects refer to the value of being connected to other users. The telephone is more valuable when more people have phones, but phone calls do not improve the telephone network itself.
  • Anti-rivalry means that the good itself improves through use. When more people use and contribute to Linux, the software gets better — more bugs are found and fixed, more features are added, more hardware is supported. The good is not just more valuable because of connectivity; it is qualitatively better.
Intuition — The Three Regimes

Think of three different goods and what happens as the number of users grows:

  • Fish in a lake (rivalrous): More fishers → fewer fish per fisher. Your gain is my loss. This is the Module 8 CPR problem.
  • A recorded song (non-rivalrous): More listeners → no change in quality. My listening to Mozart does not degrade or improve your experience of Mozart.
  • The Python programming language (anti-rivalrous): More users → more packages on PyPI → better documentation → more Stack Overflow answers → more jobs for Python developers → more people learning Python. My use of Python improves your experience of Python.

The mechanism behind anti-rivalry typically involves some combination of:

  1. User-as-contributor: Users are not passive consumers. They file bug reports, write documentation, create tutorials, answer questions, and develop extensions. Use generates contributions.
  2. Collective quality improvement: More users means more diverse testing environments, more edge cases discovered, more errors corrected.
  3. Ecosystem growth: More users attract more developers of complementary tools, creating a richer ecosystem.
  4. Knowledge accumulation: More users generate more shared knowledge (tutorials, Q&A, best practices) that benefits all users.

5.2 Mathematical Formalization of Anti-Rivalry

Let us develop a rigorous mathematical framework for anti-rivalry that contrasts it with rivalry and non-rivalry. We define a value function \(V(n)\) that gives the per-user value of a good when there are \(n\) users.

Definition — The Rivalry Classification by Value Function

Let \(V(n)\) be the per-user value when there are \(n \geq 1\) users.

  • Rivalrous: \(V'(n) < 0\). Value per user decreases with the number of users. Prototypical form: \(V(n) = \frac{R}{n}\) (fixed resource divided equally).
  • Non-rivalrous: \(V'(n) = 0\). Value per user is constant regardless of the number of users. Form: \(V(n) = V_0\).
  • Anti-rivalrous: \(V'(n) > 0\). Value per user increases with the number of users. The good gets better the more people use it.

5.2.1 A Unified Framework

We can capture all three regimes with a single parameterized family. Define:

\[ V(n) = V_0 \cdot g(n), \]

where \(V_0 > 0\) is the base value (value with a single user) and \(g(n)\) is a rivalry multiplier with \(g(1) = 1\). The behavior of \(g\) determines the regime:

  • Rivalrous: \(g'(n) < 0\), so \(g(n) < 1\) for \(n > 1\).
  • Non-rivalrous: \(g'(n) = 0\), so \(g(n) = 1\) for all \(n\).
  • Anti-rivalrous: \(g'(n) > 0\), so \(g(n) > 1\) for \(n > 1\).

5.2.2 Functional Forms for the Rivalry Multiplier

Several natural choices for \(g(n)\) capture different patterns of anti-rivalry:

Functional Forms for \(g(n)\)

1. Power law:

\[ g(n) = n^{\beta}, \quad \beta \in \mathbb{R}. \]

This is the simplest and most elegant unification. When \(\beta < 0\), the good is rivalrous (\(g(n)\) decreasing); \(\beta = 0\) gives non-rivalry (\(g(n) = 1\)); \(\beta > 0\) gives anti-rivalry (\(g(n)\) increasing). The case \(\beta = -1\) gives the classic rivalrous \(V = V_0/n\). We will use this form extensively.

2. Linear:

\[ g(n) = 1 + \alpha(n - 1), \quad \alpha \in \mathbb{R}. \]

Each additional user adds a constant increment \(\alpha\) to the value multiplier. This is analytically tractable but allows unbounded growth (or, for \(\alpha < 0\), negative values for large \(n\)).

3. Logarithmic:

\[ g(n) = 1 + \alpha \cdot \ln(n), \quad \alpha > 0. \]

Anti-rivalry with diminishing returns. Each new user adds value, but the marginal addition decreases. This may be realistic for mature commons where the easy improvements have been made.

4. Logistic (bounded anti-rivalry):

\[ g(n) = \frac{K}{1 + (K-1)e^{-\alpha(n-1)}}, \quad \alpha > 0, \; K > 1. \]

The value multiplier grows sigmoidally from \(g(1) = 1\) toward an upper bound \(K\). This models bounded anti-rivalry: the good improves with use but cannot improve beyond some maximum quality. For large \(n\), \(g(n) \to K\) and the good effectively becomes non-rivalrous.

5.2.3 Total Value and Social Welfare

The total value (social welfare from the good) is the per-user value times the number of users:

\[ W(n) = n \cdot V(n) = n \cdot V_0 \cdot g(n). \]

The behavior of total value under the three regimes is strikingly different:

  • Rivalrous (e.g., \(g(n) = n^{-1}\)): \(W(n) = n \cdot V_0 \cdot n^{-1} = V_0\). Total value is constant. This is a zero-sum (or fixed-sum) setting: adding users redistributes but does not create value.
  • Non-rivalrous (\(g(n) = 1\)): \(W(n) = n \cdot V_0\). Total value grows linearly with users. Each new user captures the full value without diminishing others. This is positive-sum.
  • Anti-rivalrous (e.g., \(g(n) = n^{\beta}\) with \(\beta > 0\)): \(W(n) = n^{1+\beta} \cdot V_0\). Total value grows superlinearly. Each new user not only captures value but creates value for existing users. This is increasingly positive-sum.
Theorem — Welfare Properties of Anti-Rivalrous Goods

For a good with value function \(V(n) = V_0 \cdot n^\beta\):

  1. The total social value is \(W(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{1+\beta}\).
  2. The marginal social value of an additional user is \(W'(n) = V_0(1+\beta)n^\beta\).
  3. If \(\beta > 0\) (anti-rivalry), then \(W'(n)\) is increasing in \(n\), meaning there are increasing returns to adoption. The social planner always wants more users.
  4. If \(\beta < 0\) (rivalry), then \(W'(n)\) is decreasing in \(n\), and for \(\beta < -1\), \(W'(n) < 0\) — more users actually reduce total welfare (overcrowding).
Proof

(1) and (2) follow by direct computation: \(W(n) = nV_0 n^\beta = V_0 n^{1+\beta}\), and \(W'(n) = V_0(1+\beta)n^\beta\).

(3) For \(\beta > 0\): \(W''(n) = V_0(1+\beta)\beta n^{\beta-1} > 0\) since \(\beta > 0\) and \(1+\beta > 0\). So \(W'\) is increasing — increasing returns to adoption.

(4) For \(\beta < 0\): \(W'(n) = V_0(1+\beta)n^\beta\). The sign of \(W'(n)\) depends on \(1+\beta\): if \(\beta > -1\), \(W'(n) > 0\) but decreasing; if \(\beta < -1\), \(W'(n) < 0\), meaning additional users reduce total welfare. ■

Intuition — Why Anti-Rivalry is Economically Revolutionary

Classical economics is built on the premise of scarcity: resources are limited, and the central problem is allocation. For rivalrous goods, this is correct — efficient allocation is paramount because waste means someone else goes without.

For non-rivalrous goods, the allocative problem disappears (everyone can have the full good), but the production problem remains (who pays the fixed cost of creating it?).

For anti-rivalrous goods, even the production problem is transformed: the good improves through use. The scarce resource is not the good itself but attention and participation. The optimal policy is not to restrict access (as with rivalrous goods) nor to subsidize production (as with standard public goods), but to maximize adoption. Every barrier to use — including intellectual property restrictions — is not just a deadweight loss (as with non-rivalrous goods) but actively degrades the good for existing users.

Example — Calculating Anti-Rivalry Multipliers

Consider a programming language ecosystem with base value \(V_0 = 10\) utility units (the value of the language to a single user with no community). Suppose the value follows a power law with \(\beta = 0.3\).

With \(n = 10\) users: \(V(10) = 10 \cdot 10^{0.3} = 10 \cdot 2.00 = 20.0\).

With \(n = 100\) users: \(V(100) = 10 \cdot 100^{0.3} = 10 \cdot 3.98 = 39.8\).

With \(n = 1{,}000\) users: \(V(1000) = 10 \cdot 1000^{0.3} = 10 \cdot 7.94 = 79.4\).

With \(n = 1{,}000{,}000\) users: \(V(10^6) = 10 \cdot (10^6)^{0.3} = 10 \cdot 63.1 = 631\).

The value to each user has increased by a factor of 63 as the community grew from 1 to 1 million. The total social value went from \(10\) to \(631{,}000{,}000\) — an increase of over 63 million times. This is the power of anti-rivalry.

5.3 Anti-Rivalry in Game Theory

Anti-rivalry fundamentally transforms the strategic structure of contribution games. To see why, let us contrast the payoff structures under the three rivalry regimes.

5.3.1 The Standard Public Goods Payoff

In the standard (non-rivalrous) public goods game from Section 2, the payoff to agent \(i\) choosing whether to contribute is:

\[ \pi_i^{\text{PG}} = b \cdot Q - c \cdot e_i, \]

where \(Q = f(\sum_j e_j)\) is the quality of the public good and \(b\) is the per-unit benefit. The value \(b \cdot Q\) is independent of the number of users — this is what non-rivalry means in payoff terms.

5.3.2 The Anti-Rivalrous Payoff

Under anti-rivalry, the value of the commons to each user depends on both the quality \(Q\) (determined by contributions) and the number of active users \(n\). We write:

\[ \pi_i^{\text{AR}} = b \cdot Q \cdot g(n) - c \cdot e_i, \]

where \(g(n)\) is the anti-rivalry multiplier from Section 5.2. There are now two channels through which value increases:

  • The quality channel: more contributions increase \(Q\).
  • The anti-rivalry channel: more users increase \(g(n)\), amplifying the value of any given quality level.

In our evolutionary model with contributors (fraction \(x\)) and free-riders (fraction \(1 - x\)), we model the interaction between these channels. Let the total population be \(N\). The quality of the commons depends on the fraction of contributors: \(Q(x) = (xN)^\alpha\) for some \(0 < \alpha \leq 1\). The number of active users is \(N\) (everyone uses the commons, since it is non-excludable and valuable).

The crucial modeling choice is that contributors get an enhanced benefit from the anti-rivalry effect, because their engagement with the good is deeper. A contributor who writes Python code benefits from the ecosystem more than a passive user. We model this as:

\[ \pi_C(x) = V(x) \cdot (1 + r) - c, \qquad \pi_F(x) = V(x), \]

where \(V(x) = b \cdot (1 + \beta x)\) captures the value of the commons that increases with the fraction of contributors (\(\beta\) is the anti-rivalry parameter), and \(r > 0\) captures the enhanced benefit to contributors (reputation, deeper engagement, skill building). The cost of contributing is \(c > 0\).

The fitness difference is:

\[ \Delta\pi(x) = \pi_C(x) - \pi_F(x) = r \cdot V(x) - c = r(1 + \beta x) \cdot b - c. \]

Expanding:

\[ \Delta\pi(x) = rb + rb\beta x - c = (rb - c) + rb\beta x. \]
Theorem — Game Structure Transformation Under Anti-Rivalry

Consider the digital commons game with fitness difference \(\Delta\pi(x) = (rb - c) + rb\beta x\). The structure of the evolutionary game depends on the parameters as follows:

  1. If \(rb > c\) and \(\beta \geq 0\): \(\Delta\pi(x) > 0\) for all \(x \in [0,1]\). Contributing is dominant — the game is a Harmony game. The unique stable equilibrium is \(x^* = 1\).
  2. If \(rb < c\) and \(rb(1 + \beta) > c\): \(\Delta\pi(0) < 0\) but \(\Delta\pi(1) > 0\). There is an interior equilibrium at \(x^* = \frac{c - rb}{rb\beta}\), and the game is a Coordination game (bistable dynamics with a tipping point).
  3. If \(rb(1 + \beta) < c\) and \(\beta > 0\): \(\Delta\pi(x) < 0\) for all \(x \in [0,1]\). Free-riding is dominant — the game is a Prisoner’s Dilemma. Anti-rivalry exists but is too weak to sustain contribution.
  4. If \(\beta \leq 0\) and \(rb < c\): \(\Delta\pi(x) < 0\) for all \(x \in [0,1]\). The standard underprovision result. Rivalry (\(\beta < 0\)) makes it even worse.
Proof

Since \(\Delta\pi(x) = (rb - c) + rb\beta x\) is linear in \(x\), it suffices to check its sign at the endpoints \(x = 0\) and \(x = 1\):

  • \(\Delta\pi(0) = rb - c\)
  • \(\Delta\pi(1) = rb - c + rb\beta = rb(1 + \beta) - c\)

Case 1: \(\Delta\pi(0) > 0\) and \(\beta \geq 0\) implies \(\Delta\pi(x) \geq \Delta\pi(0) > 0\) for all \(x\). Dominant strategy to contribute.

Case 2: \(\Delta\pi(0) < 0\) and \(\Delta\pi(1) > 0\). By the intermediate value theorem, there exists a unique \(x^* \in (0,1)\) where \(\Delta\pi(x^*) = 0\). Solving: \((rb - c) + rb\beta x^* = 0\), giving \(x^* = (c - rb)/(rb\beta)\). Since \(\Delta\pi'(x) = rb\beta > 0\) (assuming \(\beta > 0\)), the fitness of contributing is increasing in \(x\). So for \(x < x^*\), free-riding wins; for \(x > x^*\), contributing wins. This is bistable (coordination game). The interior \(x^*\) is unstable.

Case 3: Both \(\Delta\pi(0) < 0\) and \(\Delta\pi(1) < 0\). Since \(\Delta\pi\) is linear with positive slope (\(\beta > 0\)), \(\Delta\pi(x) < 0\) for all \(x \in [0,1]\). Free-riding dominates. ■

Intuition — Anti-Rivalry as a Game Transformer

The beauty of this result is that increasing the anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\) can qualitatively transform the game:

  • At \(\beta = 0\) (non-rivalrous): the game is a pure Prisoner’s Dilemma whenever \(rb < c\). Free-riding dominates.
  • At some critical \(\beta^* = \frac{c - rb}{rb}\): the game transitions from PD to a Coordination game. A tipping point emerges.
  • At \(\beta^{**} = \frac{c}{rb} - 1\) (which is the same as \(\beta^*\) when evaluated at \(x = 0\)): but as \(\beta\) increases further, \(x^*\) decreases, making the tipping point easier to reach.
  • At \(\beta \to \infty\) (or \(rb > c\)): the game becomes a Harmony game where contribution is dominant regardless of what others do.

Anti-rivalry literally transforms social dilemmas into coordination problems, and strong enough anti-rivalry eliminates the dilemma entirely.

Example — Finding the Critical Anti-Rivalry Threshold

Suppose a digital commons has parameters \(b = 5\) (base benefit), \(r = 0.3\) (reputation factor), and \(c = 2\) (contribution cost).

Without anti-rivalry (\(\beta = 0\)): \(\Delta\pi = rb - c = 0.3 \times 5 - 2 = -0.5 < 0\). Free-riding dominates. The commons will not be sustained.

The game becomes a Coordination game when \(\Delta\pi(1) > 0\): \[ rb(1 + \beta) > c \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \beta > \frac{c}{rb} - 1 = \frac{2}{1.5} - 1 = \frac{1}{3} \approx 0.33. \]

At \(\beta = 0.5\): the tipping point is at \(x^* = \frac{c - rb}{rb\beta} = \frac{2 - 1.5}{1.5 \times 0.5} = \frac{0.5}{0.75} = 0.67\). If more than 67% of the population contributes, the commons is self-sustaining.

At \(\beta = 1.0\): \(x^* = \frac{0.5}{1.5} = 0.33\). Now only 33% initial contributors are needed. Stronger anti-rivalry lowers the threshold.

At \(\beta = 2.0\): \(x^* = \frac{0.5}{3.0} = 0.17\). Just 17% is enough.

When \(rb(1 + \beta) \gg c\), the tipping point approaches zero, and any positive fraction of contributors is sufficient. This matches the observation that highly anti-rivalrous commons (like popular programming languages) seem almost impossible to kill.

5.4 Replicator Dynamics with Anti-Rivalry

We now write the full replicator equation for the digital commons game and analyze its dynamics using the tools from Modules 4 and 7.

Using the payoff functions from Section 5.3:

\[ \pi_C(x) = b(1 + \beta x)(1 + r) - c, \qquad \pi_F(x) = b(1 + \beta x), \]

the replicator equation (Module 7) is:

\[ \dot{x} = x(1-x)\bigl[\pi_C(x) - \pi_F(x)\bigr] = x(1-x)\bigl[rb(1 + \beta x) - c\bigr]. \]
Definition — The Anti-Rivalry Replicator Equation

The replicator dynamics for the digital commons with anti-rivalry parameter \(\beta\) are:

\[ \dot{x} = x(1-x)\bigl[(rb - c) + rb\beta x\bigr]. \]

This is a cubic ODE in \(x\) (the \(x(1-x)\) factor is quadratic, multiplied by the linear factor in brackets).

5.4.1 Fixed Points and Stability

The fixed points are:

  • \(x = 0\) (all free-riders)
  • \(x = 1\) (all contributors)
  • \(x^* = \frac{c - rb}{rb\beta}\) (interior, exists when \(0 < x^* < 1\))

To determine stability, we compute the derivative of the right-hand side \(F(x) = x(1-x)[(rb-c) + rb\beta x]\):

\[ F'(x) = (1-2x)[(rb-c) + rb\beta x] + x(1-x) \cdot rb\beta. \]

Evaluating at each fixed point:

Stability Analysis of the Anti-Rivalry Replicator

At \(x = 0\):

\[ F'(0) = rb - c. \]

Stable if \(rb < c\) (free-riding dominates at low contributor fraction). Unstable if \(rb > c\).

At \(x = 1\):

\[ F'(1) = -(rb - c + rb\beta) = c - rb(1 + \beta). \]

Stable if \(rb(1 + \beta) > c\) (contributing dominates at high contributor fraction). Unstable if \(rb(1 + \beta) < c\).

At \(x^*\) (when it exists in \((0,1)\)):

\[ F'(x^*) = x^*(1 - x^*) \cdot rb\beta > 0 \quad (\text{since } \beta > 0). \]

The interior equilibrium is always unstable when \(\beta > 0\) (anti-rivalry). It serves as a tipping point separating the basins of attraction of the two boundary equilibria.

5.4.2 Phase Portraits for Different Anti-Rivalry Strengths

Let us fix \(b = 5\), \(r = 0.3\), \(c = 2\) (so \(rb = 1.5\)) and vary \(\beta\):

Phase Portrait Analysis

Case 1: \(\beta = 0\) (non-rivalrous, standard public good)

\(\Delta\pi(x) = 1.5 - 2 = -0.5 < 0\) for all \(x\). The flow is always toward \(x = 0\). Free-riding wins unconditionally. This is the Prisoner’s Dilemma regime.

Case 2: \(\beta = 0.5\) (moderate anti-rivalry)

\(\Delta\pi(x) = -0.5 + 0.75x\). Zero at \(x^* = 0.5/0.75 = 0.667\). \(\Delta\pi(0) = -0.5 < 0\), \(\Delta\pi(1) = 0.25 > 0\). This is a Coordination game. Below \(x^* = 0.667\), the commons collapses; above it, the commons thrives.

Case 3: \(\beta = 1.0\) (strong anti-rivalry)

\(\Delta\pi(x) = -0.5 + 1.5x\). Zero at \(x^* = 0.333\). The tipping point is much lower. Only one-third of the population needs to contribute initially for the commons to become self-sustaining.

Case 4: \(\beta = 2.0\) (very strong anti-rivalry)

\(\Delta\pi(x) = -0.5 + 3.0x\). Zero at \(x^* = 0.167\). The tipping point is now just one-sixth. Digital commons with very strong anti-rivalry (like programming language ecosystems) can bootstrap from small communities.

Connection to Module 4

The replicator equation \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)[(rb-c) + rb\beta x]\) is a one-dimensional ODE whose solution can be analyzed with the phase-line methods from Module 4. The direction of flow at each point is determined by the sign of \(F(x)\). The fixed points partition \([0,1]\) into intervals with consistent flow direction. The unstable interior fixed point \(x^*\) acts as a separatrix dividing the basins of attraction.

5.4.3 Comparison with CPR Dynamics

It is instructive to contrast the anti-rivalry replicator dynamics with the CPR dynamics from Module 8. In the CPR game, increasing the number of extractors (cooperators who restrain harvesting vs. defectors who over-harvest) creates negative frequency dependence: more defectors degrade the resource, making cooperation less attractive. The CPR replicator often has a stable interior equilibrium.

Under anti-rivalry, we see the opposite: positive frequency dependence. More contributors make contributing more attractive (because the commons becomes more valuable, amplifying the benefits of deeper engagement). This leads to unstable interior equilibria and bistable dynamics. The two systems are mirror images:

Feature CPR (Module 8) Digital Commons (Anti-Rivalry)
Resource dynamics Depleted by use Enhanced by use
Frequency dependence Negative (more defectors → less incentive to cooperate) Positive (more contributors → more incentive to contribute)
Interior equilibrium Often stable (coexistence) Unstable (tipping point)
Long-run outcome Mixed population or tragedy All contribute or all free-ride

5.5 Examples of Anti-Rivalry in Practice

Let us examine concrete examples of anti-rivalrous goods and verify that they fit our mathematical framework.

Example — Open-Source Software (Linux, Python, Firefox)

Mechanism: Each new user of Linux is a potential contributor of bug reports, documentation, translations, and code. Each new contributor improves the software for all users. The kernel’s stability, hardware support, and feature set have grown monotonically with the contributor base.

Anti-rivalry fit: Let \(n\) be the number of active Linux users. The quality of the kernel (driver support, bug fixes, security patches) is an increasing function of \(n\). Linus’s Law states: “Given enough eyeballs, all bugs are shallow.” Formally, the bug-detection rate scales as \(\lambda(n) = \lambda_0 \cdot n^\gamma\) for some \(\gamma > 0\), meaning the software quality \(Q = Q_{\max}(1 - e^{-\lambda(n) t})\) improves faster with more users.

Estimated \(\beta\): Moderate to strong (\(\beta \approx 0.3\text{--}0.7\)). The ecosystem is highly anti-rivalrous because of the diversity of use cases driving comprehensive testing and feature development.

Example — Wikipedia

Mechanism: Each reader is a potential editor. More editors produce more articles, which attract more readers, who become editors. More editors also improve quality through cross-referencing, fact-checking, and filling gaps.

Anti-rivalry fit: The value of any given Wikipedia article increases when related articles are created (because of internal links, context, and the network effect of knowledge). More users also generate more language translations, making knowledge accessible globally.

Estimated \(\beta\): Moderate (\(\beta \approx 0.2\text{--}0.5\)). The anti-rivalry effect is bounded — beyond a certain number of editors, the marginal improvement per additional editor diminishes (logistic model is appropriate).

Example — Programming Languages (Python, JavaScript, R)

Mechanism: More users of Python → more packages on PyPI → better documentation on Stack Overflow → more tutorials and courses → more employer demand → more people learning Python → even more users. This is the strongest anti-rivalry feedback loop in the digital economy.

Anti-rivalry fit: The value function \(V(n) = V_0 \cdot n^\beta\) with \(\beta > 0\) applies well. The number of available packages grows roughly as a power of the user base, and each package increases the utility for all users.

Estimated \(\beta\): Strong (\(\beta \approx 0.5\text{--}1.0\)). Programming language ecosystems exhibit some of the strongest anti-rivalry of any good.

Example — Standards and Protocols (TCP/IP, HTML, Unicode)

Mechanism: The value of the TCP/IP protocol suite increases with every device connected to the internet. HTML becomes more valuable as more content is created in HTML. Unicode becomes more useful as more software supports it.

Anti-rivalry fit: Standards exhibit very strong anti-rivalry because adoption drives compatibility, which is the primary source of value. The more devices speak TCP/IP, the more valuable it is for every device.

Estimated \(\beta\): Very strong (\(\beta \approx 0.7\text{--}1.5\)). However, much of this is network effects (connectivity value) rather than pure anti-rivalry (the standard itself does not improve with use; it improves through deliberate revision processes).

Non-Example — A Movie File (Non-Rivalrous but NOT Anti-Rivalrous)

A digital movie file is perfectly non-rivalrous: copying it costs nothing, and one person watching does not degrade another’s experience. However, it is not anti-rivalrous: 10 million people watching a movie does not make the movie any better. The content is fixed at creation time. More viewers do not improve the film.

This distinction is critical: non-rivalry (\(\beta = 0\)) is not the same as anti-rivalry (\(\beta > 0\)). Anti-rivalry requires that use itself generates value improvements, not merely that use is non-depleting.

5.6 The Anti-Rivalry Spectrum

Rather than a binary classification (rivalrous vs. non-rivalrous), we propose a continuous spectrum parameterized by \(\beta\):

\[ V_i(n) = V_0 \cdot n^{\beta}, \qquad \beta \in \mathbb{R}. \]
Definition — The Rivalry Spectrum

The rivalry parameter \(\beta\) places a good on a continuous spectrum:

  • \(\beta \ll 0\): Strongly rivalrous. Example: fish in a finite lake (\(\beta \approx -1\)). The CPR problem of Module 8.
  • \(\beta \lesssim 0\): Weakly rivalrous. Example: a highway subject to congestion. More users reduce value, but not in proportion.
  • \(\beta = 0\): Non-rivalrous. Example: a recorded song, a movie file, a published theorem.
  • \(0 < \beta \lesssim 0.5\): Weakly anti-rivalrous. Example: a social media platform, a modest open-data project.
  • \(\beta \gtrsim 0.5\): Strongly anti-rivalrous. Example: an open-source programming language ecosystem, a major collaborative knowledge base like Wikipedia.

5.6.1 Phase Transitions in Game Structure

The most profound implication of the rivalry spectrum is that as \(\beta\) moves from negative to positive, the game-theoretic structure undergoes qualitative phase transitions. Using our model with \(\Delta\pi(x) = (rb - c) + rb\beta x\):

Theorem — Phase Transitions Along the Rivalry Spectrum

Fix \(r, b, c > 0\) with \(rb < c\) (so that contributing is not trivially dominant). As \(\beta\) increases through the rivalry spectrum:

  1. Phase I (\(\beta < 0\), rivalry): \(\Delta\pi(x) < 0\) for all \(x \in [0,1]\) (since both the constant term and the slope are negative). Free-riding is dominant. The game is a Prisoner’s Dilemma. This is the tragedy of the commons.
  2. Phase II (\(0 \leq \beta < \beta^*\), weak anti-rivalry): where \(\beta^* = \frac{c - rb}{rb}\). Still \(\Delta\pi(x) < 0\) for all \(x \in [0,1]\) because the slope is positive but too gentle to make \(\Delta\pi(1) > 0\). Free-riding still dominates. The anti-rivalry is too weak to help.
  3. Phase III (\(\beta = \beta^*\), critical point): \(\Delta\pi(1) = 0\). The game is at the boundary between PD and Coordination. The boundary \(x = 1\) becomes a non-hyperbolic (marginally stable) fixed point.
  4. Phase IV (\(\beta > \beta^*\), strong anti-rivalry): \(\Delta\pi(0) < 0\) but \(\Delta\pi(1) > 0\). The game is a Coordination game with tipping point \(x^* = \frac{c - rb}{rb\beta} \in (0,1)\). The tipping point decreases as \(\beta\) increases.
  5. Phase V (\(\beta \to \infty\) or \(rb > c\)): The tipping point \(x^* \to 0\). The game approaches a Harmony game where contributing is dominant for almost any initial condition.

This theorem shows that the rivalry parameter \(\beta\) acts as a bifurcation parameter for the evolutionary dynamics. At \(\beta = \beta^*\), the system undergoes a transcritical bifurcation: the stable boundary equilibrium \(x = 1\) exchanges stability with the interior equilibrium \(x^*\) as \(x^*\) enters the interval \([0,1]\) from above.

Connection to Module 4

The transcritical bifurcation is one of the canonical bifurcation types studied in dynamical systems theory (Module 4). As the parameter \(\beta\) passes through \(\beta^*\), two fixed points (the boundary \(x = 1\) and the interior \(x^*\)) collide and exchange their stability. This is a standard result in bifurcation theory, now given a novel economic interpretation: the rivalry parameter determines whether the digital commons can sustain itself.

6. Institutional Design for Digital Commons

Ostrom’s design principles (Module 8) were developed for rivalrous common-pool resources. Digital commons are non-rivalrous or anti-rivalrous, requiring significant adaptation of institutional design theory. In this section, we analyze how governance structures for digital commons differ from CPR governance, and develop game-theoretic models of key institutional choices.

6.1 The Fundamental Paradox

Intuition — From Preventing Overuse to Encouraging Contribution

Ostrom’s CPR framework addresses the problem of overuse: too many fishers depleting the fishery, too many herders overgrazing the commons. The design principles create institutions that restrict access, monitor extraction, and punish overuse.

For digital commons, the resource grows with use. The design challenge flips: instead of preventing overuse, the institution must encourage contribution, maintain quality (against spam and vandalism), and govern the direction of development. The scarce resource is not the commons itself but the attention, effort, and coordination capacity of the community.

6.2 Governance Models

Successful digital commons have converged on several governance models, each with distinct game-theoretic properties:

Governance Archetypes for Digital Commons
  • BDFL (Benevolent Dictator for Life): One person has final authority over all decisions. Examples: Linux (Linus Torvalds, until 2018), Python (Guido van Rossum, until 2018). Game-theoretically: a dictator game where the BDFL’s credibility comes from the community’s exit option (forking).
  • Meritocracy: Authority is earned through demonstrated contribution. Example: Apache Software Foundation. Contributors become committers, then PMC members, then Foundation members. Game-theoretically: a tournament/signaling game where effort is rewarded with governance power.
  • Democratic/Consensus: Decisions are made by community vote or rough consensus. Example: IETF (“rough consensus and running code”), Debian. Game-theoretically: a voting game subject to Arrow’s impossibility theorem and Condorcet cycling.
  • Corporate-backed: A company sponsors and controls the project while keeping it nominally open. Examples: Android (Google), React (Meta), VS Code (Microsoft). Game-theoretically: a principal-agent problem where the sponsoring corporation is the principal.

6.3 Licensing as a Game

One of the most consequential institutional choices for a digital commons is the license. In the open-source world, two broad categories exist:

  • Copyleft (e.g., GPL): Any derivative work must also be open-source under the same license. This is a “viral” or “reciprocal” license.
  • Permissive (e.g., MIT, Apache, BSD): Derivative works can be proprietary. Users can incorporate the code into closed-source products.

We can model the licensing choice as a game. Consider two firms that each produce software that can be open-sourced or kept proprietary. Each firm chooses a strategy: Copyleft (CL) or Permissive (P).

Example — The Licensing Game

Consider the payoff matrix where firms benefit from shared code but also from proprietary advantage:

Firm 2: Copyleft Firm 2: Permissive
Firm 1: Copyleft \((s + a, \; s + a)\) \((s, \; s + d)\)
Firm 1: Permissive \((s + d, \; s)\) \((s + d/2, \; s + d/2)\)

Here \(s\) is the base value of the shared code, \(a\) is the anti-rivalry benefit when both use copyleft (contributions must flow back), and \(d\) is the proprietary advantage from incorporating open code into closed products.

If \(a > d\): (Copyleft, Copyleft) is the dominant strategy equilibrium. The anti-rivalry benefit of mandatory sharing exceeds the proprietary advantage. This explains why the Linux kernel ecosystem coalesced around the GPL.

If \(a < d\): (Permissive, Permissive) dominates. The proprietary advantage exceeds the commons benefit. This explains why many corporate-backed projects use MIT/Apache licenses.

If \(d/2 < a < d\): The game is a Prisoner’s Dilemma where (Copyleft, Copyleft) is Pareto-optimal but (Permissive, Permissive) is the Nash equilibrium.

6.4 Forking as Credible Exit

A unique feature of open-source governance is the fork: any community member can copy the entire codebase and start an independent project. This is the ultimate exit option in Hirschman’s Exit-Voice-Loyalty framework.

Game-theoretically, the credible threat of forking disciplines governance. If a BDFL or a corporate sponsor makes decisions that harm the community, contributors can fork. The cost of forking includes:

  • Loss of the unified community (network effects and anti-rivalry are split between forks).
  • Duplication of effort (both forks must maintain parallel codebases).
  • Brand confusion and loss of trust.

The benefit of forking is escaping bad governance. Let \(G\) be the governance quality (higher is better), \(F\) be the cost of forking, and \(A(\beta, n)\) be the anti-rivalry value lost by splitting the community. The fork occurs when:

\[ \text{Value under bad governance} < \text{Value of fork} - F - A(\beta, n). \]

Interestingly, anti-rivalry makes forking more costly (because splitting the user base destroys anti-rivalry value), which paradoxically increases the power of bad governors. This creates a tension: anti-rivalry both increases the value of the commons and increases the community’s vulnerability to governance capture.

Connection to Module 6

The forking game is structurally similar to the Ultimatum Game from Module 6. The governor (proposer) chooses a governance policy. The community (responder) can accept or “reject” by forking. Rational proposers anticipate the fork threat and moderate their governance. The result is a subgame-perfect equilibrium where governance quality is bounded below by the fork threshold.

Exercise 1 — Classifying Goods on the Rivalry Spectrum

For each of the following goods, estimate the rivalry parameter \(\beta\) and justify your reasoning:

  1. A community vegetable garden (shared plot, limited space)
  2. A published mathematical proof
  3. The Rust programming language ecosystem
  4. A shared highway during rush hour
  5. A Creative Commons textbook with a community errata page
Solution

(a) Community vegetable garden: \(\beta \approx -0.8\). Strongly rivalrous — space and soil nutrients are limited. More gardeners means less produce per person. Close to the classic CPR.

(b) Published mathematical proof: \(\beta = 0\). Non-rivalrous. The proof is unchanged regardless of how many people read it. However, if people build on the proof, extend it, find new applications, one might argue for weak anti-rivalry (\(\beta \approx 0.1\)).

(c) Rust programming language: \(\beta \approx 0.5\text{--}0.8\). Strongly anti-rivalrous. More users means more crates (packages), better tooling, more learning resources, better compiler development, more job opportunities. Each new user-contributor makes the ecosystem more valuable for all.

(d) Shared highway during rush hour: \(\beta \approx -0.5\). Rivalrous due to congestion, but not as extreme as \(\beta = -1\) because the road is not “consumed” in the same way as fish. Congestion reduces speed (value) but the road remains.

(e) Creative Commons textbook with errata page: \(\beta \approx 0.2\text{--}0.3\). Weakly anti-rivalrous. The textbook itself is non-rivalrous (\(\beta = 0\)), but the community errata page means more readers find and report errors, improving the text for future readers. The anti-rivalry is moderate because not all readers contribute errata.

Exercise 2 — Nash Equilibrium of a Digital Commons

Consider a digital commons with \(n = 50\) potential contributors, benefit parameter \(b = 8\), cost parameter \(c = 3\), and production function \(f(E) = E^{0.4}\).

  1. Compute the symmetric Nash equilibrium effort \(e^*\) and total effort \(E^{\text{NE}}\).
  2. Compute the socially optimal total effort \(E^{\text{SO}}\).
  3. What is the ratio \(E^{\text{NE}} / E^{\text{SO}}\)? Interpret the result.
  4. How does the ratio change if \(n\) increases to 500?
Solution

(a) From the Nash equilibrium formula: \[ E^{\text{NE}} = \left(\frac{b\alpha}{c}\right)^{1/(1-\alpha)} = \left(\frac{8 \times 0.4}{3}\right)^{1/0.6} = \left(\frac{3.2}{3}\right)^{5/3} = (1.0\overline{6})^{5/3}. \] Computing: \(1.0\overline{6}^{5/3} = e^{(5/3)\ln(1.0\overline{6})} = e^{(5/3)(0.0645)} = e^{0.1076} \approx 1.114\). So \(E^{\text{NE}} \approx 1.114\) and \(e^* = E^{\text{NE}}/50 \approx 0.0223\).

(b) Social optimum: \[ E^{\text{SO}} = \left(\frac{nb\alpha}{c}\right)^{1/(1-\alpha)} = \left(\frac{50 \times 3.2}{3}\right)^{5/3} = \left(\frac{160}{3}\right)^{5/3} = (53.\overline{3})^{5/3}. \] Computing: \(53.\overline{3}^{5/3} = e^{(5/3)\ln(53.33)} = e^{(5/3)(3.976)} = e^{6.627} \approx 756.8\). So \(E^{\text{SO}} \approx 756.8\).

(c) Ratio: \[ \frac{E^{\text{NE}}}{E^{\text{SO}}} = n^{-1/(1-\alpha)} = 50^{-5/3} = \frac{1}{50^{5/3}} \approx \frac{1}{679.6} \approx 0.00147. \] The Nash equilibrium provides only about 0.15% of the socially optimal effort. The underprovision is extreme.

(d) With \(n = 500\): \[ \frac{E^{\text{NE}}}{E^{\text{SO}}} = 500^{-5/3} = \frac{1}{500^{5/3}} \approx \frac{1}{31{,}498} \approx 0.0000317. \] With a 10× larger community, the underprovision ratio decreased by a factor of about 46. Larger communities underprovide proportionally more.

Exercise 3 — Conditions for Contributor Survival

In the binary contributor/free-rider model with reputation proportional to commons quality, payoffs are \(\pi_C = b \cdot (xne)^\alpha - c + r \cdot (xne)^\alpha\) and \(\pi_F = b \cdot (xne)^\alpha\).

  1. Show that the interior equilibrium is \(x^* = \frac{1}{ne}\left(\frac{c}{r}\right)^{1/\alpha}\).
  2. For \(n = 100\), \(e = 1\), \(\alpha = 0.5\), \(c = 4\), find the minimum reputation benefit \(r\) for which an interior equilibrium exists in \((0,1)\).
  3. Is this interior equilibrium stable or unstable? What does this mean for the long-run outcome of the commons?
Solution

(a) At the interior equilibrium, \(\pi_C - \pi_F = 0\): \[ r \cdot (x^* ne)^\alpha = c \implies (x^* ne)^\alpha = \frac{c}{r} \implies x^* ne = \left(\frac{c}{r}\right)^{1/\alpha} \implies x^* = \frac{1}{ne}\left(\frac{c}{r}\right)^{1/\alpha}. \]

(b) For an interior equilibrium to exist in \((0,1)\), we need \(0 < x^* < 1\): \[ 0 < \frac{1}{100}\left(\frac{4}{r}\right)^{2} < 1 \implies \left(\frac{4}{r}\right)^2 < 100 \implies \frac{4}{r} < 10 \implies r > 0.4. \] Also \(x^* > 0\) requires \(r > 0\), which is always satisfied. So the minimum reputation benefit is \(r_{\min} = 0.4\).

At \(r = 0.4\): \(x^* = \frac{1}{100}(10)^2 = 1\), which is the boundary. For \(r\) strictly greater than 0.4, \(x^*\) drops below 1.

For example, at \(r = 1\): \(x^* = \frac{1}{100}(4)^2 = 0.16\). At \(r = 2\): \(x^* = \frac{1}{100}(2)^2 = 0.04\).

(c) The interior equilibrium is unstable (as proved in Theorem 3.4 of the module). It acts as a tipping point. Below \(x^*\), the commons collapses to \(x = 0\); above \(x^*\), it grows to \(x = 1\). The long-run outcome is binary: either the commons thrives with full contribution, or it dies with full free-riding. There is no stable coexistence.

Exercise 4 — Anti-Rivalry Parameter from Empirical Data

Value \(V(n)\) 12 22 40 75

  • \(V(100) = 6.52 \times 100^{0.265} = 6.52 \times 3.388 = 22.1\). Data: 22. Excellent fit.

Exercise 5 — CPR vs Digital Commons Dynamics

Compare two communities, each with 100 members:

  • Community A manages a fishery (CPR) with replicator dynamics \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)[2 - 3x]\), where \(x\) is the fraction of “sustainable harvesters.”
  • Community B manages an open-source project with replicator dynamics \(\dot{x} = x(1-x)[-0.5 + 1.5x]\), where \(x\) is the fraction of contributors.
  1. Find all fixed points for both systems and determine their stability.
  2. Sketch the phase lines for both systems on \([0,1]\).
  3. Starting at \(x_0 = 0.5\), what is the long-run outcome for each community?
  4. Explain the qualitative difference between the two systems in terms of rivalry vs anti-rivalry.
Solution

Community A (CPR):

Fixed points: \(x = 0\), \(x = 1\), and \(x^* = 2/3\) (from \(2 - 3x = 0\)).

Stability: \(g(x) = 2 - 3x\), \(g'(x) = -3 < 0\). So \(g\) is decreasing. For \(x < 2/3\): \(g(x) > 0\), \(\dot{x} > 0\) (sustainable harvesters grow). For \(x > 2/3\): \(g(x) < 0\), \(\dot{x} < 0\) (sustainable harvesters decline). Therefore \(x^* = 2/3\) is stable. Also \(x = 0\) is unstable, \(x = 1\) is unstable. The system converges to the interior equilibrium.

Community B (Digital Commons):

Fixed points: \(x = 0\), \(x = 1\), and \(x^* = 0.5/1.5 = 1/3\) (from \(-0.5 + 1.5x = 0\)).

Stability: \(g(x) = -0.5 + 1.5x\), \(g'(x) = 1.5 > 0\). So \(g\) is increasing. For \(x < 1/3\): \(g(x) < 0\), \(\dot{x} < 0\) (contributors shrink). For \(x > 1/3\): \(g(x) > 0\), \(\dot{x} > 0\) (contributors grow). Therefore \(x^* = 1/3\) is unstable. \(x = 0\) is stable, \(x = 1\) is stable. Bistable dynamics with tipping point.

(b) Phase lines:

  • Community A: \(0 \xrightarrow{\rightarrow} 2/3 \xleftarrow{\leftarrow} 1\). Arrows point toward \(2/3\) from both sides.
  • Community B: \(0 \xleftarrow{\leftarrow} 1/3 \xrightarrow{\rightarrow} 1\). Arrows point away from \(1/3\).

(c) Starting at \(x_0 = 0.5\):

  • Community A: \(0.5 < 2/3\), so \(\dot{x} > 0\). The system converges to \(x^* = 2/3\). Two-thirds of the community practices sustainable harvesting; one-third over-harvests. A stable but imperfect outcome.
  • Community B: \(0.5 > 1/3\), so \(\dot{x} > 0\). The system converges to \(x = 1\). All community members become contributors. The commons thrives.

(d) The CPR system has negative frequency dependence: the fitness of sustainable harvesting decreases as more people do it (because the resource is less threatened, reducing the relative advantage of restraint). This creates a stable interior equilibrium — coexistence.

The digital commons has positive frequency dependence (anti-rivalry): the fitness of contributing increases as more people contribute (because the commons becomes more valuable). This creates an unstable interior equilibrium — a tipping point. The outcome is all-or-nothing. This qualitative difference arises directly from the sign of the rivalry parameter \(\beta\).

Exercise 6 — Anti-Rivalry Transforms PD into Harmony

Consider the digital commons game with payoffs:

\[ \pi_C(x) = b(1 + \beta x)(1 + r) - c, \qquad \pi_F(x) = b(1 + \beta x). \]

Let \(b = 4\), \(r = 0.4\), \(c = 2\).

  1. Show that at \(\beta = 0\), the game is a Prisoner’s Dilemma (free-riding dominates).
  2. Find the critical \(\beta^*\) at which the game transitions from PD to a Coordination game.
  3. Find the value of \(\beta\) at which the game becomes a Harmony game (contributing dominates).
  4. Prove that for any \(b, r, c\) with \(rb < c\), there always exists a finite \(\beta^*\) that transforms the PD into a Coordination game, provided \(r > 0\).
Solution

(a) At \(\beta = 0\): \(\Delta\pi = rb - c = 0.4 \times 4 - 2 = 1.6 - 2 = -0.4 < 0\). The fitness difference is negative for all \(x\). Free-riding dominates. This is a Prisoner’s Dilemma.

(b) The game becomes a Coordination game when \(\Delta\pi(1) > 0\): \[ rb(1 + \beta) - c > 0 \implies 1.6(1 + \beta) > 2 \implies 1 + \beta > 1.25 \implies \beta > 0.25. \] So \(\beta^* = 0.25\). At \(\beta = 0.25\), the tipping point is at \(x^* = \frac{c - rb}{rb\beta} = \frac{0.4}{1.6 \times 0.25} = \frac{0.4}{0.4} = 1\) (right at the boundary). For \(\beta\) slightly above 0.25, the tipping point enters \((0,1)\).

(c) The game becomes a Harmony game when \(\Delta\pi(0) > 0\): \[ rb - c > 0 \implies 1.6 > 2. \] This does not depend on \(\beta\)! With \(rb < c\), the game can never be a Harmony game in our linear model (since \(\Delta\pi(0) = rb - c\) is independent of \(\beta\)).

However, as \(\beta \to \infty\), the tipping point \(x^* = \frac{c - rb}{rb\beta} \to 0\). The basin of attraction for \(x = 1\) covers almost all of \((0,1)\), so the game is “effectively” a Harmony game (any positive initial contributor fraction leads to full contribution). Formally, there is no finite \(\beta\) that makes it a true Harmony game in this model, but the practical difference vanishes.

(d) For general \(b, r, c\) with \(rb < c\) and \(r > 0\): The transition to Coordination requires \(rb(1 + \beta^*) = c\), giving \[ \beta^* = \frac{c - rb}{rb} = \frac{c}{rb} - 1. \] Since \(rb < c\), we have \(\beta^* > 0\). Since \(r > 0\) and \(b > 0\), \(\beta^*\) is finite. Therefore, for any parameter values where the standard game is a PD, there exists a finite anti-rivalry strength that transforms it into a Coordination game. ■

Exercise 7 — Ostrom’s Principles Adapted for Wikipedia

For each of Ostrom’s eight design principles (see Module 8):

  1. Identify how Wikipedia implements (or fails to implement) the principle.
  2. Explain whether the principle needs modification for a non-rivalrous, anti-rivalrous digital commons.
  3. For the principles that need modification, propose an adapted version suitable for digital commons governance.
Solution

Principle 1 (Clear boundaries):

Wikipedia implementation: Reading is open to all; editing is open to anyone with an account (or even without). Boundaries are very loose by design. This is the opposite of CPR boundaries, where the goal is to restrict access.

Wikipedia: Policies are determined by community consensus through discussion pages, RfCs (Requests for Comment), and community votes. Any registered user can participate.

Adaptation: Works well for digital commons. The low cost of participation in online discussion makes collective choice easier than in physical CPR settings.

Adaptation: Translates well. Digital commons may need additional mechanisms for content disputes (which are unique to knowledge commons).

Wikipedia: The Wikimedia Foundation provides legal protection and infrastructure but does not dictate editorial policy. Community self-governance is respected.

Adaptation: For digital commons, this principle extends to license choice — the legal framework (Creative Commons, GPL, etc.) that protects the commons from enclosure.

Principle 8 (Nested enterprises):

Wikipedia: Individual editors → WikiProjects → language editions → Wikimedia Foundation. Well-developed nested governance.

Adaptation: Translates directly. Digital commons often have natural nesting (subsystems, subprojects, umbrella organizations).

Exercise 8 — Network Effects and Tipping Points

A new open-source project has a total potential user base of \(N = 1000\). The adoption dynamics follow the replicator equation with Metcalfe-like network effects:

\[ \dot{x} = x(1-x)\left[\kappa (xN)^{1.5} - p\right], \]

where \(\kappa = 0.001\) and \(p = 10\) (cost of adoption).

  1. Find the tipping point \(x^*\).
  2. If the project starts with 50 users (\(x_0 = 0.05\)), will it succeed or fail?
  3. What is the minimum number of “seed users” needed for the project to take off?
  4. If the project reduces adoption cost to \(p = 5\) (e.g., through better documentation), how does the tipping point change?
Solution

(a) Setting the bracket to zero: \[ \kappa(x^* N)^{1.5} = p \implies x^* = \frac{1}{N}\left(\frac{p}{\kappa}\right)^{1/1.5} = \frac{1}{1000}\left(\frac{10}{0.001}\right)^{2/3} = \frac{1}{1000}(10{,}000)^{2/3}. \] \((10{,}000)^{2/3} = (10^4)^{2/3} = 10^{8/3} \approx 10^{2.667} \approx 464.2\). So \(x^* = 464.2/1000 = 0.464\).

(b) Starting at \(x_0 = 0.05 < 0.464 = x^*\). Since \(x_0 < x^*\), the project is below the tipping point. The dynamics push \(x\) toward 0. The project fails.

(c) The minimum number of seed users is \(x^* \times N = 0.464 \times 1000 = 464\). At least 465 initial users are needed for the project to tip into self-sustaining growth.

(d) With \(p = 5\): \[ x^* = \frac{1}{1000}\left(\frac{5}{0.001}\right)^{2/3} = \frac{1}{1000}(5000)^{2/3}. \] \((5000)^{2/3} = e^{(2/3)\ln 5000} = e^{(2/3)(8.517)} = e^{5.678} \approx 292.4\). So \(x^* = 292.4/1000 = 0.292\), requiring about 293 seed users.

Halving the adoption cost reduced the required critical mass from 465 to 293 — a 37% reduction. This shows why investing in documentation, tutorials, and easy installation is a high-leverage strategy for bootstrapping digital commons.
